<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Green National Convention</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For the next convention, see <a href="/wiki/2016_Green_National_Convention" title="2016 Green National Convention">2016 Green National Convention</a>.</div>
<p>The <b>Green National Convention</b> is the <a href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_nominating_convention" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. presidential nominating convention">Presidential nominating convention</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party of the United States</a> (GPUS).  Though the <a href="/wiki/Green_National_Committee" title="Green National Committee">Green National Committee</a> meets annually in a "national meeting", the convention is convened by the GNC once every four years in order to nominate an official candidate in the upcoming <a href="/wiki/U.S._Presidential_election" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Presidential election">U.S. Presidential election</a>, and to officially adopt the party platform and rules for the election cycle.
</p><p>The convention signals the end of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_primary" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. presidential primary">primary</a> season for Greens, and the start of campaigning for the general election of that year; as such, prominent Greens in state and local races are also spotlighted in order to give them the publicity that the convention affords.  In contrast to the <a href="/wiki/Republican_National_Convention" title="Republican National Convention">Republican</a> and <a href="/wiki/Democratic_National_Convention" title="Democratic National Convention">Democratic National Conventions</a>, however, the convention is not merely a formality which endorses the will of the electorate in the primaries, but the final determinant for the nomination.  This is because <a href="/wiki/Ballot_access" title="Ballot access">ballot access</a> laws in many states prohibit the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Party (United States)">Green Party</a> from holding a state-sponsored primary election—only five states were able to meet the requirements in 2004—and others prevent voters from actually registering into the party, thus making it difficult to credential Greens.
</p><p>As such, Green conventions often have a degree of contentiousness which was seen only in past conventions of the Democratic and Republican Parties, prior to reforms in 1968 and 1972, respectively.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_National_Convention&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The leadership of the first nationwide Green organization in the United States, The Greens/Green Party USA (G/GPUSA), were generally opposed to an entry into electoral politics.  Thus, the G/GPUSA's national meeting in <a href="/wiki/Albuquerque,_New_Mexico" title="Albuquerque, New Mexico">Albuquerque, New Mexico</a>, in 1995 ended with a resolution not to nominate a candidate for the <a href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1996" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. presidential election, 1996">1996 election</a>.  However, a group of Greens interested in a run by consumer advocate <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a> met in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles</a> in 1996 to nominate Nader for <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> and <a href="/wiki/Winona_LaDuke" title="Winona LaDuke">Winona LaDuke</a> for <a href="/wiki/Vice-President_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Vice-President of the United States">Vice-President</a>.  Arguably, though it lacked official sanction, this was the first nominating convention of the Green Party in the United States, and the only one with which the G/GPUSA, if only certain members, was at all involved.
</p><p>Following the general election in 1996, several state Green Parties formed the Association of State Green Parties (ASGP).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> As such, they met in <a href="/wiki/Denver,_Colorado" class="mw-redirect" title="Denver, Colorado">Denver, Colorado</a> in 2000 to once again nominate Nader and LaDuke.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2001, the ASGP formally adopted the name <i>Green Party of the United States</i>, and was recognized by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Election_Commission" title="Federal Election Commission">Federal Election Commission</a> as the official National Committee of the Green Party.  The first convention under that name, in <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee,_Wisconsin" class="mw-redirect" title="Milwaukee, Wisconsin">Milwaukee, Wisconsin</a> in 2004, was not without controversy.  Nader decided to forgo the Green nomination in favor of asking for an "endorsement" of his independent candidacy; the Greens instead nominated <a href="/wiki/David_Cobb" title="David Cobb">David Cobb</a> for President on the second ballot from among a number of candidates, many of whom were "favorite sons", running as placeholders for Nader.  <a href="/wiki/Pat_LaMarche" title="Pat LaMarche">Pat LaMarche</a> was named the Vice-Presidential candidate.
</p><p>As a result of the 2004 Convention, and allegations made by some Green candidates, including <a href="/wiki/Peter_Camejo" title="Peter Camejo">Peter Camejo</a>, the GPUS have appointed a <a href="/wiki/Working_group" title="Working group">working group</a> headed by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Kramer_(politician)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Steve Kramer (politician) (page does not exist)">Steve Kramer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Arnone" title="Elizabeth Arnone">Elizabeth Arnone</a> to review the rules for the Green nominating convention, such as delegate allocation and floor rules.
</p>
