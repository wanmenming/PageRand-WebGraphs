<p>The <b>Green Revolution</b>, or <b>Third Agricultural Revolution</b>, refers to a set of research and the development of <a href="/wiki/Technology_transfer" title="Technology transfer">technology transfer</a> initiatives occurring between 1950 and the late 1960s, that increased agricultural production worldwide, particularly in the developing world, beginning most markedly in the late 1960s.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The initiatives resulted in the adoption of new technologies, including <a href="/wiki/High-yielding_varieties" class="mw-redirect" title="High-yielding varieties">high-yielding varieties</a> (HYVs) of cereals, especially <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_wheat" class="mw-redirect" title="Dwarf wheat">dwarf wheats</a> and rices, in association with <a href="/wiki/Chemical_fertilizer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical fertilizer">chemical fertilizers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agro-chemicals" class="mw-redirect" title="Agro-chemicals">agro-chemicals</a>, and with controlled water-supply (usually involving <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>) and new methods of cultivation, including mechanization. All of these together were seen as a 'package of practices' to supersede 'traditional' technology and to be adopted as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Both the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Foundation" title="Ford Foundation">Ford Foundation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Foundation" title="Rockefeller Foundation">Rockefeller Foundation</a> were heavily involved.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  
One key leader was <a href="/wiki/Norman_Borlaug" title="Norman Borlaug">Norman Borlaug</a>, the "Father of the Green Revolution", who received the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a> in 1970. He is credited with saving over a billion people from starvation. The basic approach was the development of high-yielding varieties of cereal grains, expansion of irrigation infrastructure, modernization of management techniques, distribution of <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridized seeds</a>, synthetic fertilizers, and <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a> to farmers.
</p><p>The term "Green Revolution" was first used in a March 8, 1968, speech by the administrator of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Agency_for_International_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Agency for International Development">U.S. Agency for International Development</a> (USAID), <a href="/w/index.php?title=William_S._Gaud&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="William S. Gaud (page does not exist)">William S. Gaud</a>, who noted the spread of the new technologies: "These and other developments in the field of agriculture contain the makings of a new revolution. It is not a violent <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">Red Revolution</a> like that of the Soviets, nor is it a <a href="/wiki/White_Revolution" title="White Revolution">White Revolution</a> like that of the <a href="/wiki/Mohammad_Reza_Pahlavi" title="Mohammad Reza Pahlavi">Shah of Iran</a>. I call it the Green Revolution."<sup id="cite_ref-agbio_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-agbio-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Mexico">In Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Agriculture_in_Mexico" title="Agriculture in Mexico">Agriculture in Mexico</a></div>
<p>It has been argued that "during the twentieth century two 'revolutions' transformed rural Mexico: the Mexican Revolution (1910–1920) and the Green Revolution (1950–1970)".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> With the support of the Mexican government, the U.S. government, the United Nations, the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> (FAO), and the <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Foundation" title="Rockefeller Foundation">Rockefeller Foundation</a>, Mexico made a concerted effort to transform agricultural productivity, particularly with irrigated rather than dry-land cultivation in its northwest, to solve its problem of lack of food self-sufficiency.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  In the center and south of Mexico, where large-scale production faced challenges, agricultural production languished.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  Increased production meant food self-sufficiency in Mexico to feed its growing and urbanizing population,  with the number of calories consumed per Mexican increasing.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Technology was seen as a valuable way to feed the poor, and would relieve some pressure of the land redistribution process.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mexico was the recipient of Green Revolution knowledge and technology, and it was an active participant with financial support from the government for agriculture as well as Mexican agronomists.  Although the Mexican Revolution had broken the back of the <a href="/wiki/Hacienda" title="Hacienda">hacienda</a> system and <a href="/wiki/Land_reform_in_Mexico" title="Land reform in Mexico">land reform in Mexico</a> had by 1940 distributed a large expanse of land in central and southern Mexico, agricultural productivity had fallen.  During the administration of <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Avila_Camacho" class="mw-redirect" title="Manuel Avila Camacho">Manuel Avila Camacho</a> (1940–46), the government put resources into developing new breeds of plants and partnered  with the <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Foundation" title="Rockefeller Foundation">Rockefeller Foundation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cotter,_p._11_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cotter,_p._11-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In 1943, the Mexican government founded the <a href="/wiki/International_Maize_and_Wheat_Improvement_Center" title="International Maize and Wheat Improvement Center">International Maize and Wheat Improvement Center</a> (CIMMYT), which became a base for international agricultural research.
</p><p>Agriculture in Mexico had been a sociopolitical issue, a key factor in some regions' participation in the Mexican Revolution. It was also a technical issue, enabled by a cohort of trained agronomists, who were to advise peasants how to increase productivity.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  In the post-World War II era, the government sought development in agriculture that bettered technological aspects of agriculture in regions that were not dominated by small-scale peasant cultivators.  This drive for agricultural transformation would have the benefit of keeping Mexico self-sufficient in food and in the political sphere with the Cold War, potentially stem unrest and the appeal of Communism.<sup id="cite_ref-Cotter,_p._11_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cotter,_p._11-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Technical aid can be seen as also serving political ends in the international sphere. In Mexico, it also served political ends,  separating peasant agriculture based on the <a href="/wiki/Ejido" title="Ejido">ejido</a> and considered one of the victories of the Mexican Revolution,  from agribusiness that requires large-scale land ownership, irrigation, specialized seeds, fertilizers, and pesticides, machinery, and a low-wage paid labor force.
</p><p>The government created the Mexican Agricultural Program (MAP) to be the lead organization in raising productivity.   One of their successes was wheat production, with varieties the agency's scientists helped create dominating wheat production as early as 1951 (70%), 1965 (80%), and 1968 (90%).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Mexico became the showcase for extending the Green Revolution to other areas of Latin America and beyond, into Africa and Asia. New breeds of maize, beans, and wheat produced bumper crops with proper inputs (such as fertilizer and pesticides) and careful cultivation. Many Mexican farmers who had been dubious about the scientists or hostile to them (often a mutual relationship of discord) came to see the scientific approach to agriculture as worth adopting.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_rice:_IR8_and_the_Philippines">In rice: IR8 and the Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In rice: IR8 and the Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1960, the Government of the Republic of the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Foundation" title="Ford Foundation">Ford Foundation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Foundation" title="Rockefeller Foundation">Rockefeller Foundation</a> established the <a href="/wiki/International_Rice_Research_Institute" title="International Rice Research Institute">International Rice Research Institute</a> (IRRI). A <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> crossing between Dee-Geo-woo-gen and Peta was done at IRRI in 1962. In 1966, one of the breeding lines became a new <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivar</a>, <a href="/wiki/IR8" title="IR8">IR8</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ir8_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ir8-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  IR8 required the use of fertilizers and pesticides, but produced substantially higher yields than the traditional cultivars. Annual rice production in the Philippines increased from 3.7 to 7.7&#160;million tons in two decades.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  The switch to IR8 rice made the Philippines a rice exporter for the first time in the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Start_in_India">Start in India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Start in India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Green_Revolution_in_India" title="Green Revolution in India">Green Revolution in India</a></div>
<p>In 1961, India was on the brink of mass famine.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="existing reference does not fully support the statement that India was on the brink of mass famine in 1961 (February 2017)">additional citation(s) needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Norman_Borlaug" title="Norman Borlaug">Norman Borlaug</a> was invited to India by the adviser to the Indian minister of agriculture <a href="/wiki/C._Subramaniam" class="mw-redirect" title="C. Subramaniam">C. Subramaniam</a>. Despite bureaucratic hurdles imposed by India's grain monopolies, the Ford Foundation and Indian government collaborated to import wheat seed from the International Maize and Wheat Improvement Center (CIMMYT). <a href="/wiki/Punjab_(India)" class="mw-redirect" title="Punjab (India)">Punjab</a> was selected by the Indian government to be the first site to try the new crops because of its reliable water supply and a history of agricultural success. India began its own Green Revolution program of plant breeding, irrigation development, and financing of agrochemicals.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>India soon adopted IR8 – a semi-dwarf rice variety developed by the International Rice Research Institute (IRRI) that could produce more grains of rice per plant when grown with certain fertilizers and irrigation.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In 1968, Indian agronomist <a href="/wiki/Surajit_Kumar_De_Datta" title="Surajit Kumar De Datta">S.K. De Datta</a> published his findings that IR8 rice yielded about 5 tons per hectare with no fertilizer, and almost 10 tons per hectare under optimal conditions. This was 10 times the yield of traditional rice.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>  IR8 was a success throughout Asia, and dubbed the "Miracle Rice". IR8 was also developed into <a href="/wiki/Semi-dwarf_IR36" title="Semi-dwarf IR36">Semi-dwarf IR36</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wheat_yields_in_Least_Developed_Countries.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Wheat_yields_in_Least_Developed_Countries.svg/220px-Wheat_yields_in_Least_Developed_Countries.svg.png" width="220" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Wheat_yields_in_Least_Developed_Countries.svg/330px-Wheat_yields_in_Least_Developed_Countries.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Wheat_yields_in_Least_Developed_Countries.svg/440px-Wheat_yields_in_Least_Developed_Countries.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="550" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wheat_yields_in_Least_Developed_Countries.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wheat yields in <a href="/wiki/Least_developed_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Least developed countries">least developed countries</a> since 1961, in kilograms per hectare.</div></div></div>
<p>In the 1960s, rice yields in India were about two tons per hectare; by the mid-1990s, they had risen to six tons per hectare. In the 1970s, rice cost about $550 a ton; in 2001, it cost under $200 a ton.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> India became one of the world's most successful rice producers, and is now a major rice exporter, shipping nearly 4.5&#160;million tons in 2006.
</p>
<h3><span id="Consultative_Group_on_International_Agricultural_Research_.E2.80.93_CGIAR"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Consultative_Group_on_International_Agricultural_Research_–_CGIAR">Consultative Group on International Agricultural Research – CGIAR</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Consultative Group on International Agricultural Research – CGIAR">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1970, foundation officials proposed a worldwide network of agricultural research centers under a permanent secretariat. This was further supported and developed by the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a>; on 19 May 1971, the <a href="/wiki/Consultative_Group_on_International_Agricultural_Research" class="mw-redirect" title="Consultative Group on International Agricultural Research">Consultative Group on International Agricultural Research</a> (CGIAR) was established. co-sponsored by the FAO, IFAD and UNDP. CGIAR has added many research centers throughout the world.
</p><p>CGIAR has responded, at least in part, to criticisms of Green Revolution methodologies. This began in the 1980s, and mainly was a result of pressure from donor organizations.<sup id="cite_ref-oasa_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oasa-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Methods like Agroecosystem Analysis and Farming System Research have been adopted to gain a more holistic view of agriculture.
</p>
<h3><span id="Brazil.27s_agricultural_revolution"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil's_agricultural_revolution">Brazil's agricultural revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Brazil&#039;s agricultural revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Brazil's vast inland <a href="/wiki/Cerrado" title="Cerrado">cerrado</a> region was regarded as unfit for farming before the 1960s because the soil was too acidic and poor in nutrients, according to <a href="/wiki/Norman_Borlaug" title="Norman Borlaug">Norman Borlaug</a>. However, from the 1960s, vast quantities of lime (pulverised chalk or limestone) were poured on the soil to reduce acidity. The effort went on for decades; by the late 1990s, between 14 million and 16 million tonnes of lime were being spread on Brazilian fields each year. The quantity rose to 25 million tonnes in 2003 and 2004, equalling around five tonnes of lime per hectare. As a result, Brazil has become the world's second biggest <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybean</a> exporter. Soybeans are also widely used in animal feed, and the large volume of soy produced in Brazil has contributed to Brazil's rise to become the biggest exporter of beef and poultry in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Several parallels can also be found in Argentina's boom in soybean production as well.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems_in_Africa">Problems in Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Problems in Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There have been numerous attempts to introduce the successful concepts from the Mexican and Indian projects into Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> These programs have generally been less successful. Reasons cited include widespread corruption, insecurity, a lack of infrastructure, and a general lack of will on the part of the governments. Yet environmental factors, such as the availability of water for irrigation, the high diversity in slope and soil types in one given area are also reasons why the Green Revolution is not so successful in Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-inwent.org_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inwent.org-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A recent program in western Africa is attempting to introduce a new high-yielding 'family' of rice varieties known as "<a href="/wiki/New_Rice_for_Africa" title="New Rice for Africa">New Rice for Africa</a>" (NERICA). NERICA varieties yield about 30% more rice under normal conditions, and can double yields with small amounts of fertilizer and very basic irrigation. However, the program has been beset by problems getting the rice into the hands of farmers, and to date the only success has been in <a href="/wiki/Guinea" title="Guinea">Guinea</a>, where it currently accounts for 16% of rice cultivation.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After a famine in 2001 and years of chronic hunger and poverty, in 2005 the small African country of <a href="/wiki/Malawi" title="Malawi">Malawi</a> launched the "Agricultural Input Subsidy Program" by which vouchers are given to smallholder farmers to buy subsidized nitrogen fertilizer and maize seeds.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Within its first year, the program was reported to have had extreme success, producing the largest maize harvest of the country's history, enough to feed the country with tons of maize left over. The program has advanced yearly ever since. Various sources claim that the program has been an unusual success, hailing it as a "miracle".<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Agricultural_production_and_food_security">Agricultural production and food security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Agricultural production and food security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technologies">Technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wheat-haHula-ISRAEL2.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Wheat-haHula-ISRAEL2.JPG/220px-Wheat-haHula-ISRAEL2.JPG" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Wheat-haHula-ISRAEL2.JPG/330px-Wheat-haHula-ISRAEL2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Wheat-haHula-ISRAEL2.JPG/440px-Wheat-haHula-ISRAEL2.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2773" data-file-height="2055" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wheat-haHula-ISRAEL2.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>New varieties of wheat and other grains were instrumental to the green revolution.</div></div></div>
<p>The Green Revolution spread technologies that already existed, but had not been widely implemented outside industrialized nations. Two kinds of technologies were used in the Green Revolution and aim at cultivation and breeding area respectively. The technologies in cultivation are targeted at providing excellent growing conditions, which included modern <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> projects, <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_nitrogen_fertilizer" class="mw-redirect" title="Synthetic nitrogen fertilizer">synthetic nitrogen fertilizer</a>. The breeding technologies aimed at improving crop varieties developed through the conventional, science-based methods available at the time. These technologies included <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a>, combining modern genetics with selections.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="High-Yielding_Varieties">High-Yielding Varieties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: High-Yielding Varieties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The novel technological development of the Green Revolution was the production of novel wheat <a href="/wiki/Cultivars" class="mw-redirect" title="Cultivars">cultivars</a>. <a href="/wiki/Agronomist" class="mw-redirect" title="Agronomist">Agronomists</a> <a href="/wiki/Plant_breeding" title="Plant breeding">bred</a> cultivars of maize, wheat, and rice that are generally referred to as HYVs or "<a href="/wiki/High-yielding_varieties" class="mw-redirect" title="High-yielding varieties">high-yielding varieties</a>". HYVs have higher nitrogen-absorbing potential than other varieties. Since cereals that absorbed extra nitrogen would typically lodge, or fall over before harvest, semi-dwarfing <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a> were bred into their <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genomes</a>. A Japanese dwarf wheat cultivar <a href="/wiki/Norin_10_wheat" title="Norin 10 wheat">Norin 10</a> developed by a Japanese agronomist Gonjiro Inazuka, which was sent to <a href="/wiki/Orville_Vogel" title="Orville Vogel">Orville Vogel</a> at <a href="/wiki/Washington_State_University" title="Washington State University">Washington State University</a> by <a href="/wiki/Cecil_Salmon" title="Cecil Salmon">Cecil Salmon</a>, was instrumental in developing Green Revolution wheat cultivars. IR8, the first widely implemented HYV rice to be developed by IRRI, was created through a cross between an Indonesian variety named "Peta" and a Chinese variety named "Dee-geo-woo-gen".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> In the 1960s, when a food crisis happened in Asia, the spread of HYV rice was aggravated intensely.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Dr. <a href="/wiki/Norman_Borlaug" title="Norman Borlaug">Norman Borlaug</a>, who is usually recognized as the "Father of the Green Revolution", bred rust-resistant cultivars which have strong and firm stems, preventing them from falling over under extreme weather at high levels of fertilization. <a href="/wiki/CIMMYT" class="mw-redirect" title="CIMMYT">CIMMYT</a>(Centro Internacional de Mejoramiento de Maiz y Trigo—International Center for Maize and Wheat Improvements) conducted these breeding programs and helped spread <a href="/wiki/High-yielding_varieties" class="mw-redirect" title="High-yielding varieties">high-yielding varieties</a> in Mexico and countries in Asia like India and Pakistan. These programs successfully led the harvest double in these countries.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Plant scientists figured out several parameters related to the high yield and identified the related genes which control the plant height and tiller number.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> With advances in <a href="/wiki/Molecular_genetics" title="Molecular genetics">molecular genetics</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mutant" title="Mutant">mutant</a> <a href="/wiki/Genes" class="mw-redirect" title="Genes">genes</a> responsible for <i><a href="/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana" title="Arabidopsis thaliana">Arabidopsis thaliana</a></i> genes (GA 20-oxidase,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> <i>ga1</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> <i>ga1-3</i><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>), wheat reduced-height genes (<i>Rht</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>  and  a rice semidwarf gene (<i>sd1</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> were <a href="/wiki/Cloned" class="mw-redirect" title="Cloned">cloned</a>. These were identified as <a href="/wiki/Gibberellin" title="Gibberellin">gibberellin</a> <a href="/wiki/Biosynthesis" title="Biosynthesis">biosynthesis</a> genes or <a href="/wiki/Cell_signaling" title="Cell signaling">cellular signaling</a> component genes. <a href="/wiki/Plant_stem" title="Plant stem">Stem</a> growth in the mutant background is significantly reduced leading to the <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Dwarf plant">dwarf</a> <a href="/wiki/Phenotype" title="Phenotype">phenotype</a>. <a href="/wiki/Photosynthetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Photosynthetic">Photosynthetic</a> investment in the stem is reduced dramatically as the shorter plants are inherently more stable mechanically. Assimilates become redirected to grain production, amplifying in particular the effect of chemical fertilizers on commercial yield.
</p><p>HYVs significantly outperform traditional varieties in the presence of adequate irrigation, pesticides, and fertilizers. In the absence of these inputs, traditional varieties may outperform HYVs. Therefore, several authors have challenged the apparent superiority of HYVs not only compared to the traditional varieties alone, but by contrasting the monocultural system associated with HYVs with the polycultural system associated with traditional ones.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_increases">Production increases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Production increases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cereal production more than doubled in developing nations between the years 1961–1985.<sup id="cite_ref-conway1_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conway1-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Yields of rice, maize, and wheat increased steadily during that period.<sup id="cite_ref-conway1_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conway1-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The production increases can be attributed roughly equally to irrigation, fertilizer, and seed development, at least in the case of Asian rice.<sup id="cite_ref-conway1_41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conway1-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While agricultural output increased as a result of the Green Revolution, the energy input to produce a crop has increased faster,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>  so that the ratio of crops produced to energy input has decreased over time. Green Revolution techniques also heavily rely on chemical <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herbicide" title="Herbicide">herbicides</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Defoliant" title="Defoliant">defoliants</a> and rely on machines, which as of 2014 rely on or are derived from <a href="/wiki/Crude_oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a>, making agriculture increasingly reliant on crude oil extraction.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>  Proponents of the <a href="/wiki/Peak_Oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Peak Oil">Peak Oil</a> theory fear that a future decline in oil and gas production would lead to a decline in food production or even a <a href="/wiki/Malthusian_catastrophe" title="Malthusian catastrophe">Malthusian catastrophe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:World_population_history.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/World_population_history.svg/220px-World_population_history.svg.png" width="220" height="132" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/World_population_history.svg/330px-World_population_history.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/World_population_history.svg/440px-World_population_history.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="750" data-file-height="450" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:World_population_history.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>World population 1950–2010</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_food_security">Effects on food security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Effects on food security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">Food security</a></div>
<p>The effects of the Green Revolution on global <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a> are difficult to assess because of the complexities involved in food systems.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">world population</a> has grown by about five billion<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> since the beginning of the Green Revolution and many believe that, without the Revolution, there would have been greater <a href="/wiki/Famine" title="Famine">famine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">malnutrition</a>. <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> saw annual wheat production rise from 10 million tons in the 1960s to 73&#160;million in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The average person in the developing world consumes roughly 25% more calories per day now than before the Green Revolution.<sup id="cite_ref-conway1_41-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conway1-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>  Between 1950 and 1984, as the Green Revolution transformed agriculture around the globe, world grain production increased by about 160%.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The production increases fostered by the Green Revolution are often credited with having helped to avoid widespread <a href="/wiki/Famine" title="Famine">famine</a>, and for feeding billions of people.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are also claims that the Green Revolution has decreased food security for a large number of people. One claim involves the shift of subsistence-oriented cropland to cropland oriented towards production of grain for export or animal feed. For example, the Green Revolution replaced much of the land used for <a href="/wiki/Pulse_(legume)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pulse (legume)">pulses</a> that fed Indian peasants for wheat, which did not make up a large portion of the peasant diet.<sup id="cite_ref-spitz_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spitz-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_security">Food security</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Food security">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Malthusian_criticism">Malthusian criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Malthusian criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some criticisms generally involve some variation of the <a href="/wiki/Malthusian" class="mw-redirect" title="Malthusian">Malthusian</a> principle of population. Such concerns often revolve around the idea that the Green Revolution is unsustainable,<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> and argue that humanity is now in a state of <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">overpopulation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Overshoot_(population)" title="Overshoot (population)">overshoot</a> with regards to the sustainable <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity#Humans" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological demands</a> on the Earth.
</p><p>Although 36 million people die each year as a direct or indirect result of hunger and poor nutrition,<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Malthus's more extreme predictions have frequently failed to materialize. In 1798 Thomas Malthus made his prediction of impending famine.<sup id="cite_ref-Green_Revolutionary_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Green_Revolutionary-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>  The world's population had doubled by 1923 and doubled again by 1973 without fulfilling Malthus's prediction. Malthusian <a href="/wiki/Paul_R._Ehrlich" title="Paul R. Ehrlich">Paul R. Ehrlich</a>, in his 1968 book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Population_Bomb" title="The Population Bomb">The Population Bomb</a></i>, said that "India couldn't possibly feed two hundred million more people by 1980" and "Hundreds of millions of people will starve to death in spite of any crash programs."<sup id="cite_ref-Green_Revolutionary_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Green_Revolutionary-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>  Ehrlich's warnings failed to materialize when India became self-sustaining in cereal production in 1974 (six years later) as a result of the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Norman_Borlaug" title="Norman Borlaug">Norman Borlaug</a>'s dwarf wheat varieties.<sup id="cite_ref-Green_Revolutionary_52-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Green_Revolutionary-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, Borlaug was well aware of the implications of population growth. In his Nobel lecture he repeatedly presented improvements in food production within a sober understanding of the context of population. "The green revolution has won a temporary success in man's war against hunger and deprivation; it has given man a breathing space. If fully implemented, the revolution can provide sufficient food for sustenance during the next three decades. But the frightening power of human reproduction must also be curbed; otherwise the success of the green revolution will be ephemeral only. Most people still fail to comprehend the magnitude and menace of the "Population Monster"...Since man is potentially a rational being, however, I am confident that within the next two decades he will recognize the self-destructive course he steers along the road of irresponsible population growth..."
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hubbert_peak_oil_plot.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Hubbert_peak_oil_plot.svg/220px-Hubbert_peak_oil_plot.svg.png" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Hubbert_peak_oil_plot.svg/330px-Hubbert_peak_oil_plot.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Hubbert_peak_oil_plot.svg/440px-Hubbert_peak_oil_plot.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hubbert_peak_oil_plot.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/M._King_Hubbert" title="M. King Hubbert">M. King Hubbert</a>'s prediction of world petroleum production rates. Modern agriculture is largely reliant on petroleum energy.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Famine">Famine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Famine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>To some modern Western sociologists and writers, increasing food production is not synonymous with increasing food security, and is only part of a larger equation. For example, Harvard professor <a href="/wiki/Amartya_Sen" title="Amartya Sen">Amartya Sen</a> wrote that large <a href="/wiki/List_of_famines" title="List of famines">historic famines</a> were not caused by decreases in food supply, but by socioeconomic dynamics and a failure of public action.<sup id="cite_ref-sen_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sen-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Economist <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Bowbrick&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Peter Bowbrick (page does not exist)">Peter Bowbrick</a> disputes Sen's theory, arguing that Sen relies on inconsistent arguments and contradicts available information, including sources that Sen himself cited.<sup id="cite_ref-Bowbrick1986_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bowbrick1986-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Bowbrick further argues that Sen's views coincide with that of the <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">Bengal</a> government at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Bengal_famine_of_1943" title="Bengal famine of 1943">Bengal famine of 1943</a>, and the policies Sen advocates failed to relieve the famine.<sup id="cite_ref-Bowbrick1986_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bowbrick1986-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Quality_of_diet">Quality of diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Quality of diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some have challenged the value of the increased food production of Green Revolution agriculture. <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Altieri" title="Miguel Altieri">Miguel A. Altieri</a>, (a pioneer of agroecology and peasant-advocate), writes that the comparison between traditional systems of agriculture and Green Revolution agriculture has been unfair, because Green Revolution agriculture produces <a href="/wiki/Monocultures" class="mw-redirect" title="Monocultures">monocultures</a> of cereal grains, while traditional agriculture usually incorporates <a href="/wiki/Polyculture" title="Polyculture">polycultures</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>These monoculture crops are often used for export, feed for animals, or conversion into biofuel. According to Emile Frison of <a href="/wiki/Bioversity_International" title="Bioversity International">Bioversity International</a>, the Green Revolution has also led to a change in dietary habits, as fewer people are affected by hunger and die from starvation, but many are affected by <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">malnutrition</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Iron_deficiency" title="Iron deficiency">iron</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_A_deficiency" title="Vitamin A deficiency">vitamin-A deficiencies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-inwent.org_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inwent.org-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Frison further asserts that almost 60% of yearly deaths of children under age five in developing countries are related to malnutrition.<sup id="cite_ref-inwent.org_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inwent.org-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The strategies developed by the Green Revolution focused on fend off starvation and was very successful in raising overall yields of cereal grains, but did not give sufficient relevance to nutritional quality.<sup id="cite_ref-SandsMorris2009_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SandsMorris2009-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> High yield-cereal crops have low <a href="/wiki/Protein_quality" title="Protein quality">quality proteins</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Essential_amino_acid" title="Essential amino acid">essential amino acid</a> deficiencies, are high in <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>, and lack balanced <a href="/wiki/Essential_fatty_acid" title="Essential fatty acid">essential fatty acids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> and other quality factors.<sup id="cite_ref-SandsMorris2009_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SandsMorris2009-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>High-yield rice (HYR), introduced since 1964 to poverty-ridden Asian countries, such as the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, was found to have inferior flavor and be more glutinous and less savory than their native varieties.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This caused its price to be lower than the average market value.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the Philippines the introduction of heavy pesticides to rice production, in the early part of the Green Revolution, poisoned and killed off fish and weedy green vegetables that traditionally coexisted in <a href="/wiki/Rice_paddy" class="mw-redirect" title="Rice paddy">rice paddies</a>. These were nutritious food sources for many poor Filipino farmers prior to the introduction of pesticides, further impacting the diets of locals.<sup id="cite_ref-rice-robbery_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rice-robbery-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_impact">Political impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Political impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A major critic<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> of the Green Revolution, U.S. <a href="/wiki/Investigative_journalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Investigative journalist">investigative journalist</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Dowie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mark Dowie (page does not exist)">Mark Dowie</a>, writes:<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>The primary objective of the program was geopolitical: to provide food for the populace in undeveloped countries and so bring social stability and weaken the fomenting of communist insurgency.</p></blockquote>
<p>Citing internal Foundation documents, Dowie states that the Ford Foundation had a greater concern than Rockefeller in this area.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is significant evidence that the Green Revolution weakened socialist movements in many nations. In countries such as India, Mexico, and the Philippines, <i>technological solutions</i> were sought as an alternative to expanding <i><a href="/wiki/Agrarian_reform" title="Agrarian reform">agrarian reform</a></i> initiatives, the latter of which were often linked to socialist politics.<sup id="cite_ref-ross1_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ross1-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Socioeconomic_impacts">Socioeconomic impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Socioeconomic impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The transition from traditional agriculture, in which inputs were generated on-farm, to Green Revolution agriculture, which required the purchase of inputs, led to the widespread establishment of rural credit institutions. Smaller farmers often went into <a href="/wiki/Debt" title="Debt">debt</a>, which in many cases results in a loss of their farmland.<sup id="cite_ref-oasa_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oasa-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ponting_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ponting-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>  The increased level of mechanization on larger farms made possible by the Green Revolution removed a large source of employment from the rural economy.<sup id="cite_ref-oasa_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oasa-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  Because wealthier farmers had better access to credit and land, the Green Revolution increased class disparities, with the rich–poor gap widening as a result. Because some regions were able to adopt Green Revolution agriculture more readily than others (for political or geographical reasons), interregional economic disparities increased as well. Many small farmers are hurt by the dropping prices resulting from increased production overall.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> However, large-scale farming companies only account for less than 10% of the total farming capacity. This is a criticism held by many small producers in the <a href="/wiki/Food_sovereignty" title="Food sovereignty">food sovereignty</a> movement.
</p><p>The new economic difficulties of small holder farmers and landless farm workers led to increased <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">rural-urban migration</a>. The increase in food production led to a cheaper food for urban dwellers, and the increase in urban population increased the potential for industrialization.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="I&#39;ve heard this before, so I know reliable sources should be available; UN stats? (December 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>According to a 2018 paper, a 10 percentage points increase in the use of high-yielding crop varieties in developing countries in the period 1960-2000 led to increases in GDP per capita of approximately 15 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact">Environmental impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Environmental impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Irrigation1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Irrigation1.jpg/220px-Irrigation1.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Irrigation1.jpg/330px-Irrigation1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Irrigation1.jpg/440px-Irrigation1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="597" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Irrigation1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Increased use of irrigation played a major role in the green revolution.</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Biodiversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The spread of Green Revolution agriculture affected both agricultural <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> (or agrodiversity) and wild biodiversity.<sup id="cite_ref-rice-robbery_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rice-robbery-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> There is little disagreement that the Green Revolution acted to reduce agricultural biodiversity, as it relied on just a few high-yield varieties of each crop.
</p><p>This has led to concerns about the susceptibility of a food supply to pathogens that cannot be controlled by agrochemicals, as well as the permanent loss of many valuable genetic traits bred into traditional varieties over thousands of years. To address these concerns, massive seed banks such as <a href="/wiki/Consultative_Group_on_International_Agricultural_Research" class="mw-redirect" title="Consultative Group on International Agricultural Research">Consultative Group on International Agricultural Research</a>’s (CGIAR) International Plant Genetic Resources Institute (now <a href="/wiki/Bioversity_International" title="Bioversity International">Bioversity International</a>) have been established (see <a href="/wiki/Svalbard_Global_Seed_Vault" title="Svalbard Global Seed Vault">Svalbard Global Seed Vault</a>).
</p><p>There are varying opinions about the effect of the Green Revolution on wild biodiversity. One hypothesis speculates that by increasing production per unit of land area, agriculture will not need to expand into new, uncultivated areas to feed a growing human population.<sup id="cite_ref-Davies2003_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies2003-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> However, land degradation and soil nutrients depletion have forced farmers to clear up formerly forested areas in order to keep up with production.<sup id="cite_ref-Shiva1991_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shiva1991-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> A counter-hypothesis speculates that biodiversity was sacrificed because traditional systems of agriculture that were displaced sometimes incorporated practices to preserve wild biodiversity, and because the Green Revolution expanded agricultural development into new areas where it was once unprofitable or too arid. For example, the development of wheat varieties tolerant to acid soil conditions with high aluminium content, permitted the introduction of agriculture in sensitive <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_of_Brazil" title="Wildlife of Brazil">Brazilian ecosystems</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Cerrado" title="Cerrado">Cerrado</a> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_savanna_climate" title="Tropical savanna climate">semi-humid</a> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_savanna" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical savanna">tropical savanna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amazon_rainforest" title="Amazon rainforest">Amazon rainforest</a> in the geoeconomic macroregions of <a href="/wiki/Brazil_socio-geographic_division" title="Brazil socio-geographic division">Centro-Sul and Amazônia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Davies2003_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies2003-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Before the Green Revolution, other Brazilian ecosystems were also significantly damaged by human activity, such as the once 1st or 2nd main contributor to Brazilian megadiversity <a href="/wiki/Mata_Atl%C3%A2ntica" class="mw-redirect" title="Mata Atlântica">Atlantic Rainforest</a> (above 85% of deforestation in the 1980s, about 95% after the 2010s) and the important <a href="/wiki/Xeric_shrubland" class="mw-redirect" title="Xeric shrubland">xeric shrublands</a> called <a href="/wiki/Caatinga" title="Caatinga">Caatinga</a> mainly in <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Region,_Brazil" title="Northeast Region, Brazil">Northeastern Brazil</a> (about 40% in the 1980s, about 50% after the 2010s — deforestation of the Caatinga biome is generally associated with greater risks of <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a>). This also caused many animal species to suffer due to their damaged habitats.
</p><p>Nevertheless, the world community has clearly acknowledged the negative aspects of <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_expansion" title="Agricultural expansion">agricultural expansion</a> as the 1992 <a href="/wiki/Rio_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Rio Treaty">Rio Treaty</a>, signed by 189 nations, has generated numerous national <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_Action_Plans" class="mw-redirect" title="Biodiversity Action Plans">Biodiversity Action Plans</a> which assign significant biodiversity loss to agriculture's expansion into new domains.
</p><p>The Green Revolution has been criticized for an agricultural model which relied on a few staple and market profitable crops, and pursuing a model which limited the biodiversity of Mexico. One of the critics against these techniques  and the Green Revolution as a whole was <a href="/wiki/Carl_O._Sauer" title="Carl O. Sauer">Carl O. Sauer</a>, a geography professor at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>. According to Sauer these techniques of plant breeding would result in negative effects on the country's resources, and the culture:
</p><p>"A good aggressive bunch of American agronomists and plant breeders could ruin the native resources for good and all by pushing their American commercial stocks… And Mexican agriculture cannot be pointed toward standardization on a few commercial types without upsetting native economy and culture hopelessly... Unless the Americans understand that, they'd better keep out of this country entirely. That must be approached from an appreciation of native economies as being basically sound".<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gas_emissions">Greenhouse gas emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Greenhouse gas emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>According to a study published in 2013 in <a href="/wiki/PNAS" class="mw-redirect" title="PNAS">PNAS</a>, in the absence of the crop germplasm improvement associated with the Green Revolution, greenhouse gas emissions would have been 5.2-7.4 Gt higher than observed in 1965–2004.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> High yield agriculture has dramatic effects on the amount of carbon cycling in the atmosphere. The way in which farms are grown, in tandem with the seasonal carbon cycling of various crops, could alter the impact carbon in the atmosphere has on global warming. Wheat, rice, and soybean crops account for a significant amount of the increase in carbon in the atmosphere over the last 50 years.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dependence_on_non-renewable_resources">Dependence on non-renewable resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Dependence on non-renewable resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Most high intensity agricultural production is highly reliant on non-renewable resources. Agricultural machinery and transport, as well as the production of pesticides and nitrates all depend on fossil fuels.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> Moreover, the essential mineral nutrient phosphorus is often a limiting factor in crop cultivation, while phosphorus mines are rapidly being depleted worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> The failure to depart from these non-sustainable agricultural production methods could potentially lead to a large scale collapse of the current system of intensive food production within this century.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_impact">Health impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Health impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Health_effects_of_pesticides" title="Health effects of pesticides">Health effects of pesticides</a></div>
<p>The consumption of the <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a> used to kill <a href="/wiki/Pest_(organism)" title="Pest (organism)">pests</a> by humans in some cases may be increasing the likelihood of cancer in some of the rural villages using them.<sup id="cite_ref-Loyn_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Loyn-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> Poor farming practices including non-compliance to usage of masks and over-usage of the chemicals compound this situation.<sup id="cite_ref-Loyn_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Loyn-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> In 1989, WHO and UNEP estimated that there were around 1 million human pesticide poisonings annually. Some 20,000 (mostly in developing countries) ended in death, as a result of poor labeling, loose safety standards etc.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pesticides_and_cancer">Pesticides and cancer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Pesticides and cancer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Contradictory epidemiologic studies in humans have linked phenoxy acid herbicides or contaminants in them with <a href="/wiki/Soft_tissue_sarcoma" class="mw-redirect" title="Soft tissue sarcoma">soft tissue sarcoma</a> (STS) and malignant <a href="/wiki/Lymphoma" title="Lymphoma">lymphoma</a>, organochlorine insecticides with STS, non-Hodgkin's lymphoma (NHL), <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemia</a>, and, less consistently, with cancers of the <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung</a> and <a href="/wiki/Breast_cancer" title="Breast cancer">breast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organophosphorous" class="mw-redirect" title="Organophosphorous">organophosphorous</a> compounds with NHL and leukemia, and triazine herbicides with <a href="/wiki/Ovarian_cancer" title="Ovarian cancer">ovarian cancer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Punjab_case">Punjab case</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Punjab case">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Green_Revolution_in_India" title="Green Revolution in India">Green Revolution in India</a></div>
<p>The Indian state of <a href="/wiki/Punjab_(India)" class="mw-redirect" title="Punjab (India)">Punjab</a> pioneered green revolution among the other states transforming India into a food-surplus country.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> The state is witnessing serious consequences of intensive farming using chemicals and pesticides. A comprehensive study conducted by <a href="/wiki/Post_Graduate_Institute_of_Medical_Education_and_Research" class="mw-redirect" title="Post Graduate Institute of Medical Education and Research">Post Graduate Institute of Medical Education and Research</a> (<a href="/wiki/PGIMER" class="mw-redirect" title="PGIMER">PGIMER</a>) has underlined the direct relationship between indiscriminate use of these chemicals and increased incidence of cancer in this region.<sup id="cite_ref-cancer_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cancer-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> An increase in the number of cancer cases has been reported in several villages including Jhariwala, Koharwala, Puckka, Bhimawali, and Khara.<sup id="cite_ref-cancer_78-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cancer-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Environmental activist <a href="/wiki/Vandana_Shiva" title="Vandana Shiva">Vandana Shiva</a> has written extensively about the social, political and economic impacts of the Green Revolution in Punjab. She claims that the Green Revolution's reliance on heavy use of chemical inputs and monocultures has resulted in water scarcity, vulnerability to pests, and incidents of violent conflict and social marginalization.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a> Research Laboratories investigation of 50 villages in <a href="/wiki/Muktsar" class="mw-redirect" title="Muktsar">Muktsar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bathinda" title="Bathinda">Bathinda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ludhiana_district" title="Ludhiana district">Ludhiana districts</a> revealed that twenty percent of the sampled wells had nitrate levels above WHO limits for drinking water. The 2009 study linked the nitrate pollution with high use of synthetic nitrogen <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Norman_Borlaug.27s_response_to_criticism"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Norman_Borlaug's_response_to_criticism">Norman Borlaug's response to criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Norman Borlaug&#039;s response to criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Borlaug dismissed certain claims of critics, but also cautioned, "There are no miracles in agricultural production. Nor is there such a thing as a miracle variety of wheat, rice, or maize which can serve as an elixir to cure all ills of a stagnant, traditional agriculture."<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Of environmental lobbyists, he said:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"some of the environmental lobbyists of the Western nations are the <a href="/wiki/Salt_and_Light#Cultural_references" class="mw-redirect" title="Salt and Light">salt of the earth</a>, but many of them are <a href="/wiki/Elitist" class="mw-redirect" title="Elitist">elitists</a>. They've never experienced the physical sensation of hunger. They do their lobbying from comfortable office suites in Washington or <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>...If they lived just one month amid the misery of the developing world, as I have for fifty years, they'd be crying out for tractors and fertilizer and irrigation canals and be outraged that fashionable elitists back home were trying to deny them these things".<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_New_Green_Revolution">The New Green Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: The New Green Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Second_Green_Revolution" title="Second Green Revolution">Second Green Revolution</a></div>
<p>Although the Green Revolution has been able to improve agricultural output in some regions in the world, there was and is still room for improvement. As a result, many organizations continue to invent new ways to improve the techniques already used in the Green Revolution. Frequently quoted inventions are the <a href="/wiki/System_of_Rice_Intensification" title="System of Rice Intensification">System of Rice Intensification</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Marker-assisted_selection" title="Marker-assisted selection">marker-assisted selection</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Agroecology" title="Agroecology">agroecology</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> and applying existing technologies to agricultural problems of the developing world.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> Current challenges for nations trying to modernize their agriculture include closing the urban-rural income gap, integration of smallholders into value chains, and maintaining competitiveness in the market.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> However, in low-income countries, chronic problems such as poverty and hunger cause agricultural modernization efforts constrained.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> It is projected that global populations by 2050 will increase by one-third and as such will require a 70% increase in the production of food.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> As such, the Second Green Revolution will likely focus on improving tolerances to pests and disease in addition to technological input use efficiency.
</p>
