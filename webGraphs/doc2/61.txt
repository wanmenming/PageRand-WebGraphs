<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">History of the Green Party of Canada</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>
<b>The Green Party of Canada</b> was founded at a conference held at Carleton University in Ottawa in 1983. Under its first leader, Dr. <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Hancock" title="Trevor Hancock">Trevor Hancock</a>, the party ran 60 candidates in the <a href="/wiki/1984_Canadian_federal_election" class="mw-redirect" title="1984 Canadian federal election">1984 Canadian federal election</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-greenparty.ca_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenparty.ca-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_British_Columbia" title="Green Party of British Columbia">BC Greens</a> ran Canada's first Green candidate. Later that year, the founding conference of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Canada" title="Green Party of Canada">Canadian Greens</a> was held at <a href="/wiki/Carleton_University" title="Carleton University">Carleton University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a>, Ontario.<sup id="cite_ref-greenparty.ca_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenparty.ca-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Close to 200 people from 55 communities attended, coming from every province except <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward_Island" title="Prince Edward Island">Prince Edward Island</a>.
</p><p>The birthing process was difficult, with deep divisions between those arguing for a national structure, and those in favour of a process that would build from the regions following the <a href="/wiki/Bioregional_democracy" class="mw-redirect" title="Bioregional democracy">bioregional democracy</a> structure.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Trevor_Hancock" title="Trevor Hancock">Trevor Hancock</a> was the party's first registered leader. Party members chose a radically decentralized party structure, and for several years a kind of <a href="/wiki/Green_anarchism" title="Green anarchism">green anarchism</a> prevailed. Eventually, an uneasy agreement was reached for a federation of regional parties, with strong support for building upwards from the bottom. The question arose: "Is the priority to redefine politics from the ground up, or to play the electoral game according to the present rules? Or both?"
</p><p>Many members saw the party as a way to protest Canada's political system, and not much more. Nonetheless it did run candidates.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Canada" title="Green Party of Canada">Green Party of Canada</a> was registered in 1983 by Paul George, Ted Mousseau, William Marshall, Ed McDonough and Seymour Trieger in June - just preceding a byelection in Mission-Port Moody during which Betty Nickerson ran as a Green Party of Canada candidate. The party had not gained official status at that point so the party name did not appear on the ballot - though it did on all her communications. The first convention in Ontario later that fall elected the next set of party officers. The <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Canada" title="Green Party of Canada">Green Party of Canada</a> contested its first <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_1984" title="Canadian federal election, 1984">federal election in September 1984</a>. Approximately 27,000 Canadians voted Green (0.2% of the votes cast). But ongoing discussions about the party's <i>modus operandi</i> became so exhausting that, at one point in the mid-1980s, there was a near collapse of the party. It survived — though not particularly active — for almost a decade under the stewardship of the BC Greens.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_1988" title="Canadian federal election, 1988">1988 federal election</a>, Greens concentrated on <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, where <i>le Parti Vert</i> (not the same as the current <a href="/wiki/Parti_Vert_du_Qu%C3%A9bec" class="mw-redirect" title="Parti Vert du Québec">Parti Vert du Québec</a>) ran 29 candidates, up from just 4 in the previous election. <i>Les Verts</i> received higher results than Green candidates anywhere else in Canada, polling an average of 2.4% of the vote. The Quebec wing hosted the 1990 Canadian Greens conference in Montreal. But soon after that, Canada's constitutional problems interfered, and many Quebec candidates abandoned the Greens in favour of a <a href="/wiki/Quebec_sovereignty" class="mw-redirect" title="Quebec sovereignty">Quebec sovereigntist</a> party, the <a href="/wiki/Bloc_Qu%C3%A9b%C3%A9cois" title="Bloc Québécois">Bloc Québécois</a>. There were only six Green candidates from Quebec in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_1993" title="Canadian federal election, 1993">1993 election</a>.
</p><p>In the summer of 1988, the BC Greens, under the <i>de facto</i> leadership of electoral-reform activist <a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Kisby&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Steve Kisby (page does not exist)">Steve Kisby</a>, tried to get the Green Party of Canada onto its feet by hosting a conference — the first federal gathering since the founding meeting in 1983. The main accomplishment of that conference was the acceptance, after five years as a registered party, of a constitution. The party continued to field candidates at the federal level, and provincial parties were organized in a few other provinces, led by consistently strong efforts in British Columbia.
</p><p>In 1988, however, despite minimal on-the-ground organization, Quebec produced the lion's share of Green candidates and votes thanks to the efforts of Quebec organizer and candidate Rolf Bramann. A year later, the provincial Greens in Quebec scored 2% of the popular vote, averaging 5% in the constituencies in which they ran under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Jean_Ouimet" title="Jean Ouimet">Jean Ouimet</a>. Montreal's municipal Ecology Party also scored very well in elections in this period under the leadership of publisher <a href="/wiki/Dimitrios_Roussopoulos" title="Dimitrios Roussopoulos">Dimitrios Roussopoulos</a>.
</p><p>Ouimet, a strong sovereigntist, maintained a party wholly independent of the federal Greens during his leadership; as a result Bramann created an organization called the Green Party of Canada in Quebec, a predominantly Anglophone entity that nominated federal candidates only. There was open antipathy between Ouimet and Bramann. Neither was affiliated with Écologie-Montreal.
</p><p>At the same time as the Parti Vert began to collapse due to Ouimet's defection to the PQ in 1992, Bramann was removed from his position in the federal party due to anti-Semitic comments he and some of his candidates had made. This led to a precipitous decline in all Green Party organizations in Quebec despite a very promising start a mere four years previous.
</p><p>From 1988 onwards, a pattern developed whereby the federal party tended to function alternately as an appendage of the BC and Ontario provincial parties. Lacking sufficient funding and an administrative base of its own, control of the federal Greens was sometimes a prize (when the provincial affiliate and its leader wanted to demonstrate its success), and at others, a burden (when the provincial affiliate was forced to invest significant volunteer energy or money for its maintenance) for the Greens in BC and Ontario. Successful candidates for the positions of Leader and Chief Financial Officer were typically personal associates of either the BC or Ontario party's <i>de facto</i> or <i>de jure</i> leader, for whom the leader publicly mobilized and delivered votes.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the spring of 1996, although the hopes of electing a representative to the BC legislature proved premature, Andy Shadrack in the interior of the province received over 11% of the vote. Overall, the party's proportion of the popular vote surged to a new high. Shadrack was also the most popular Green candidate in the 1997 federal election, scoring over 6% of the popular vote in West Kootenay-Okanagan.
</p><p>At the party's sixth annual gathering in <a href="/wiki/Castlegar,_British_Columbia" title="Castlegar, British Columbia">Castlegar, British Columbia</a>, hosted by Shadrack's riding association, in August 1996, a complete overhaul of the party's constitution was made, spearheaded by <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Parker_(politician)" title="Stuart Parker (politician)">Stuart Parker</a>, leader of the provincial Greens in BC. The party's new constitutional framework both democratized and centralized the party which had been previously hobbled by an unworkably decentralized structure. These changes also ended de jure (it had ended de facto some three years earlier) the constitutional prohibition against the party's registered leader acting as its spokesperson or representative. Policy was also agreed to in a wide variety of areas. An important step forward was the structuring of a <a href="/wiki/Shadow_Cabinet" title="Shadow Cabinet">Shadow Cabinet</a>, whose mandate was to create a platform for the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_1997" title="Canadian federal election, 1997">next election in 1997</a>.
</p><p>The Castlegar gathering marked the beginning of a new era in Canadian Green history, and a somewhat uneasy one at that. In spite of a concern about the nature of leadership in a decentralized party, the Greens' first leadership campaign had been underway for the previous six months. Four candidates contested the leadership. A mail-in ballot was held: <a href="/wiki/Wendy_Priesnitz" title="Wendy Priesnitz">Wendy Priesnitz</a> (from Ontario) beat Don Francis (Quebec), Jason Crummey (<a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a>), and Harry Garfinkle (<a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>) to become the Registered Leader of the Green Party of Canada.
</p><p>In January 1997, although initially recruited by <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Ontario" title="Green Party of Ontario">Ontario Green Party</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Frank_de_Jong" title="Frank de Jong">Frank de Jong</a>, Wendy Priesnitz resigned over what she characterized as domination of the party by an "old boys' network" comprising the BC and Ontario provincial leaders and their male-dominated circles of organizers and advisors. Harry Garfinkle stepped in to be the interim Registered Leader of the Green Party of Canada, and a leadership convention by mail-in ballot was held.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Joan_Russow">Joan Russow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Joan Russow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>British Columbia's <a href="/wiki/Joan_Russow" title="Joan Russow">Joan Russow</a> became leader of the Green Party of Canada on April 13, 1997. Russow won 52% of the ballots cast in the 1997 leadership race, surpassing Ontario's Jim Harris (39%) and Rachelle Small (8%). Immediately upon attaining the leadership, Russow was plunged into a federal general election. Russow's campaign in 1997 set a number of important precedents. The 1997 election was the first campaign in which the Greens conducted a national leader's tour, presented a national platform and a bilingual campaign (Russow is trilingual, speaking Spanish, French and English). Previous campaigns, due in part to the party's few resources and, in part, to the party's constitutional straitjacket, had been characterized by policy and spokespeople operating, at best, province-by-province and, at worst, riding-by-riding. In her own riding of Victoria, Russow received just shy of 3000 votes and 6% of the popular vote.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2000" title="Canadian federal election, 2000">2000 election</a>, the party nominated 111 candidates, in nine out of ten provinces — all but Newfoundland and Labrador — and in one of three territories (<a href="/wiki/Nunavut" title="Nunavut">Nunavut</a>). These candidates collected 0.81% of the total popular vote.
</p><p>Candidates were not run in Newfoundland and Labrador, as a result of ongoing divisions over Joan Russow's refusal to endorse the Green candidate in an earlier St. John's West <a href="/wiki/By-election" title="By-election">by-election</a>. (The candidate in question supported the seal hunt and mining development, as most locals did.) This caused much uncertainty and friction between Newfoundland's Terra Nova Green Party Association and the Green Party leader as the party gradually adapted to the realities of functioning as a true national party rather than a disorganized federation of local activists.
</p><p>In the 2001 <a href="/wiki/Quebec_City_protest" class="mw-redirect" title="Quebec City protest">Quebec City protest</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Free_Trade_Area_of_the_Americas" title="Free Trade Area of the Americas">Free Trade Area of the Americas</a>, Russow was the first person incarcerated in a jail built specially for protesters, for taking a photograph of it from outside. Russow promoted the Green Party as a leader in the <a href="/wiki/Anti-globalization_movement" title="Anti-globalization movement">anti-globalization movement</a>, in particular the anti-<a href="/wiki/Corporatist" class="mw-redirect" title="Corporatist">corporatist</a> and pro-peace movement, but felt undermined when the <a href="/wiki/German_Greens" class="mw-redirect" title="German Greens">German Greens</a> supported the <a href="/wiki/1999_NATO_bombing_of_Yugoslavia" class="mw-redirect" title="1999 NATO bombing of Yugoslavia">bombing of Belgrade</a>. As other members of her party had supported military intervention, Russow's leadership was called into question. She stepped down as party leader in 2001 and left the party to join the <a href="/wiki/New_Democratic_Party_(Canada)" class="mw-redirect" title="New Democratic Party (Canada)">New Democratic Party</a> (NDP). Because the NDP's federal and provincial wings are integrated, this also entailed joining the <a href="/wiki/New_Democratic_Party_of_British_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="New Democratic Party of British Columbia">New Democratic Party of British Columbia</a>.
</p><p>Another factor in her resignation may have been ongoing conflicts within the party provincially and municipally in her home city of Victoria, where she had switched allegiance from the Parker faction to the <a href="/wiki/Adriane_Carr" title="Adriane Carr">Adriane Carr</a> faction. Her late conversion left her on unsure footing with the powerful new provincial leader and in public conflict with City Councilor Art Vanden Berg and other members of the Parker-affiliated team that had backed her leadership during the 1997 and 1998 leadership contests.
</p><p>The conflicts left Russow isolated and alienated from most members of the party. Volunteer efforts were substantially absorbed in provincial campaigns between 2001 and 2003, and the federal party became dormant between elections, as was typical in the past. <a href="/wiki/Chris_Bradshaw" title="Chris Bradshaw">Chris Bradshaw</a> served the party as interim leader from 2001 to February 2003.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Jim_Harris">Jim Harris</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Jim Harris">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jim_Harris_Calgary_2006.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Jim_Harris_Calgary_2006.jpg/200px-Jim_Harris_Calgary_2006.jpg" width="200" height="299" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Jim_Harris_Calgary_2006.jpg/300px-Jim_Harris_Calgary_2006.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Jim_Harris_Calgary_2006.jpg/400px-Jim_Harris_Calgary_2006.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="3872" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jim_Harris_Calgary_2006.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Jim_Harris_(politician)" title="Jim Harris (politician)">Jim Harris</a>, Former Green Party Leader in <a href="/wiki/Calgary,_Alberta" class="mw-redirect" title="Calgary, Alberta">Calgary, Alberta</a></div></div></div>
<p>In February 2003, <a href="/wiki/Jim_Harris_(politician)" title="Jim Harris (politician)">Jim Harris</a>, in his second bid for the leadership, defeated John Grogan of <a href="/wiki/Valemount,_British_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="Valemount, British Columbia">Valemount, British Columbia</a>, and Jason Crummey. Crummey was originally from Newfoundland and involved with <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a> Terra Nova Greens. Harris, an author and public speaker, and GPC member since 1987 (though not active in several previous elections), had the support of all provincial Green Party leaders, breaking 15 years of precedent of the BC leader backing a BC candidate from their own political circle. His election was taken by many as reflecting a desire among the members to "become serious" in achieving electoral progress, and to steer away from any explicit anti-political ideas. However, his campaign also included a hard line against <a href="/wiki/Red-green_alliance" class="mw-redirect" title="Red-green alliance">Red-Green</a> (i.e., alliances with NDP members and <a href="/wiki/Labour_movement" title="Labour movement">organized labour</a>) coalitions that Russow had supported at the municipal level in Victoria. While adopting the rhetoric of pragmatism, some argue, Harris eschewed the only strategy that has ever elected Greens under the first-past-the-post system. Harris announced on April 24, 2006, that he would stand down as leader at the party's convention in August.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Full_slate">Full slate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Full slate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Green Party ran a fundraising campaign in 2003 to realize Harris' goal of running a full slate in the upcoming election. This party also had to borrow hundreds of thousands of dollars against the $1.75/vote expected to accrue to the party after the election. The party began organizing in all provinces with paid staff.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2004_election_and_aftermath">2004 election and aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2004 election and aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2004" title="Canadian federal election, 2004">2004 election</a>, the party received a significant increase in media coverage on the strength of its <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Canada_candidates,_2004_federal_election" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Party of Canada candidates, 2004 federal election">308 candidates</a>, the platform, and a national leaders' tour. The party began to be included in almost all national political polls. Its popular support peaked at 7% during the campaign, and the party finished with 4.3% of the vote. The party's strongest candidate, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrew_Lewis_(politician)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Andrew Lewis (politician) (page does not exist)">Andrew Lewis</a> in the riding of <a href="/wiki/Saanich%E2%80%94Gulf_Islands" title="Saanich—Gulf Islands">Saanich—Gulf Islands</a>, won over 10,000 votes, the first Green Party candidate to do so. Lewis still finished fourth in the riding, however.
</p><p>In August 2004 at the national convention near Calgary, Alberta, Jim Harris was re-elected, with a reduced majority of only 56%. Rival Tom Manley polled nearly 37%.
</p><p>Most conference debate centred on significant constitutional reform proposals, and the role of membership in ruling on matters of policy and the constitution. The conference ended with a re-affirmation of a hybrid that was developed during the campaign: a centralized executive with decentralized policy and constitutional development.
</p><p>Politicians from different political backgrounds have expressed interest in the party. Former Deputy Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Sheila_Copps" title="Sheila Copps">Sheila Copps</a> on March 2, 2005, spoke publicly to a group of Greens in Toronto, advising the party on its electoral strategy. Former Progressive Conservative leadership candidate <a href="/wiki/David_Orchard" title="David Orchard">David Orchard</a> not only attended but met with members of the GPC Council; however, Orchard's involvement with the Greens dates back to the mid-1990s when he worked with Russow on a variety of trade and international issues. (Ontario leader <a href="/wiki/Frank_de_Jong" title="Frank de Jong">Frank de Jong</a> and BC leader <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Parker_(politician)" title="Stuart Parker (politician)">Stuart Parker</a> were featured speakers at pro-Orchard rallies early in his first bid for the Tory leadership in 1998.) It was also rumoured in the media in 2004 that <a href="/wiki/David_Anderson_(British_Columbia_politician)" title="David Anderson (British Columbia politician)">David Anderson</a>, the former Minister of the Environment in Chrétien's government, was considering joining the party. Anderson, however, successfully ran for re-election as a Liberal.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2006_election">2006 election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2006 election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Green_Party_of_Canada_leadership_convention,_2006.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Green_Party_of_Canada_leadership_convention%2C_2006.jpg/220px-Green_Party_of_Canada_leadership_convention%2C_2006.jpg" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Green_Party_of_Canada_leadership_convention%2C_2006.jpg/330px-Green_Party_of_Canada_leadership_convention%2C_2006.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Green_Party_of_Canada_leadership_convention%2C_2006.jpg/440px-Green_Party_of_Canada_leadership_convention%2C_2006.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="473" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Green_Party_of_Canada_leadership_convention,_2006.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Green Party of Canada leadership convention, 2006</div></div></div>
<p>None of the party's candidates were elected, but the party received 4.5% of the vote nationally (up from 4.3% in 2004) and 665,940 votes (an increase of about 80,000 votes from 2004). Its best performance was in Alberta, where it received 6.6%. Sean Maw won 10.84% of the vote in <a href="/wiki/Wild_Rose_(electoral_district)" title="Wild Rose (electoral district)">Wild Rose</a> and finished a very distant second to Conservative <a href="/wiki/Myron_Thompson" title="Myron Thompson">Myron Thompson</a>. <a href="/wiki/Shane_Jolley" title="Shane Jolley">Shane Jolley</a> won 12.9% of the vote in <a href="/wiki/Bruce%E2%80%94Grey%E2%80%94Owen_Sound" title="Bruce—Grey—Owen Sound">Bruce—Grey—Owen Sound</a> riding, the largest share of the vote won by any of its candidates. In the riding <a href="/wiki/Ottawa_Centre" title="Ottawa Centre">Ottawa Centre</a>, the Green candidate <a href="/wiki/David_Chernushenko" title="David Chernushenko">David Chernushenko</a> received 6,766 votes, the largest number of votes of any of the party's candidates. It had been hoped by Greens that the party's deputy leader, Andrew Lewis, would achieve a breakthrough in <a href="/wiki/Saanich%E2%80%94Gulf_Islands" title="Saanich—Gulf Islands">Saanich—Gulf Islands</a> where he won 16.7% of the vote in the 2004 election and carried 17 of 238 polling divisions, making Lewis the first Green candidate to win even one polling division. However, running again in Saanich—Gulf Islands in 2006 he lost one-third of his 2004 vote share, winning only 9.6% of the ballots cast.
</p><p>The party's <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2006" title="Canadian federal election, 2006">2006 election campaign</a> was disrupted by allegations made by Matthew Pollesell, the party's former assistant national organizer, that Harris had not filed a proper accounting of money spent during his 2004 leadership campaign, as required by law. Pollesell issued a request that Elections Canada investigate. Pollesell and another former party member, Gretchen Schwarz, were subsequently warned by the party's legal counsel to retract allegations they had made or face a possible legal action. Dana Miller, who served in the party's shadow cabinet with responsibility for human-rights issues, made public her earlier complaints that the party has violated election law and its own constitution and has also asked for an Elections Canada investigation. Miller was expelled from the party after filing a complaint within the party in April.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elizabeth_May">Elizabeth May</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Elizabeth May">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Elizabeth_May.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Elizabeth_May.jpg/120px-Elizabeth_May.jpg" width="120" height="181" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Elizabeth_May.jpg/180px-Elizabeth_May.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Elizabeth_May.jpg/240px-Elizabeth_May.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="1807" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Elizabeth_May.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_May" title="Elizabeth May">Elizabeth May</a>, Green Party Leader since August 2006</div></div></div>
<p>Elizabeth May was elected leader of the Green Party of Canada in August 2006. She ran in the November 27, 2006 <a href="/wiki/London_North_Centre" title="London North Centre">London North Centre</a> by-election, finishing second with 26% of the votes. It was the Green Party's best-ever result in a federal election, easily eclipsing the previous mark of 16.7% achieved in  2004 in the B.C. riding of <a href="/wiki/Saanich-Gulf_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Saanich-Gulf Islands">Saanich-Gulf Islands</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008_election">2008 election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2008 election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2008" title="Canadian federal election, 2008">2008 general election</a>, the Green Party's vote was up again to 6.8% and 937,613 votes. On August 30, the former Liberal MP <a href="/wiki/Blair_Wilson" title="Blair Wilson">Blair Wilson</a> announces he is joining the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Canada" title="Green Party of Canada">Green Party of Canada</a>, becoming the party's first MP and He was defeated in the 2008 federal election, which was called before he had a chance to officially sit in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_Canada" title="House of Commons of Canada">House of Commons</a> as a Green MP. The best showing was in Elizabeth May's riding of Central Nova where May captured 32.2% of the vote against Conservative cabinet minister Peter MacKay.  Part of the reason for May's strong showing is that she brokered a deal with Liberal leader Stéphane Dion in which he agreed not to run a Liberal candidate.  In return, May agreed not to run a candidate in Dion's riding.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_election">2011 election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_Canada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2011 election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Both for the first time, the leader <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_May" title="Elizabeth May">Elizabeth May</a> became the first <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament#Canada" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">Member</a> of <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Canada" title="Parliament of Canada">Parliament</a> elected to represent the party. She defeated Conservative Incumbent and cabinet minister <a href="/wiki/Gary_Lunn" title="Gary Lunn">Gary Lunn</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-StarResultsPostElection_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StarResultsPostElection-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
