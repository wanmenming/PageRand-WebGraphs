<p>The <b>Green Party of Alaska</b><sup id="cite_ref-GM1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GM1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is the state party organization for <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Party (United States)">Green Party</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States of America</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-doe_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-doe-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  Alaska was the first state to gain Green Party <a href="/wiki/Ballot_access" title="Ballot access">ballot access</a>, in 1990, when <a href="/wiki/Jim_Sykes" title="Jim Sykes">Jim Sykes</a> ran for governor.  Sykes had previously filed a ballot access lawsuit, citing an earlier case, <i><a href="/wiki/Joe_Vogler" title="Joe Vogler">Vogler</a> v. <a href="/wiki/Terry_Miller_(politician)" title="Terry Miller (politician)">Miller</a></i>.
</p><p>Like the <a href="/wiki/Alaska_Libertarian_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Alaska Libertarian Party">Alaska Libertarian Party</a>, the Green Party organizes local affiliate groups by regions of the state rather than election districts.  It is known for calling these groups <a href="/wiki/Bioregion" title="Bioregion">bioregions</a>.  The organized bioregions of the GPAK include the Southcentral Bioregion (Anchorage area) and the Tanana-Yukon Bioregion (the Interior, around the Tanana and <a href="/wiki/Yukon_River" title="Yukon River">Yukon River</a> areas).
</p><p>The Green Party of Alaska has gained more than 10% of the votes in past presidential and congressional elections.  The most notable example was in 2000, when Alaska voters gave presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a> his highest state percentage.  Nader made headlines when he carried the <a href="/wiki/Girdwood,_Alaska" class="mw-redirect" title="Girdwood, Alaska">Girdwood</a> precinct, located at the extreme southern end of <a href="/wiki/Anchorage" class="mw-redirect" title="Anchorage">Anchorage</a> corporate limits.  In 1996, the party's U.S. Senate nominee <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jed_Whittaker&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jed Whittaker (page does not exist)">Jed Whittaker</a> came in second, out-polling Democratic nominee <a href="/wiki/Theresa_Obermeyer" title="Theresa Obermeyer">Theresa Obermeyer</a>, who had been disowned by her party.
</p><p>The first election victory associated with the party was in 1991, when <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kelley_Weaverling&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kelley Weaverling (page does not exist)">Kelley Weaverling</a> was elected <a href="/wiki/List_of_mayors_of_Cordova,_Alaska" title="List of mayors of Cordova, Alaska">mayor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cordova,_Alaska" title="Cordova, Alaska">Cordova</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-UPI1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UPI1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  Municipal elections in Alaska are nonpartisan, though Weaverling's association with the party was highly publicized at the time.
</p><p>In 2005, the party sued the State of Alaska over the issue of joint primary ballots and won in the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Alaska" class="mw-redirect" title="Supreme Court of Alaska">Supreme Court of Alaska</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FLaw1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FLaw1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Issues">Issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Alaska&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_policy">Energy policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Alaska&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Energy policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Green Party of Alaska is the only political party in the state which is opposed to industrial oil development in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_National_Wildlife_Refuge" title="Arctic National Wildlife Refuge">Arctic National Wildlife Refuge</a>. Like other Green parties, it supports the development of alternative fuels and energy sources, in particular <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a>.
</p><p>GPAK also supports an all-Alaska gas line.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_care">Health care</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Alaska&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Health care">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>GPAK supports a national single-payer healthcare system.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Alaska&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ballot_status">Ballot status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Alaska&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ballot status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Green Party first gained ballot access in 1990, but lost its Recognized Political Party status in 2002. Ballot access was regained in 2003 based on a court order,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> lost again in 2005, and regained in February 2006 when Superior Court Judge <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stephanie_Joannides&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stephanie Joannides (page does not exist)">Stephanie Joannides</a> issued a preliminary injunction against the State of Alaska, preventing the state from denying access to the Green Party. On June 3, 2007 a lower Alaska state court upheld Alaska's new definition of "political party" and the  Green Party of Alaska was removed from the ballot. The judge wrote that she had to uphold the new definition of "political party", because the Alaska Supreme Court had upheld the old definition of "political party" on November 17, 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-BA1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BA1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, the Alaska Green party put forth a statewide petition, seeking status as a "limited political party" which would allow them to put names on the ballot for presidential and vice-presidential candidates.  A total of 3,273 signatures is needed in Alaska to qualify as a limited political party.  The Alaskan Greens submitted approximately 4,500 signatures to account for any nitpicking the two major parties would inevitably assign to this petition.<sup id="cite_ref-BA2_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BA2-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
