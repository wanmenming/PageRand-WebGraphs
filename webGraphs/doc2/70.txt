<p>The <b>Green Party of Quebec</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Parti vert du Québec</i>; <b>PVQ</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political party</a> whose platform is the promotion of <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green values</a>. It has not won any seats in the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Quebec" title="National Assembly of Quebec">National Assembly of Quebec</a>. Its platform is orientated towards promotion of green values, sustainable development, and participatory democracy. The Party wants to differentiate itself from other <a href="/wiki/Political_parties_in_Quebec" class="mw-redirect" title="Political parties in Quebec">political parties in Quebec</a> by representing the left federalists of the province.
</p><p>The Green Party of Quebec is a coalition of activists and citizens for whom environmental questions are a priority. They believe that the government should help in creating a green, just, democratic and equal society.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Their main principles are inspired from the <a href="/wiki/Global_Greens_Charter" title="Global Greens Charter">Global Greens Charter</a> which revolves around six main ideas: <a href="/wiki/Ecological_wisdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological wisdom">ecological wisdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Participatory_democracy" title="Participatory democracy">participatory democracy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nonviolence" title="Nonviolence">nonviolence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> and <a href="/wiki/Respect_for_diversity" class="mw-redirect" title="Respect for diversity">respect for diversity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It received 1.69% of the popular vote in the 2018 Quebec election. The current leader is <a href="/wiki/Alex_Tyrrell" title="Alex Tyrrell">Alex Tyrrell</a>. Nominated as party leader on September 21, 2013 and then aged 25, he became the youngest party leader of Quebec and Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Quebec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="First_Green_Party_of_Quebec_.281985.E2.80.931998.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Green_Party_of_Quebec_(1985–1998)">First Green Party of Quebec (1985–1998)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Quebec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: First Green Party of Quebec (1985–1998)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A first version of the Green Party of Québec was founded in the 1980s and had candidates in the 1985, 1989 and 1994 <a href="/wiki/List_of_Quebec_general_elections" title="List of Quebec general elections">Quebec general elections</a>. The 1989 elections results were at the time the strongest showing for any Green Party in Canada. On average, candidates collected 5.55% of votes in contested <a href="/wiki/Legislative_seat" class="mw-redirect" title="Legislative seat">seats</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Although the party had a small budget, it attempted to run a province-wide campaign with organizers from Montreal, Québec City and Sherbrooke, as well as some relatively independent local campaigns in rural ridings. Many meetings were held at Le Commensal restaurant in Montréal, a strong supporter. Attempts were made to involve the various environmental groups, but most shied away from officially supporting the PVQ in order to maintain political neutrality and protect financial interests. In the party structure of 1989, sovereignty and economical neutrality were promoted rather than left-wing policies, under the slogan of "not left or right but forward". This caused some strife within the party, as many members were more left-leaning.
</p><p>The party disintegrated in 1994 due to its leader, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Ouimet" title="Jean Ouimet">Jean Ouimet</a>, and many of his colleagues leaving for the <a href="/wiki/Parti_Qu%C3%A9b%C3%A9cois" title="Parti Québécois">Parti Québécois</a>. Ouimet, a strong <a href="/wiki/Quebec_sovereignty_movement" title="Quebec sovereignty movement">sovereigntist</a>, maintained a party wholly independent of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Canada" title="Green Party of Canada">federal Green Party</a> during his leadership. Members of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Canada" title="Green Party of Canada">Green Party of Canada</a> formed an organization called the Green Party of Canada in Quebec, a predominantly anglophone entity that nominated federal candidates only. There was open antipathy between Ouimet and the GPCQ's leader, Rolf Bramann. (Neither was affiliated with Montreal's municipal Green Party of the time, Écologie-Montreal, led by Dmitri Roussopolis.) At the same time as the PVQ began to collapse due to Ouimet's departure, Rolf Bramann was removed from his position. This led to a precipitous decline in federal organization in the province contemporaneous with the collapse of the provincial Greens.
</p><p>It lost its recognition as an official political party in 1998 when it ran no candidates in the <a href="/wiki/Quebec_general_election,_1998" title="Quebec general election, 1998">1998 Quebec general election</a>. (Quebec law at the time required parties to run at least 20 candidates to maintain their official status. The <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Canada" title="Supreme Court of Canada">Supreme Court of Canada</a> <a href="/wiki/Figueroa_v._Canada_(Attorney_General)" class="mw-redirect" title="Figueroa v. Canada (Attorney General)">ruled minimum candidate laws unconstitutional</a> in 2003.)
</p>
<h3><span id="Current_Green_Party_of_Quebec_.282001.E2.80.93present.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_Green_Party_of_Quebec_(2001–present)">Current Green Party of Quebec (2001–present)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Quebec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Current Green Party of Quebec (2001–present)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The second (and current) version of the PVQ was founded in 2001 by members of the Green Party of Canada in Quebec after receiving more support in Quebec in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_federal_election,_2000" title="Canadian federal election, 2000">2000 federal election</a> than they had expected. The founding meeting, in the basement of the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Biodome" title="Montreal Biodome">Montreal Biodome</a>, was attended by about 20 people, and it contested the <a href="/wiki/Quebec_general_election,_2003" title="Quebec general election, 2003">2003 provincial election</a> with few candidates and almost no money.
</p><p>In 2002, three leftist political parties (<a href="/wiki/Rassemblement_pour_l%27alternative_progressiste" title="Rassemblement pour l&#39;alternative progressiste">Rassemblement pour l'alternative progressiste</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parti_de_la_d%C3%A9mocratie_socialiste" class="mw-redirect" title="Parti de la démocratie socialiste">Parti de la démocratie socialiste</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parti_Communiste_du_Qu%C3%A9bec" class="mw-redirect" title="Parti Communiste du Québec">Parti Communiste du Québec</a>) merged to form the <a href="/wiki/Union_des_forces_progressistes_(Canada)" class="mw-redirect" title="Union des forces progressistes (Canada)">Union des forces progressistes</a>. The PVQ pledged to try to avoid running candidates in ridings where there was a UFP candidate, although it reserved the right to run anywhere it wanted to (even ridings with a UFP candidate), and did not merge with the UFP. In May 2006, the Party pledged to stay independent after several appeals to join <a href="/wiki/Qu%C3%A9bec_solidaire" title="Québec solidaire">Québec solidaire</a>, the UFP's successor.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Scott_McKay" title="Scott McKay">Scott McKay</a> was elected as party leader in 2006. The party had its most successful showing ever in the <a href="/wiki/Quebec_general_election_2007" class="mw-redirect" title="Quebec general election 2007">2007 general election</a>, placing fourth with just under four per cent of the popular vote. Unlike the previous version of the party, the new version did not adopt a position on whether Quebec should become <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereign</a>. As a result, it was most competitive in western Montreal where there was a drop in <a href="/wiki/Quebec_Liberal_Party" title="Quebec Liberal Party">Liberal Party</a> support but little enthusiasm for <a href="/wiki/Quebec_sovereignty_movement" title="Quebec sovereignty movement">sovereigntist</a> alternatives such as the <a href="/wiki/Parti_Qu%C3%A9b%C3%A9cois" title="Parti Québécois">Parti Québécois</a>. The Green Party placed second or third in the popular vote in some western Montreal <a href="/wiki/Electoral_district_(Canada)" title="Electoral district (Canada)">ridings</a>.
</p><p>In 2008, the PVQ held a leadership review, during which <a href="/wiki/Guy_Rainville" title="Guy Rainville">Guy Rainville</a> defeated Scott McKay. McKay then joined the Parti Québécois and was elected as an MNA in the <a href="/wiki/Quebec_general_election,_2008" title="Quebec general election, 2008">2008 election</a>, while the Green Party itself fell to two per cent of the popular vote, fifth place among political parties and the only one of the top five parties not to win a <a href="/wiki/Legislative_seat" class="mw-redirect" title="Legislative seat">seat</a> in the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Quebec" title="National Assembly of Quebec">National Assembly</a>. On September 10, 2010, Rainville announced that he would not seek another two-year term as leader.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Claude_Sabourin" title="Claude Sabourin">Claude Sabourin</a> narrowly defeated party president Paul-André Martineau for the position.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Martineau had been president of the Green Party since 2006, except for a brief period in 2008. He has a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">Bachelor of Arts</a> degree in Political Science from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Montreal" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Montreal">University of Montreal</a> (1985) and a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor&#39;s degree">bachelor's degree</a> in business administration from <a href="/wiki/HEC_Montr%C3%A9al" title="HEC Montréal">HEC Montréal</a> (2001). At the time of the leadership contest, he was working in information technology and pursuing a specialized graduate degree in environment and sustainable development from the <a href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_du_Qu%C3%A9bec_%C3%A0_Montr%C3%A9al" title="Université du Québec à Montréal">Université du Québec à Montréal</a>. He has not run for federal or provincial office.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2012, the leader Claude Sabourin went to a meeting in La Pinière riding, where he recruited four future candidates, two of which — Alex Tyrrell<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> &amp; Marc André Beauchemin<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> — would later run for the leadership of the party.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Overall the party lost over half its support from the previous election, obtaining 1% of the overall popular vote and running candidates in only 66 of Quebec's 125 ridings during the <a href="/wiki/Quebec_general_election,_2012" title="Quebec general election, 2012">September 4, 2012 election</a>. Sabourin finished fifth in NDG riding with 1,531 votes and 5.77% of the vote. Alex Tyrrell finished third in <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Cartier" title="Jacques Cartier">Jacques Cartier</a> with 1,522 votes and 4.54% of the vote.
</p><p>Claude Sabourin resigned as party leader on Sunday, February 24, 2013 at the Green Party of Quebec convention held that weekend in Montreal.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Jean Cloutier was elected interim leader that same day.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On September 21, 2013, Alex Tyrrell was elected leader of the PVQ defeating other leadership candidates Patricia Domingos, Marc-André Beauchemin and Pierre-Étienne Loignon.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Another candidate Lisa Julie Cahn<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> had withdrawn earlier in the race. At the age of 25 years Mr. Tyrrell became the youngest current party leader in Quebec politics.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Parti vert du Québec intended to diversify its policies in view of the 2014 general elections, in order to increase its support.  Tyrrell explained that the PVQ would be an eco-socialist group. Thus, the environment would remain its priority but the party would also defend a public health system and would propose among other things a universal program of dental care for all Quebecers. It is also in favour of <a href="/wiki/Free_public_transport" title="Free public transport">free public transport</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
