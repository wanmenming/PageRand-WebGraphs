<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party of the United States</a></div>
<p>The Green Party is a United States political party. It has its origins dating back to 1984, when 62 people from around the U.S. came to St. Paul, MN to found the first national Green organization - the Committees of Correspondence. Since then, U.S. Greens have gone through several evolutions, from debating theory and praxis in the 1980s, to starting state parties in the 1990s, to the founding of a national political party (recognized by the Federal Elections Commission) in the 2000s.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial">Initial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Initial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the first <a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Bio-regional_Congress&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="North American Bio-regional Congress (page does not exist)">North American Bio-regional Congress</a> in May 1984, (convened by <a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Haenke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="David Haenke (page does not exist)">David Haenke</a> of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ozark_Area_Community_Congress&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ozark Area Community Congress (page does not exist)">Ozark Area Community Congress</a> and in Missouri),<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> a group met to discuss the need for a green movement in the U.S.. They approved a Green Movement Committee statement "concerning the formation of a Green political organization in the USA."<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> From this initial gathering, a larger meeting was planned for August 1984.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_Committees_of_Correspondence">Green Committees of Correspondence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Green Committees of Correspondence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 1984, 62 people met at <a href="/wiki/Macalester_College" title="Macalester College">Macalester College</a> in <a href="/wiki/St._Paul,_Minnesota" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Paul, Minnesota">St. Paul, Minnesota</a> and founded the Committees of Correspondence (or CoC, so named after the <a href="/wiki/Committees_of_Correspondence" class="mw-redirect" title="Committees of Correspondence">Committees of Correspondence</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>).
</p><p>The three-day meeting included activists from peace, ecology and justice groups; veterans of the women's, civil rights, and community movements; and farmers, community leaders, church activists and teachers. There were social ecologists, deep ecologists, eco-feminists, anarchists, socialists and more.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The organizing committee was made up of professor <a href="/wiki/Charlene_Spretnak" title="Charlene Spretnak">Charlene Spretnak</a> of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Harry_Boyte&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Harry Boyte (page does not exist)">Harry Boyte</a>, Catherine Burton, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Goldberg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gloria Goldberg (page does not exist)">Gloria Goldberg</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Haenke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="David Haenke (page does not exist)">David Haenke</a>; and they invited 200 people from 27 issues areas.
</p><p>The CoC was broadly formed to organize local Green groups and work toward creating a Green political organization in the U.S, including the forming of an interim Inter-Regional Committee (IC).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (January 2016)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The meeting also started the creation of the Green <a href="/wiki/Ten_Key_Values" class="mw-redirect" title="Ten Key Values">Ten Key Values</a>, as the new organization's guiding principles. Differing accounts of this process have been written by Mark Satin<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and Spretnak.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first CoC clearinghouse was established in late 1984 in St. Paul with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Harry_Boyte&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Harry Boyte (page does not exist)">Harry Boyte</a>, but was hampered by a division at the Macalester meeting as to its role, with a division between those who favored coordinated decentralization and those favoring radical decentralization, to the degree that the clearinghouse be a mail drop and information resource, but not an outreach vehicle.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>At the December 1985 IC meeting in Kansas City, the decision was taken that both the IC and the clearinghouse should actively support organizing efforts through a number of services. The clearinghouse was moved to Kansas City where there was a local organisation (the Prairie Greens) to actively support it. At that time IC Bulletin, the CoC newsletter and the <i>Green Letter</i>, a newsletter published by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jerry_Gwathney&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jerry Gwathney (page does not exist)">Jerry Gwathney</a>, were the main channels of information.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="First_National_Green_Gathering.2C_1987"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="First_National_Green_Gathering,_1987">First National Green Gathering, 1987</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First National Green Gathering, 1987">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first "National Green Gathering" was held July 1987, at Hampshire College in Amherst, MA and was entitled "Building the Green Movement - A National Conference for a New Politics." The conference brochure stated it as an educational conference, not as a decision making conference.
</p><p>Over 600 were in attendance. Some of the tensions within the U.S. Green movement were on display at the time - 'party vs. movement', 'deep ecology vs. social ecology' and 'New Left vs. New Age.'<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Featured speakers included <a href="/wiki/Grace_Lee_Boggs" title="Grace Lee Boggs">Grace Lee Boggs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Murray_Bookchin" title="Murray Bookchin">Murray Bookchin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walt_Bresette" class="mw-redirect" title="Walt Bresette">Walt Bresette</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guy_Chichester" title="Guy Chichester">Guy Chichester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Epstein" title="Barbara Epstein">Barbara Epstein</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Danny_Moses&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Danny Moses (page does not exist)">Danny Moses</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Rensenbrink" title="John Rensenbrink">John Rensenbrink</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ynestra_King&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ynestra King (page does not exist)">Ynestra King</a>. Workshops included a session on Independent Political Action. Other well-known Greens in attendance included <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dee_Berry&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dee Berry (page does not exist)">Dee Berry</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kathy_Christensen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kathy Christensen (page does not exist)">Kathy Christensen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greta_Gaard" title="Greta Gaard">Greta Gaard</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gerald_Goldfarb&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gerald Goldfarb (page does not exist)">Gerald Goldfarb</a>, <a href="/wiki/Howie_Hawkins" title="Howie Hawkins">Howie Hawkins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phil_Hill" title="Phil Hill">Phil Hill</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Myra_Levy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Myra Levy (page does not exist)">Myra Levy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Mendoza" class="mw-redirect" title="Roberto Mendoza">Roberto Mendoza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lorna_Salzman" title="Lorna Salzman">Lorna Salzman</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brian_Tokar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brian Tokar (page does not exist)">Brian Tokar</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nancy_Vogl&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nancy Vogl (page does not exist)">Nancy Vogl</a>.
</p><p>ink]] of Maine were selected principal coordinators of what would come to be called the SPAKA process - Strategic Policy Approaches in Key Areas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The process started with a call for subjects, which went out to all the Green locals, and many others. The responses to were bundled into 11 major categories. Later they were bundled in 19 categories: Energy, Forest and Forestry, Life Forms, Materials Use and Waste Management, Water/Air, General Economic Analysis, Finance, Land Use, Politics, Social Justice, Eco-Philosophy, Spirituality, Education, Food and Agriculture, Health, Peace and Non-violence, Community, Organizing, and Strategy. 190 position papers (SPAKAS) were received.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>At the "Green Program Gathering" in <a href="/wiki/Eugene,_Oregon" title="Eugene, Oregon">Eugene, Oregon</a>, policy approaches were provisionally approved by delegates, in order to send them back to the Green locals (GCoCs) for an additional year of review and input. The final document was approved at the Third Green Gathering, held in <a href="/wiki/Estes_Park,_Colorado" title="Estes Park, Colorado">Estes Park, Colorado</a> in September 1990.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Greening_the_West.2C_1988"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Greening_the_West,_1988">Greening the West, 1988</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Greening the West, 1988">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Greening the West conference was held in (<a href="/wiki/San_Mateo_County" class="mw-redirect" title="San Mateo County">San Mateo County</a>), California in September/October 1988. It was hosted by the Northern California Greens. More than 1,000 people attended.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The conference featured a workshop entitled "Towards a Green Party of the West: Local and Regional Electoral Strategies", which would turn out to be an early stepping stone in the development of a U.S. Green electoral politics. Some 150 people attended the workshop and moved ahead with forming <a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_the_West&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Green Party of the West (page does not exist)">Green Party of the West</a>, a support network. That network form the nucleus for the founding of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_California" title="Green Party of California">Green Party of California</a> 15 months later.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> On December 31, 1991 <a href="/wiki/California_Secretary_of_State" class="mw-redirect" title="California Secretary of State">California Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/March_Fong_Eu" title="March Fong Eu">March Fong Eu</a> announced that the California Green Party had registered the prerequisite number of voters to be qualify for the 1992 ballot, becoming the California's seventh political party with ballot access.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Second_National_Green_Gathering.2C_1989"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_National_Green_Gathering,_1989">Second National Green Gathering, 1989</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Second National Green Gathering, 1989">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Second National Green Gathering was held in June 1989 in Eugene, OR. It was entitled 'Green Program Gathering', because it centered upon the SPAKA process (Strategic Policy Approaches in Key Areas), to craft a nationally agreed upon set of Green policy approaches for the GCoC. The local GCoC in Eugene produced a daily Green Tidings, reporting on the Gathering, and contained a daily report on all changes in the 19 subject areas.
</p><p>Afterward, the Politics Working Group issued a statement encouraging Green electoral activity but recommending that Greens begin running candidates at the local level and only proceed to the state and then to the national level when there were a substantial number of Green officeholders at the level immediately below.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Greens.2FGreen_Party_USA"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Greens/Green_Party_USA">Greens/Green Party USA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Greens/Green Party USA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Greens/Green_Party_USA" title="Greens/Green Party USA">Greens/Green Party USA</a> (Greens/GPUSA) was founded at the August 1991 Green Gathering in <a href="/wiki/Elkins,_West_Virginia" title="Elkins, West Virginia">Elkins, West Virginia</a> restructuring the Green Committees of Correspondence with the idea that the Green movement and Green Party would operate as part of a single organization. A press conference was held in Washington, DC to announce the new organization, featuring <a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Betz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Charles Betz (page does not exist)">Charles Betz</a> (G/GPUSA Coordinating Committee member), <a href="/wiki/Howie_Hawkins" title="Howie Hawkins">Howie Hawkins</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Joni_Whitmore&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Joni Whitmore (page does not exist)">Joni Whitmore</a> (Chair, Green Party of Alaska), as well as <a href="/wiki/Hilda_Mason" title="Hilda Mason">Hilda Mason</a> of the D.C. Statehood Party.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Association_of_State_Green_Parties">Association of State Green Parties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Association of State Green Parties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the aftermath of the first Green presidential campaign in 1996, 62 Greens from 30 states gathered in November 1996 to found the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_State_Green_Parties" title="Association of State Green Parties">Association of State Green Parties</a> (ASGP).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (January 2016)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The meeting was held in <a href="/wiki/Middleburg,_Virginia" title="Middleburg, Virginia">Middleburg, Virginia</a>.
</p><p>After the Elkins meeting in 1991, there was a committee tasked with examining the options for an eventual Green Party. The committee produced a report with contributions from 6 authors. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Greg_Gerritt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Greg Gerritt (page does not exist)">Greg Gerritt</a>'s suggestion was to create an Association of State Green Parties based on sovereign state parties, as all political parties are organised in the USA. The reaction of the GPUSA to this suggestion was to throw Gerritt out of the GPUSA.  What was founded in 1996 in Middleburg generally followed the proposal Gerritt had submitted in early 1992.
</p><p>Green Parties from 13 states were the founding members,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and approved its mission: (1) Assist in the development of State Green Parties and (2) Create a legally structured national Green Party.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The founding meeting also established a national newsletter <a href="/wiki/Green_Pages" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Pages">Green Pages</a>.
</p><p>The concept of the ASGP began in 1992 with those involved in the establishment of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Politics_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Green Politics Network (page does not exist)">Green Politics Network</a>. The ASGP was predominantly focused on establishing state Green Parties and running and electing Greens for public office, even while its member state parties and the individual Greens involved remained involved in issue activism. From 1997 to 1999, both the ASGP and the Greens/GPUSA aimed to win affiliation of the newly form state parties.
</p><p>In December 1999, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Feinstein" title="Mike Feinstein">Mike Feinstein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Howie_Hawkins" title="Howie Hawkins">Howie Hawkins</a> met in New York and wrote the <i>Plan for a Single National Green Party</i>, which was more generally known as the Feinstein/Hawkins Proposal. Its idea was to create a single national Green Party from among the ASGP and GPUSA. It had support in both organisations but stalled.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In October 2000, the Feinstein/Hawkins proposal was revived and renamed the "Boston Agreement".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The Boston agreement was approved was by the ASGP at its December 2000 meeting in Hiawasee, GA, but did not pass at the April 2001 G/GPUSA Congress. This caused a schism in membership among the G/GPUSA from which they never recovered.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In an article on the G/GPUSA’s website, the organization characterized the split between itself and the ASGP as akin to the <a href="/wiki/Fundi_(politics)" title="Fundi (politics)">fundi</a>–realo split in the <a href="/wiki/German_Greens" class="mw-redirect" title="German Greens">German Greens</a>, with itself being the fundi wing and ASGP the realos.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At its July 2001 meeting in Santa Barbara, the ASGP voted to change its name to "The Green Party of the United States" and apply for recognition of National Committee status by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Election_Commission" title="Federal Election Commission">Federal Election Commission</a>, which it was granted later that year and has retained since.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electoral_participation">Electoral participation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Electoral participation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first U.S. Greens to run for public office, 1985, were <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wes_Hare&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wes Hare (page does not exist)">Wes Hare</a> for Mayor of <a href="/wiki/Chapel_Hill,_North_Carolina" title="Chapel Hill, North Carolina">Chapel Hill, North Carolina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_D._Wolff" title="Richard D. Wolff">Richard D. Wolff</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Joel_Schecter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Joel Schecter (page does not exist)">Joel Schecter</a> for Mayor and Alderman of <a href="/wiki/New_Haven,_Connecticut" title="New Haven, Connecticut">New Haven, Connecticut</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The first U.S. Greens to be elected were <a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Conley_(politician)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="David Conley (politician) (page does not exist)">David Conley</a>, County Board of Supervisors, <a href="/wiki/Douglas_County,_Wisconsin" title="Douglas County, Wisconsin">Douglas County, Wisconsin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frank_Koehn" title="Frank Koehn">Frank Koehn</a>, County Board of Supervisors, <a href="/wiki/Bayfield_County,_Wisconsin" title="Bayfield County, Wisconsin">Bayfield County, Wisconsin</a>. Also in 1986, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Greg_Gerritt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Greg Gerritt (page does not exist)">Greg Gerritt</a> would become the first Green to run for a state legislature, running for the Maine House of Representatives.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The first state Green party to achieve ballot status was Alaska in 1990, based upon <a href="/wiki/Jim_Sykes" title="Jim Sykes">Jim Sykes</a> receiving 3.4% for governor of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. The next state parties to qualify came in California, Arizona, New Mexico and Hawaii in 1992, and Maine in 1994.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The first Green to be elected to a state legislature was <a href="/wiki/Audie_Bock" title="Audie Bock">Audie Bock</a>, who won a March 1999 special election as a state legislator in the <a href="/wiki/California_Assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="California Assembly">California Assembly</a> from the Alameda/Oakland area. In 2000, Bock lost re-election, running as an independent after leaving the Greens just five months after taking office.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In November 2002, <a href="/wiki/John_Eder" title="John Eder">John Eder</a>'s election to the <a href="/wiki/Maine_House_of_Representatives" title="Maine House of Representatives">Maine House of Representatives</a> marked the first Green Party state legislator in the United States elected in a regular election. His party designation was "<a href="/wiki/Maine_Green_Independent_Party" title="Maine Green Independent Party">Green Independent</a>". In 2004, he won re-election.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (January 2016)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> However, he was defeated in 2006.
</p><p>In January 2003, New Jersey Democrat <a href="/wiki/Matt_Ahearn" title="Matt Ahearn">Matt Ahearn</a> became the first sitting state legislator to change affiliation to Green. He was defeated in November 2003 in his bid for re-election.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2008, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Carroll_(politician)" title="Richard Carroll (politician)">Richard Carroll</a> was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_House_of_Representatives" title="Arkansas House of Representatives">Arkansas House of Representatives</a>. However, Carroll announced on April 29, 2009 his departure from the Greens and registering as a Democrat, citing ideological differences that were more in line of the Democratic Party.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1996_Presidential_Election">1996 Presidential Election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 1996 Presidential Election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the 1995 national Green Gathering in <a href="/wiki/Albuquerque,_New_Mexico" title="Albuquerque, New Mexico">Albuquerque, New Mexico</a>, a proposal to put a candidate for president on 40 states was adopted. A significant minority of Greens voiced strong ideological objections (based on the principle of decentralization) to the proposal to become involved in such a large-scale political arena for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Those who wished to run a candidate for president continued to pursue the possibility. Working within their state parties, as well as through an independent organization called Third Parties '96,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> they convinced <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a> to accept placement on the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_California" title="Green Party of California">Green Party of California's</a> March 1996 primary ballot. Eventually he accepted placement on more ballots, but ran a limited campaign with a self-imposed campaign spending limit of $5,000 (which allowed him to avoid being subject to the obligation to file campaign finance statements with the FEC). He chose <a href="/wiki/Winona_LaDuke" title="Winona LaDuke">Winona LaDuke</a> as his vice-presidential candidate. A convention was held at UCLA in Los Angeles on August 20, 1996<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> where Nader accepted the nomination.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (January 2016)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Nader/LaDuke were on the ballot in twenty-two states and received 685,297 votes, or 0.7% of all votes cast.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000_Presidential_Election">2000 Presidential Election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2000 Presidential Election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September 1998, the New Mexico Green Party proposed that an ASGP Presidential Exploratory Committee be established for the 2000 elections. The ASGP Coordinating Committee agreed and in December the ASGP Steering Committee appointed a seven-person committee. In February 1999 it sent out a letter questionnaire to potential candidates.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ralphnaderspeech.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Ralphnaderspeech.jpg/220px-Ralphnaderspeech.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Ralphnaderspeech.jpg/330px-Ralphnaderspeech.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Ralphnaderspeech.jpg/440px-Ralphnaderspeech.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2531" data-file-height="1682" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ralphnaderspeech.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ralph Nader, 1996 and 2000 nominee</div></div></div><p> At it June convention in Denver,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> the ASGP nominated <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a> and <a href="/wiki/Winona_LaDuke" title="Winona LaDuke">Winona LaDuke</a> for president and vice-president. The pair appeared on 44 state ballots and received 2,883,105 votes, or 2.7 percent of all votes cast.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to Republican candidate <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>'s tiny margin of victory in Florida and New Hampshire,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> some Democrats accused Nader of "<a href="/wiki/Spoiler_effect" title="Spoiler effect">spoiling</a>" the election of <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a> nominee.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2004_Presidential_Election">2004 Presidential Election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 2004 Presidential Election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_2004" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. presidential election, 2004">2004 presidential election</a>, the candidate of the Green Party of the United States for <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> was <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> attorney and GPUS legal counsel <a href="/wiki/David_Cobb" title="David Cobb">David Cobb</a>, and its candidate for vice-president was labor activist <a href="/wiki/Pat_LaMarche" title="Pat LaMarche">Pat LaMarche</a> of <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>.
</p><p>In the Summer of 2003 the  Greens began an debate on party presidential strategy. Interest groups were pressuring the Green Party and Ralph Nader not to run a presidential ticket. In response, a section of U.S. Greens issued the statement "Green &amp; Growing: 2004 in Perspective".<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Eve" title="Christmas Eve">Christmas Eve</a> 2003, Ralph Nader declared that he would not seek the Green Party's nomination for president in 2004, and in February 2004 announced his intention to run as an independent. However he did seek endorsement (rather than the nomination) of the Green Party and other third parties.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Cobb-LaMarche ticket in 2004 appeared on 28 of the 51 ballots around the country, down from the Greens' 44 in 2000; the Nader-Camejo ticket in 2004 appeared on 35 ballots.  In 2004, Cobb was on the ballot in California (and Nader was not), whereas Nader was on the ballot in New York (and Cobb was not).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Cobb-LaMarche received 119,859 votes.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Some Greens were not discouraged by the relatively low presidential vote yield in 2004 for Cobb and for Nader, because the Green Party continued to grow in many parts of the country, increasing Green Party affiliation numbers and fielding Green candidates for congressional, state, and local offices. Nader, running in most states as an independent (but with high-profile Green Party activist Peter Camejo as his running mate), received 465,650 votes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The number of registered Greens declined by about 23,000 between January 2004 and March 2005, in contrast to a previous period of uninterrupted growth from 1998; the number of Green candidacies declined compared to 2002, and these candidates fared worse than in the past, particularly during the presidential campaign cycle.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Endorsement_versus_nomination_controversy">Endorsement versus nomination controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Endorsement versus nomination controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Nader announced that he would run as an independent candidate (he had never become a party member).  Later he explained that he was not seeking the Green Party's nomination, but would (as an independent) seek the party's "endorsement".<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Factions within the party which had been lining up behind potential candidates solidified into a camp favoring a Nader endorsement and a camp favoring nominating an actual Green Party member (the latter favoring primarily David Cobb).
</p><p>On June 26, 2004, the Green National Convention nominated Cobb, who promised to focus on building the party.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>  Just over a third of the delegates voted "No Nominee" with the intent to later vote for a Nader endorsement.  Pat LaMarche of Maine was nominated for vice-president.  Cobb and Nader emphasized different strategies. Cobb promised to run a "strategic states" campaign based on the preferences and needs of the individual state Green parties; as a result, Cobb campaigned heavily in some battleground states and not in others.  Nader intended to run a national multiparty ticket uniting the Greens with other parties.
</p><p>After David Cobb received the party's 2004 presidential nomination at the Green National Convention<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a>, apparently in a show of unity, Nader's <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice Presidential</a> running mate, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Camejo" title="Peter Camejo">Peter Camejo</a>, said, "I'm going to walk out of here arm in arm with David Cobb."  However, the nominating convention and the political discussions and maneuvering before it generated considerable controversy within the party. At issue was the apportionment of delegates and the method used to determine how many delegates each state received. The group Greens for Democracy and Independence, inspired by the principles in Peter Camejo's <a href="/w/index.php?title=Avocado_Declaration&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Avocado Declaration (page does not exist)">Avocado Declaration</a> (in part a response to Nader's declaration not to seek the Green nomination), arose and became an organizing group for Greens disaffected with the internal policies and procedures of the GPUS, and sought reforms.
</p><p>Two supporters of Camejo, Carol Miller and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Forrest_Hill_(politician)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Forrest Hill (politician) (page does not exist)">Forrest Hill</a>, wrote one of a number of articles printed after the convention, including <i>Rigged Convention; Divided Party'</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> alleging that the convention elections had been undemocratic. Many Green Party members were upset at the nomination convention's process and results, and some expressed "embarrassment" that Nader was not the party's 2004 candidate.
</p><p>Other Green Party members responded<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> that the analysis they gave in the article was fundamentally flawed<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> to produce skewed results.  One such response was that of the national party Secretary, Greg Gerritt, who self-published a book on the subject, <i>Green Party Tempest</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2006_Elections">2006 Elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 2006 Elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Greens fielded candidates in a number of races in 2006. The party won 66 races nationwide, including 21 in California and 11 in Wisconsin. One of the biggest victories included the election of <a href="/wiki/Gayle_McLaughlin" title="Gayle McLaughlin">Gayle McLaughlin</a> as mayor in <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_California" title="Richmond, California">Richmond, California</a>. Richmond now has become the first city with over 100,000 residents to have a Green mayor. In <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pat_LaMarche" title="Pat LaMarche">Pat LaMarche</a> received nearly 10% of the vote in the <a href="/wiki/Maine_gubernatorial_election,_2006" title="Maine gubernatorial election, 2006">state's gubernatorial race</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Maine_Green_Independent_Party" title="Maine Green Independent Party">Maine Green Independent Party</a> also won two seats on the Portland City Council. In the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_gubernatorial_election,_2006" title="Illinois gubernatorial election, 2006">Illinois governor's race</a>, candidate <a href="/wiki/Rich_Whitney" title="Rich Whitney">Rich Whitney</a> received 10%, making the Green Party one of only three legally <i>established</i>, statewide political parties in Illinois.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In <a href="/wiki/Colorado%27s_1st_congressional_district" title="Colorado&#39;s 1st congressional district">Colorado's First District</a>, Tom Kelly received 21% of the vote in his run for the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Congress" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Congress">U.S. Congress</a>.  However, the party lost its only elected state representative, <a href="/wiki/John_Eder" title="John Eder">John Eder</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Pennsylvania" title="Green Party of Pennsylvania">Green Party of Pennsylvania</a>, faced with an exceptionally high ballot access petition requirement, chose to run Green Party <a href="/wiki/Organizer_(party)" class="mw-redirect" title="Organizer (party)">organizer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Pennsylvania" title="Green Party of Pennsylvania">Carl Romanelli</a>, for U.S. Senate.  The race between incumbent, <a href="/wiki/Rick_Santorum" title="Rick Santorum">Rick Santorum</a>, and the son of a former Governor, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Casey,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Bob Casey, Jr.">Bob Casey</a>, was already prominent on the national scene.  Although a strong volunteer petition effort gathered 20,000 to 30,000 signatures, it was clear that paid petitioners would be needed to clear the 67,000 signature threshold.
After Romanelli filed 99,000 signatures the Democrats challenged the petitions, and the Judge ordered the lawyers and nine representatives from each side to work full-time reviewing signatures line by line, which continued for six weeks.  Near the end of September the Judge abruptly ruled that Romanelli would be removed from the ballot.  Following the controversial precedent set in the 2004 challenge to Nader's petitions in <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>,  Romanelli and his lawyer were later assessed $81,000 for court costs and the challenger's expenses.  The Green Party, having no statewide candidates on the ballot to get the required vote threshold, lost its "minor party" status in Pennsylvania, leaving only two parties still recognized by the state.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Approximately 8.7 million Americans voted for withdrawal of U.S. troops from <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> and for <a href="/wiki/Impeachment" title="Impeachment">impeachment</a> resolutions on local and state ballots that were initiated or supported by Greens. Troop withdrawal initiatives won in 34 of 42 localities in <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madison,_WI" class="mw-redirect" title="Madison, WI">Madison</a>, and <a href="/wiki/La_Crosse,_WI" class="mw-redirect" title="La Crosse, WI">La Crosse</a>, and all 11 communities in <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>. Of 139 cities and towns in <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> voting on the troop withdrawal measures, only a handful voted nay on initiatives demanding that Congress and the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> end the war immediately.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008_Presidential_election">2008 Presidential election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 2008 Presidential election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_election,_2008" title="United States presidential election, 2008">2008 U.S. presidential election</a>, the Green Party nominated former six-term Congresswoman <a href="/wiki/Cynthia_McKinney" title="Cynthia McKinney">Cynthia McKinney</a> of Georgia as its 2008 <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">Presidential</a> nominee and <a href="/wiki/Rosa_Clemente" title="Rosa Clemente">Rosa Clemente</a> as its 2008 <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice Presidential</a> nominee at the party's 2008 National Convention on July 12, 2008 in Chicago, IL. McKinney received 161,797 votes [0.12%] nationwide.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
The following candidates also sought the Green nomination and were recognized as such by the [Green Party of the United States]:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Jesse_Johnson_(politician)" title="Jesse Johnson (politician)">Jesse Johnson</a> of <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mountain_Party" title="Mountain Party">Mountain Party</a> 2004 nominee for Governor of WV and 2006 nominee for <a href="/wiki/U.S._Senate" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Senate">U.S. Senate</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kent_Mesplay" class="mw-redirect" title="Kent Mesplay">Kent Mesplay</a><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, environmentalist and CA Delegate to the <a href="#Green_National_Committee">Green National Committee</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kat_Swift" title="Kat Swift">Kat Swift</a><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, Co-chair of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Texas" title="Green Party of Texas">Green Party of Texas</a> and 2007 Green Party nominee for <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio" title="San Antonio">San Antonio</a> City Council</li></ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Minnesota" title="Green Party of Minnesota">Green Party of Minnesota</a> hosted a Green Party Presidential Forum on Saturday January 5 in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> It was followed by a January 13 presidential debate in <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a>, co-sponsored by the Green Party of <a href="/wiki/Alameda_County" class="mw-redirect" title="Alameda County">Alameda County</a>, the San Francisco Green Party and the National Delegates Committee of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_California" title="Green Party of California">Green Party of California</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> About 800 people attended, with most paying a suggested donation of $10 to $20 to attend.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The three-hour event was co-moderated by <a href="/wiki/Cindy_Sheehan" title="Cindy Sheehan">Cindy Sheehan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aimee_Allison" title="Aimee Allison">Aimee Allison</a>.
</p><p>Former Green Party presidential nominee and 2004 independent candidate, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> announced in early 2008 that he would seek the presidency for the fourth time, running with San Francisco lawyer and Green politician <a href="/wiki/Matt_Gonzalez" title="Matt Gonzalez">Matt Gonzalez</a> as his running mate.  However, Nader and Gonzalez declined to seek the Green Party's nomination.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Despite not being a formally announced candidate at the time, Nader won the Feb. 5th California and Massachusetts Green Party primaries.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Primaries_and_caucuses">Primaries and caucuses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Primaries and caucuses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Green Party primaries in Arkansas, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> were held on February 5, 2008. California and Massachusetts were won by <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a>, while Illinois was won by <a href="/wiki/Cynthia_McKinney" title="Cynthia McKinney">Cynthia McKinney</a>. <a href="/wiki/Washington,_DC" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington, DC">Washington, DC</a> held the <a href="/wiki/DC_Statehood_Green_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="DC Statehood Green Party">DC Statehood Green Party</a> primary on February 12 which was won by McKinney as was the February 19 <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a> <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_Green_Party" title="Wisconsin Green Party">Green</a> primary.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> On May 13 Mckinney won the Nebraska primary with 57% of the vote.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ballot_access">Ballot access</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Green_Party_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Ballot access">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are 31 <a href="#State_Parties">states</a> plus the <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="District of Columbia">District of Columbia</a> where the Green Party has achieved a ballot line in 2008<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> representing just over 70% of voters<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> and 68% of <a href="/wiki/Electoral_College_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Electoral College (United States)">Electoral Votes</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cynthia_McKinney" title="Cynthia McKinney">Cynthia McKinney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rosa_Clemente" title="Rosa Clemente">Rosa Clemente</a> were <a href="/wiki/Write-in_candidate" title="Write-in candidate">write-in candidates</a> in all other states with the exceptions of <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Dakota" title="South Dakota">South Dakota</a> which do not allow write-ins.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
