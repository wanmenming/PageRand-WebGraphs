<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Green New Deal</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For the New Economics Foundation report, see <a href="/wiki/A_Green_New_Deal" title="A Green New Deal">A Green New Deal</a>.</div>
<p>The term <b>Green New Deal</b> (also <b>New New Deal</b>)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> is used to describe any <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(economics)" title="Stimulus (economics)">stimulus package</a> that aims to address both <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis" title="Financial crisis">financial crises</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_issue" title="Environmental issue">environmental issues</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">climate change</a>. The term references the <a href="/wiki/New_Deal" title="New Deal">New Deal</a>, the social and economic stimulus package undertaken by President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> in response to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Supporters of a Green New Deal most likely advocate for a combination of Roosevelt's fiscal ideals with more contemporary approaches such as <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_commercialization" title="Renewable energy commercialization">investment in renewable energies</a> and the promotion of greater <a href="/wiki/Resource_efficiency" title="Resource efficiency">resource efficiency</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_concept">History of the concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_New_Deal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of the concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TVA_Results_of_Fertilizer.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/TVA_Results_of_Fertilizer.gif/220px-TVA_Results_of_Fertilizer.gif" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/TVA_Results_of_Fertilizer.gif/330px-TVA_Results_of_Fertilizer.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/TVA_Results_of_Fertilizer.gif/440px-TVA_Results_of_Fertilizer.gif 2x" data-file-width="590" data-file-height="471" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TVA_Results_of_Fertilizer.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>New Deal - use of synthetic fertilizers to boost agricultural production</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Solar_panels_in_Oregon_vineyard.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Solar_panels_in_Oregon_vineyard.jpg/220px-Solar_panels_in_Oregon_vineyard.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Solar_panels_in_Oregon_vineyard.jpg/330px-Solar_panels_in_Oregon_vineyard.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Solar_panels_in_Oregon_vineyard.jpg/440px-Solar_panels_in_Oregon_vineyard.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Solar_panels_in_Oregon_vineyard.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Green New Deal - Sustainable agriculture combined with the generation of renewable energy</div></div></div>
<p>An early influential use of the term Green New Deal, which gave it prominence in the US and well beyond, was by journalist <a href="/wiki/Thomas_L._Friedman" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas L. Friedman">Thomas L. Friedman</a>. He  argued in favor of such concepts in two pieces that appeared in the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Magazine" title="The New York Times Magazine">The New York Times Magazine</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In January 2007 Friedman wrote:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>If you have put a windmill in your yard or some solar panels on your roof, bless your heart. But we will only green the world when we change the very nature of the electricity grid -- moving it away from dirty coal or oil to clean coal and renewables. And that is a huge industrial project -- much bigger than anyone has told you. Finally, like the New Deal, if we undertake the green version, it has the potential to create a whole new clean power industry to spur our economy into the 21st century.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>This approach was subsequently taken up by the Green New Deal Group,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> which published its eponymous report on July 21, 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The concept was further popularized and put on a wider footing when the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">United Nations Environment Programme</a> (UNEP) began to promote it. On October 22, 2008 UNEP's Executive Director <a href="/wiki/Achim_Steiner" title="Achim Steiner">Achim Steiner</a> unveiled the <i>Global Green New Deal</i> initiative that aims to create jobs in "green" industries, thus boosting the world economy and curbing climate change at the same time.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_proposals">General proposals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_New_Deal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: General proposals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Broadly, the proposals for a Green New Deal echo the recommendations of UN-mandated organizations such as <a href="/wiki/ICLEI" title="ICLEI">ICLEI</a> or the <a href="/wiki/TEEB" class="mw-redirect" title="TEEB">TEEB</a>, of global NGOs, and of the <a href="/wiki/Basel_II" title="Basel II">Basel II</a> and related monetary accords, especially as these relate to reforms to measurement of fundamental ecosystem risk and financial liabilities. The <a href="/wiki/Reinsurance" title="Reinsurance">reinsurance</a> industry has also expressed support for the general principles of global carbon and emissions charges, for metrics of ecosystem destabilization risk, and for raising the price companies and individuals have to pay when using <a href="/wiki/Nature%27s_services" class="mw-redirect" title="Nature&#39;s services">nature's services</a> and <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a>.
</p><p>Several measures proposed as part of a Green New Deal have already been implemented in one or more G8 or G20 countries including Norway, South Korea, the UK, Germany, and the US. The financial proposals echo some of the programs already underway at the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">IMF</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bank_for_International_Settlements" title="Bank for International Settlements">BIS</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_Central_Bank" title="European Central Bank">ECB</a> that aim to better reflect the value of ecosystems and reduce systematic incentives to invest in "dirty" or destructive industries.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Actual_measures">Actual measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_New_Deal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Actual measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Government-led investment in energy and <a href="/wiki/Resource_efficiency" title="Resource efficiency">resource efficiency</a>, as well as reusable energies and <a href="/wiki/Microgeneration" title="Microgeneration">microgeneration</a>;</li>
<li>Low-carbon infrastructure redevelopment in order to create jobs;</li>
<li>A directed tax on the profits of oil and gas companies with proceeds being invested in renewable energy and energy efficiency;</li>
<li>Financial incentives for <a href="/wiki/Eco-investing" title="Eco-investing">green investment</a> and reduced energy usage, including low interest rates for green investment;</li>
<li>Re-regulation of international finance, including capital controls, and increased scrutiny of <a href="/wiki/Financial_derivative" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial derivative">financial derivatives</a> - likely along the lines of <a href="/wiki/Basel_II" title="Basel II">Basel II</a>;</li>
<li>Curbing corporate <a href="/wiki/Tax_evasion" title="Tax evasion">tax evasion</a> through compulsory financial reporting and by clamping down on <a href="/wiki/Tax_haven" title="Tax haven">tax havens</a>;</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Global_Marshall_Plan_Initiative" title="Global Marshall Plan Initiative">Global Marshall Plan Initiative</a> using "green quantitative easing" to create money to fund the "great transition" to a society free of fossil fuels and other measures that aim to preserve the biosphere.</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critique">Critique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_New_Deal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Critique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some developing countries have argued that a global Green New Deal may undermine national sovereignty regarding a country's control over its natural resources. Brazil, Mexico, and India have emphasized national sovereignty when discussing the Green New Deal, with India also expressing fears of a green "economic straitjacket". Bolivia, on its part, has argued that a Green New Deal may signal the "privatization and <a href="/wiki/Commodification_of_nature" title="Commodification of nature">commodification of nature</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> China, while voicing some support for a Green New Deal, also pointed out that it may result in "trade protectionism under the pretext of environmental protection".
</p>

The New Deal of that era entailed a strong government role in economic planning and a series of stimulus packages launched between 1933 and 1938 that created jobs through ambitious governmental programs, including the construction of roads, trails, dams, and schools. Today's Green New Deal proposals are also premised on the importance of decisive governmental action, but incorporate policies to respond to pressing environmental challenges through a new paradigm of sustainable economic progress."</span>
