<p><b>Robyn Eckersley</b> (born 1958) is a Professor and Head of Political Science in the School of Social and Political Sciences, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Melbourne" title="University of Melbourne">University of Melbourne</a>, Australia.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robyn_Eckersley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eckersley grew up in Perth and graduated in law from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Western_Australia" title="University of Western Australia">University of Western Australia</a>. She studied at the University of Cambridge, and has a PhD in environmental politics from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tasmania" title="University of Tasmania">University of Tasmania</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  She was previously a public lawyer, then a lecturer at <a href="/wiki/Monash_University" title="Monash University">Monash University</a> until 2001 when she moved to the University of Melbourne.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Green_State"><i>The Green State</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robyn_Eckersley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Green State">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eckersley's arguments are largely conducted in the domain of <a href="/wiki/Political_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Political theory">political theory</a>, but have proven influential in <a href="/wiki/Environmental_politics" title="Environmental politics">environmental politics</a>. Her 1992 book <i>Environmentalism and Political Theory: Toward an Ecocentric Approach</i> was one of the first to argue for an <a href="/wiki/Ecocentrism" title="Ecocentrism">ecocentric</a> form of <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a>.
</p><p>In her 2004 book <i>The Green State: Rethinking Democracy and Sovereignty</i>,  Eckersley proposes "critical political ecology" as a paradigm to explore what it might take to create a <b>green state</b> or <b>green democratic state</b>, a government where the <a href="/wiki/Regulation" title="Regulation">regulatory</a> ideals and <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democratic</a> procedures of the democratic <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a> are informed by <a href="/wiki/Ecological_democracy" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological democracy">ecological democracy</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign state</a> is recast in the role of <a href="/wiki/Ecological_stewardship" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological stewardship">ecological steward</a> and facilitator of <a href="/wiki/Transnationalism" title="Transnationalism">transnational</a> democracy. The green democratic state is proposed as an <a href="/wiki/Reformism" title="Reformism">evolutionary</a> alternative to the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_democracy" title="Liberal democracy">liberal democratic</a> state, the <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Neoliberalism" title="Neoliberalism">neoliberal</a> state.<sup id="cite_ref-Eckersley_2004_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eckersley_2004-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
