<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chartreuse_(color)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> word <i>chartreuse</i> means "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/charterhouse" class="extiw" title="wikt:charterhouse">charterhouse</a>".  The <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monasteries</a> that the monks of the <a href="/wiki/Carthusian" class="mw-redirect" title="Carthusian">Carthusian</a> order (who started producing <a href="/wiki/Chartreuse_liqueur" class="mw-redirect" title="Chartreuse liqueur">Chartreuse liqueur</a> in 1764) live in, of which the first one was established in 1082 by <a href="/wiki/Bruno_of_Cologne" title="Bruno of Cologne">Saint Bruno</a>, are called charter houses because they were chartered—and given generous material support—by the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Burgundy" title="Duke of Burgundy">Duke of Burgundy</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Philip_the_Bold" title="Philip the Bold">Philip the Bold</a> when he took over the area in 1378.  Philip the Bold's elaborately decorated tomb was initially installed at a Carthusian charterhouse when he died in 1404.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>At right is displayed the web color <b>chartreuse</b>.
</p><p>The term <i>chartreuse</i> is defined by the Oxford English Dictionary as: "A shade of colour; a pale apple-green". The dictionary gives a quotation in the British publication <i><a href="/wiki/Western_Daily_Press" title="Western Daily Press">Western Daily Press</a></i> (26 Dec. 1884) Vol. 7 No. 5 as being the earliest occurrence found in print of the term 'chartreuse' used as the name of a color. However the source does not define or describe the color referred to.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <i>The Domestic Monthly</i> (1885) is written, "The delicate, pale green, with a yellow tinge, entitled 'Chartreuse,' is a rival to the renewed apple green," and, "The new shade of Chartreuse green, from light to dark, is lovely in the large feather fans. ... Some of the corded silks have fancy stripes in a combination of colors such as ... mousse and Chartreuse, which is the stylish yellow green."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <i>The Ladies' Home Journal</i> of May 1889, is written, "Chantilly cloaks come shaped like the old-fashioned rotonde, with collar of narrow lace, and are worn over a lining of chartreuse green or jonquil yellow."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <i>The Millinery Trade Review</i> (1889) is written, "From Madame Catlin of Paris, a hat of velvet in moss-green of medium tone, or of strong Chartreuse-green."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <i>The Mineral Industry</i> (1898) is written, "The characteristic twin colors of a few doubly refractive gems will prove of interest ... tourmaline green (chartreuse green and bluish green).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <i>Dry Goods Reporter</i> (1905), it is noted under "Choosing an Easter Hat"&#160;— "Chartreuse greens are among the colors hardest of all to combine artistically, and yet with the new popular bluet are charming."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <i>Pure Products</i> (1910) is written, "The following colors can be bought in powder form ... chartreuse green".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>"Chartreuse Green" is also listed in <i>Plochere Color System</i> (1948).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In a 1956 edition of <a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)"><i>Billboard</i></a>, a <a href="/wiki/Juke_box" class="mw-redirect" title="Juke box">juke box</a> is advertised as being available in "Delft blue, cherry red, embered charcoal, chartreuse green, bright sand, canary yellow, atoll coral and night-sky black."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <i>Color: Universal Language and Dictionary of Names</i> (1976), "Chartreuse Green"  is listed under "116. Brilliant Yellow Green".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chartreuse green was codified to refer to this brighter color when the <a href="/wiki/X11_color_names" title="X11 color names">X11</a> colors were formulated in 1987; by the early 1990s, they became known as the <a href="/wiki/Web_color" class="mw-redirect" title="Web color">X11 web colors</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Web_color" class="mw-redirect" title="Web color">web color</a> <i>chartreuse</i> is the color precisely halfway between green and yellow, so it is 50% green and 50% yellow. It is one of the tertiary colors of the <a href="/wiki/HSV_color_space" class="mw-redirect" title="HSV color space">HSV color wheel</a>, also known as the <a href="/wiki/File:RBG_color_wheel.svg" title="File:RBG color wheel.svg">RGB color wheel</a>. Another name for this color is <b>chartreuse green</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1988, Margaret Walch, director of the <a href="/wiki/Color_Association_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Color Association of the United States">Color Association of the United States</a> is reported to have said, "The hottest color out there now is an ugly chartreuse green.... It suggests what we don't have: nature, youth, energy, growth."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The color name <b>chartreuse yellow</b>, also known as <b>chartreuse (traditional)</b> or <b>traditional chartreuse</b>, refers to the much more yellowish tone of chartreuse than the web color "chartreuse" that was generally regarded as "chartreuse" in the United States (and still is regarded as such by many in that country) before the X11 color <i>chartreuse</i> (shown above) was formulated in 1987.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The first recorded use of <i>chartreuse</i> (meaning the color that is now called <i>chartreuse yellow</i>) in American English was in 1892.<sup id="cite_ref-mp_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mp-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the book <i>Color Standards and Color Nomenclature</i> (1912), "Chartreuse Yellow" is listed and illustrated.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p><b>Lemon Lime</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence" title="Fluorescence">fluorescent</a> chartreuse color that refers to a theoretical combination of the two colors of the lemon and lime fruit rinds.  Such a name might have been popularized by the advertising of various carbonated soft drinks such as <a href="/wiki/Sprite_(soft_drink)" title="Sprite (soft drink)">Sprite</a>, <a href="/wiki/7_Up" title="7 Up">7 Up</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Mist" title="Sierra Mist">Sierra Mist</a>, although all three of these drinks actually appear clear and colorless.
</p>

<p>Displayed at right is the color <b>apple green</b>, a representation of the color of the outer skin of a <a href="/wiki/Granny_Smith_apple" class="mw-redirect" title="Granny Smith apple">Granny Smith apple</a>. A darker version of this color has been used for the <a href="/wiki/IRT_Lexington_Avenue_Line" title="IRT Lexington Avenue Line">IRT Lexington Avenue Line</a> since June 1979, when the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Transit_Authority" title="New York City Transit Authority">NYCTA</a> decided to assign line colors to all the routes within the major trunk lines in the <a href="/wiki/Central_Business_District" class="mw-redirect" title="Central Business District">Central Business District</a>, plus different colors for services not entering <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>. By doing this, they scrapped the 1967 colors that were assigned separately to each service.
</p><p>The first recorded use of <i>apple green</i> as a color name in English was in 1648.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>

<p><b>Avocado</b> is a color that is a representation of the color of the outer surface of an <a href="/wiki/Avocado" title="Avocado">avocado</a>.
</p><p>The color "avocado" is a dark yellow-green color.
</p><p>Avocado was a common color for metal surfaces (including <a href="/wiki/Automobiles" class="mw-redirect" title="Automobiles">automobiles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Household_appliances" class="mw-redirect" title="Household appliances">household appliances</a>), as well as the color <a href="/wiki/Gold_(color)#Harvest_gold" title="Gold (color)">harvest gold</a>, during the whole decade of the 1970s.  They were both also popular colors for <a href="/wiki/Shag_carpet" class="mw-redirect" title="Shag carpet">shag carpets</a>.  Both colors (as well as shag carpets) went out of style by the early 1980s.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chartreuse_in_nature">Chartreuse in nature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chartreuse_(color)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Chartreuse in nature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Algae">Algae</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chartreuse_(color)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Algae">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Yellow-green_algae" title="Yellow-green algae">Yellow-green algae</a>, also called <i>xanthophytes</i>, are a class of <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Heterokontophyta" class="mw-redirect" title="Heterokontophyta">Heterokontophyta</a> <a href="/wiki/Division_(botany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Division (botany)">division</a>. Most live in freshwater, but some are found in marine and soil habitats. They vary from <a href="/wiki/Single-celled_organism" class="mw-redirect" title="Single-celled organism">single-celled</a> <a href="/wiki/Flagellate" title="Flagellate">flagellates</a> to simple <a href="/wiki/Colony_(biology)" title="Colony (biology)">colonial</a> and filamentous forms. Unlike other heterokonts, the <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplasts</a> of yellow-green algae do not contain <a href="/wiki/Fucoxanthin" title="Fucoxanthin">fucoxanthin</a>, which is why they have a lighter color.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chartreuse_(color)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traffic_safety">Traffic safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chartreuse_(color)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Traffic safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Chartreuse Yellow is used on traffic safety vests to provide increased visibility for employees working near traffic.  The <a href="/wiki/Chartreuse_yellow" class="mw-redirect" title="Chartreuse yellow">Chartreuse Yellow</a> background material, together with a retro-reflective satisfy the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://multimedia.3m.com/mws/media/646966O/ansi-made-easy.pdf">ANSI 107-2010 standard</a> since 1999.  <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.hi-vis.net">Traffic Safety vest</a> ANSI Standards were adopted as an <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.osha.gov">OSHA (Occupational Safety Health Administration)</a> requirement in 2008 (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.gpo.gov/fdsys/granule/CFR-2009-title23-vol1/CFR-2009-title23-vol1-sec634-2">23 CFR part 634</a>)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronics">Electronics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chartreuse_(color)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Electronics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>The early green LEDs are now sometimes called chartreuse to distinguish them from more modern, deeper-green LEDs.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chartreuse_(color)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>The 1960 Universal film <i>Chartroose Caboose</i> featured a "bright green" colored train car.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Firefighting">Firefighting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chartreuse_(color)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Firefighting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ACTFB_tankers.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/ACTFB_tankers.jpg/220px-ACTFB_tankers.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/ACTFB_tankers.jpg/330px-ACTFB_tankers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/ACTFB_tankers.jpg/440px-ACTFB_tankers.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ACTFB_tankers.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/ACT_Fire_and_Rescue" title="ACT Fire and Rescue">ACT Fire and Rescue</a> tankers in Chartreuse green</div></div></div>
<ul><li>Since about 1973, a sort of <a href="/wiki/Fluorescent" class="mw-redirect" title="Fluorescent">fluorescent</a> chartreuse green has been adopted as the color of <a href="/wiki/Fire_apparatus" class="mw-redirect" title="Fire apparatus">fire engines</a> in parts of the United States and elsewhere. The use of chartreuse fire engines began when New York <a href="/wiki/Ophthalmologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Ophthalmologist">ophthalmologist</a> Stephen Solomon produced research claiming that sparkling bright lime-green paint would boost the night time visibility of emergency vehicles compared to those painted the traditional <a href="/wiki/Fire_engine_red" title="Fire engine red">fire engine red</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The reason for this is the <a href="/wiki/Purkinje_Effect" class="mw-redirect" title="Purkinje Effect">Purkinje Effect</a>, i.e., the <a href="/wiki/Cone_cell" title="Cone cell">cones</a> do not function as efficiently in dim light, so red objects appear to be black. In Australia and New Zealand this form of chartreuse yellow is also known as "ACT Yellow" as this is the color of the fire engines in the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Capital_Territory" title="Australian Capital Territory">Australian Capital Territory</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chartreuse_(color)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>In 2012, <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_Handler" title="Chelsea Handler">Chelsea Handler</a> began to author a spoof on the popular book series <i><a href="/wiki/50_Shades_of_Grey" class="mw-redirect" title="50 Shades of Grey">50 Shades of Grey</a></i> called <i>50 Shades of Chartreuse: This Time It's Personal</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chartreuse_(color)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Jazz/Jump Blues saxophonist, singer, and bandleader <a href="/wiki/Louis_Jordan" title="Louis Jordan">Louis Jordan</a> recorded the song "(You Dyed Your Hair) Chartreuse" in 1950. Composed by <a href="/wiki/John_Leslie_McFarland" title="John Leslie McFarland">J. Leslie McFarland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Billy_Moore_(musician)" title="Billy Moore (musician)">Billy Moore, Jr.</a>, the song is about a girl who dyes her hair "chartreuse." Although she thinks that the color is "mighty cute," the speaker suggests that the change of color is a ridiculous act of rebellion.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>A song on the <a href="/wiki/Homestuck" title="Homestuck">Homestuck</a> album 'The Felt' is named Chartreuse Rewind, in reference to the color associated with the album, its subject matter, and by extension the traditional color of the felt on a <a href="/wiki/Billiards" class="mw-redirect" title="Billiards">billiards</a> table.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>American blues-rock band <a href="/wiki/ZZ_Top" title="ZZ Top">ZZ Top</a> recorded a song called "Chartreuse" for their 2012 album <a href="/wiki/La_Futura" title="La Futura">La Futura</a>.</li>
<li>American indie-pop duo <a href="/wiki/Capital_Cities_(band)" title="Capital Cities (band)">Capital Cities</a> recorded a song called "Chartreuse" for their 2013 album <a href="/wiki/In_a_Tidal_Wave_of_Mystery" title="In a Tidal Wave of Mystery">In a Tidal Wave of Mystery</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/CW_McCall" class="mw-redirect" title="CW McCall">CW McCall</a>'s song <a href="/wiki/Convoy_(song)" title="Convoy (song)">Convoy</a>, one of the vehicles participating in the convoy is a chartreuse <a href="/wiki/VW_Microbus" class="mw-redirect" title="VW Microbus">VW Microbus</a>, which is assigned by the singer/speaker as an escort to a truck hauling <a href="/wiki/Dynamite" title="Dynamite">dynamite</a> (due to the microbus carrying "eleven long-haired friends of Jesus" and the dynamite truck needing "all the help it can get").</li>
<li>"Chartreuse" is a song on <a href="/wiki/Ken_Nordine" title="Ken Nordine">Ken Nordine</a>'s spoken word album <a href="/wiki/Colors_(Ken_Nordine_album)" title="Colors (Ken Nordine album)">"Colors"</a> (1967).</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vexillology">Vexillology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chartreuse_(color)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Vexillology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
