<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Punjab_Monsoon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Punjab_Monsoon.jpg/250px-Punjab_Monsoon.jpg" width="250" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Punjab_Monsoon.jpg/375px-Punjab_Monsoon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Punjab_Monsoon.jpg/500px-Punjab_Monsoon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="685" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Punjab_Monsoon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The state of <a href="/wiki/Punjab_(India)" class="mw-redirect" title="Punjab (India)">Punjab</a> led India's <a href="/wiki/Green_Revolution" title="Green Revolution">Green Revolution</a> and earned the distinction of being the country's bread basket.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Green Revolution in India</b> refers to a period of time when <a href="/wiki/Agriculture_in_India" title="Agriculture in India">agriculture in India</a> changed to an industrial system due to the adoption of modern methods and technology such as <a href="/wiki/High-yielding_variety" title="High-yielding variety">high yielding variety (HYV) seeds</a>, tractors, irrigation facilities, pesticides, fertilizers etc. This was part of the larger <a href="/wiki/Green_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Green revolution">Green revolution</a> started by <a href="/wiki/Norman_Borlaug" title="Norman Borlaug">Norman Borlaug</a>, which leveraged agricultural research and technology to increase agricultural productivity in the developing world. Within <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, this started in the early 1960s and led to an increase in food grain production, especially in <a href="/wiki/Punjab,_India" title="Punjab, India">Punjab</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haryana" title="Haryana">Haryana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a> during the early phase. The main development was higher-yielding varieties of <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-about_IARI_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about_IARI-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Rust_(fungus)" title="Rust (fungus)">rust</a> resistant strains of wheat.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practices">Practices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Practices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wheat">Wheat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wheat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main development was higher-yielding varieties of <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-about_IARI_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about_IARI-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> for developing <a href="/wiki/Rust_(fungus)" title="Rust (fungus)">rust</a> resistant strains of wheat.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The introduction of high-yielding varieties(HYV) of seeds and the increased quality of <a href="/wiki/Fertilizers" class="mw-redirect" title="Fertilizers">fertilizers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> technique led to the increase in production to make the country self-sufficient in food grains, thus improving <a href="/wiki/Agriculture_in_India" title="Agriculture in India">agriculture in India</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The methods adopted included the use of <a href="/wiki/High-yielding_varieties" class="mw-redirect" title="High-yielding varieties">high-yielding varieties</a> (HYVs) of seeds<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> with modern farming methods.
</p><p>The production of wheat has produced the best results in fueling self-sufficiency of India. Along with high-yielding seeds and irrigation facilities, the enthusiasm of farmers mobilized the idea of agricultural revolution. Due to the rise in use of chemical pesticides and fertilizers, there was a negative effect on the soil and the land (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Land_degradation" title="Land degradation">land degradation</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems_that_were_addressed">Problems that were addressed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Problems that were addressed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Frequent_famines">Frequent famines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Frequent famines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Famine" title="Famine">Famines</a> in India were very frequent during the period 1940s to 1970s. Due to faulty distribution of food, and because farmers did not receive the true value for their labour, the majority of the population did not get enough food.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">Malnutrition</a> and <a href="/wiki/Starvation" title="Starvation">starvation</a> was a huge problem.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_finance">Lack of finance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lack of finance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Marginal farmers found it very difficult to get <a href="/wiki/Financing" class="mw-redirect" title="Financing">finance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Credit" title="Credit">credit</a> at economical rates from the government and banks and hence, fell as easy prey to the <a href="/wiki/Moneylender" title="Moneylender">money lenders</a>. They took loans from <a href="/wiki/Zamindars" class="mw-redirect" title="Zamindars">zamindars</a>, who charged high rates of <a href="/wiki/Interest" title="Interest">interests</a> and also exploited the farmers later on to work in their fields to repay the loans (<a href="/wiki/Farm_labourer" class="mw-redirect" title="Farm labourer">farm labourers</a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Proper financing was not given during the Green Revolution period, which created a lot of problems and sufferings to the farmers of India. Government also helped those under loans.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_self-sufficiency">Lack of self-sufficiency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lack of self-sufficiency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to traditional agricultural practices, low productivity, and a growing population, often <a href="/wiki/Food_grains" class="mw-redirect" title="Food grains">food grains</a> were imported — draining scarce foreign reserves. It was thought that with the increased production due to the Green Revolution, the government could maintain buffer stock and India could achieve self-sufficiency and self-reliability.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Agriculture was basically for subsistence and, therefore, less agricultural product was offered for sale in the market. Hence, the need was felt to encourage the farmers to increase their production and offer a greater portion of their products for sale in the market. The new methods in agriculture increased the yield of rice and wheat, which reduced India's dependence on food imports.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_Economic_Sovereignty">Indian Economic Sovereignty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Indian Economic Sovereignty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Criticism of the effects of the green revolution include the cost for many small farmers using HYV seeds, with their associated demands of increased irrigation systems and pesticides. A case study is found in India, where farmers are buying <a href="/wiki/Monsanto" title="Monsanto">Monsanto</a> BT cotton seeds—sold on the idea that these seeds produced 'natural insecticides'. In reality, they need to still pay for expensive pesticides and irrigation systems, which might lead to increased borrowing to finance the change from traditional seed varieties. Many farmers have difficulty in paying for the expensive technologies, especially if they have a bad harvest.
</p><p>Indian environmentalist <a href="/wiki/Vandana_Shiva" title="Vandana Shiva">Vandana Shiva</a> writes that this is the "second Green Revolution". The first Green Revolution, she suggests, was mostly publicly funded (by the Indian Government). This new Green Revolution, she says, is driven by private (and foreign) interest – notably MNCs like Monsanto. Ultimately, this is leading to foreign ownership over most of India's farmland.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Environmental_Damage.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Environmental_Damage&quot;">"Environmental Damage"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: &quot;Environmental Damage&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Excessive and inappropriate use of fertilizers and pesticides has polluted waterway, killed beneficial insects and wild life. It has caused over-use of soil and rapidly depleted its nutrients. The rampant irrigation practices have led to eventually soil degradation. Groundwater practices have fallen dramatically. Further, heavy dependence on few major crops has led to <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_loss" title="Biodiversity loss">loss of biodiversity</a> of farmers. These problems were aggravated due to absence of training to use modern technology and vast illiteracy leading to excessive use of chemicals.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="https://sol.du.ac.in/mod/book/view.php?id=1735&amp;chapterid=1695">[1]</a>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Increased_Regional_disparities">Increased Regional disparities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Revolution_in_India&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Increased Regional disparities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Green revolution spread only in irrigated and high-potential rain fed areas. The villages or regions without the access of sufficient water were left out that widened the regional disparities between adopters and non-adopters. Since, the HYV seeds technically can be applied only in land with assured water supply and availability of other inputs like chemicals, fertilizers etc. The application of the new technology in the dry-land areas is simply ruled out.
</p><p>The states like Punjab, Haryana, Western UP etc. having good irrigation and other infrastructure facilities were able to derive the benefits of green revolution and achieve faster economic development while other states have recorded slow growth in agriculture production.
</p>
