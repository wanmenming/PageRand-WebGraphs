<p>The <b>Green Party</b> (<a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <i lang="sv">Miljöpartiet de gröna</i>, literally "Environment Party the Greens", commonly referred to in Swedish as "Miljöpartiet" or <b>MP</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_Sweden" title="List of political parties in Sweden">political party</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> based on <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green politics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Swedish_Green_Party_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Swedish_Green_Party-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The party was founded in 1981, emerging out of a sense of discontent with the existing parties' environmental policies, and sparked by the anti-<a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> movement following the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_nuclear_power_referendum,_1980" title="Swedish nuclear power referendum, 1980">1980 nuclear power referendum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ljunggren_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ljunggren-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The party's breakthrough would come in the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_general_election,_1988" title="Swedish general election, 1988">1988 general election</a> when they won seats in the <a href="/wiki/Riksdag_(Sweden)" class="mw-redirect" title="Riksdag (Sweden)">Swedish Riksdag</a> for the first time, capturing 5.5 percent of the vote, and becoming the first new party to enter parliament in seventy years.<sup id="cite_ref-1988_riksdag_election_results_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1988_riksdag_election_results-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Three years later, they dropped back below the 4 percent <a href="/wiki/Election_threshold" title="Election threshold">threshold</a>, but returned to parliament again in 1994, and since have retained representation there. The party is represented nationally by two spokespeople, always one man and one woman.  These roles are currently held by <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Fridolin" title="Gustav Fridolin">Gustav Fridolin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isabella_L%C3%B6vin" title="Isabella Lövin">Isabella Lövin</a>.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_general_election,_2014" title="Swedish general election, 2014">2014 general election</a>, the Greens received 6.9% of the vote and 25 seats, making the party the fourth largest in the <a href="/wiki/Riksdag" title="Riksdag">Riksdag</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2014_results_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2014_results-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since 3 October 2014, the Green Party is the minor partner to the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Social_Democratic_Party" title="Swedish Social Democratic Party">Swedish Social Democratic Party</a> in the <a href="/wiki/L%C3%B6fven_Cabinet" title="Löfven Cabinet">Löfven Cabinet</a> minority coalition government, the first time in its history that the Greens have entered government.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideology">Ideology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Sweden)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ideology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fundamental_principles">Fundamental principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Sweden)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fundamental principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In their party platform, the Greens describe their ideology as being based on "a solidarity that can be expressed in three ways: solidarity with animals, nature, and the ecological system", "solidarity with coming generations", and "solidarity with all of the world's people".  The platform then describes these solidarities being expressed in "several fundamental ideas", these being <a href="/wiki/Participatory_democracy" title="Participatory democracy">participatory democracy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecological_wisdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological wisdom">ecological wisdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_rights" title="Children&#39;s rights">children's rights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Circular_economy" title="Circular economy">circular economy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Global_justice" title="Global justice">global justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nonviolence" title="Nonviolence">nonviolence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_equality" title="Social equality">equality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animal_rights" title="Animal rights">animal rights</a>, self-reliance and self-administration, <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">freedom</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">long-sightedness</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-party_platform_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-party_platform-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change_and_the_environment">Climate change and the environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Sweden)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate change and the environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Green Party was the first political party in Sweden to raise the issue of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  Fighting climate change is a major policy issue for the party.  For example, the party's main criticism of <a href="/wiki/Alliance_(Sweden)" title="Alliance (Sweden)">The Alliance</a>'s 2010 election manifesto was the "entirely astonishing" lack of effort in fighting climate change,<sup id="cite_ref-Fullständigt_häpnadsväckande_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fullständigt_häpnadsväckande-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and in 2013, the party announced a budget proposal that was dominated by a 49 million <a href="/wiki/Swedish_krona" title="Swedish krona">kronor</a> "climate package".<sup id="cite_ref-2013_budget_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2013_budget-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The party supports a general shift in taxation policy, towards high taxes on environmentally unfriendly or unsustainable products and activities, hoping to thus influence people's behavior towards the more sustainable.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_power">Nuclear power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Sweden)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nuclear power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The anti-nuclear movement was a major factor in the party's creation.<sup id="cite_ref-Ljunggren_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ljunggren-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The party's party platform reads that "we oppose the construction of new reactors in Sweden, or an increase in the output of existing reactors, and instead want to begin immediately phasing out nuclear power."<sup id="cite_ref-party_platform_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-party_platform-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Member_of_parliament" title="Member of parliament">MP</a> <a href="/wiki/Per_Bolund" title="Per Bolund">Per Bolund</a> clarified in 2010 that the party "does not propose shutting down nuclear power reactors today, but rather phasing them out as new and renewable electricity is phased in."<sup id="cite_ref-bolund_chat_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bolund_chat-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_integration">European integration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Sweden)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: European integration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The party was initially opposed to membership in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, and sought a new referendum on the issue.  The party's EU-opposition captured them 17 percent of the votes in the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_1995_(Sweden)" title="European Parliament election, 1995 (Sweden)">1995 European Parliament election</a>, the first following Sweden's EU ascension.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  The Greens included withdrawal from the EU in their party platform as recently as 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-2006_manifesto_dn_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2006_manifesto_dn-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This policy was abolished in a September 2008 internal party referendum.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> However, the party remains somewhat Eurosceptic. The section of the party platform on the subject opens by citing how decentralization and making decisions as locally as reasonably possible is a central part of green politics.  It continues to state that the Greens "are warm adherents to international cooperation.  We want to see Europe as a part of a world of democracies, where people move freely over borders, and where people and countries trade and cooperate with each other."<sup id="cite_ref-party_platform_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-party_platform-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leadership_and_organisation">Leadership and organisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Sweden)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Leadership and organisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Greens, like many other green parties around the world, do not have a party leader in the traditional sense.  The party is represented by two spokespeople, always one male and one female. The current spokespersons are <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Fridolin" title="Gustav Fridolin">Gustav Fridolin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isabella_Lovin" class="mw-redirect" title="Isabella Lovin">Isabella Lovin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dn_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dn-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  The spokespeople are elected annually by the party congress, up to a maximum of nine, one-year terms.<sup id="cite_ref-constitution_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-constitution-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The party congress, consisting of elected representatives of all of the party's local groups, is the highest decision-making organ in the Green Party.  The congress, in addition to the two spokespeople, also fills many other important posts in the party, including a party board (<a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <i lang="sv">partistyrelse</i>), which is the party's highest decision-making authority between party congresses, and the day-to-day operation of the party's national organisation.  The congress also elects a party secretary (<a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <i lang="sv">partisekreterare</i>), who is an internal, organisational leader for the party.<sup id="cite_ref-constitution_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-constitution-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The current party secretary, initially elected by the 2011 party congress, is <a href="/wiki/Anders_Wallner" title="Anders Wallner">Anders Wallner</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Sweden)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_extremism">Islamic extremism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Sweden)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Islamic extremism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Green Party was hit by a political scandal in April 2016, as images emerged of Green Party housing minister <a href="/wiki/Mehmet_Kaplan" title="Mehmet Kaplan">Mehmet Kaplan</a> attending a dinner party alongside leading members of the Turkish far-right extremist group <a href="/wiki/Grey_Wolves_(organization)" title="Grey Wolves (organization)">Grey Wolves</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AlJazeera_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AlJazeera-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SvD_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SvD-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Following attention to comments made by Kaplan in 2009 comparing <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> to <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>, Kaplan resigned as minister, while still defended by the party leadership.<sup id="cite_ref-AlJazeera_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AlJazeera-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TheLocal_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheLocal-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> After his resignation, images emerged of Kaplan and other members of the Green Party displaying hand gestures associated with the <a href="/wiki/Muslim_Brotherhood" title="Muslim Brotherhood">Muslim Brotherhood</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AlJazeera_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AlJazeera-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TheLocal_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheLocal-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Another controversy ensued as a rising Green-Party star, <a href="/wiki/Yasri_Khan" title="Yasri Khan">Yasri Khan</a>, refused to shake hands with a female TV reporter.<sup id="cite_ref-SvD_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SvD-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TheLocal_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheLocal-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Lars Nicander, director of the Centre for Asymmetric Threat Studies at the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Defence_University" title="Swedish Defence University">Swedish Defence University</a>, compared the revelations with how the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> sought to infiltrate democratic Western parties during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, alleging that the Green Party similarly may have been "infiltrated by Islamists".<sup id="cite_ref-AlJazeera_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AlJazeera-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2016, a Green Party politician from the council of <a href="/wiki/Burl%C3%B6v_Municipality" title="Burlöv Municipality">Burlöv Municipality</a> and the chairman of the local Syrian commity group invited, in his latter capacity, <a href="/wiki/Osama_bin_Ladin" class="mw-redirect" title="Osama bin Ladin">Osama bin Ladin</a>'s former advisor <a href="/wiki/Salman_al-Ouda" title="Salman al-Ouda">Salman al-Ouda</a> to lecture for them together with two other community groups. This invitation was controversial in Sweden as <a href="/wiki/Salafist" class="mw-redirect" title="Salafist">salafist</a> al-Ouda had stated antisemitic views and denied the Holocaust. He was suspended for a time by the party leadership.<sup id="cite_ref-sydsv28april2016_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sydsv28april2016-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-svt27apr2016_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-svt27apr2016-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2016 Green Party co-spokesperson and Environmental Minister <a href="/wiki/%C3%85sa_Romson" title="Åsa Romson">Åsa Romson</a> confirmed she would resign from both positions as a result of her leadership during the party crisis, along with controversies of her own, such as referring to the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a> as the September 11 "olycka" (which can be translated as "accident", or alternatively as "misfortune" which Romson later claimed as her intention) in a television interview.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electoral_politics">Electoral politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Sweden)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Electoral politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is often believed that the party is situated on the left on a left-right scale due to its co-operation with the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Social_Democratic_Party" title="Swedish Social Democratic Party">Social Democratic Party</a>. The party participated in a <a href="/wiki/Political_coalition" class="mw-redirect" title="Political coalition">political and electoral coalition</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Red-Greens_(Sweden)" title="Red-Greens (Sweden)">Red-Greens</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Social_Democratic_Party" title="Swedish Social Democratic Party">Social Democrats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Left_Party_(Sweden)" title="Left Party (Sweden)">Left Party</a> from October 2008 until the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_general_election,_2010" title="Swedish general election, 2010">2010 general election</a> in September 2010, and has vowed to co-operate with the Social Democrats until 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-aftonbladet_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aftonbladet-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> In several municipalities, however, the Greens cooperate with liberal and conservative parties, and the party does not define itself as left, nor right. Rather, they place themselves on one end of a scale between sustainability and growth. In an article published in 2009, Maria Wetterstrand, then party co-spokesperson, defined the party as a natural home also for green-minded <a href="/wiki/Social_liberalism" title="Social liberalism">social liberals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Libertarian_Socialism" class="mw-redirect" title="Libertarian Socialism">libertarian socialists</a>, by referring to its liberal policy regarding immigration and its support of personal integrity, participation and entrepreneurship, among other issues.<sup id="cite_ref-newsmill_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newsmill-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of  2006<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Sweden)&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, the party is in opposition in Sweden, and its prioritized issues are <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anti-discrimination" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-discrimination">anti-discrimination</a> and <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil rights">equal rights</a>.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2009_(Sweden)" title="European Parliament election, 2009 (Sweden)">2009 European Parliament election</a>, the Greens received 11.02% and elected 2 <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">MEPs</a>.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_general_election,_2010" title="Swedish general election, 2010">2010 general election</a>, the Greens received 7.3% of the vote, winning 25 seats in the <a href="/wiki/Riksdag" title="Riksdag">Riksdag</a>.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2014_(Sweden)" title="European Parliament election, 2014 (Sweden)">2014 European elections</a>, the Greens came in second place nationally, ahead of the ruling <a href="/wiki/Moderate_Party" title="Moderate Party">Moderate Party</a> and behind the Social Democrats, with 15.4% of the vote, returning 4 MEPs.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_general_election,_2014" title="Swedish general election, 2014">2014 general election</a>, the Greens received 6.9%  of the vote, again winning 25 seats in the Riksdag.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Church_politics">Church politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Sweden)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Church politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The party does not directly participate in elections to the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Sweden" title="Church of Sweden">Church of Sweden</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_in_the_Church_of_Sweden" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Party in the Church of Sweden">Miljöpartister i Svenska kyrkan</a> (English: <span lang="en">Greens in the Church of Sweden</span>), an independent <a href="/wiki/Nominating_groups" class="mw-redirect" title="Nominating groups">nominating group</a>, participates in church elections at all levels.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_other_parties">Relationship with other parties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_(Sweden)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Relationship with other parties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Green Party has a good relationship with the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Social_Democratic_Party" title="Swedish Social Democratic Party">Social Democrats</a>, and to a lesser extent, with the <a href="/wiki/Left_Party_(Sweden)" title="Left Party (Sweden)">Left Party</a>. The party does not rule out participation in a government with the minor <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">liberal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Centre-right" class="mw-redirect" title="Centre-right">centre-right</a> parties in Sweden. The Green Party on first entering the Riksdag, allied with the Conservative Bloc in opposition to the Social Democrats. The Green Party has made clear that its preference among cooperative arrangements with the Conservative Bloc does not include support of a government led by the liberal-conservative <a href="/wiki/Moderate_Party" title="Moderate Party">Moderate Party</a>. Historically, however, there have been political deals concluded with the parties forming the centre-right <a href="/wiki/Alliance_(Sweden)" title="Alliance (Sweden)">Alliance</a>, as an example concerning education. Co-operation with the Moderate Party on the municipal level are relatively frequent.
</p>
