<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Green belt (United Kingdom)</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Green_belt_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Green belt (disambiguation)">Green belt (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Metropolitan_Green_Belt_among_the_green_belts_of_England.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/The_Metropolitan_Green_Belt_among_the_green_belts_of_England.svg/220px-The_Metropolitan_Green_Belt_among_the_green_belts_of_England.svg.png" width="220" height="254" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/The_Metropolitan_Green_Belt_among_the_green_belts_of_England.svg/330px-The_Metropolitan_Green_Belt_among_the_green_belts_of_England.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/The_Metropolitan_Green_Belt_among_the_green_belts_of_England.svg/440px-The_Metropolitan_Green_Belt_among_the_green_belts_of_England.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="420" data-file-height="485" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Metropolitan_Green_Belt_among_the_green_belts_of_England.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Designated areas of green belt in England; the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Green_Belt" title="Metropolitan Green Belt">Metropolitan Green Belt</a> outlined in red</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">town planning</a>, the <b>green belt</b> is a policy for controlling <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">urban growth</a>. The idea is for a ring of countryside where <a href="/wiki/Urbanisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Urbanisation">urbanisation</a> will be resisted for the foreseeable future, maintaining an area where agriculture, forestry and outdoor leisure can be expected to prevail. The fundamental aim of <a href="/wiki/Green_belt" title="Green belt">green belt</a> policy is to prevent <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">urban sprawl</a> by keeping land permanently open, and consequently the most important attribute of green belts is their <a href="/wiki/Open_space_reserve" title="Open space reserve">openness</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Green_Belt" title="Metropolitan Green Belt">Metropolitan Green Belt</a> around London was first proposed by the Greater London Regional Planning Committee in 1935. The <a href="/wiki/Town_and_Country_Planning_Act_1947" title="Town and Country Planning Act 1947">Town and Country Planning Act 1947</a> then allowed local authorities to include green belt proposals in their development plans. In 1955, Minister of Housing <a href="/wiki/Duncan_Sandys" title="Duncan Sandys">Duncan Sandys</a> encouraged local authorities around the country to consider protecting land around their towns and cities by the formal designation of clearly defined green belts.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Green belt policy has been criticised for reducing the amount of land available for building and therefore pushing up house prices, as 70% of the cost of building new houses is the purchase of the land (up from 25% in the late 1950s).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="England_and_Wales">England and Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: England and Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Government formerly set out its policies and principles towards green belts in England and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> in <a href="/wiki/PPG_2" class="mw-redirect" title="PPG 2">Planning Policy Guidance Note 2: Green Belts</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> but this planning guidance was superseded by the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.communities.gov.uk/publications/planningandbuilding/nppf">National Planning Policy Framework</a> (NPPF) in March 2012. Planning Authorities are strongly urged to follow the NPPF's detailed advice when considering whether to permit additional development in the green belt. In the green belt there is a general presumption against inappropriate development, unless very special circumstances can be demonstrated to show that the benefits of the development will outweigh the harm caused to the green belt. The NPPF sets out what would constitute appropriate development in the green belt.
</p><p>According to the NPPF, there are five stated purposes of including land within the green belt:
</p>
<ul><li>To check the unrestricted sprawl of large built-up areas</li>
<li>To prevent neighbouring towns from merging into one another</li>
<li>To assist in safeguarding the countryside from encroachment</li>
<li>To preserve the setting and special character of historic towns</li>
<li>To assist in urban regeneration, by encouraging the recycling of derelict and other urban land.</li></ul>
<p>Once an area of land has been defined as green belt, the stated opportunities and benefits include:
</p>
<ul><li>Providing opportunities for access to the open countryside for the urban population</li>
<li>Providing opportunities for outdoor sport and outdoor recreation near urban areas</li>
<li>The retention of attractive landscapes and the enhancement of landscapes, near to where people live</li>
<li>Improvement of damaged and derelict land around towns</li>
<li>The securing of nature conservation interests</li>
<li>The retention of land in agricultural, forestry and related uses.</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland">Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg" class="image"><img alt="[icon]" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" width="20" height="14" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="44" data-file-height="31" /></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Green_belt_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small>  <small><i>(January 2009)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table><p>Northern Ireland has 30 green belt areas,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> accounting for approximately 226,600 hectares, about 16 percent of its total area.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Green belt policy in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> is set out in Scottish Planning Policy (SPP) 21, published by the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a> in February 2010. On 29 November, the Government published "Green Belt Policy in Scotland 10/85"
</p><p>As of 2010 Scotland had 10 green belt areas: <a href="/wiki/Aberdeen" title="Aberdeen">Aberdeen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ayr" title="Ayr">Ayr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clackmannanshire" title="Clackmannanshire">Clackmannanshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Lothian" title="East Lothian">East Lothian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Falkirk" title="Falkirk">Falkirk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grangemouth" title="Grangemouth">Grangemouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greater_Glasgow" title="Greater Glasgow">Greater Glasgow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Midlothian" title="Midlothian">Midlothian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stirling" title="Stirling">Stirling</a>. There are also plans for green belts around <a href="/wiki/Dunfermline" title="Dunfermline">Dunfermline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Scotland" title="Perth, Scotland">Perth</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Andrews" title="St Andrews">St Andrews</a>.
</p><p>The Scottish Government is clear that the purpose of green belt designation in the development plan as part of the settlement strategy for an area is to:
</p>
<blockquote>
<ul><li>direct planned growth to the most appropriate locations and support regeneration,</li>
<li>protect and enhance the quality, character, landscape setting and identity of towns and cities, and</li>
<li>protect and give access to open space within and around towns and cities<sup id="cite_ref-scotland_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scotland-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
</blockquote>
<p>However, the Scottish Government recognises that certain types of development might actually promote and support appropriate rural diversification:
</p>
<blockquote>
<ul><li>Development associated with agriculture, including the re-use of historic agricultural buildings,</li>
<li>Woodland and forestry, including community woodlands,</li>
<li>Horticulture, including market gardening and directly connected retailing,</li>
<li>Recreational uses that are compatible with an agricultural or natural setting, and</li>
<li>Essential infrastructure such as electronic communications infrastructure and electricity grid connections<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
</blockquote>
<p>The Government requires that locally established green belt plans: maintain the identity of a city by the clearly establishing physical boundaries and preventing coalescence; provide countryside for recreation of denizens; and maintain the landscape setting of the city in question. In its Planning Policy (129), the Scottish Government states that:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“All public bodies, including planning authorities, have a duty to further the conservation of biodiversity under the Nature Conservation (Scotland) Act 2004, and this should be reflected in development plans and development management decisions. Biodiversity is important because it provides natural services and products that we rely on, is an important element of sustainable development and makes an essential contribution to Scotland's economy and cultural heritage.”<sup id="cite_ref-scotland_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scotland-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term emerged from continental Europe where broad boulevards were increasingly used to separate new development from the centre of historic towns; most notably the <a href="/wiki/Ringstra%C3%9Fe" class="mw-redirect" title="Ringstraße">Ringstraße</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>. Various proposals were put forward from 1890 onwards but the first to garner widespread support was put forward by the <a href="/wiki/London_Society" title="London Society">London Society</a> in its "Development Plan of Greater London" 1919. Alongside the <a href="/wiki/Campaign_to_Protect_Rural_England" title="Campaign to Protect Rural England">CPRE</a> they lobbied for a continuous belt (of up to two miles wide) to prevent urban sprawl, beyond which new development could occur.
</p><p>Implementation of the notion dated from <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Morrison" title="Herbert Morrison">Herbert Morrison</a>'s 1934 leadership of the <a href="/wiki/London_County_Council" title="London County Council">London County Council</a>. It was first formally proposed by the Greater London Regional Planning Committee in 1935, "to provide a reserve supply of public open spaces and of recreational areas and to establish a green belt or girdle of open space". It was again included in an advisory Greater London Plan prepared by <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Abercrombie" title="Patrick Abercrombie">Patrick Abercrombie</a> in 1944 (which sought a belt of up to six miles wide). However, it was some 14 years before the elected local authorities responsible for the area around London had all defined the area on scaled maps with some precision (encouraged by <a href="/wiki/Duncan_Sandys" title="Duncan Sandys">Duncan Sandys</a> to designate a belt of some 7–10 miles wide).
</p><p>New provisions for compensation in the 1947 Town and Country Planning Act allowed local authorities around the country to incorporate green belt proposals in their first <a href="/wiki/Development_plans" class="mw-redirect" title="Development plans">development plans</a>. The codification of Green Belt policy and its extension to areas other than London came with the historic Circular 42/55 inviting local planning authorities to consider the establishment of Green Belts. This decision was made in tandem with the 1946 New Towns Act, which sought to depopulate urban centres in the South East of England and accommodate people in new settlements elsewhere. Green belt could therefore be designated by local authorities without worry that it would come into conflict with pressure from population growth.
</p><p>As the outward growth of London was seen to be firmly repressed, residents owning properties further from the built-up area also campaigned for this policy of urban restraint, partly to safeguard their own investments but often invoking an idealised scenic/rustic argument which laid the blame for most social ills upon urban influences. In mid-1971, for example, the government decided to extend the Metropolitan Green Belt northwards to include almost all of <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire" title="Hertfordshire">Hertfordshire</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Green_Belt" title="Metropolitan Green Belt">Metropolitan Green Belt</a> now covers parts of 68 different Districts or Boroughs.
</p><p>Since 1955 London's green belt has extended significantly, stretching some 35 miles out in places. London's green belt now covers an area of 516,000 hectares, an area broadly three times larger than that of London itself. With London's population set to increase by 2,000,000 over the period 2015-2030 it is therefore under increasing pressure for limited land release. The <a href="/wiki/London_Society_(organisation)" title="London Society (organisation)">London Society</a> began debate about the city's green belt in 2014 with publication of a report entitled "Green Sprawl".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Other organisations, including the Planning Officers Society,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> have since responded with specific calls for a review and proposals to balance land release with environmental protection.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> In 2016, the London Society and All Party Parliamentary Group (APPG) for London's Planning and Built Environment published plans for a 'green web' to replace the green belt in some locations.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The ambition is to create a "multifunctional green infrastructure landscape" in which new-build and publicly accessible natural space sat side-by-side.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several academics, policy groups and town planning organisations in recent years have criticised the idea and implementation of green belts in the UK. Green belt policy has been attacked as too rigid in the face of new urban and environmental challenges, principally the <a href="/wiki/Affordability_of_housing_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Affordability of housing in the United Kingdom">lack of housing available</a> in many cities in the UK. The policy has been criticised for reducing the amount of land available for building and therefore pushing up house prices, as 70% of the cost of building new houses is the purchase of the land (up from 25% in the late 1950s).<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> It has also been claimed that areas of green belt can be of unremarkable environmental quality, and may not be well managed or provide the recreational opportunities originally envisaged.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental">Environmental</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Environmental">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Town_and_Country_Planning_Association" title="Town and Country Planning Association">Town and Country Planning Association</a>, an organisation heavily involved in initiating the concept several decades previously, published a policy statement in 2002,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> which proposed a more flexible policy which would allow the introduction of green wedge and strategic gap policies rather than green belts, and so permit the expansion of some urban areas. In October 2007, Sir <a href="/wiki/Martin_Doughty" title="Martin Doughty">Martin Doughty</a>, then Chair of <a href="/wiki/Natural_England" title="Natural England">Natural England</a>, argued for a review of green belts, saying: "The time has come for a greener green belt. We need a 21st century solution to England's housing needs which puts in place a network of green wedges, gaps and corridors, linking the natural environment and people.".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, the <a href="/wiki/London_Society_(organisation)" title="London Society (organisation)"> London Society</a> published a comprehensive history of the green belt (as it emerged in the first part of the twentieth century) in 2014. Authored by the influential English <a href="/wiki/Urbanist" class="mw-redirect" title="Urbanist">urbanist</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jonathan_Manns&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jonathan Manns (page does not exist)">Jonathan Manns</a>, this called for a "move away from the simplistic and naïve idea that countryside is a sacrosanct patchwork of medieval hedgerows and towards an empirically informed position which once more recognises housing as a need to be met in locations with appropriate environmental capacity".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_house_prices">Effect on house prices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Effect on house prices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Affordability_of_housing_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Affordability of housing in the United Kingdom">Affordability of housing in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Value_of_land_and_buildings_in_the_UK.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Value_of_land_and_buildings_in_the_UK.png/220px-Value_of_land_and_buildings_in_the_UK.png" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Value_of_land_and_buildings_in_the_UK.png/330px-Value_of_land_and_buildings_in_the_UK.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Value_of_land_and_buildings_in_the_UK.png/440px-Value_of_land_and_buildings_in_the_UK.png 2x" data-file-width="590" data-file-height="383" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Value_of_land_and_buildings_in_the_UK.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Value of land and buildings in the UK from 1995 to 2016 (trillions).<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a></i> has criticised green belt policy, saying that unless more houses are built through reforming planning laws and releasing green belt land, then housing space will need to be rationed out.  It noted that if general inflation had risen as fast as housing prices had since 1971, a chicken would cost £51; and that Britain is "building less [sic] homes today than at any point since the 1920s".<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> According to the independent <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Economic_Affairs" title="Institute of Economic Affairs">Institute of Economic Affairs</a>, there is "overwhelming empirical evidence that that planning restrictions have a substantial impact on housing costs" and are the main reason why housing is two and a half times more expensive in 2011 than it was in 1975.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The free market <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith_Institute" title="Adam Smith Institute">Adam Smith Institute</a> is a particular critic of the green belt,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> and has claimed that removing the green belt from land within ten minutes walk of a railway station would release enough land to build 1 million homes.
</p><p>On the other hand, the <a href="/wiki/Council_for_the_Protection_of_Rural_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Council for the Protection of Rural England">Council for the Protection of Rural England</a> say it is a myth to connect green belts to rising house prices, since there is no clear difference in house prices between cities with green belts and cities without them, and both land and house prices are inflated by other factors such as investment.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lewis Abbott has identified green belt barriers to urban expansion as one of several major protectionist political-economic barriers to house building with negative effects on the supply, cost/prices, and quality of new homes. (The others include new housing development taxes and quasi-taxes; political discrimination against particular classes of new housing supplier, household consumer, and housing product; and controls on housing technical-product development – in particular, the blocking of innovative low-cost house building using new materials and production technologies). Abbott argues that the greenbelts actually defeat their own stated objective of saving the countryside and open spaces. By preventing existing towns and cities from extending normally and organically, they result in more land-extensive housing developments further out – i.e., the establishment beyond the greenbelts of new communities with lower building densities, their own built infrastructure and other facilities, and greater dependence on cars and commuting, etc. Meanwhile, valuable urban green space and brownfield sites best suited to industry and commerce are lost in existing conurbations as more and more new housing is crammed into them.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Commentators such as James Heartfield,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Alan Evans<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> and Tom Papworth<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> have called for outright abolition of green belts, principally on the grounds that by inhibiting the free use of land they restrict home ownership.
</p><p>However, in England, where 65% of people are property-owners who benefit from scarcity of building land, the concept of "green belt" has become entrenched as a fundamental part of government policy, and the possibility of reviewing boundaries is often viewed with considerable hostility by neighbouring communities and their elected representatives.<sup id="cite_ref-oxfordgreenbelt_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oxfordgreenbelt-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-foe_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-foe-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_concepts">Related concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_belt_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Related concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The general concept of "green belt" has evolved in recent years to encompass "Greenspace" and "Greenstructure", taking into account urban greenspace, an important aspect of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> in the 21st century. However, while in general  these concepts are quite distinct in the UK from the green belt as a statutory development plan designation, an exception occurs in London where land may be designated as "<a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Open_Land" title="Metropolitan Open Land">Metropolitan Open Land</a>" (MOL). Areas of MOL are subject to the same planning restrictions as the Green Belt while lying within the urban area. In 2005, the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a>'s <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20060329125020/http://www.map21ltd.com/COSTC11-book/index.htm">COST Action C11</a> (<a href="/wiki/COST" class="mw-redirect" title="COST">COST</a> European Cooperation in Science and Technology) undertook in-depth city case studies into cities across 15 European countries. <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a> was one such case study city for the UK. Conclusions were published in <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20060329125428/http://www.map21ltd.com/COSTC11-book/pdfs/b-Cases.pdf">"Case studies in Greenstructure Planning"</a>.
</p>
