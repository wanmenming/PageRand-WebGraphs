<p>The <b>Green Party of Aotearoa New Zealand</b> (<a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Māori language">Māori</a>: <i lang="mi">Rōpū Kākāriki o Aotearoa, Niu Tireni</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Left-wing" class="mw-redirect" title="Left-wing">left-wing</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_New_Zealand" title="List of political parties in New Zealand">political party in New Zealand</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Turner_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turner-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mazzoleni_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mazzoleni-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Like many <a href="/wiki/Green_party" title="Green party">Green parties</a> around the world it has four organisational pillars: <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_responsibility" title="Social responsibility">social responsibility</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grassroots_democracy" title="Grassroots democracy">grassroots democracy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nonviolence" title="Nonviolence">nonviolence</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The party's ideology combines <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a> with left-wing economic policies, including well-funded, locally controlled public services within the confines of a <a href="/wiki/Steady-state_economy" title="Steady-state economy">steady-state economy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Internationally, it is affiliated to the <a href="/wiki/Global_Greens" title="Global Greens">Global Greens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-globalgreens_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globalgreens-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Green Party has co-leaders: one male and one female. <a href="/wiki/James_Shaw_(New_Zealand_politician)" title="James Shaw (New Zealand politician)">James Shaw</a> is the party's male co-leader. The party's female co-leader is <a href="/wiki/Marama_Davidson" title="Marama Davidson">Marama Davidson</a>, who was announced as the new co-leader on 8 April 2018.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2017" title="New Zealand general election, 2017">2017 general election</a>, the Green Party secured 6.3% of the <a href="/wiki/Mixed-member_proportional_representation" title="Mixed-member proportional representation">party vote</a> and returned eight MPs.<sup id="cite_ref-2017_general_election_results_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2017_general_election_results-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This is down from 10.7% and 14 seats in the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2014" title="New Zealand general election, 2014">2014 general election</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2014_general_elections_results_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2014_general_elections_results-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In addition, the Green Party contests local government elections throughout New Zealand, including <a href="/wiki/Auckland_Council" title="Auckland Council">Auckland</a> where it campaigns under the <a href="/wiki/City_Vision_(Auckland,_New_Zealand_political_ticket)" class="mw-redirect" title="City Vision (Auckland, New Zealand political ticket)">City Vision</a> banner together with the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Labour_Party" title="New Zealand Labour Party">Labour Party</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> It is currently the fourth largest political party in the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_House_of_Representatives" title="New Zealand House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2017_general_election_results_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2017_general_election_results-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and has <a href="/wiki/Confidence_and_supply" title="Confidence and supply">agreed to support</a> the <a href="/wiki/Sixth_Labour_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Sixth Labour Government of New Zealand">Sixth Labour Government</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principles_and_policies">Principles and policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Principles and policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Green Party was founded to counter what it sees as the threats to the natural environment and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_issues_in_New_Zealand" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental issues in New Zealand">environmental issues</a> remain its main focus. In recent times, it has expressed concerns about <a href="/wiki/Mining_in_New_Zealand" title="Mining in New Zealand">mining</a> of <a href="/wiki/National_parks_of_New_Zealand" title="National parks of New Zealand">national parks</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Water_in_New_Zealand" title="Water in New Zealand">fresh water</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_New_Zealand" title="Climate change in New Zealand">climate change</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">peak oil</a><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and the release of <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetically engineered</a> organisms.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The party also spoken out in support of <a href="/wiki/Human_rights_in_New_Zealand" title="Human rights in New Zealand">human rights</a><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and against <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> operations conducted by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and other countries in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In its <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_New_Zealand" title="Economy of New Zealand">economic policies</a>, the Green Party stresses factors such as <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">taxing</a> the indirect costs of pollution, and <a href="/wiki/Fair_trade" title="Fair trade">fair trade</a>. It also states that measuring economic success should concentrate on <a href="/wiki/Measuring_well-being" class="mw-redirect" title="Measuring well-being">measuring well-being</a> rather than analysing <a href="/wiki/Economic_indicators" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic indicators">economic indicators</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The party said that if it formed a government in the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2017" title="New Zealand general election, 2017">2017 election</a>, it would <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_in_New_Zealand" title="Cannabis in New Zealand">legalise cannabis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> It would also "remove penalties for any person with a terminal illness, chronic or debilitating condition to cultivate, possess or use cannabis and/or cannabis products for therapeutic purposes, with the support of a registered medical practitioner".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Executive">Executive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Executive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundations">Foundations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Foundations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jeanette_Fitzsimons.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Jeanette_Fitzsimons.jpg/170px-Jeanette_Fitzsimons.jpg" width="170" height="208" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Jeanette_Fitzsimons.jpg/255px-Jeanette_Fitzsimons.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Jeanette_Fitzsimons.jpg/340px-Jeanette_Fitzsimons.jpg 2x" data-file-width="996" data-file-height="1221" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jeanette_Fitzsimons.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Former Green Party co-leader <a href="/wiki/Jeanette_Fitzsimons" title="Jeanette Fitzsimons">Jeanette Fitzsimons</a></div></div></div>
<p>The Green Party traces its origins to the <a href="/wiki/Values_Party" title="Values Party">Values Party</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Green_History_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Green_History-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> the world's first national-level environmentalist party.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The Values Party originated in 1972 at <a href="/wiki/Victoria_University_of_Wellington" title="Victoria University of Wellington">Victoria University of Wellington</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Green_History_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Green_History-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> While it gained a measure of public support in several elections, the then <a href="/wiki/First-past-the-post_electoral_system" class="mw-redirect" title="First-past-the-post electoral system">first-past-the-post electoral system</a> meant that the party did not win any seats in parliament. Some of the founding members of the Green Party of Aotearoa New Zealand, notably <a href="/wiki/Jeanette_Fitzsimons" title="Jeanette Fitzsimons">Jeanette Fitzsimons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rod_Donald" title="Rod Donald">Rod Donald</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mike_Ward_(New_Zealand_politician)" title="Mike Ward (New Zealand politician)">Mike Ward</a>, had been active members of the Values Party at the outset of the <a href="/wiki/Green_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Green movement">Green movement</a> in the 1970s.
</p><p>In May 1990, remnants of the Values Party merged with a number of other environmentalist organizations to form the modern Green Party. This sparked a resurgence of support, with the new group winning 6.85% of the vote (but no seats) in the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1990" title="New Zealand general election, 1990">1990 election</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Alliance_years">The Alliance years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Alliance years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following year, the Greens became co-founder members of the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_(New_Zealand_political_party)" title="Alliance (New Zealand political party)">Alliance</a>, a five-party grouping that also consisted of the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Democratic_Party_for_Social_Credit" title="New Zealand Democratic Party for Social Credit">Democrats</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Liberal_Party_(1992)" title="New Zealand Liberal Party (1992)">Liberals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mana_Motuhake" title="Mana Motuhake">Mana Motuhake</a> and <a href="/wiki/NewLabour_Party_(New_Zealand)" title="NewLabour Party (New Zealand)">NewLabour Party</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Green_History_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Green_History-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The Greens contested the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1993" title="New Zealand general election, 1993">1993</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1996" title="New Zealand general election, 1996">1996</a> elections as part of the Alliance.
</p><p>Until the 1995 annual conference in Taupō, the Greens had no elected leaders. At that conference, Fitzsimons was elected unopposed as female co-leader, and Donald defeated Joel Cayford and Mike Smith in a three-way contest to become male co-leader.
</p><p>With the adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Mixed-member_proportional_representation" title="Mixed-member proportional representation">mixed-member proportional</a> (MMP) electoral system in 1996, the Alliance gained entry to parliament, bringing three Green MPs with them: Fitzsimons, Donald and <a href="/wiki/Phillida_Bunkle" title="Phillida Bunkle">Phillida Bunkle</a>.
</p><p>In 1997, feeling that membership of the Alliance had subsumed their identity, the Greens took the decision to stand candidates independently of the Alliance at the next election.<sup id="cite_ref-Green_History_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Green_History-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> While most of the Green party members left the Alliance, some decided instead to leave the Green Party and stay in the Alliance (notably MP Phillida Bunkle). Conversely, some of the Alliance party members who joined the Alliance via other parties decided to leave the Alliance and join the Green Party, notably <a href="/wiki/Sue_Bradford" title="Sue Bradford">Sue Bradford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Keith_Locke" title="Keith Locke">Keith Locke</a>, who both joined the Alliance via NewLabour.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="1999_election">1999 election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 1999 election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_1999" title="New Zealand general election, 1999">1999 election</a>, the Greens gained 5.16% of the vote and seven seats in Parliament. Jeanette Fitzsimons also won the electorate seat of Coromandel, believed to be a world-first in a first-past-the-post election.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> However, the final result only became clear after the counting of special votes, so the Greens had a 10-day wait before officials could confirm their election to Parliament. During this time, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Labour_Party" title="New Zealand Labour Party">Labour</a> concluded a coalition agreement with the Alliance which excluded the Greens. However, the party supported the government on <a href="/wiki/Confidence_and_supply" title="Confidence and supply">confidence and supply</a> in return for some input into the budget and legislation. This led to the Greens gaining a $15 million energy efficiency and environmental package in the new government's first budget.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Over the term, the Greens developed a good working relationship with the government and also had some input into policy, notably Sue Bradford's amendments to the ERC legislation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="What is the ERC? (October 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2002_election">2002 election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 2002 election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2002" title="New Zealand general election, 2002">2002 election</a>, the Greens polled 7.00%, increasing their strength in parliament to nine seats, although they lost the Coromandel electorate.<sup id="cite_ref-2002_election_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2002_election-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Coromandel,_2002_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coromandel,_2002-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The electoral campaign featured strong tensions between the Greens and Labour. The Greens sharply criticised Labour for its plans to allow a moratorium on genetic engineering to expire, and believing that Labour would require their support to form a government, intended to make the extension of this moratorium a non-negotiable part of any deal. After the election, however, Labour and their coalition partner, the Jim Anderton-led <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Progressive_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="New Zealand Progressive Party">Progressive Coalition</a>, opted to rely on support from <a href="/wiki/United_Future_New_Zealand" class="mw-redirect" title="United Future New Zealand">United Future</a>, a party with conservative Christian overtones, shutting the Greens out of power.
</p><p>Although the Greens no longer had any input into the budget, they maintained a close working relationship with the government, and the Greens remained involved in the legislation process. Often the government needed to rely on Green votes in the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_House_of_Representatives" title="New Zealand House of Representatives">House</a> to pass legislation not approved by United Future, a conservative <a href="/wiki/Family_values" title="Family values">family-values</a> party. The government won praise from political commentators for juggling the two diametrically-opposed parties.
</p><p>While the moratorium on genetic modification has now expired, the Greens remain heavily involved in attempts to prevent any GM releases under the new regulatory framework, and genetic engineering remains a major topic for the party.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2005_election">2005 election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 2005 election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2005" title="New Zealand general election, 2005">2005 election</a>, the Greens won 5.30%, returning six of their MPs to Parliament. Despite expressing clear support for a <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Labour_Party" title="New Zealand Labour Party">Labour</a>-led government during the campaign,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> they were excluded from the resulting coalition, due to a refusal by United Future and NZ First to work with the Greens in cabinet.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> They were however able to negotiate a cooperation agreement which saw limited input into the budget and broad consultation on policy.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Both co-leaders were appointed as government spokespeople outside cabinet, with Fitzsimons responsible for Energy Efficiency, and Donald responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Buy_Kiwi_Made" class="mw-redirect" title="Buy Kiwi Made">Buy Kiwi Made</a> campaign.
</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Child_Discipline_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Child Discipline Act">Child Discipline Act</a> was introduced by Green Party member <a href="/wiki/Sue_Bradford" title="Sue Bradford">Sue Bradford</a>. It sought to outlaw the legal defence of "reasonable force" for parents prosecuted for assault against children, and was drawn from the ballot in 2005. It led to widespread debate and accusations that MPs supporting the bill were fostering a '<a href="/wiki/Nanny_state" title="Nanny state">nanny state</a>' approach. Despite this, the Bill became law after it passed its third reading on 16 May 2007 with an overwhelming majority of 113 votes for and 7 votes against.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2008_election">2008 election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: 2008 election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2008" title="New Zealand general election, 2008">2008 election</a> the Greens increased their share of the vote to 6.72%, enough for 9 MPs, even though there was a swing throughout the country to the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_National_Party" title="New Zealand National Party">National Party</a>. This initially gave the Greens two extra MPs, but counting the special votes brought in a third.<sup id="cite_ref-NZHerald_10544503_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NZHerald_10544503-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> They became the third largest parliamentary party in New Zealand.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Metiria_Turei" title="Metiria Turei">Metiria Turei</a> was elected at the 2009 annual general meeting after former female co-leader <a href="/wiki/Jeanette_Fitzsimons" title="Jeanette Fitzsimons">Jeanette Fitzsimons</a> left the party in February 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_election">2011 election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: 2011 election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2011" title="New Zealand general election, 2011">2011 election</a>, the Green Party received nearly a quarter of a million party votes (247,372), equating to 11.06% of the total valid party votes nationwide, earning them 14 seats in the new 50th Parliament. Preliminary results on election night showed them with 10.6% of the vote, equivalent to 13 seats, but special votes increased their support enough to gain an extra seat.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> They remained the third largest parliamentary party in New Zealand.<sup id="cite_ref-STUFF_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-STUFF-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2014_election">2014 election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: 2014 election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2014" title="New Zealand general election, 2014">2014 general election</a>, the Green Party's share of the party vote fell slightly to 10.70%. Despite this, they retained all of their 14 seats and remained the third largest party in parliament.
</p><p>James Shaw was elected at the party's 2015 annual general meeting over fellow MPs <a href="/wiki/Gareth_Hughes_(politician)" title="Gareth Hughes (politician)">Gareth Hughes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Hague" title="Kevin Hague">Kevin Hague</a>, and party member <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand_male_co-leadership_election,_2015#Vernon_Tava" title="Green Party of Aotearoa New Zealand male co-leadership election, 2015">Vernon Tava</a>. He succeeds <a href="/wiki/Russel_Norman" title="Russel Norman">Russel Norman</a>, who resigned in November 2015 to work as Executive Director of <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace_Aotearoa_New_Zealand" title="Greenpeace Aotearoa New Zealand">Greenpeace Aotearoa New Zealand</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2017_election">2017 election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: 2017 election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Green Party announced their final list of candidates for the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_general_election,_2017" title="New Zealand general election, 2017">2017 election</a> on 30 May 2017, with a number of lower listed members becoming one of the top 14–15 members most likely to enter parliament after the election.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> During the party's campaign launch on 9 July, the Green Party proposed charging bottling companies a ten percent tax for exporting water with the resulting revenue being split between local councils and Māori tribes or iwi. In addition, the Greens announced that they would ban new resource consents for bottling companies until the establishment of a new comprehensive commercial water pricing scheme.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2017, the Green Party Co-Leader Metiria Turei criticised the populist <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_First" title="New Zealand First">New Zealand First</a> party and its leader <a href="/wiki/Winston_Peters" title="Winston Peters">Winston Peters</a> for its alleged racism, particularly towards immigration.<sup id="cite_ref-Turei_vs_Peters_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turei_vs_Peters-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Coates also penned an article in the left-wing "The Daily Blog" claiming that the Greens would call a snap election rather than be excluded from a prospective Labour and New Zealand First coalition government.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Turei and Coates' comments were fiercely criticised by both Peters and Deputy Leader <a href="/wiki/Tracey_Martin" title="Tracey Martin">Tracey Martin</a>, who warned that this would affect post-election negotiations between the two parties. Fellow Co-Leader Shaw later clarified that Coates' remarks did not represent Green Party policy.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 16 July, in order to raise awareness of the inadequacies of the welfare system, Turei disclosed that she had committed benefit fraud in the past.<sup id="cite_ref-Metiria_Turei’s_speech_to_the_Green_Party_2017_AGM_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Metiria_Turei’s_speech_to_the_Green_Party_2017_AGM-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Turei also advocated raising the domestic purposes benefit for families during the Green Party's electoral campaign. Her disclosure generated considerable interest from the media, politicians, and the New Zealand blogosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> On 7 August, two Green MPs <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Graham" title="Kennedy Graham">Kennedy Graham</a> and party whip <a href="/wiki/David_Clendon" title="David Clendon">David Clendon</a> resigned as Green Party candidates due to their disagreement with Turei's actions and handling of the situation. They formally resigned from the Green Party's parliamentary caucus the following day after the Party made moves to remove them "involuntarily."<sup id="cite_ref-clendon-graham-resign_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clendon-graham-resign-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 9 August, Turei resigned as Co-leader and as a List MP; stating that the media scrutiny on her family had become unbearable. Co-leader <a href="/wiki/James_Shaw_(New_Zealand_politician)" title="James Shaw (New Zealand politician)">James Shaw</a> will remain the Green Party's sole leader for the 2017 election.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rnz-turei-resigns_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rnz-turei-resigns-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Clendon has stated that he would not be returning to the Green Party list despite Turei's resignation. On 12 August, the Green Party Executive declined Graham's application to return to the Party list following Turei's explanation. Leader James Shaw indicated that there was considerable animosity within the Party towards Clendon and Graham for their actions.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 17 August, it was reported that the Green Party had fallen by 11 points to 4 percent in the <a href="/wiki/1_News" title="1 News">1 News</a>–Colmar Brunton Poll. This could mean that the Party would fall short of the five percent threshold needed to enter Parliament under New Zealand's <a href="/wiki/Electoral_system_of_New_Zealand#MMP_in_New_Zealand" title="Electoral system of New Zealand">Mixed Member Proportional</a> system. The Party's sharp drop in the opinion poll was attributed to negative publicity around the Green Party's infighting and the ascension of <a href="/wiki/Jacinda_Ardern" title="Jacinda Ardern">Jacinda Ardern</a> as leader of the center-left <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_of_New_Zealand" class="mw-redirect" title="Labour Party of New Zealand">Labour Party</a>, the Greens' nominal ally.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> By contrast, the Roy Morgan opinion poll placed public support for the Green Party at 9 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the 2017 general election, the Green Party's party vote dropped to 6.3% with the Party gaining eight seats in the House of Representatives.<sup id="cite_ref-2017_general_election_results_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2017_general_election_results-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The Green parliamentary caucus' newest members are <a href="/wiki/Chl%C3%B6e_Swarbrick" title="Chlöe Swarbrick">Chlöe Swarbrick</a>, who is currently the <a href="/wiki/Baby_of_the_House#New_Zealand" title="Baby of the House">youngest member</a> of the House, and <a href="/wiki/Golriz_Ghahraman" title="Golriz Ghahraman">Golriz Ghahraman</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Refugee" title="Refugee">refugee</a> member of the House.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> Following the election results, Party Leader Shaw stated that the Greens would not be seeking a coalition with the National Party. He added that the Party was pursuing a coalition rather than a support agreement with the Labour and socially-conservative <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_First" title="New Zealand First">New Zealand First</a> parties.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 9 October, the Greens leader Shaw took part in negotiations with the Labour Party.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
During the coalition–forming negotiations, NZ First leader Peters turned down Shaw's invitation for the two parties to negotiate directly on the grounds that the Greens and Labour had campaigned together under a memorandum of understanding during the 2017 election.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Green_Party_in_Government">The Green Party in Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: The Green Party in Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sixth_Labour_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Sixth Labour Government of New Zealand">Sixth Labour Government of New Zealand</a></div>
<p>In October 2017, the Greens entered a confidence and supply arrangement with the Labour Party which gives them three ministers outside cabinet and one under secretary role.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> This marks the first time the Greens have been in government.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> Party leader James Shaw was appointed Minister for Climate Change and Statistics and Associate Minister of Finance. <a href="/wiki/Julie_Anne_Genter" title="Julie Anne Genter">Julie Anne Genter</a> was made <a href="/wiki/Ministry_for_Women" title="Ministry for Women">Minister for Women</a> and Associate Minister of Health and Transport. <a href="/wiki/Eugenie_Sage" title="Eugenie Sage">Eugenie Sage</a> was made Minister of Conservation and Land Information and Associate Minister for the Environment. <a href="/wiki/Jan_Logie" title="Jan Logie">Jan Logie</a> was appointed Parliamentary Undersecretary to the <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Justice_(New_Zealand)" title="Minister of Justice (New Zealand)">Minister of Justice</a> <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Little_(New_Zealand_politician)" title="Andrew Little (New Zealand politician)">Andrew Little</a> with a focus on domestic and sexual violence issues.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As a support partner of the <a href="/wiki/Sixth_Labour_Government_of_New_Zealand" title="Sixth Labour Government of New Zealand">Labour-New Zealand First coalition government</a>, the Greens secured several policies and concessions including a proposed <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero_Carbon_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Zero Carbon Act (page does not exist)">Zero Carbon Act</a>, a referendum on legalizing personal <a href="/wiki/Cannabis" title="Cannabis">cannabis</a> use by 2020, establishing a proposed <a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_Commission_(New_Zealand)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Climate Commission (New Zealand) (page does not exist)">Climate Commission</a>, a proposed <a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Transport_Card&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Green Transport Card (page does not exist)">Green Transport Card</a> to reduce public transportation costs, investing in rail and cycle infrastructure, light rail construction to <a href="/wiki/Auckland_Airport" title="Auckland Airport">Auckland Airport</a>, increasing the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Conservation_(New_Zealand)" title="Department of Conservation (New Zealand)">Department of Conservation</a>'s funding, eliminating "excessive" benefit sanctions and the gender pay gap, a rent-to-own-scheme as part of <a href="/wiki/KiwiBuild" title="KiwiBuild">KiwiBuild</a>, and re-establishing the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mental_Health_Commission&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mental Health Commission (page does not exist)">Mental Health Commission</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_body_elections">Local body elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Local body elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2013_local_elections">2013 local elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: 2013 local elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_local_elections,_2013" title="New Zealand local elections, 2013">2013 local elections</a>, Greens won three city council and two regional council seats in <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">Wellington</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> a council seat in <a href="/wiki/Dunedin" title="Dunedin">Dunedin</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> and also enjoyed success in <a href="/wiki/Christchurch" title="Christchurch">Christchurch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gisborne,_New_Zealand" title="Gisborne, New Zealand">Gisborne</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2016_local_body_elections">2016 local body elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: 2016 local body elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_local_elections,_2016" title="New Zealand local elections, 2016">2016 local elections</a>, Green Dunedin candidate Aaron Hawkins was re-elected to the <a href="/wiki/Dunedin_City_Council" title="Dunedin City Council">Dunedin City Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Wellington_local_elections,_2016" title="Wellington local elections, 2016">2016 Wellington local election</a>, four Green candidates <a href="/wiki/Sue_Kedgley" title="Sue Kedgley">Sue Kedgley</a>, Iona Pannett, Sarah Free, and David Lee were elected onto the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Wellington_Regional_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Greater Wellington Regional Council">Greater Wellington Regional Council</a> and the Lambton, Eastern, and Southern Wards of the <a href="/wiki/Wellington_City_Council" title="Wellington City Council">Wellington City Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> Several Green candidates also contested seats on the <a href="/wiki/Auckland_Council" title="Auckland Council">Auckland Council</a>, local boards, and licensing trusts during the <a href="/wiki/Auckland_local_elections,_2016" title="Auckland local elections, 2016">2016 Auckland local body elections</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Male_Policy_Co-Convenors">Male Policy Co-Convenors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Aotearoa_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Male Policy Co-Convenors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Policy Co-Convenors are the leaders of the Policy Committee, which is autonomous from both the caucus and the party executive. While lower in profile than the party Co-Convenors, the policy co-convenors are considered to have the same status as the party co-convenors, and are elected in the same way. There is always one male policy co-convenor and one female policy co-convenor.</i>
</p>
<ul><li>Matthew Grant (2001–2004)</li>
<li>Bill Brislen (2004–2005)</li>
<li>Ivan Sowry (2005–2009)</li>
<li>Richard Leckinger (2009–2013)</li>
<li>Paul Bailey (2013–2016)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Barry_Coates" title="Barry Coates">Barry Coates</a> (2016)</li>
