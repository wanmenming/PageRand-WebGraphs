<p>The <b>Green Party of Manitoba</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Parti vert du Manitoba</i>) is a provincial political party in <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a>, Canada, founded on November 11, 1998.   The party is legally autonomous from the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Canada" title="Green Party of Canada">Green Party of Canada</a>, though for several years many of its members also belonged to the <i>Green Party of Canada in Manitoba</i>, a federal organization established in 1996 (the two organizations were separated in May 2005).  The GPM has maintained a position as the fourth largest party in Manitoba since the <a href="/wiki/Manitoba_general_election,_2003" title="Manitoba general election, 2003">2003 election</a>, both in number of votes received and candidates run.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Manitoba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Green_Party_of_Manitoba_(logo_until_2011).png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/24/Green_Party_of_Manitoba_%28logo_until_2011%29.png/150px-Green_Party_of_Manitoba_%28logo_until_2011%29.png" width="150" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/24/Green_Party_of_Manitoba_%28logo_until_2011%29.png/225px-Green_Party_of_Manitoba_%28logo_until_2011%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/24/Green_Party_of_Manitoba_%28logo_until_2011%29.png 2x" data-file-width="296" data-file-height="286" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Green_Party_of_Manitoba_(logo_until_2011).png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Logo until 2011.</div></div></div>
<p>The GPM is not the first "Green Party" in Manitoba history.  Former <a href="/wiki/New_Democratic_Party_of_Manitoba" title="New Democratic Party of Manitoba">New Democrat</a> Nick Ternette established a "Green Party" in Winnipeg in 1989, and fielded candidates under its banner in that year's municipal elections.  Ternette was from the left-wing of the NDP, and opposed the party's centrist direction in the 1980s.  His "Green Party" supported several progressive and environmental causes, and was further to the left than is the current GPM.  None of its candidates were elected, and the party never ran candidates at the provincial level.  Ternette is not affiliated with the GPM, although neither has he opposed it.
</p><p>The current party was established by Winnipeg electoral reform activist Chris Billows in November 1998, with the assistance of the Green Party of Canada. Billows, Eymond Toupin, and future provincial leader <a href="/wiki/Markus_Buchart" title="Markus Buchart">Markus Buchart</a> hosted the Green Party of Canada's national convention in Winnipeg in August 1998.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policies">Policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Manitoba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The GPM's policies are generally progressive.  The party is primarily focused on environmental issues, and promotes the conservation of land and non-renewable natural resources.  It has expressed concern about "urban sprawl" in Winnipeg's suburbs, has called for reform in Manitoba's commercial hog sector, and generally supports the rights of small farming interests over corporations.
</p><p>The GPM also favours liberal positions on social issues such as <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex marriage</a>, and promotes accessible public health care with emphasis on healthy lifestyles and illness prevention.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The party supports the extension of labour protection laws to farmworkers and a reduction of Manitoba's standard work week from 40 to 32 hours. It has also endorsed full employment, and has criticized <a href="/wiki/Gary_Doer" title="Gary Doer">Gary Doer</a>'s NDP government for not reversing welfare cutbacks enacted by the previous <a href="/wiki/Progressive_Conservative_Party_of_Manitoba" title="Progressive Conservative Party of Manitoba">Tory</a> government of <a href="/wiki/Gary_Filmon" title="Gary Filmon">Gary Filmon</a>.
</p>

<p>The GPM released its <a href="/wiki/Manitoba_general_election,_2016" title="Manitoba general election, 2016">2016 election</a> platform, entitled "Building a Sustainable Manitoba", on April 5, 2016. The document included promises to introduce <a href="/wiki/Guaranteed_Annual_Income" class="mw-redirect" title="Guaranteed Annual Income">Guaranteed Annual Income</a> to Manitoba, a $50/tonne <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tax" title="Carbon tax">carbon tax</a>, fare-free transit, and oppose the <a href="/wiki/Energy_East" title="Energy East">Energy East pipeline</a>, among other items.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The GPM ran six candidates in the <a href="/wiki/Manitoba_general_election,_1999" title="Manitoba general election, 1999">provincial election of 1999</a>, and scored its best result in the Winnipeg riding of <a href="/wiki/Wolseley_(Manitoba_riding)" class="mw-redirect" title="Wolseley (Manitoba riding)">Wolseley</a>, where Phyllis Abbé, also a prominent former New Democrat, received 386 votes. Former party leader <a href="/wiki/Markus_Buchart" title="Markus Buchart">Markus Buchart</a> ran against <a href="/wiki/Premier_of_Manitoba" title="Premier of Manitoba">Premier</a> <a href="/wiki/Gary_Filmon" title="Gary Filmon">Gary Filmon</a> in the riding of <a href="/wiki/Tuxedo_(Manitoba_riding)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tuxedo (Manitoba riding)">Tuxedo</a>, and received 126 votes.  The party received 0.2% of all votes cast in the province.
</p><p>The GPM ran fourteen candidates in the <a href="/wiki/Manitoba_general_election,_2003" title="Manitoba general election, 2003">2003 election</a>. Buchart received 1193 votes (19.5%) in Wolseley, placing second against New Democrat <a href="/wiki/Rob_Altemeyer" title="Rob Altemeyer">Rob Altemeyer</a>. The GPM as a whole received 4.08% of the vote in the ridings that it contested.
</p><p>In the December 13, 2005, by-election held in the strongly conservative <a href="/wiki/Fort_Whyte" title="Fort Whyte">Fort Whyte</a> constituency, GPM candidate Shelagh Pizey-Allen garnered 1.77% of the vote.
</p><p>The GPM ran fifteen candidates in the <a href="/wiki/Manitoba_general_election,_2007" title="Manitoba general election, 2007">2007 election</a>. The fifteen captured 1.33% of the vote, or 5.5% in the electoral divisions that were contested. The Green candidates received 12.32% in <a href="/wiki/Wolseley_(Manitoba_electoral_district)" title="Wolseley (Manitoba electoral district)">Wolseley</a> placing second, 8.46% in <a href="/wiki/Lord_Roberts_(electoral_district)" title="Lord Roberts (electoral district)">Lord Roberts</a>, 7.76% in <a href="/wiki/St._Boniface_(provincial_electoral_district)" title="St. Boniface (provincial electoral district)">St. Boniface</a>, 6.38% in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Rouge_(electoral_district)" title="Fort Rouge (electoral district)">Fort Rouge</a>, and third place in <a href="/wiki/Minnedosa_(electoral_district)" title="Minnedosa (electoral district)">Minnedosa</a> ahead of the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Manitoba" class="mw-redirect" title="Liberal Party of Manitoba">Liberal Party of Manitoba</a>.
</p><p>In 2011, the GPM ran 32 candidates across the province and took 2.52% of the vote province-wide, or 10,886 votes, and won 4.56% of votes in ridings they contested. Party leader <a href="/wiki/James_Beddome" title="James Beddome">James Beddome</a> placed second in the <a href="/wiki/Wolseley_(Manitoba_electoral_district)" title="Wolseley (Manitoba electoral district)">Wolseley</a> constituency with 19.64% of the vote, while nine other candidates placed third in various ridings ahead of <a href="/wiki/Manitoba_Liberal_Party" title="Manitoba Liberal Party">Liberal</a> candidates.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leadership">Leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Manitoba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Leaders of the Green Party of Manitoba</b>
</p>

<li>Daniel Drimes, 2005</li>

<p>Markus Buchart resigned as party leader in late February 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The party's second leader was Daniel Drimes. He served as leader from April 2 to 22, 2005.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The party's first leadership contest under the rules of <a href="/wiki/Elections_Manitoba" title="Elections Manitoba">Elections Manitoba</a> began on July 1, 2005, and a new leader was chosen on November 20, 2005.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> She was <a href="/wiki/Holly_Nelson" title="Holly Nelson">Holly Nelson</a>, a retired electrical technologist and professional writer who owned a Winnipeg New Age book store, the Philosopher's Stone, during the 1990s.
</p><p>In September 2006, a few weeks before the party's leadership convention, she stepped down, having accepted an out-of-province job.  On November 19, 2006, the party chose 23-year-old <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Basham" title="Andrew Basham">Andrew Basham</a> as its new leader.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>On November 15, 2008, <a href="/wiki/James_Beddome" title="James Beddome">James Beddome</a> was elected leader, defeating incumbent leader Andrew Basham and contender Shane Nestruck. Beddome's first term expired November 2010, and he was acclaimed a second term.
</p>
