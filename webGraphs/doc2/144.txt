<p>The <b>Political Party of Radicals</b> (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i lang="nl">Politieke Partij Radikalen</i>, PPR) was a <a href="/wiki/Christian_left" title="Christian left">progressive Christian</a><sup id="cite_ref-Voerman1995_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voerman1995-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_the_Netherlands" title="List of political parties in the Netherlands">political party in the Netherlands</a>. The PPR played a relatively small role in Dutch politics and merged with other left-wing parties to form <a href="/wiki/GreenLeft" class="mw-redirect" title="GreenLeft">GreenLeft</a> (Dutch: GroenLinks) in 1991.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Party_History">Party History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_Party_of_Radicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Party History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Before_1968">Before 1968</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_Party_of_Radicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Before 1968">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The foundation of the PPR is linked to formation of the <a href="/wiki/De_Jong_cabinet" title="De Jong cabinet">De Jong cabinet</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Appeal" title="Christian Democratic Appeal">Christian Democratic Appeal</a> (CDA).
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_general_election,_1967" title="Dutch general election, 1967">1967 general election</a> it became clear that a <a href="/wiki/Centre-right_politics" title="Centre-right politics">centre-right</a> cabinet would be formed by the <a href="/wiki/Anti-Revolutionary_Party" title="Anti-Revolutionary Party">Anti-Revolutionary Party</a> (ARP) and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Historical_Union" title="Christian Historical Union">Christian Historical Union</a> (CHU), the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_People%27s_Party" title="Catholic People&#39;s Party">Catholic People's Party</a> (KVP) and the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Party_for_Freedom_and_Democracy" title="People&#39;s Party for Freedom and Democracy">People's Party for Freedom and Democracy</a> (VVD). <a href="/wiki/Progressivism" title="Progressivism">Progressive</a> forces within the KVP and ARP had hoped for the formation of a <a href="/wiki/Centre-left" class="mw-redirect" title="Centre-left">centre-left</a> cabinet with the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(Netherlands)" title="Labour Party (Netherlands)">Labour Party</a> (PvdA) without the participation of the CHU and VVD.
</p><p>In March 1967 a group of "regret voters" (ARP-members who regretted voting ARP) published an advertisement in the Protestant newspaper <a href="/wiki/Trouw" title="Trouw">Trouw</a>, aimed at the leadership of the ARP: they claimed that the left-wing, so called "evangelically radical", ideal of the ARP could not be realised in a cabinet with the VVD. In April this group began to meet regularly with dissidents from the KVP in the Hotel Americain, this gave the group the name "American Group". The group included <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_de_Gaay_Fortman" class="mw-redirect" title="Wilhelm de Gaay Fortman">Wilhelm de Gaay Fortman</a>, prominent ARP politician, his son <a href="/wiki/Bas_de_Gaay_Fortman" title="Bas de Gaay Fortman">Bas de Gaay Fortman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jo_Cals" title="Jo Cals">Jo Cals</a>, former KVP prime minister, and <a href="/wiki/Ruud_Lubbers" title="Ruud Lubbers">Ruud Lubbers</a>, member of the KVP and future <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_Netherlands" title="Prime Minister of the Netherlands">Prime Minister</a>. In May the group became a formal organisation, the Working Group Christian Radicals, which was oriented at making their mother parties more progressive. They had some success in the KVP, which was seeking new allies and a new image, after it had lost the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_general_election,_1967" title="Dutch general election, 1967">1967 general election</a>.
</p>

<p>In February 1968 the leaders of the KVP, <a href="/wiki/Norbert_Schmelzer" title="Norbert Schmelzer">Norbert Schmelzer</a>, ARP, <a href="/wiki/Barend_Biesheuvel" title="Barend Biesheuvel">Barend Biesheuvel</a> and CHU, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jur_Mellema&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jur Mellema (page does not exist)">Jur Mellema</a> made a public appearance, stating that the three parties wanted to work together more closely. This cooperation would eventually lead to the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Appeal" title="Christian Democratic Appeal">Christian Democratic Appeal</a> (CDA) in 1974. With this appearance the hopes of the Christian Radicals within the KVP that a progressive alliance with the Labour Party would be formed, were shattered.
</p>
<h3><span id="1968.E2.80.9377"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1968–77">1968–77</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_Party_of_Radicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1968–77">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1974_ppr_ov-1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a3/1974_ppr_ov-1.jpg/150px-1974_ppr_ov-1.jpg" width="150" height="217" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/a/a3/1974_ppr_ov-1.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="207" data-file-height="300" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1974_ppr_ov-1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Poster for the 1974 elections showing the green nature of the party</div></div></div>
<p>On 27 April 1968 part of the group of Catholic radicals left the KVP and formed the Political Party of Radicals (PRP).<sup id="cite_ref-Irwin1989_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Irwin1989-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Prominent radicals, like Lubbers and Cals, did not join the party. A group of radical KVP MPs led by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jacques_Aarden&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jacques Aarden (page does not exist)">Jacques Aarden</a> left the KVP parliamentary party and formed their own group-Aarden, the parliamentary party of the PPR. The party is joined by some prominent "regret-voters" from the ARP, most prominently Bas de Gaay Fortman.
</p><p>The party began to co-operate closely with the Labour Party (PvdA), the newly founded <a href="/wiki/Democrats_66" title="Democrats 66">Democrats 66</a> (D66) and initially with the left-wing <a href="/wiki/Pacifist_Socialist_Party" title="Pacifist Socialist Party">Pacifist Socialist Party</a> (PSP) in the so-called Progressive Accords (PAK). The parties proposed common election manifestos and formed a <a href="/wiki/Shadow_cabinet" class="mw-redirect" title="Shadow cabinet">shadow cabinet</a>. The PSP left the alliance before the negotiations ended, because the alliance was not <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialist</a> enough. The PPR participated in the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_general_election,_1971" title="Dutch general election, 1971">1971 general election</a> as part of the PAK. The PPR won only two seats, while the PAK wins only 52 seats, a third of parliament. Jacques Aarden led the party in parliament. Some prominent members left the PPR, because they think the party has failed. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Biesheuvel_cabinet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Biesheuvel cabinet (page does not exist)">Biesheuvel cabinet</a> was formed by the ARP, KVP, CHU, VVD and the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Socialists_%2770" title="Democratic Socialists &#39;70">Democratic Socialists '70</a>.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_general_election,_1972" title="Dutch general election, 1972">1972 general election</a> the parties tried again. The PAK now won 56 seats and the PPR 7. Former ARP-politician <a href="/wiki/Bas_de_Gaay_Fortman" title="Bas de Gaay Fortman">Bas de Gaay Fortman</a> led the party in the elections. A continuation of the <a href="/wiki/First_Biesheuvel_cabinet" title="First Biesheuvel cabinet">Biesheuvel cabinet</a>, which fell within one year is excluded. The only possibility is a centre-left government with the PAK parties and the Christian democratic parties. The PAK parties refuse this possibility and want to form a PAK minority cabinet. A compromise is found in the progressive <a href="/wiki/Den_Uyl_cabinet" title="Den Uyl cabinet">Den Uyl cabinet</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_the_Netherlands" title="Cabinet of the Netherlands">extra-parliamentary cabinet</a> composed out of PvdA, D66 and PPR and progressive individuals from the ARP and the KVP, including former Radicals such as Lubbers and Wilhelm de Gaay Fortman. The PPR supplied two ministers, <a href="/wiki/Harry_van_Doorn" title="Harry van Doorn">Harry van Doorn</a> Minister for Culture, Recreation and Social Work, and <a href="/wiki/Boy_Trip" title="Boy Trip">Boy Trip</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minister_without_portfolio" title="Minister without portfolio">Minister without Portfolio</a> for Science and one <a href="/wiki/Staatssecretaris" class="mw-redirect" title="Staatssecretaris">state secretary</a>, Michel van Hulten, for Transport, Public Works and Water Management. The fact that the PPR was co-operating with the ARP and KVP, which many members of the party had just left led to considerable upheaval within the party. The party congress adopted a resolution stating that the party would not co-operate with these parties in the next cabinet.
</p>
<h3><span id="1977.E2.80.9389"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1977–89">1977–89</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_Party_of_Radicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1977–89">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PPR3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/ac/PPR3.jpg/150px-PPR3.jpg" width="150" height="211" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/a/ac/PPR3.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="213" data-file-height="300" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PPR3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Poster for the 1986 elections showing party leader Ria Beckers in front of the 1983 anti-nuclear weapons demonstration</div></div></div>
<p>Before the 1977 elections Bas de Gaay Fortman was replaced as political leader by <a href="/wiki/Ria_Beckers" title="Ria Beckers">Ria Beckers</a>. The election results were especially disastrous: the party lost four seats: this is attributed to the political competition between PvdA Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Joop_den_Uyl" title="Joop den Uyl">Joop den Uyl</a> and his Christian Democrat competitor <a href="/wiki/Dries_van_Agt" title="Dries van Agt">Dries van Agt</a>, which caused many PSP-sympathisers to vote for Den Uyl, and also the anti-KVP/ARP resolution adopted by the congress, which made serious participation in cabinet impossible.
</p><p>in 1979, following the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_1979_(Netherlands)" title="European Parliament election, 1979 (Netherlands)">first direct elections to the European Parliament</a>, the PRP was involved with the <a href="/wiki/European_Green_Party" title="European Green Party">Coordination of European Green and Radical Parties</a> (CEGRP) and its unsuccessful efforts to create a single pan-European platform for green and radical politics.<sup id="cite_ref-Bomberg2005_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bomberg2005-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the early 1980s the placement of American <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_and_weapons_of_mass_destruction" title="Netherlands and weapons of mass destruction">nuclear weapons</a> became an important political issue. The PPR was involved in the organisation of national demonstrations against nuclear weapons and more than 80% of the members of the PPR attended one of the two <a href="/wiki/Hollanditis" title="Hollanditis">mass protests against the placement nuclear weapons</a> of 1981 and 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The party began to debate its political course: some members (known as the Godebald-group) wanted to continue co-operation with the PvdA. Many of the party's founders and former ministers, such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Erik_Jurgens&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Erik Jurgens (page does not exist)">Erik Jurgens</a> were part of this group. Others wanted to co-operate with the Pacifist Socialist Party and the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_the_Netherlands" title="Communist Party of the Netherlands">Communist Party of the Netherlands</a>. They were called the Wageningen Group. Another group wanted to reform the party's course and continue as an independent Green party: <a href="/wiki/Bas_de_Gaay_Fortman" title="Bas de Gaay Fortman">Bas de Gaay Fortman</a> and former <a href="/wiki/Provo_(movement)" title="Provo (movement)">Provo</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kabouter_(movement)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kabouter (movement) (page does not exist)">Kabouter</a> <a href="/wiki/Roel_van_Duijn" title="Roel van Duijn">Roel van Duijn</a> were important exponents of this group. At the party congress of 1981 the party voted on these options, which were colour-coded: the Red option (cooperation with the PSP and CPN), the Blue option (co-operation with the D66 and the PvdA) and the Green option (independent green party). An alliance was struck between the Reds and Greens. The party decided to break its alliance with D66 and the PvdA and try to form an alliance with the PSP and CPN, which would have a strong green identity. In the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_general_election,_1981" title="Dutch general election, 1981">1981 general election</a> it kept its three seats. After the elections a CDA/PvdA/D66 cabinet was formed – a continuation of the <a href="/wiki/Den_Uyl_cabinet" title="Den Uyl cabinet">Den Uyl cabinet</a> without the PPR. The cabinet fell after several months in the subsequent <a href="/wiki/Dutch_general_election,_1982" title="Dutch general election, 1982">1982 election</a> where the party lost one seat. In 1985 CDA-dissident Stef Dijkman joined the PPR parliamentary party. He had split from the CDA in 1983 together with Nico Sholten, who joined the PvdA parliamentary party.
</p><p>In the 1980s the cooperation between PPR, CPN and PSP began to take shape. The parties co-operated mainly in municipal and provincial elections and legislatures, because a higher percentage of votes is necessary to gain seats in such elections. In the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_1984_(Netherlands)" title="European Parliament election, 1984 (Netherlands)">1984 European election</a> the PPR, CPN and PSP formed the <a href="/wiki/Green_Progressive_Accord" title="Green Progressive Accord">Green Progressive Accord</a> that entered with one list in the European elections. They won one seat, which rotated between the PSP and PPR. Party-members also met each other in grassroots extraparliamentary protest against <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> and nuclear weapons. Both the PSP and CPN were unwilling to co-operate intensively with the PPR, which was slightly larger in seats and which they saw as a non-socialist party.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_1989">After 1989</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_Party_of_Radicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: After 1989">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1989 the PSP initiated talks with the PPR and the PSP. Their initiative was supported by an open letter from members of <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade unions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmental movements</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Arts">arts</a> which called for one progressive formation to the left of the PvdA. After long negotiations, which were pressured by the fall of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Lubbers_cabinet" title="Second Lubbers cabinet">second Lubbers cabinet</a> and the subsequent earlier elections, the party entered in the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_general_election,_1989" title="Dutch general election, 1989">1989 general election</a> as part of GreenLeft. They were joined by the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_People%27s_Party_(Netherlands)" title="Evangelical People&#39;s Party (Netherlands)">Evangelical People's Party</a> (EVP). Ria Beckers was <a href="/wiki/Lijsttrekker" title="Lijsttrekker">top candidate</a> and she became chair of the GreenLeft parliamentary party. In 1991 the PPR dissolved itself into GreenLeft when GreenLeft became a formal political party.<sup id="cite_ref-Voerman1995_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voerman1995-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In the same year, GreenLeft's only MEP, former PPR-chair, Verbeek announced that he would not give up his seat in the European Parliament, to allow a former member of the PSP to enter the European Parliament. He would continue as an independent and would be top candidate for <a href="/wiki/The_Greens_(Netherlands)" title="The Greens (Netherlands)">The Greens</a> in the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_1994" title="European Parliament election, 1994">1994 European election</a>, without success.
</p>

<p>The PPR left a considerable mark on GreenLeft. In particular the green, environmentalist ideals of the PPR still play an important role.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_Party_of_Radicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name "Political Party of Radicals" referenced the origin of the party, it was founded by the so-called <a href="/wiki/Christian_left" title="Christian left">Christian Radicals</a>: progressive Catholics. Because they wanted to open their party to all Christians as well as to non-Christians, the dropped the reference to Christianity in their name.
</p>
<h2><span id="Ideology_.26_issues"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ideology_&amp;_issues">Ideology &amp; issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_Party_of_Radicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ideology &amp; issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The party did not have a manifesto of principles, instead election manifestos which addressed current issues guided the party's behaviour.
</p><p>Although the party had Christian roots, it denounced a direct relationship between religion and politics. The party can be seen as an early <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">green party</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Post-materialism" title="Post-materialism">post-materialist agenda</a> consisting of <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmental protection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Third_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Third world">third world</a> <a href="/wiki/Development_cooperation" class="mw-redirect" title="Development cooperation">development</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_disarmament" title="Nuclear disarmament">nuclear disarmament</a>, <a href="/wiki/Industrial_democracy" title="Industrial democracy">democratisation of the economy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grassroots_democracy" title="Grassroots democracy">grass roots democracy</a>. The party favoured the implementation of a <a href="/wiki/Basic_income_in_the_Netherlands" title="Basic income in the Netherlands">basic income</a>.
</p><p>During its existence the party changed from a Christian ally of the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(Netherlands)" title="Labour Party (Netherlands)">Labour Party</a> (PvdA) with its roots in the Catholic <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">trade union</a> movement to a party on the left of the PvdA with links to the environmental movement. Several decisions were important in this, but especially the 1981 congress in which the party decided not to cooperate, but try to found a <a href="/wiki/Political_alliance" title="Political alliance">political alliance</a> left of the PvdA with a green program.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Municipal_and_Provincial_Government">Municipal and Provincial Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_Party_of_Radicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Municipal and Provincial Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The PPR supplied several <a href="/wiki/Gemeenteraad" class="mw-redirect" title="Gemeenteraad">municipal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Provinciale_Staten" class="mw-redirect" title="Provinciale Staten">provincial councillors</a>. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electorate">Electorate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_Party_of_Radicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Electorate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The PPRs electorate consisted of young, well educated voters, who often had a Catholic or Protestant background. The electorate was slightly more concentrated in the West (North, Utrecht and South Holland) and South (Brabant and Limburg).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisation">Organisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_Party_of_Radicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Organisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisational_structure">Organisational structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_Party_of_Radicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Organisational structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The highest organ of the PPR was the <a href="/wiki/Party_congress" class="mw-redirect" title="Party congress">Congress</a>. It convened once every year. It appointed the party board and decided the order of candidates on electoral lists for the House of Representatives, Senate and European Parliament and had the final say over the party program.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Linked_organisations">Linked organisations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_Party_of_Radicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Linked organisations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The PPR published its own magazine which was called <i>Radicals Paper</i> (Dutch: Radikalenkrant) between 1968 and 1973 and 1982 and 1990 and <i>PPR Action Paper</i> (Dutch: PPR aktiekrant; PPRAK) between 1973 and 1981.
</p><p>The PPRs youth was organised in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_Party_of_Radicals_Youth&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Political Party of Radicals Youth (page does not exist)">Political Party of Radicals Youth</a> (Dutch: Politieke Partij Radicalen Jeugd; PPRJ) In 1991 the PPRJ merged into <a href="/wiki/DWARS" title="DWARS">DWARS</a> GreenLeft youth.
</p><p>In the 1980s the scientific institute of the PPR cooperated strongly with the scientific institutes of the PSP and CPN. They published <i>De Helling</i> together since 1987. The <i>Rode Draad</i> was published since 1985 it was a magazine for municipal and provincial councillors of both the PSP, PPR and CPN.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Cooperation">International Cooperation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_Party_of_Radicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: International Cooperation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 1979 the party cooperated with other Green and left-wing parties in organisations like <a href="/wiki/Grael" class="mw-redirect" title="Grael">Grael</a>, which later became the <a href="/wiki/European_Green_Party" title="European Green Party">European Green Party</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships_to_other_parties">Relationships to other parties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Political_Party_of_Radicals&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Relationships to other parties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cooperation has been an important theme for the PPR as the party was founded as party of <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">left-wing</a> Christians who wanted to cooperate with the PvdA, which later became committed to forming a political alliance left of the PvdA.
</p><p>Between 1971 and 1977 the relations with PvdA and Democrats 66 were especially close. The three parties formed the core of the <a href="/wiki/Den_Uyl_cabinet" title="Den Uyl cabinet">Den Uyl cabinet</a>. After the elections of 1977, when the PPR lost a lot of seats, and 1981 when the PPR was excluded from the <a href="/wiki/Second_Van_Agt_cabinet" title="Second Van Agt cabinet">second Van Agt cabinet</a>.
</p><p>The relations with the CPN and PSP started out badly, as the CPN and the PSP saw the party as a reformist, non-socialist party. After 1981 when the PPR had committed itself to extra-parliamentary protest the relations with the reforming CPN and PSP became better. In 1989 this resulted in the formation of the <a href="/wiki/GreenLeft" class="mw-redirect" title="GreenLeft">GreenLeft</a>
</p>
