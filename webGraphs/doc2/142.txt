<p>The <b>Green Party of Alabama</b><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> is a state-level political party in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. They subscribe to the 10 key values of the US Green Party. Their nominated candidate for President in 2016 was Dr. <a href="/wiki/Jill_Stein" title="Jill Stein">Jill Stein</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-al.com1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-al.com1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alaska">Alaska</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Alaska">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Alaska" title="Green Party of Alaska">Green Party of Alaska</a></div>
<p>The <b>Green Party of Alaska</b><sup id="cite_ref-GM1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GM1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> is the state party organization for <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Party (United States)">Green Party</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States of America</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-doe_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-doe-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Alaska was the first state to gain Green Party <a href="/wiki/Ballot_access" title="Ballot access">ballot access</a>, in 1990, when <a href="/wiki/Jim_Sykes" title="Jim Sykes">Jim Sykes</a> ran for governor.  Sykes had previously filed a ballot access lawsuit, citing an earlier case, <i><a href="/wiki/Joe_Vogler" title="Joe Vogler">Vogler</a> v. <a href="/wiki/Terry_Miller_(politician)" title="Terry Miller (politician)">Miller</a></i>.
</p><p>Like the <a href="/wiki/Alaska_Libertarian_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Alaska Libertarian Party">Alaska Libertarian Party</a>, the Green Party organizes local affiliate groups by regions of the state rather than election districts.  It is known for calling these groups <a href="/wiki/Bioregion" title="Bioregion">bioregions</a>.  The organized bioregions of the GPAK include the Southcentral Bioregion (Anchorage area) and the Tanana-Yukon Bioregion (the Interior, around the Tanana and <a href="/wiki/Yukon_River" title="Yukon River">Yukon River</a> areas).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arizona">Arizona</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Arizona">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arizona_Green_Party" title="Arizona Green Party">Arizona Green Party</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Arizona_Greens_ballot_status_signature_collectors_20080209.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Arizona_Greens_ballot_status_signature_collectors_20080209.jpg/220px-Arizona_Greens_ballot_status_signature_collectors_20080209.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Arizona_Greens_ballot_status_signature_collectors_20080209.jpg/330px-Arizona_Greens_ballot_status_signature_collectors_20080209.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Arizona_Greens_ballot_status_signature_collectors_20080209.jpg/440px-Arizona_Greens_ballot_status_signature_collectors_20080209.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Arizona_Greens_ballot_status_signature_collectors_20080209.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Activists of the Arizona Green Party collecting signatures for <a href="/wiki/Ballot_status" class="mw-redirect" title="Ballot status">ballot status</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Green Party of Arizona</b> (AZPG) is the affiliate of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Party (United States)">Green Party</a> in the state of <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>. It was founded by Carolyn Campbell alongside others in the 1990s. The two current co-chairs of the Arizona Green Party are Maritza Broce and Angel Torres.
</p><p>The Arizona Green Party is best known for its strong stances on environmental protection, which the party was founded upon. Apart from this, the Arizona Green Party identifies with ten key values: grassroots democracy, social justice, ecological wisdom, non-violence, decentralization, feminism and gender equality, community-based economics, respect for diversity, personal and global responsibility, and future focus and sustainability.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, The Green Party committee openly opposes the militarization of our borders and protests the idea of building a wall between <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, according to the party's official platform committee website. Arizona Green Party stand against <a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">free-trade</a> and in support of repealing the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Free_Trade_Agreement" title="North American Free Trade Agreement">North American Free Trade Agreement</a> (NAFTA), <a href="/wiki/Fast_track_(trade)" title="Fast track (trade)">Fast Track</a>, and other globalizing trade policies.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arkansas">Arkansas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Arkansas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Arkansas" title="Green Party of Arkansas">Green Party of Arkansas</a></div>
<p>The <b>Green Party of Arkansas</b> is the state party organization for <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Party (United States)">Green Party of the United States</a>.
</p><p>With the November 2008 election of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Carroll_(politician)" title="Richard Carroll (politician)">Richard Carroll</a> as representative for the 39th District (<a href="/wiki/Little_Rock,_AR" class="mw-redirect" title="Little Rock, AR">Little Rock</a>) in the <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_House_of_Representatives" title="Arkansas House of Representatives">Arkansas House of Representatives</a>, the Arkansas Green Party gained its first ever state representative in the state's history for a time was the only elected state representative of any U.S. Green Party.
</p><p>Greens achieved their first electoral victory in Arkansas in 1992 when Stephan Miller was elected Alderman for <a href="/wiki/Fayetteville,_Arkansas" title="Fayetteville, Arkansas">Fayetteville</a>, Ward 1.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He was joined on the City Council in 1996 by Randy Zurcher when he was elected to represent Fayetteville, Ward 2.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="California">California</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: California">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_California" title="Green Party of California">Green Party of California</a></div>
<p>The <b>Green Party of California</b> (<b>GPCA</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> affiliate of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party of the United States</a>. The party is a ballot-qualified in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, first established as such in 1991, using the petition method of gaining state recognition.
</p><p>As of October 20, 2014, there were 110,511 registered party members accounting for 0.62 percent of registered voters in California,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mendocino_County" class="mw-redirect" title="Mendocino County">Mendocino</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nevada_County,_California" title="Nevada County, California">Nevada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_County,_California" title="Humboldt County, California">Humboldt</a> counties have the highest per-capita number of Green Party members.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>To maintain qualified status<sup id="cite_ref-CSS1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CSS1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> in California, a party must have registered voters equal to or more than 0.33% percent of the number of voters, or by the petition method, according to  which a party must get petition signatures of 10% of registered voters in the previous gubernatorial election.<sup id="cite_ref-CSS2_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CSS2-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Colorado">Colorado</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Colorado">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Colorado" title="Green Party of Colorado">Green Party of Colorado</a></div>
<p>The <b>Green Party of Colorado</b> is the state party organization for <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party of the United States</a>.
</p><p>The Green Party of Colorado first attempted to qualify for statewide ballot status in 1994. While the party was unsuccessful in gaining ballot access, the party did qualify for Qualified Political Organization status. This made it possible to register as a Green in Colorado.
</p><p>The Green Party of Colorado qualified for statewide ballot status in July 1998 and has retained its ballot status ever since.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Connecticut">Connecticut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Connecticut">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_Green_Party" title="Connecticut Green Party">Connecticut Green Party</a></div>
<p>The <b>Connecticut Green Party (CTGP)</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a> affiliate of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party of the United States</a>. It is governed by three co-chairs, one of whom must be a woman, all of whom are elected at their Annual Meeting each May. The party is committed to grassroots democracy, social justice, non-violence and <a href="/wiki/Ecological_wisdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological wisdom">ecological wisdom</a>. Those are also the four pillars of Green politics. 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a>, one of the more well-known Green Party figures in the United States, is from <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, though he has never been a party member. For the 2006 election year, the Connecticut Green Party endorsed <a href="/wiki/Cliff_Thornton" title="Cliff Thornton">Cliff Thornton</a> to run for Governor, and Ralph Ferrucci to run for U.S. Senate. The Connecticut Greens also endorsed Daniel Sumrall and Richard Duffee to run for Congress in the 3rd and 4th districts respectively.
</p><p>In 2007 elections two party members were elected. In New Haven Allan Brison was elected <a href="/wiki/Alderman" title="Alderman">alderman</a> in the 10th Ward <sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> (386 votes to 283),<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and in Windham, party Co-chair Jean deSmet was elected <a href="/wiki/First_Selectman" class="mw-redirect" title="First Selectman">First Selectman</a>. DeSmet is the first Green Party candidate in the state to win a top municipal office. <sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="District_of_Columbia">District of Columbia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: District of Columbia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/D.C._Statehood_Green_Party" title="D.C. Statehood Green Party">D.C. Statehood Green Party</a></div>
<p>The <b>D.C. Statehood Green Party</b>, also known as the <b>D.C. Statehood Party</b>, is a left-wing political party in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> The party is the D.C. affiliate of the national <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Party (United States)">Green Party</a>, but has traditionally been involved primarily with issues related to the <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia_statehood_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="District of Columbia statehood movement">District of Columbia statehood movement</a>. Party members sometimes call it the second most popular party in the city because in the 2006 election its candidates won more total votes than the Republican candidates.<sup id="cite_ref-nbc4_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nbc4-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> As of March 31, 2016, there are 3,419 registered voters affiliated with the D.C. Statehood Green Party.<sup id="cite_ref-registeredvoters_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-registeredvoters-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> That is 0.79% of all registered voters.<sup id="cite_ref-registeredvoters_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-registeredvoters-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Florida">Florida</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Florida">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Florida" title="Green Party of Florida">Green Party of Florida</a></div>
<p>The <b>Green Party of Florida</b> is the state party organization for <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party of the United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Green Party of Florida was organized in 1992. At that time the State of Florida had a very stringent standard applied to what were considered <a href="/wiki/Minor_party" title="Minor party">minor party</a> candidates in elections. To have statewide ballot status, minor parties had to file a petition with at least 3% of all registered voters. To keep this status, they had to maintain a number of party members equal to 5% of all registered voters.
</p><p>In 1998 state law concerning access to the state ballot was eased. In February 1999 the state legislature implemented changes allowing any party organized on a state basis to field candidates in elections. This allowed the Green Party and other parties to qualify to field candidates on the ballot. The Green Party has retained its statewide ballot status ever since.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Illinois">Illinois</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Illinois">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Green_Party" title="Illinois Green Party">Illinois Green Party</a></div>
<p>The <b>Illinois Green Party</b> is a statewide <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political party</a> in <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>. The party is state affiliate of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party of the United States</a>. Its stated mission and purpose are to advance the <i>Ten Key Values of the Green Party</i> in Illinois through political means and to support individual members and the formation of Green Party locals.
</p><p>In 2006, the party ran its first statewide candidates led by <a href="/wiki/Rich_Whitney" title="Rich Whitney">Rich Whitney</a>, candidate for Illinois Governor, who received 361,336 votes for 10% of the total vote, making the Green Party one of only three legally <i>established</i>, statewide political parties in Illinois, in addition to the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_of_Illinois" title="Democratic Party of Illinois">Democratic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Republican_Party" title="Illinois Republican Party">Republican</a> parties until it lost that status in 2010.
</p><p>There are currently 12 local chapters affiliated with the party,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> as well as ten members holding elected office in the state.<sup id="cite_ref-elected_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elected-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Maine">Maine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Maine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Maine_Green_Independent_Party" title="Maine Green Independent Party">Maine Green Independent Party</a></div>
<p>The <b>Maine Green Independent Party</b> is a state-level political party affiliated with the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party of the United States</a>. It is the oldest state <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">green party</a> in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> It was founded following an informal meeting of 18 environmental advocates, including <a href="/wiki/Bowdoin_College" title="Bowdoin College">Bowdoin College</a> professor <a href="/wiki/John_Rensenbrink" title="John Rensenbrink">John Rensenbrink</a> and others in <a href="/wiki/Augusta,_Maine" title="Augusta, Maine">Augusta, Maine</a> in January 1984.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> From 1994 to 2006, the party's gubernatorial nominees received between 6% and 10% of the vote.
</p><p>On September 21, 2017, state representative <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Chapman_(politician)" title="Ralph Chapman (politician)">Ralph Chapman</a> switched party affiliation from independent to the Green Independent Party.<sup id="cite_ref-BDNM05_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BDNM05-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GPN2017_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GPN2017-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> A month later, non voting member <a href="/wiki/Henry_John_Bear" title="Henry John Bear">Henry John Bear</a> also switched his party affiliation to the Green Independent Party from the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Massachusetts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green-Rainbow_Party" title="Green-Rainbow Party">Green-Rainbow Party</a></div>
<p>The <b>Green-Rainbow Party</b> is one of twenty-six <a href="/wiki/Political_parties_and_political_designations_in_Massachusetts" title="Political parties and political designations in Massachusetts">political designations</a> officially recognized by the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Secretary_of_the_Commonwealth" title="Massachusetts Secretary of the Commonwealth">Secretary</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Massachusetts" title="Government of Massachusetts">Commonwealth of Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sos_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sos-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The party is a local <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States#State_parties" title="Green Party of the United States">state affiliate</a> for the wider <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States of America</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AO_2015-01_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AO_2015-01-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Originally the Massachusetts Green Party, it was formed in 1996 and recognized in 2000. It merged with the <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_Coalition_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Rainbow Coalition Party">Rainbow Coalition Party</a> in 2002 and rebranded as the Green-Rainbow Party.<sup id="cite_ref-Galvin_battle_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Galvin_battle-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> The party has supported candidates on the national level such as <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Cobb" title="David Cobb">David Cobb</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jill_Stein" title="Jill Stein">Jill Stein</a>. It has also run candidates and pushed for political support at the state and municipal level.  As of February 2012 the official party's membership ranks stood at roughly 5,300, which makes up about 0.12% of the Commonwealth's <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Massachusetts#Party_registration" title="Politics of Massachusetts">eligible electorate</a>; though the number of votes for party candidates are usually significantly higher attributed to independent voters.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hampshire_County,_Massachusetts" title="Hampshire County, Massachusetts">Hampshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berkshire_County,_Massachusetts" title="Berkshire County, Massachusetts">Berkshire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nantucket_County,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Nantucket County, Massachusetts">Nantucket</a> counties have the highest per-capita number of Green-Rainbow Party members in <a href="/wiki/Ratio" title="Ratio">ratio</a> to county population.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Michigan">Michigan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Michigan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Michigan" title="Green Party of Michigan">Green Party of Michigan</a></div>
<p>The <b>Green Party of Michigan</b> is a political party in <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>. It is the state affiliate of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party of the United States</a> (GPUS). The party has had ballot access in Michigan since November 2000, When their Presidential candidate, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a> captured 2.74% of the national vote and 2% in Michigan.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> In 2016, the Green Party of Michigan elected 5 officers to local governments.
</p><p>In Michigan the Green Party elected a candidate to office in its first year. That candidate was JoAnne Beemon who became the first Green elected in Michigan, when on election day 2000 she received 5,349 votes (86%) to become Drain Commissioner in <a href="/wiki/Charlevoix_County,_Michigan" title="Charlevoix County, Michigan">Charlevoix County</a>. Beemon was credited with thwarting construction of a Wal-Mart store, by formulating storm water runoff regulations stricter than the county stormwater ordinance. She informed Wal-Mart of this on February 12, 2004. Two months later in a phone call to Beemon on April 6, 2004, Wal-Mart project manager Allen Oertel acknowledged that the company altered its plan based on information from Beemon that it did not previously know of. Wal-Mart later ended the project.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>  The Michigan party is a member of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Michigan_Third_Parties_Coalition&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Michigan Third Parties Coalition (page does not exist)">Michigan Third Parties Coalition</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

The Green Party of Minnesota was organized in December 1993. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nebraska">Nebraska</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Nebraska">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nebraska_Green_Party" title="Nebraska Green Party">Nebraska Green Party</a></div>
<p>The <b>Nebraska Green Party</b> is the state party organization for <a href="/wiki/Nebraska" title="Nebraska">Nebraska</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party of the United States</a>.
</p><p>The Nebraska Green Party is a small but swiftly growing party. The party held its first convention in August 2000 at a Unitarian church in <a href="/wiki/Lincoln,_Nebraska" title="Lincoln, Nebraska">Lincoln, Nebraska</a>. In the 2004 election three Congressional candidates, Roy Guisinger, party Co-Chair Steve Larrick and Dante Salvatierra garnered a total of over 10,000 votes statewide.  The party lost its ballot access after the 2004 general election. In order to maintain status as an officially recognized party in Nebraska, Green Party candidates must garner at least five percent of the vote in federal or state electoral races. In 2004, these offices were limited to President and the House of Representatives. This led to the Green Party being recognized in the 1st District but not in the 2nd District and 3rd District. Petition drives qualified the Greens in all three districts in 2006.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_York">New York</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: New York">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_New_York" title="Green Party of New York">Green Party of New York</a></div>
<p>The <b>Green Party of New York</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Qualified_New_York_political_parties" title="Qualified New York political parties">ballot-qualified political party</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>. It was founded in 1992 and is a part of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">national Green Party</a> movement. The party regained ballot status for four years when <a href="/wiki/Howie_Hawkins" title="Howie Hawkins">Howie Hawkins</a> received over 50,000 votes in the <a href="/wiki/New_York_gubernatorial_election,_2010" title="New York gubernatorial election, 2010">2010 gubernatorial election</a> and retained it for another four years in <a href="/wiki/New_York_gubernatorial_election,_2014" title="New York gubernatorial election, 2014">the 2014 election</a>, when the party moved up to line D, the fourth line on state ballots, passing the <a href="/wiki/Working_Families_Party" title="Working Families Party">Working Families</a> and <a href="/wiki/Independence_Party_of_New_York" title="Independence Party of New York">Independence</a> parties, with 5 percent of the vote.
</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Green_Party" title="North Carolina Green Party">North Carolina Green Party</a></div>
<p>The <b>North Carolina Green Party</b> is a political party in the state of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, and the NC affiliate of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party of the United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-StateParties_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StateParties-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> It does not currently hold <a href="/wiki/Ballot_access" title="Ballot access">ballot access</a> but as of  October&#160;2015,<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> it was gathering signatures to meet state requirements for party certification.
</p><p>In 2016, the party came close to gaining statewide ballot access, closer than the other six new parties, but still fell short of getting the required amount of signatures.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> The party, in collaboration with the <a href="/wiki/Jill_Stein_presidential_campaign,_2016" title="Jill Stein presidential campaign, 2016">Stein/Baraka presidential campaign</a>, helped garner more write-in votes for <a href="/wiki/Jill_Stein" title="Jill Stein">Jill Stein</a> than any presidential write-in candidate has ever received in North Carolina.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ohio">Ohio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Ohio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Ohio" title="Green Party of Ohio">Green Party of Ohio</a></div>
<p>The Green Party of Ohio is the state party organization for <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Party (United States)">Green Party of the United States</a>. According to the current <a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_bylaws&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Green Party bylaws (page does not exist)">Green Party bylaws</a> each state county is authorized 15 <a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_Central_Committee&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Green Party Central Committee (page does not exist)">Green Party Central Committee </a> members per county, Parish or reservation. There are <a href="/w/index.php?title=88_counties_in_Ohio&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="88 counties in Ohio (page does not exist)">88 counties in Ohio</a> and 3,041 counties parishes or reservations in the United States. 
</p><p>The Green Party of Ohio was founded as the Green Party of Northeast Ohio (the Northeast Ohio Greens) in the early 1990s. The Green Party of Northeast Ohio was a recognized local of the <a href="/wiki/Greens/Green_Party_USA" title="Greens/Green Party USA">Greens/Green Party USA</a> (GPUSA), the only national Green organization at the time.
</p><p>In the mid-1990s, and leading up to the 1996 US presidential election and <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader" title="Ralph Nader">Ralph Nader</a>'s minimalist candidacy, the Greens in Ohio were caught up in the strategic debate that found its expression at the national level in the competing GPUSA/<a href="/wiki/Association_of_State_Green_Parties" title="Association of State Green Parties">Association of State Green Parties</a> (ASGP) tendencies. Some Ohio Greens were decidedly non-electoral and did not support the ASGP effort for a Nader candidacy. The Green Party of Ohio's effort to put Nader on the ballot in 1996 fell about 315 signatures short.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Oklahoma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Oklahoma" title="Green Party of Oklahoma">Green Party of Oklahoma</a></div>
<p>The <b>Green Party of <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a></b> is a <a href="/wiki/Third_party_(United_States)" title="Third party (United States)">third party</a> founded on the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Four_Pillars_of_the_Green_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Four Pillars of the Green Party (page does not exist)">Four Pillars of the Green Party</a>: ecological wisdom, social justice, grass-roots democracy and non-violence.
</p><p>The state party's original roots are not certain, but the best known history is that there were two local Green Party chapters active in the late 1990s, the Central Oklahoma Green Party (which later split into the Oklahoma County Green Party and the Cleveland County Green Party) and the Green Country Green Party (representing the Tulsa metropolitan area and Northeastern Oklahoma).
</p><p>Prior to the formation of a statewide party, Green Party members in Oklahoma (through statewide nominating conventions) sent delegates to the national Green Party nominating conventions in 1996 and 2000. Greens statewide also cooperated in the publication of The Greenleaf (a state Green Party newspaper).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oregon">Oregon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Oregon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Green_Party" title="Pacific Green Party">Pacific Green Party</a></div>
<p>The Pacific Green Party of Oregon (PGP) is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_Oregon" title="List of political parties in Oregon">political party</a> in the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>. It is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party of the United States</a>.
</p><p>The party first gained widespread public attention during <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Nader_presidential_campaign,_2000" title="Ralph Nader presidential campaign, 2000">Ralph Nader</a>'s presidential campaign in <a href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_2000" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. presidential election, 2000">2000</a>.
</p><p>Pacific Green Party candidates have won elected office mostly at the <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">local</a> level; most winners of public office in Oregon who are considered Greens have won <a href="/wiki/Nonpartisan" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonpartisan">nonpartisan</a>-ballot elections (that is, elected to positions for which no candidate is listed with any party on the <a href="/wiki/Ballot" title="Ballot">ballot</a>).
</p><p>Pacific Greens emphasize <a href="/wiki/Grassroots_democracy" title="Grassroots democracy">grassroots democracy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nonviolence" title="Nonviolence">nonviolence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Decentralization" title="Decentralization">decentralization</a> and local <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_entity" class="mw-redirect" title="Autonomous entity">autonomy</a>, in keeping with the Green parties' endorsement of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States#Ideology" title="Green Party of the United States">Ten Key Values (10KV)</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Pennsylvania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Pennsylvania" title="Green Party of Pennsylvania">Green Party of Pennsylvania</a></div>
<p>The <b>Green Party of Pennsylvania</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> state party affiliate of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Party (United States)">Green Party of the United States</a>. Since 2017 the party is again recognized as a minor political party under Pennsylvania law due to receiving the required voter turnout in the 2016 elections.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> As of early 2018, the party has at least 15 members elected to local office statewide.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhode_Island">Rhode Island</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=44" title="Edit section: Rhode Island">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Rhode_Island" title="Green Party of Rhode Island">Green Party of Rhode Island</a></div>
<p>The <b>Green Party of Rhode Island</b> (GPRI) is one of the oldest active <a href="/wiki/Green_parties" class="mw-redirect" title="Green parties">Green parties</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. The party was founded on March 6, 1992, at a meeting of 40 activists from <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>. In November 1996, GPRI was one of 12 founding parties in the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_State_Green_Parties" title="Association of State Green Parties">Association of State Green Parties</a>, renamed the Green Party of the United States in 2001. Several Rhode Island party leaders have served as officers of the national Green Party.  The party's candidates run for municipal councils in several cities and towns, such as running for Mayor of <a href="/wiki/Providence,_Rhode_Island" title="Providence, Rhode Island">Providence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island_Senate" title="Rhode Island Senate">the State Senate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island_House_of_Representatives" title="Rhode Island House of Representatives">the State House of Representatives</a>, <a href="/wiki/U.S._Congress" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Congress">U.S. Congress</a>, and for <a href="/wiki/List_of_lieutenant_governors_of_Rhode_Island" title="List of lieutenant governors of Rhode Island">Lieutenant governor</a>. The Green Party of Rhode Island has been involved in nationwide <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">Green politics</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Texas">Texas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=48" title="Edit section: Texas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Texas" title="Green Party of Texas">Green Party of Texas</a></div>
<p>The <b>Green Party of Texas</b> is the state party organization for <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party of the United States</a>. The party was founded as the electoral arm of the political movements for grassroots democracy, social justice, ecological wisdom, and peace/nonviolence. The aim of the movement is to bring change to the Government such that it is brought in line with the <a href="/wiki/Global_Greens_Charter" title="Global Greens Charter">Global Greens Charter</a>.
</p><p>After November 2016, the Texas Green Party does not have <a href="/wiki/Ballot_access" title="Ballot access">ballot access</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-TTrib_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TTrib-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> though they had it continuously since 2010, and the two cycles following its founding, 2000–2002.<sup id="cite_ref-TxObs_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TxObs-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> Greens have won local offices in Texas in the past.<sup id="cite_ref-TxObs_79-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TxObs-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Virginia">Virginia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=52" title="Edit section: Virginia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_Party_of_Virginia">Green Party of Virginia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=53" title="Edit section: Green Party of Virginia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Virginia" title="Green Party of Virginia">Green Party of Virginia</a></div>
<p>The <b>Green Party of Virginia</b> (GPVA) is a state-level <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political party</a> in <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> and the state affiliate of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Party (United States)">Green Party of the United States</a>.
</p><p>The GPVA's focuses on <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmental issues</a> and promoting candidates for local elections. The party had its first candidates running for elections in 1993, and got its first successful candidates elected to office in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Independent_Greens_of_Virginia">Independent Greens of Virginia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=54" title="Edit section: Independent Greens of Virginia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Independent_Greens_of_Virginia" title="Independent Greens of Virginia">Independent Greens of Virginia</a></div>
<p>The <b>Independent Greens of Virginia</b>, (also known as the <b>Indy Greens</b>), was the state affiliate of the <a href="/wiki/Independence_Party_of_America" title="Independence Party of America">Independence Party of America</a> in the Commonwealth of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>. It became a state party around 2003 when a faction of the <a href="/wiki/Arlington,_Virginia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arlington, Virginia">Arlington</a> local chapter of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Virginia" title="Green Party of Virginia">Green Party of Virginia</a> (GPVA) split from the main party. As of 2011, it bills itself as a "fiscally conservative, socially responsible green party", with an emphasis on rail transportation and "more candidates".<sup id="cite_ref-joinrun_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-joinrun-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> In support of wider ballot participation, it endorses many independent candidates who are not affiliated with the party.
</p>

As of 2017, GPWA has 9 affiliated local parties<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> with at-large members and numerous locals in formation throughout the state. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="West_Virginia">West Virginia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=56" title="Edit section: West Virginia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mountain_Party" title="Mountain Party">Mountain Party</a></div>
<p>The <b>Mountain Party</b> is a state-level political party in <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WVSOS1_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WVSOS1-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> It is the <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a> affiliate of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Party (United States)">United States Green Party</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> The party is headed by <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Johnson_(politician)" title="Jesse Johnson (politician)">Jesse Johnson</a>, State Chairman.
</p><p>It is a <a href="/wiki/Progressivism" title="Progressivism">progressive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmentalist" title="Environmentalist">environmentalist</a> party whose party platform primarily focuses on "Grassroots Democracy", "Social Justice &amp; Equal Opportunity", "Ecological Wisdom" and "Non-Violence".<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_state_Green_Parties_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=57" title="Edit section: Wisconsin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin_Green_Party" title="Wisconsin Green Party">Wisconsin Green Party</a></div>
<p>The <b>Wisconsin Green Party</b> (WIGP) is one of five recognized political parties in the state of <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> and is an active member of the <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_the_United_States" title="Green Party of the United States">Green Party of the United States</a>.
</p><p>The Wisconsin Green Party emerged in the late 1980s when several independent local Green groups combined.  <a href="/wiki/Walter_Bresette" title="Walter Bresette">Walter Bresette</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frank_Koehn" title="Frank Koehn">Frank Koehn</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Superior" title="Lake Superior">Lake Superior</a> Greens were instrumental figures in the early years of the party's development. Koehn's election to the <a href="/wiki/Bayfield_County,_Wisconsin" title="Bayfield County, Wisconsin">Bayfield County</a> board in 1986 was the first time a Green Party candidate had ever been elected to an office in the United States. Dennis Boyer, Richard Latker, Joyce Melville and others established a large chapter in Madison that brought together veteran activists (many of them former members of the <a href="/wiki/Labor-Farm_Party_of_Wisconsin" title="Labor-Farm Party of Wisconsin">Labor-Farm Party</a>, which disintegrated in 1987 after Greens and Marxists in the party failed to agree on a platform) and student activists affiliated with the UW-Madison Greens.<sup id="cite_ref-WIGP_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WIGP-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
