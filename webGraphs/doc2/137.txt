<p><b>The Greens – The Green Alternative</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Die Grünen – Die Grüne Alternative</i> or <i>Die Grünen</i>, also called the <b>Austrian Green Party</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_parties_in_Austria" title="List of political parties in Austria">political party in Austria</a>.
</p><p>The party was formed in 1986 under the name <b>Grüne Alternative</b>, following the merger of the more conservative Green party <i>Vereinte Grüne Österreichs</i> (United Greens of Austria VGÖ, founded 1982) and the more progressive party <i>Alternative Liste Österreichs</i> (Alternative List Austria, ALÖ, founded 1982). Since 1993, the party has carried the official name <i>Die Grünen – Die Grüne Alternative (Grüne)</i>, but refers to itself in English as "Austrian Greens". There are still differences between the former members of the old Alternative and VGÖ factions within the party, reflected in the differing approaches of the national and state parties.
</p><p>Apart from <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> issues such as <a href="/wiki/Environmental_protection" title="Environmental protection">environmental protection</a>, the Greens also campaign for the rights of <a href="/wiki/Minority_group" title="Minority group">minorities</a> and advocate a socio-ecological (<i>ökosozial</i>) <a href="/wiki/Tax_reform" title="Tax reform">tax reform</a>.  Their basic values according to their charter in 2001 are: "<a href="/wiki/Direct_democracy" title="Direct democracy">direct democracy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nonviolence" title="Nonviolence">nonviolence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solidarity" title="Solidarity">solidarity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Self-determination" title="Self-determination">self-determination</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The party is a member of the <a href="/wiki/European_Green_Party" title="European Green Party">European Green Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/Global_Greens" title="Global Greens">Global Greens</a>.
</p><p>The Greens currently have no seats in the  National Council.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Over the past decade, it had polled 11.0, 10.4, and 12.4 percent in federal elections.  However, in 2017, the party was beset with internal difficulties including the defection of its entire youth wing. In the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_legislative_election,_2017" title="Austrian legislative election, 2017">2017 general election</a>, the party suffered a disastrous defeat, losing all 24 of its seats by failing to clear the 4% hurdle to get representation in parliament. The Green Party spokesperson from Lower Austria, Helga Krismer-Huber described it as a 'catastrophe'.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The party has 2 seats but no parliamentary group  in the less powerful Federal Council.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_%E2%80%93_The_Green_Alternative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the Austrian <a href="/wiki/Green_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Green movement">Green movement</a> began in 1978 with the successful campaign to prevent the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> plant in <a href="/wiki/Zwentendorf" title="Zwentendorf">Zwentendorf</a> (which had been favoured by <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Kreisky" title="Bruno Kreisky">Bruno Kreisky</a>'s government), the Green Party was born in 1984 during the <a href="/wiki/Occupation_of_the_Hainburger_Au" title="Occupation of the Hainburger Au">sit-in protests</a> which prevented the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> power plant at <a href="/wiki/Hainburg_an_der_Donau" title="Hainburg an der Donau">Hainburg</a> from being built.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Federal_level">Federal level</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_%E2%80%93_The_Green_Alternative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Federal level">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_legislative_election,_1986" title="Austrian legislative election, 1986">1986 parliamentary elections</a> the Green Party started off with 4.82% of all votes cast and entered parliament with eight <a href="/wiki/National_Council_of_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="National Council of Austria">National Council</a> mandates.  In the early elections to National Council in <a href="/wiki/Austria_legislative_election,_2002" class="mw-redirect" title="Austria legislative election, 2002">2002</a>, the Green Party nationwide received 9.47% of votes, and won 17 mandates to the National Council.  At that time, it was the highest number of votes garnered by any European Green party.
</p><p>When the Greens took their seats in parliament for the first time, they chose to appear somewhat unconventional.  They initially refused to adapt their behaviour to that of the other parties; an example of this is their refusal to elect a chairperson (<i>Klubobmann/Klubobfrau</i>) and designated a puppet made out of straw instead. Delegates would appear in parliament dressed in casual wear such as jeans and trainers. Worldwide attention was drawn when the Green delegate Andreas Wabl hoisted a <a href="/wiki/Swastika" title="Swastika">swastika</a> flag on the speakers podium in the Austrian parliament, protesting against then <a href="/wiki/President_of_Austria" title="President of Austria">Federal President</a> <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Waldheim" title="Kurt Waldheim">Kurt Waldheim</a>.
</p><p>After the national election in <a href="/wiki/Austria_legislative_election,_2002" class="mw-redirect" title="Austria legislative election, 2002">2002</a>, the Greens entered into preliminary negotiations about a possible coalition government with the conservative ÖVP.  During negotiations, party leadership was accused of internally black-mailing skeptical members.  Negotiations between the two parties were subsequently called off, after the results with the ÖVP were not sufficient.  The Green youth organisation <i>Grünalternative Jugend</i> (Green Alternative Youth or GAJ) briefly occupied the rooms of the Green parliamentary club in the Austrian parliament building in protest.
</p><p>In 2003 three Green federal counsellors formed their own club in the Upper House <a href="/wiki/Federal_Council_of_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Council of Austria">Federal Council</a> (<i>Bundesrat</i>) of Parliament.
</p><p>After the 2006 elections the Greens gained four seats and ended up with 21 seats and became the third largest party in Parliament, however did not have enough mandates to form a coalition government with either the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_People%27s_Party" title="Austrian People&#39;s Party">Austrian People's Party</a> (ÖVP) or <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Austria" title="Social Democratic Party of Austria">Social Democratic Party</a> (SPÖ) and became the largest opposition party, while the SPÖ and ÖVP formed a <a href="/wiki/Grand_coalition" title="Grand coalition">grand coalition</a> government.
</p><p>As of 2018, the Greens have no seats in the <a href="/wiki/National_Council_(Austria)" title="National Council (Austria)">Nationalrat</a> or official group status in the <a href="/wiki/Bundesrat_(Austria)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bundesrat (Austria)">Bundesrat</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="State_level">State level</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_%E2%80%93_The_Green_Alternative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: State level">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Green party also entered the parliaments or assemblies (<i><a href="/wiki/Landtag" title="Landtag">Landtag</a></i>) of <a href="/wiki/States_of_Austria" title="States of Austria">Austrian states</a> and communal governments. Following is an analysis of the party on the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Austria" title="States of Austria">state</a> (<i>Länder</i>) level:
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Burgenland">Burgenland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_%E2%80%93_The_Green_Alternative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Burgenland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Burgenland" title="Burgenland">Burgenland</a> Greens were able to take their seats in the <i>Landtag</i> (state assembly) for the first time in 2000. The party received 5.49% of the tally, which meant two mandates. In the state elections in 2005 these two seats were reaffirmed with 5.21% received of all votes cast.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Carinthia">Carinthia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_%E2%80%93_The_Green_Alternative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Carinthia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the southernmost state of <a href="/wiki/Carinthia_(state)" class="mw-redirect" title="Carinthia (state)">Carinthia</a>, different Green parties ran state elections: the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=KEL/AL&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="KEL/AL (page does not exist)">KEL/AL</a></i> in 1984, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anderes_K%C3%A4rnten&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Anderes Kärnten (page does not exist)">Anderes Kärnten</a></i> in 1989 and 1994, and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Demokratie_99&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Demokratie 99 (page does not exist)">Demokratie 99</a></i> in 1999. These parties were however never able to enter the <i>Landtag</i> (state assembly) since the Carinthian voting system requires a party to win a direct mandate in one of the four regional election districts, which means effectively a 10%-<a href="/wiki/Election_threshold" title="Election threshold">threshold</a> in order to enter. <br />
Only in 2004 were the Carinthian Greens finally able to take their seats in the state assembly, where they are represented by the <a href="/wiki/Cabaret" title="Cabaret">cabaret</a> player <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rolf_Holub&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rolf Holub (page does not exist)">Rolf Holub</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbara_Lesjak&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Barbara Lesjak (page does not exist)">Barbara Lesjak</a>. On a regional level, for example in the state capital <a href="/wiki/Klagenfurt" title="Klagenfurt">Klagenfurt</a>, the Carinthian Greens have already played a political role for a longer time. In the Klagenfurt <a href="/wiki/City_council" title="City council">city council</a> the Greens are represented by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrea_Wulz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Andrea Wulz (page does not exist)">Andrea Wulz</a>, Matthias Koechl, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Angelika_Hoedl&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Angelika Hoedl (page does not exist)">Angelika Hoedl</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Reinhold_Gasper&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Reinhold Gasper (page does not exist)">Reinhold Gasper</a>. Since the local election in 2003 the Klagenfurt Greens were able to take one of nine seats in the proportional city-government, Andrea Wulz is the town councillor for issues relating to women, family matters and social <a href="/wiki/Housing_project" class="mw-redirect" title="Housing project">housing projects</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lower_Austria">Lower Austria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_%E2%80%93_The_Green_Alternative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lower Austria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1998 the <a href="/wiki/Lower_Austria" title="Lower Austria">Lower Austrian</a> Greens were represented with two delegates in the state assembly. In the state elections in 2003 the Greens received 7.22% and thus won four mandates, which meant they had official club status in the assembly. With <a href="/wiki/Madeleine_Petrovic" title="Madeleine Petrovic">Madeleine Petrovic</a> the Lower Austrian Greens have a former federal spokeswoman and one of the most outspoken animal activists of Austria as their leader (<i>Klubobfrau</i>). In 2005 the Lower Austrian Greens managed to win and take their seats in 100 municipal assemblies and as of 2005 had four vice-mayors. Their managing director in Lower Austria is Thomas Huber.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Salzburg">Salzburg</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_%E2%80%93_The_Green_Alternative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Salzburg">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>After the state elections in 1989 the <a href="/wiki/Salzburg_(state)" title="Salzburg (state)">Salzburg</a> Greens had two mandates in the Salzburg state assembly, in 1994 three and in 1999 again two. Under the leadership of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyriak_Schwaighofer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cyriak Schwaighofer (page does not exist)">Cyriak Schwaighofer</a> the Greens performed under their expectations in the 2004 state elections and could not achieve the desired club status of at least three mandates. As voter-current analyses showed, the small increases in votes were largely due to former voters of the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Forum" title="Liberal Forum">Liberal Forum</a> (LiF), which did not run in the Salzburg elections. In March 2009 they were down from 8% to 7.3%, keeping their two seats in the Parliament of <a href="/wiki/Salzburgerland" class="mw-redirect" title="Salzburgerland">Salzburgerland</a>.
</p><p>The Bürgerliste (Citizen List) is the common platform of the Greens of the city of <a href="/wiki/Salzburg" title="Salzburg">Salzburg</a>. Like many other autonomous municipal groups it carries its own name.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Styria">Styria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_%E2%80%93_The_Green_Alternative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Styria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2005, the <a href="/wiki/Styria" title="Styria">Styrian</a> Greens had three delegates sitting in the state assembly, Chairperson Ingrid Lechner Sonnek, Edith Zitz and Peter Hagenauer. There are two independent Greens parties: on the one hand the state party, on the other hand there is the <i>Die Grünen - Alternative Liste Graz</i> party for the state capital <a href="/wiki/Graz" title="Graz">Graz</a>. In the Graz city-council the Greens are represented by Sigi Binder, Lisa Rücker, Hermann Candussi and Christina Jahn.
</p><p>Styria has the largest Austrian Green youth organization in Austria, called <i>Grüne Jugend Steiermark</i> (Green Youth Styria). Beside the Green Youth Styria there also exists Austria's first Green student's organization, the <i>ECO Students</i>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tyrol">Tyrol</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_%E2%80%93_The_Green_Alternative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Tyrol">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Tyrol_(state)" title="Tyrol (state)">Tyrol</a> the Greens (official name: <i>Die Grünen – die Grüne Alternative Tirol</i>) were able to win seats and placed in 1994 <a href="/wiki/Eva_Lichtenberger" title="Eva Lichtenberger">Eva Lichtenberger</a> as Austria's first Green state <a href="/wiki/Councillor" title="Councillor">councillor</a> in a local government, responsible for environmental affairs.
</p><p>The 2003 Tyrolean <i><a href="/wiki/Landtag" title="Landtag">Landtag</a></i> (state assembly) elections were the best ever for the Austrian Greens, winning 15.59% of all votes cast. In the capital city of <a href="/wiki/Innsbruck" title="Innsbruck">Innsbruck</a> the Greens reached approximately 27% of the vote. The Tyrolean election result also meant that the Greens could for the first time in history nominate a member to the Upper House of Parliament. Since 2003 the Green delegate to the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Council_of_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Council of Austria">Federal Council</a> (<i>Bundesrat</i>) of Parliament is <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eva_Konrad&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eva Konrad (page does not exist)">Eva Konrad</a>, former chairlady of the Austrian National Union of Students (<i>Österreichische HochschülerInnenschaft</i>) of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Innsbruck" title="University of Innsbruck">University of Innsbruck</a>.
</p><p>The communal elections of 2004 brought a doubling of the mandates for the Tyrolean Greens. City elections in <a href="/wiki/Innsbruck" title="Innsbruck">Innsbruck</a> in 2006 were a success for the Greens and they now have 8 of the 40 seats in the parliament of <a href="/wiki/Innsbruck" title="Innsbruck">Innsbruck</a>.
</p><p>In the elections to the European parliament the Tyrolean Greens obtained 17.32%, their best result until then. Eva Lichtenberger subsequently changed her position to become a <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">Member of the European Parliament</a> (MEP). The results in Innsbruck were particularly good: there the Green party received 28.28%, which made it the strongest party, even before the Christian-democratic ÖVP and the social-democratic SPÖ. The Greens were able to score on a number of issues that they have been fighting for years. Besides the social topics above all the problems of transit traffic over the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> was important.
</p><p>The Tyrolean Greens have experts on traffic issues with MEP Eva Lichtenberger, the national speaker and club chairperson <a href="/w/index.php?title=Georg_Willi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Georg Willi (page does not exist)">Georg Willi</a> and the speaker of group of regional of Innsbruck Gerhard Fritz. The issue of transit traffic through the Tyrol is of great importance, because the state is troubled by the massive transit traffic between Germany and Italy over the <a href="/wiki/Brenner_Pass" title="Brenner Pass">Brenner Pass</a>. Since the Tyrol sits right in between Germany and Italy, the bulk of the commercial traffic passes through there. This heavy-duty traffic has devastating effects on the fragile alpine environment and decreases the quality of life for the inhabitants. Since the entry to the European Union, Austria had to give up any quota limitations on how much international traffic coming from EU-countries is allowed to pass through its territory.
</p><p>The Tyrolean Greens accused to federal government of not having pushed for a better deal with the European Union concerning transit-traffic and in effect abandoning the concerns of the citizens. They also heavily criticised the government's failure to negotiate a follow-up of the 1994 transit-treaty signed with the EU. Apart from the Greens, various anti-transit <a href="/w/index.php?title=Civic_movement&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Civic movement (page does not exist)">civic movements</a> have formed to protest against the environmental damages caused by the traffic.
</p><p>Sitting in the National Council is <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kurt_Gr%C3%BCnewald&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kurt Grünewald (page does not exist)">Kurt Grünewald</a>, a Tyrolean member of parliament, as well as the former leader of the Greens <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Van_der_Bellen" title="Alexander Van der Bellen">Alexander Van der Bellen</a>, who has Tyrolean roots (he spent a part of his youth there and went to high school in Innsbruck).

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Upper_Austria">Upper Austria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_%E2%80%93_The_Green_Alternative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Upper Austria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1997 the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Austria" title="Upper Austria">Upper Austrian</a> Greens successfully entered the Upper Austrian <i>Landtag</i> (state assembly) for the first time. After the state elections in 2003 (state elections in Upper Austria are held every six years, not five like in the other states), the Greens were able to win even further seats. The campaign was already aimed at gaining ministerial seats in the state government. Since the conservative Christian-democratic ÖVP was the strongest party, this would have meant for the Greens to enter into a coalition government with them (the so-called "<i>Schwarz-Grün</i>" [Black-Green] coalition, named after the party-colours). This new political constellation was quite controversial amongst party members on both sides. In the Green party, the leader <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rudi_Anschober&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rudi Anschober (page does not exist)">Rudi Anschober</a> was able to convince party members and after some dealing became state councillor for environmental affairs. The Greens of the state capital of <a href="/wiki/Linz" title="Linz">Linz</a> under the leadership of city councillor <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Himmelbauer" title="Jürgen Himmelbauer">Jürgen Himmelbauer</a> were most against this black-green project.
</p><p>On the national level, the Upper Austrian Greens were able to nominate and send to the parliamentary Upper House <a href="/wiki/Federal_Council_of_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Council of Austria">Federal Council</a> (<i>Bundesrat</i>) councillor <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruperta_Lichtenecker&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ruperta Lichtenecker (page does not exist)">Ruperta Lichtenecker</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Vorarlberg">Vorarlberg</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_%E2%80%93_The_Green_Alternative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Vorarlberg">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Vorarlberg" title="Vorarlberg">Vorarlberg</a> Greens were the first to ever win mandates in an Austrian state assembly election. Already in 1984 they were able to win 13% of the votes in the Vorarlberg state assembly elections, which for that time was an absolute sensation. The charismatic alpine farmer <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kaspanaze_Simma&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kaspanaze Simma (page does not exist)">Kaspanaze Simma</a> from <a href="/wiki/Bregenzerwald" class="mw-redirect" title="Bregenzerwald">Bregenzerwald</a> was the leading candidate, it was mainly due to his efforts why the party was so instantly successful.  Because of their strength, the Greens were allowed to form their own parliamentary fraction (<i>Klub</i>), which caused some logistical problems as the newly constructed <i>Landtag</i> building in 1981 only provided space for the traditional three parties (ÖVP, SPÖ, FPÖ), not four.  Since the traditional <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">organic farming</a> sector is important in the western Austrian regions, the Greens were able to gain support.
</p><p>In the following years the Greens were able to consolidate their position by gaining seats on the communal and municipal level.  Occasionally they lost their official club status in the state assembly, when they fared poorly from 1999 to 2004.  In 2006 the speaker of the Vorarlberg Greens was <a href="/w/index.php?title=Johannes_Rauch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Johannes Rauch (page does not exist)">Johannes Rauch</a>.

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Vienna">Vienna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_%E2%80%93_The_Green_Alternative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Vienna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Viennese</a> Greens started nominating candidates in the Vienna <i>Gemeinderat</i> (municipal council or state assembly) in 1983 and were able to enter in 1991. Over the years they have been able to continually gather support. A lot of support has been coming from former <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Forum" title="Liberal Forum">Liberal Forum</a> voters, after the liberals failed to enter any legislature. The traditional strongholds in Vienna for the Greens are the districts of <a href="/wiki/Neubau" title="Neubau">Neubau</a> (2005: 43.26%), <a href="/wiki/Josefstadt" title="Josefstadt">Josefstadt</a> (32.26%), <a href="/wiki/Alsergrund" title="Alsergrund">Alsergrund</a> (29.43%), <a href="/wiki/Mariahilf" title="Mariahilf">Mariahilf</a> (28.97%) and <a href="/wiki/Wieden" title="Wieden">Wieden</a> (25.14%).
</p><p>In the 2001 <i>Gemeinderat</i> elections, the Greens were able to win the majority of a district for the first time. In the district of <a href="/wiki/Neubau" title="Neubau">Neubau</a> they won 32.55% and were able to nominate the <i>Bezirksvorsteher</i> (mayor of the district). The results of 2001 also allowed the Viennese Greens to nominate <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stefan_Schennach&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stefan Schennach (page does not exist)">Stefan Schennach</a> as federal councilor to the Upper House of Parliament (<i><a href="/wiki/Federal_Council_of_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Council of Austria">Bundesrat</a></i>). But despite the strong gains, the Greens were not able to enter into a coalition government with the SPÖ, since the social-democrats were able to win an absolute majority.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament_election,_2004" title="European Parliament election, 2004">European Parliament election, 2004</a> were the best for the Viennese Greens so far. From the total tally, they received 22%, which put them ahead of the Christian-democratic ÖVP and placed them on second position behind the SPÖ (37.7%). In Neubau the Greens received 41%. They were also able to win first place in the districts of Wieden, Mariahilf, Josefstadt and Alsergrund.
</p><p>In the 2005 <i>Gemeinderat</i> elections, the Greens were able to win votes, but missed their target of becoming the second most powerful party and ended up on fourth place, right behind the right-wing <a href="/wiki/Freedom_Party_of_Austria" title="Freedom Party of Austria">Freedom Party</a> (FPÖ). Because of the different weighing by districts, the Greens received 14 mandates, one more than the FPÖ. They were also able to place another city-councillor. In the districts, the party was able to consolidate their holding on Neubau, as well as win the majority of votes in Josefstadt. With that, the Greens were able to nominate a second Green district-mayor. The second place was won in the districts of <a href="/wiki/Leopoldstadt" title="Leopoldstadt">Leopoldstadt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Margareten" title="Margareten">Margareten</a>, Mariahilf, <a href="/wiki/Rudolfsheim-F%C3%BCnfhaus" title="Rudolfsheim-Fünfhaus">Rudolfsheim-Fünfhaus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alsergrund" title="Alsergrund">Alsergrund</a>.
</p><p>The Green delegates to the Viennese <i>Gemeinderat</i> or <i>Landtag</i> as of 2006 were: <a href="/wiki/Maria_Vassilakou" title="Maria Vassilakou">Maria Vassilakou</a> (club-chairlady [<i>Klubobfrau</i>]), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waltraut_Antonov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Waltraut Antonov (page does not exist)">Waltraut Antonov</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heidi_Cammerlander&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Heidi Cammerlander (page does not exist)">Heidi Cammerlander</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christoph_Chorherr" title="Christoph Chorherr">Christoph Chorherr</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabine_Gretner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sabine Gretner (page does not exist)">Sabine Gretner</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Susanne_Jerusalem&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Susanne Jerusalem (page does not exist)">Susanne Jerusalem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alev_Korun" title="Alev Korun">Alev Korun</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=R%C3%BCdiger_Maresch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rüdiger Maresch (page does not exist)">Rüdiger Maresch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martin_Margulies" title="Martin Margulies">Martin Margulies</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sigrid_Pilz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sigrid Pilz (page does not exist)">Sigrid Pilz</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ingrid_Puller&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ingrid Puller (page does not exist)">Ingrid Puller</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marie_Ringler&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Marie Ringler (page does not exist)">Marie Ringler</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marco_Schreuder&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Marco Schreuder (page does not exist)">Marco Schreuder</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Claudia_Sommer-Smolik&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Claudia Sommer-Smolik (page does not exist)">Claudia Sommer-Smolik</a>. The two city-councillors are <a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Ellensohn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="David Ellensohn (page does not exist)">David Ellensohn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monika_Vana" title="Monika Vana">Monika Vana</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Viennese_state_election,_2010" title="Viennese state election, 2010">2010 results</a> meant that the SPÖ was unable to hold the majority of seats in the Vienna city council and therefore had to rule together with the Greens performing for the first time as coalition partner. The current vice-governor/vice-mayor of Vienna is Maria Vassilakou.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisation">Organisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_%E2%80%93_The_Green_Alternative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Organisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2004 the Greens had about 3,000 members nationwide, although at present there are no uniform regulations for membership. Apart from the members, the Greens rely on a large number of volunteers. The party used to function on the principles of <a href="/wiki/Grassroots_democracy" title="Grassroots democracy">grassroots democracy</a> (<i>Basisdemokratie</i>) and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotation_principle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rotation principle (page does not exist)">rotation principle</a> (<i>Rotationsprinzip</i>), but this was stopped in the course of the time. The last basic-democratic element is the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urabstimmung&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Urabstimmung (page does not exist)">Urabstimmung</a></i>, which is a vote on any issue that can be initiated with the petition of at least 100 members. As of 2003 however, no such vote has taken place.
</p><p>The highest body is the <i>Bundeskongress</i> (Federal Congress), which convenes at least once a year. All state organisations send delegates, also the immigrants-organisation is allowed to send delegates as "the tenth <a href="/wiki/States_of_Austria" title="States of Austria">Austrian state</a>". The Federal Congress decides the electoral lists for the <a href="/wiki/National_Council_of_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="National Council of Austria">National Council</a> elections or elections to the <a href="/wiki/European_parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="European parliament">European parliament</a>. The congress also elects the federal spokesperson (<i>BundesprecherIn</i>). The congress also decides the party program and sets the party guidelines.
</p><p>The <i>Bundesvorstand</i> (Federal <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Trustees" class="mw-redirect" title="Board of Trustees">Board of Trustees</a>) has in the last few years developed itself into the actual decision-making centre. It meets at least once a week, mostly on Tuesdays, and determines the guidelines of the daily politics. The board also has decides over the party finances.
The extended federal board of trustees (<i>Erweiterter Bundesvorstand</i>) consists of a smaller number of delegates from each state and meets at least once a month. It takes care of the implementation of the party-guidelines, which were set by the party congress. It also chooses the representatives of the party spokesperson.
</p><p>The state organisations (<i>Landesorganisationen</i>) are for their most part organised similarly: There are state meetings, which sometimes convene as a members meeting or a delegates meeting. Similar to the Federal Board of Trustees, there are the State Board of Trustees (<i>Landesvorstände</i>). The party charter also allows for each state group to hold a vote on basic issues as well that affect the whole party.
</p><p>Independently in the <a href="/wiki/National_Council_of_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="National Council of Austria">National Council</a> there also exists a Green National Council Club (faction), which can independently specify its guidelines. In the last years however an increasing fusion of the work between party and its club was noticeable. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Michaela_Sburny&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Michaela Sburny (page does not exist)">Michaela Sburny</a>, successor of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Franz_Raft&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Franz Raft (page does not exist)">Franz Raft</a> since June 2004 as federal executive manager of the Greens, was allowed to keep her National Council mandate. This means she is allowed to hold two offices at the same time, something that was frowned upon by the Greens previously.
</p><p>There are different Green or Greenish organisations within the party and associated with it. These include:
</p>
<ul><li>The <i>Grünen Andersrum</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Gay" title="Gay">gay</a>-, <a href="/wiki/Lesbian" title="Lesbian">lesbian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transgender" title="Transgender">transgender</a> organisation, which is organised differently from state to state, and exists in all states except Vorarlberg and Burgenland. In Vienna, the <i>Grünen Andersrum</i> are a part of the party itself.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gr%C3%BCnen_SeniorInnen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Grünen SeniorInnen (page does not exist)">Grünen SeniorInnen</a></i> (DGS) is the organisation for <a href="/wiki/Senior_citizen" class="mw-redirect" title="Senior citizen">senior citizens</a>. It was founded on March 9, 2001 in Vienna. The DGS fights for a policy more friendly to senior citizens and their right to lead an active, fulfilling and self-determined life.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Initiative_Gr%C3%BCne_MigrantInnen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Initiative Grüne MigrantInnen (page does not exist)">Initiative Grüne MigrantInnen</a></i> (IGM) is the Green group for <a href="/wiki/Immigrant" class="mw-redirect" title="Immigrant">immigrants</a> in Austria. Their demands are a facilitation of <a href="/wiki/Racial_integration" title="Racial integration">integration</a> into life in Austria, <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil rights">equal rights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Equal_opportunity" title="Equal opportunity">equal opportunities</a>, fight against <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a> and other issues concerning migrants.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gr%C3%BCne_und_Alternative_Studierende&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Grüne und Alternative Studierende (page does not exist)">Grüne und Alternative Studierende</a></i> (GRAS) is a separate party which candidates in the elections for the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_National_Union_of_Students" class="mw-redirect" title="Austrian National Union of Students">Austrian National Union of Students</a> (<i>Österreichische HochschülerInnenschaft</i> – ÖH). There they are the biggest faction, together with the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Students_of_Austria" title="Socialist Students of Austria">Socialist Students of Austria</a> (<i>Verband Sozialistischer StudentInnen Österreichs</i> – VSStÖ) they form the executive committee of the Austrian National Union for Students.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gr%C3%BCnalternative_Jugend&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Grünalternative Jugend (page does not exist)">Grünalternative Jugend</a></i> (GAJ) is the youth organisation of the Green party. The GAJ existed since the 1990s. It is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Federation_of_Young_European_Greens" title="Federation of Young European Greens">Federation of Young European Greens</a> (FYEG). The GAJ sees itself rather as extreme left. The organisation is subdivided into smaller groups for each state.</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gr%C3%BCne_Frauenorganisation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Grüne Frauenorganisation (page does not exist)">Grüne Frauenorganisation</a></i> is the organisation for women. As of 2005, it does not exist yet in every state.</li>
<li><i>ECO Students</i> is a Green student's organisation, which currently only exists in <a href="/wiki/Styria" title="Styria">Styria</a>.</li>
<li>The <i>Grüne Wirtschaft</i> is the Green economic organisation and runs in the elections for the Economic Parliament of the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Federal_Economic_Chamber" class="mw-redirect" title="Austrian Federal Economic Chamber">Austrian Federal Economic Chamber</a> (<i>Wirtschaftskammer Österreich</i> – WKÖ).</li>
<li>The <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alternative_und_Unabh%C3%A4ngige_GewerkschafterInnen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Alternative und Unabhängige GewerkschafterInnen (page does not exist)">Alternative und Unabhängige GewerkschafterInnen</a></i> (AUGE/UG) is the Green <a href="/wiki/Labour_union" class="mw-redirect" title="Labour union">labour union</a>. It runs in the elections for the labour parliament of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Austrian_Labour_Chamber&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Austrian Labour Chamber (page does not exist)">Austrian Labour Chamber</a> (<i>Arbeiterkammer</i> – AK).</li></ul>
<p>The education and training of new Green politicians is done by the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gr%C3%BCne_Bildungswerkstatt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Grüne Bildungswerkstatt (page does not exist)">Grüne Bildungswerkstatt</a></i>, which is an independent <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_association" title="Voluntary association">voluntary association</a>. The <i>Grüne Bildungswerkstatt</i> is financed by the republic, as regulated by Austrian law for the equal treatment of all parliamentary parties.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="President">President</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_%E2%80%93_The_Green_Alternative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: President">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_presidential_election,_2016" title="Austrian presidential election, 2016">2016 Austrian presidential election</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Van_der_Bellen" title="Alexander Van der Bellen">Alexander Van der Bellen</a> won the election with 50.35% of the votes and defeated <a href="/wiki/Norbert_Hofer" title="Norbert Hofer">Norbert Hofer</a> the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_Party_of_Austria" title="Freedom Party of Austria">Freedom Party of Austria</a> politician who received 49.65% of the vote.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Van der Bellen will become the first president from the Greens. On 1 July, the Constitutional Court overturned the result of the election and ordered a re-do because of irregularities during the counting process. On 4 December 2016 Van der Bellen won the re-run of the second round with 53.79% of the votes to Hofer's 46.21%.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prominent_members">Prominent members</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Greens_%E2%80%93_The_Green_Alternative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Prominent members">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Among the most notable founding members and mentors are or were Professor <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Tollmann" title="Alexander Tollmann">Alexander Tollmann</a>, the painter <a href="/wiki/Friedensreich_Hundertwasser" title="Friedensreich Hundertwasser">Friedensreich Hundertwasser</a>, the late actor <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Fux" title="Herbert Fux">Herbert Fux</a>, the mayor of <a href="/wiki/Steyregg" title="Steyregg">Steyregg</a> <a href="/wiki/Josef_Buchner" title="Josef Buchner">Josef Buchner</a> (the first Green mayor in Austria – in 1987 excluded from the Green parliamentary club), <a href="/wiki/Freda_Meissner-Blau" title="Freda Meissner-Blau">Freda Meissner-Blau</a> and <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCnther_Nenning" title="Günther Nenning">Günther Nenning</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Nobel_prize" class="mw-redirect" title="Nobel prize">Nobel prize</a> laureate <a href="/wiki/Konrad_Lorenz" title="Konrad Lorenz">Konrad Lorenz</a> supporting the 1984 protests at Hainburg.
