<p><b>Olive</b> is a dark yellowish-green color,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> like that of unripe or green <a href="/wiki/Olive" title="Olive">olives</a>.
</p><p>As a color word in the English language, it appears in late <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a>. Shaded toward gray, it becomes olive drab.
</p>

<p><b>Olive drab</b> is variously described as a "dull olive-green colour" (<i>Oxford English Dictionary</i>);<sup id="cite_ref-auto_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> "a shade of greenish-brown" (<i>Webster's New World Dictionary</i>);<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> "a dark gray-green" (<i>MacMillan English dictionary</i>); "a grayish olive to dark olive brown or olive gray" (<i>American Heritage Dictionary</i>);<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> or "A dull but fairly strong gray-green color" (<i>Collins English Dictionary</i>). It was widely used as a camouflage color for uniforms and equipment in the armed forces, particularly by the U.S. Army during the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>.
</p><p>The first recorded use of <i>olive drab</i> as a color name in English was in 1892.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Drab_(color)" title="Drab (color)">Drab</a> is an older color name, from the middle of the 16th century. It refers to a dull light brown color, the color of cloth made from undyed homespun wool. It took its name from the old French word for cloth, <i>drap</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Olive drab was the color of the standard fighting uniform for U.S. <a href="/wiki/GI_(military)" class="mw-redirect" title="GI (military)">GIs</a> and military vehicles during World War II. U.S. soldiers often referred to their uniforms as "OD's" due to the color. The color used at the beginning of the war by the U.S. Army was officially called <i>Olive Drab #3</i>, which was replaced by <i>Olive Drab #7</i> (hex code #3C341F<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>) by 1944, and which was again replaced by <i>Olive Green 107</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/OG-107" title="OG-107">OG-107</a></i> in 1952,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and continued as the official uniform color for combat fatigues through the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> until replaced in 1981 by <a href="/wiki/M81_woodland" class="mw-redirect" title="M81 woodland">M81 woodland</a> camo fatigues as the primary U.S. uniform pattern (based on the <a href="/wiki/ERDL_pattern" title="ERDL pattern">ERDL pattern</a> used by some soldiers in Vietnam), which retained olive drab as one of the color swatches in the pattern.
</p><p>As a solid color, it is not as effective for camouflage as multiple-color camo schemes (e.g., U.S. <a href="/wiki/Army_Combat_Uniform" title="Army Combat Uniform">Army Combat Uniform</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tigerstripe" title="Tigerstripe">Tigerstripe</a>, <a href="/wiki/MARPAT" title="MARPAT">MARPAT</a>, <a href="/wiki/Multicam" class="mw-redirect" title="Multicam">Multicam</a> etc.), though it is still used by the U.S. military to color webbing and accessories. The armies of <a href="/wiki/Israel_Defense_Forces" title="Israel Defense Forces">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_Army" title="Indian Army">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Armed_Forces" class="mw-redirect" title="Cuban Armed Forces">Cuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_Armed_Forces" class="mw-redirect" title="Venezuelan Armed Forces">Venezuela</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Bundesheer" class="mw-redirect" title="Austrian Bundesheer">Austria</a> wear solid-color olive drab uniforms.
</p><p>In the American novel <i><a href="/wiki/A_Separate_Peace" title="A Separate Peace">A Separate Peace</a></i>, Finny says to Gene, "...and in these times of war, we all see olive drab, and we all know it is the patriotic color. All others aren't about the war; they aren't patriotic."
</p><p>There are many shades and variations of olive drab; one common version is defined by <a href="/wiki/Federal_Standard_595" title="Federal Standard 595">Federal Standard 595</a> in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p><b>Black olive</b> is a color in the <a href="/wiki/RAL_(color_space_system)" class="mw-redirect" title="RAL (color space system)">RAL color matching system</a>. It is designated as <a href="/wiki/RAL_6015" class="mw-redirect" title="RAL 6015">RAL 6015</a>.
</p><p>The color "black olive" is a representation of the color of <a href="/wiki/Black_olives" class="mw-redirect" title="Black olives">black olives</a>.
</p><p>This is one of the colors in the RAL color matching system, a color system widely used in Europe. The RAL color list originated in 1927, and it reached its present form in 1961.
</p>

<dl><dt>Ethnography</dt></dl>
<ul><li>The term <a href="/wiki/Olive_skin" title="Olive skin">"olive-skinned"</a> is sometimes used to denote shades of medium-toned skin that is darker than the average color for <a href="/wiki/White_people" title="White people">Caucasians</a>, especially in connection with a Mediterranean ethnicity.</li></ul>
