<p><b>Bright green environmentalism</b> is an ideology based on the belief that the convergence of <a href="/wiki/Technological_change" title="Technological change">technological change</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_innovation" title="Social innovation">social innovation</a> provides the most successful path to <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_evolution_of_bright_green_thinking">Origin and evolution of bright green thinking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bright_green_environmentalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin and evolution of bright green thinking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>bright green</i>, coined in 2003 by writer <a href="/wiki/Alex_Steffen" title="Alex Steffen">Alex Steffen</a>, refers to the fast-growing new wing of <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a>, distinct from traditional forms.<sup id="cite_ref-ref_Newman_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ref_Newman-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  Bright green environmentalism aims to provide prosperity in an ecologically sustainable way through the use of new technologies and improved design.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Proponents promote and advocate for <a href="/wiki/Green_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Green energy">green energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicles" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric vehicles">electric automobiles</a>, efficient manufacturing systems, <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">bio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nanotechnology" title="Nanotechnology">nanotechnologies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ubiquitous_computing" title="Ubiquitous computing">ubiquitous computing</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_urbanism" class="mw-redirect" title="New urbanism">dense urban settlements</a>, <a href="/wiki/Circular_economy" title="Circular economy">closed loop</a> materials cycles and sustainable product designs. <i>One-planet living</i> is a commonly used phrase.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Their principal focus is on the idea that through a combination of well-built communities, new technologies and sustainable living practices, quality of life can actually be improved even while ecological footprints shrink.
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Around the middle of the century we’ll see global population peak at something like 9 billion people, all of whom will want to live with a reasonable amount of prosperity, and many of whom will want, at the very least, a European lifestyle. They will see escaping poverty as their nonnegotiable right, but to deliver that prosperity at our current levels of efficiency and resource use would destroy the planet many times over. We need to invent a new model of prosperity, one that lets billions have the comfort, security, and opportunities they want at the level of impact the planet can afford. We can’t do that without embracing technology and better design.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>The term <i>bright green</i> has been used with increased frequency due to the promulgation of these ideas through the Internet and recent coverage in the traditional media.<sup id="cite_ref-Schechner2008_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schechner2008-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ref_Robertson_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ref_Robertson-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Dark_greens.2C_light_greens_and_bright_greens"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dark_greens,_light_greens_and_bright_greens">Dark greens, light greens and bright greens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bright_green_environmentalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dark greens, light greens and bright greens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alex_Steffen" title="Alex Steffen">Alex Steffen</a> describes contemporary <a href="/wiki/Environmentalist" title="Environmentalist">environmentalists</a> as being split into three groups, <i>dark</i>, <i>light</i>, and <i>bright</i> greens.<sup id="cite_ref-Steffen,2009_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steffen,2009-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Colors_of_green.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Colors_of_green.png/220px-Colors_of_green.png" width="220" height="327" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Colors_of_green.png/330px-Colors_of_green.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Colors_of_green.png/440px-Colors_of_green.png 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="744" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Colors_of_green.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Colors of the Greens</div></div></div>
<p><i>Light greens</i> see protecting the environment first and foremost as a personal responsibility. They fall in on the <a href="/wiki/Activism#Transformational_activism" title="Activism">transformational activist</a> end of the spectrum, but light greens do not emphasize environmentalism as a distinct political ideology, or even seek fundamental <a href="/wiki/Reformism" title="Reformism">political reform</a>. Instead they often focus on environmentalism as a <a href="/wiki/Lifestyle_(sociology)" title="Lifestyle (sociology)">lifestyle</a> choice.<sup id="cite_ref-Steffen,2009_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steffen,2009-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The motto "Green is the new black" sums up this way of thinking, for many.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> This is different from the term <i>lite green</i>, which some environmentalists use to describe products or practices they believe are <a href="/wiki/Greenwash" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenwash">greenwashing</a>.
</p><p>In contrast, <i>dark greens</i> believe that environmental problems are an inherent part of industrialized civilization, and seek <a href="/wiki/Political_radicalism" title="Political radicalism">radical political change</a>. Dark greens believe that currently and historically dominant political ideologies (sometimes referred to as <a href="/wiki/Industrialism" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrialism">industrialism</a>) inevitably lead to <a href="/wiki/Consumerism" title="Consumerism">consumerism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Overconsumption" title="Overconsumption">overconsumption</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">waste</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_alienation" title="Social alienation">alienation</a> from nature and <a href="/wiki/Resource_depletion" title="Resource depletion">resource depletion</a>. Dark greens claim this is caused by the emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a> that exists within all existing ideologies, a tendency referred to as <i>growth mania</i>. The dark green brand of environmentalism is associated with ideas of <a href="/wiki/Ecocentrism" title="Ecocentrism">ecocentrism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deep_ecology" title="Deep ecology">deep ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Degrowth" title="Degrowth">degrowth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anti-consumerism" title="Anti-consumerism">anti-consumerism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Post-materialism" title="Post-materialism">post-materialism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holism" title="Holism">holism</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Gaia_hypothesis" title="Gaia hypothesis">Gaia hypothesis</a> of <a href="/wiki/James_Lovelock" title="James Lovelock">James Lovelock</a>, as well as support for a <a href="/wiki/Human_population_control" class="mw-redirect" title="Human population control">reduction in human numbers</a> and/or a <a href="/wiki/Anarcho-primitivism" title="Anarcho-primitivism">relinquishment of technology</a> to reduce humanity's effect on the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a>.
</p><p>
More recently, <i>bright greens</i> emerged as a group of environmentalists who believe that radical changes are needed in the economic and political operation of society in order to make it sustainable, but that better designs, new technologies and more widely distributed social innovations are the means to make those changes—and that society can neither stop nor protest its way to <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> As Ross Robertson writes, </p><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>[B]right green environmentalism is less about the problems and limitations we need to overcome than the "tools, models, and ideas" that already exist for overcoming them. It forgoes the bleakness of protest and dissent for the energizing confidence of constructive solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-ref_Robertson_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ref_Robertson-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_perspective">International perspective</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bright_green_environmentalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: International perspective">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While bright green environmentalism is an intellectual current among North American environmentalists (with a number of businesses, <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/NGO" class="mw-redirect" title="NGO">NGOs</a> and even governments now explicitly calling themselves <i>bright green</i>—for instance, the City of Vancouver's strategic planning document is called "Vancouver 2020: A Bright Green Future"<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>), it is in Northern Europe, especially Scandinavia, Germany, the Netherlands and the United Kingdom, that the idea of bright green environmentalism has become most widespread and most widely discussed. For instance, the official technology showcase and business expo for the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change</a> in <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a> is called <i>Bright Green</i> in reference to this idea,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> while the Danish youth <a href="/wiki/Climate_activism" class="mw-redirect" title="Climate activism">climate activism</a> movement is called <i>Bright Green Youth</i>.
</p>
