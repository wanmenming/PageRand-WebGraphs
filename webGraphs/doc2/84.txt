<p>The <b>politics of Saudi Arabia</b> takes place in the context of a <a href="/wiki/Totalitarianism" title="Totalitarianism">totalitarian</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolute monarchy</a> with some <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islamic</a> lines, where the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="King of Saudi Arabia">King</a> is both the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a> and <a href="/wiki/Head_of_government" title="Head of government">government</a>. Decisions are, to a large extent, made on the basis of consultation among the senior princes of the royal family and the religious establishment. The <a href="/wiki/Qur%27an" class="mw-redirect" title="Qur&#39;an">Qur'an</a> is declared to be the constitution of the country, which is governed on the basis of <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">Islamic law (Shari'a)</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Allegiance_Council" title="Allegiance Council">Allegiance Council</a> is responsible to determine the new King and the new Crown Prince. All citizens of full age have a right to attend, meet, and petition the king directly through the traditional tribal meeting known as the majlis.<sup id="cite_ref-Cavendish_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Government is dominated by the vast royal family, the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Saud" title="House of Saud">Al Saud</a>, which has often been divided by internal disputes and into factions. The members of the family are the principal political actors allowed by the government. Political participation outside the royal family is limited.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Constitution">Constitution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Constitution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Iqra.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Iqra.jpg/200px-Iqra.jpg" width="200" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Iqra.jpg/300px-Iqra.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Iqra.jpg/400px-Iqra.jpg 2x" data-file-width="550" data-file-height="416" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Iqra.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Verses from the Quran, the official constitution of the country</div></div></div>
<p>Saudi Arabia is an <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolute monarchy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Cavendish78_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish78-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> although, according to the <a href="/wiki/Basic_Law_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Basic Law of Saudi Arabia">Basic Law of Saudi Arabia</a> adopted by royal decree in 1992, the king must comply with <a href="/wiki/Sharia" title="Sharia">Sharia</a> (that is, Islamic law) and the <a href="/wiki/Quran" title="Quran">Quran</a>. The Quran and the <a href="/wiki/Sunnah" title="Sunnah">Sunnah</a> are declared to be the country's constitution.<sup id="cite_ref-Gerhard_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gerhard-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> There is no legally binding written constitution and the Quran and the Sunna remain subject to interpretation. This is carried out by the <a href="/wiki/Ulama" title="Ulama">ulama</a>, the Saudi religious establishment.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_government">National government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: National government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The government of Saudi Arabia is led by the monarch, <a href="/wiki/Salman_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Salman of Saudi Arabia">King Salman</a>, who acceded to the throne on 23 January 2015. No political parties or national elections are permitted<sup id="cite_ref-Cavendish_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and according to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Economist" title="The Economist">The Economist</a>'s</i> 2010 <a href="/wiki/Democracy_Index" title="Democracy Index">Democracy Index</a>, the Saudi government was the seventh most authoritarian regime from among the 167 countries rated.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Government is dominated by the royal family.<sup id="cite_ref-LoC_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LoC-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_King">The King</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The King">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/King_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="King of Saudi Arabia">King of Saudi Arabia</a></div>
<p>The Basic Law specifies that the king must be chosen from among the sons of the first king, <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Saud_of_Saudi_Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Ibn Saud of Saudi Arabia">Abdul Aziz Al Saud</a>, and their male descendants<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> subject to the subsequent approval of religious leaders (the <a href="/wiki/Ulama" title="Ulama">ulama</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Cavendish78_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish78-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 2007, an "<a href="/wiki/Allegiance_Council" title="Allegiance Council">Allegiance Council</a>" was created, comprising King Abdulaziz's surviving sons plus a son of each his deceased sons, to determine who will be the heir apparent (the Crown Prince) after the previous heir apparent dies or accedes to the throne.<sup id="cite_ref-Economist_Succession_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Economist_Succession-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mohammad_bin_Salman" title="Mohammad bin Salman">Prince Mohammad bin Salman</a> is the current Crown Prince, and is widely regarded as the country's de facto ruler.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Salman_bin_Abdull_aziz_December_9,_2013.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Salman_bin_Abdull_aziz_December_9%2C_2013.jpg/220px-Salman_bin_Abdull_aziz_December_9%2C_2013.jpg" width="220" height="224" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Salman_bin_Abdull_aziz_December_9%2C_2013.jpg/330px-Salman_bin_Abdull_aziz_December_9%2C_2013.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Salman_bin_Abdull_aziz_December_9%2C_2013.jpg/440px-Salman_bin_Abdull_aziz_December_9%2C_2013.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1360" data-file-height="1386" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Salman_bin_Abdull_aziz_December_9,_2013.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Salman_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Salman of Saudi Arabia">King Salman</a> of Saudi Arabia</div></div></div>
<p>The king combines legislative, executive, and judicial functions<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopædia_Britannica_Online:_Saudi_Arabia_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopædia_Britannica_Online:_Saudi_Arabia-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and royal decrees form the basis of the country's legislation.<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The king is also the prime minister, and presides over <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Ministers_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Council of Ministers of Saudi Arabia">the Council of Ministers (Majlis al-Wuzarāʾ)</a>, which comprises the first and second deputy prime ministers (usually the first and second in line to the throne respectively) and 23 ministers with portfolio and five ministers of state.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The king makes appointments to and dismissals from the Council, which is responsible for such executive and administrative matters as foreign and domestic policy, defense, finance, health, and education, administered through numerous separate agencies.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopædia_Britannica_Online:_Saudi_Arabia_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopædia_Britannica_Online:_Saudi_Arabia-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> There is also a 150-member <a href="/wiki/Consultative_Assembly_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Consultative Assembly of Saudi Arabia">Consultative Assembly</a>, appointed by the King,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> which can propose legislation to the King but has no legislative powers itself,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> including no role in budget formation. The government budget itself is not fully disclosed to the public. "Fully 40%" ... is labeled 'Other sectors' (including defense, security, intelligence, direct investment of the kingdom's revenues outside the country, and how much goes to directly to the royal family).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lacey-251_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lacey-251-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although, in theory, the country is an <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolute monarchy</a>, in practice major policy decisions are made outside these formal governmental structures and not solely by the king. Decisions are made by establishing a consensus within the royal family (comprising the numerous descendants of the kingdom's founder, <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Saud_of_Saudi_Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Ibn Saud of Saudi Arabia">King Abdulaziz</a>). In addition, the views of important members of Saudi society, including the <a href="/wiki/Ulama" title="Ulama">ulama</a> (religious scholars), leading tribal sheikhs, and heads of prominent commercial families are considered.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopædia_Britannica_Online:_Saudi_Arabia_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopædia_Britannica_Online:_Saudi_Arabia-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As an absolute monarchy, the personality and capabilities of the reigning monarch influence the politics and national policies of the country. <a href="/wiki/Saud_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Saud of Saudi Arabia">King Saud</a> (1953–1964) was considered incompetent and extravagant and his reign led to an economic and political crisis that resulted in his forced abdication.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Faisal_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Faisal of Saudi Arabia">King Faisal</a> (1964–1975) was a "modernist" who favored economic, technological and governmental progress but was also politically and religiously conservative. He directed the country's rapid economic and bureaucratic development of the early 1970s, but also made concessions to the religious establishment, and abandoned plans to broaden political participation.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Khalid_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Khalid of Saudi Arabia">King Khalid</a> (1975–1982) left government largely to his Crown Prince, <a href="/wiki/Fahd_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Fahd of Saudi Arabia">Fahd</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> who succeeded him as King (1982–2005). Prince Fahd was a talented administrator who initiated significant industrial development in the Kingdom. He was regarded by many as the "father of the country's modernization".<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> However, during the last 10 years of his reign, ill-health prevented him from fully functioning. In the absence of a king who could provide strong central leadership, the state structure began to fragment<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and the country stagnated.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> King Abdullah, who came to the throne in 2005, was seen as a reformer<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and has introduced economic reforms (limited deregulation, encouragement of foreign investment, and privatization) and made modernizing changes to the judiciary and government ministries.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_family">Royal family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Royal family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/House_of_Saud" title="House of Saud">House of Saud</a></div>
<p>The royal family dominates the political system. The family's vast numbers allow it to hold most of the kingdom's important posts and to have an involvement and presence at all levels of government.<sup id="cite_ref-LoC_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LoC-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> 
The number of princes is estimated to be anything from 7,000 upwards, with the most power and influence being wielded by the 200 or so male descendants of <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Saud_of_Saudi_Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Ibn Saud of Saudi Arabia">King Abdulaziz</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FT_Saud_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FT_Saud-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> 
The key ministries have historically been reserved for the royal family,<sup id="cite_ref-Cavendish78_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish78-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> as are the thirteen regional governorships.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> With the large number of family members seeking well paying jobs, critics complain that even "middle management" jobs in the Kingdom are out of reach for non-royal Saudis, limiting upward mobility and incentive for commoners to excel.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The one exception to this rule was <a href="/wiki/Khaled_al-Tuwaijri" title="Khaled al-Tuwaijri">Khaled al-Tuwaijri</a>, Secretary General of the Court and King Abdullah's <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%89minence_grise" title="Éminence grise">éminence grise</a></i>. He was a commoner and immensely powerful, which meant he was despised by most royals, especially the Suderis, who sacked him as soon as the old king died.
</p><p>Long term political and government appointments result in the creation of "power fiefdoms" for senior princes.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> 
Examples include: <a href="/wiki/Abdullah_bin_Abdul_Aziz" class="mw-redirect" title="Abdullah bin Abdul Aziz">King Abdullah</a>, who was the Commander of the <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabian_National_Guard" title="Saudi Arabian National Guard">National Guard</a> from 1963 until 2010, when he then appointed his son to replace him;<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> 
Crown <a href="/wiki/Prince_Sultan" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince Sultan">Prince Sultan</a>, was Minister of Defense and Aviation from 1962 to 2011; <a href="/wiki/Nayef_bin_Abdul_Aziz" class="mw-redirect" title="Nayef bin Abdul Aziz">Prince Nayef</a>  was the Minister of Interior from 1975 until his death in 2012; <a href="/wiki/Saud_bin_Faisal_bin_Abdul_Aziz" class="mw-redirect" title="Saud bin Faisal bin Abdul Aziz">Prince Saud</a> had been Minister of Foreign Affairs from 1975 to just before his death in 2015;<sup id="cite_ref-bio_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bio-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> 
and <a href="/wiki/Salman_bin_Abdul_Aziz" class="mw-redirect" title="Salman bin Abdul Aziz">King Salman</a>, was the Governor of the <a href="/wiki/Riyadh" title="Riyadh">Riyadh</a> Region from 1962 to 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the absence of national elections and political parties,<sup id="cite_ref-Cavendish78_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish78-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> politics in Saudi Arabia takes place in two distinct arenas: within the royal family, the Al Saud, and between the royal family and the rest of Saudi society.<sup id="cite_ref-Noreng97_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Noreng97-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The royal family is politically divided by factions based on clan loyalties, personal ambitions and ideological differences.<sup id="cite_ref-Noreng97_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Noreng97-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The most powerful clan faction is known as the '<a href="/wiki/Sudairi" class="mw-redirect" title="Sudairi">Sudairi Seven</a>', comprising the late King Fahd and his full brothers and their descendants.<sup id="cite_ref-Economist_Succession_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Economist_Succession-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Ideological divisions include issues over the speed and direction of reform,<sup id="cite_ref-Kostiner236_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kostiner236-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> and whether the role of the ulama should be increased or reduced. There were also divisions within the family over who should succeed Crown Prince Sultan.<sup id="cite_ref-Economist_Succession_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Economist_Succession-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-David33_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-David33-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Leading figures in the royal family with differing ideological orientations included <a href="/wiki/Nayef_bin_Abdul-Aziz_Al_Saud" title="Nayef bin Abdul-Aziz Al Saud">Prince Nayef</a>, the late Interior Minister, and <a href="/wiki/Saud_bin_Faisal_bin_Abdul-Aziz" class="mw-redirect" title="Saud bin Faisal bin Abdul-Aziz">Prince Saud Al-Faisal</a>, the Foreign Minister. Prince Nayef was personally committed to maintaining Saudi Arabia's conservative Wahhabi values. Of the senior princes, he was probably the least comfortable with King Abdullah's desire for reform. Following the <a href="/wiki/9/11" class="mw-redirect" title="9/11">11 September 2001 attacks</a> in the United States, perpetrated mostly by Saudi nationals, Prince Nayef was strongly criticized by the U.S. for his reaction. It also took pressure from within the royal family for him to launch a hunt for Islamist militants who had attacked Western targets in Saudi Arabia. By contrast, Prince Saud Al Faisal is one of the strongest supporters of political and social reform.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> For example, he (as well as King Abdullah) has spoken in favor of women having the right to vote, to follow the career path they wish and to be able to drive a car. Women would be able to vote in municipal elections beginning in 2012.<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-us-canada-15052030">BBC</a><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_influence_of_the_ulama">The influence of the ulama</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The influence of the ulama">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The significance of the <a href="/wiki/Ulama" title="Ulama">ulama</a> (the body of Islamic religious leaders and jurists) is derived from the central role of religion in Saudi society. It has been said that Islam is more than a religion, it is a way of life in Saudi Arabia, and, as a result, the influence of the ulama is pervasive.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Saudi Arabia is almost unique in giving the ulama a direct role in government,<sup id="cite_ref-Goldstein118_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstein118-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> the only other example being <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Prior to 1971, a council of senior ulama advising the king was headed by the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Mufti" title="Grand Mufti">Grand Mufti</a> and met informally.  In that year, the council was formalized in a <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Senior_Scholars_(Saudi_Arabia)" title="Council of Senior Scholars (Saudi Arabia)">Council of Senior Scholars</a>, appointed by the king and with salaries paid by the government.<sup id="cite_ref-ComingStorm_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ComingStorm-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Not only is royal succession subject to the approval of the ulama,<sup id="cite_ref-Cavendish78_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish78-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> so are all new laws (royal decrees).<sup id="cite_ref-Goldstein118_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldstein118-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The ulama have also influenced major executive decisions, for example the imposition of the <a href="/wiki/1973_Oil_Crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="1973 Oil Crisis">oil embargo in 1973</a> and the <a href="/wiki/1990_Gulf_War" class="mw-redirect" title="1990 Gulf War">invitation of foreign troops to Saudi Arabia in 1990</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-meforum.org_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-meforum.org-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> It plays a major role in the judicial and education systems<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> and has a monopoly of authority in the sphere of religious and social morals.<sup id="cite_ref-Hassner_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hassner-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the 1970s, as a result of oil wealth and the modernization of the country initiated by King Faisal, important changes to Saudi society were under way and the power of the ulama was in decline.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> However, this changed following <a href="/wiki/Grand_Mosque_Seizure" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Mosque Seizure">the seizure of the Grand Mosque in Mecca in 1979</a> by <a href="/wiki/Islamist" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamist">Islamist</a> radicals.<sup id="cite_ref-Abir21_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Abir21-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> The government's response to the crisis included strengthening the ulama's powers and increasing their financial support:<sup id="cite_ref-Hegghammer24_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hegghammer24-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> in particular, they were given greater control over the education system<sup id="cite_ref-Abir21_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Abir21-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> and allowed to enforce stricter observance of <a href="/wiki/Wahhabism" title="Wahhabism">Wahhabi</a> rules of moral and social behaviour.<sup id="cite_ref-Hegghammer24_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hegghammer24-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Following his accession to the throne in 2005, <a href="/wiki/Abdullah_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Abdullah of Saudi Arabia">King Abdullah</a> took steps to rein back the powers of the ulama, for instance transferring their control over girls' education to the Ministry of Education.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Abdullah_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Abdullah-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The ulama have historically been led by the <a href="/wiki/Al_ash-Sheikh" title="Al ash-Sheikh">Al ash-Sheikh</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> the country's leading religious family.<sup id="cite_ref-Hassner_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hassner-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> The Al ash-Sheikh are the descendants of <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Abd_al-Wahhab" title="Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab">Muhammad ibn Abd al-Wahhab</a>, the 18th century founder of the <a href="/wiki/Wahhabi" class="mw-redirect" title="Wahhabi">Wahhabi</a> form of <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">Sunni Islam</a> which is today dominant in Saudi Arabia.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> The family is second in prestige only to the Al Saud (the royal family)<sup id="cite_ref-Long11_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Long11-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> with whom they formed a "mutual support pact"<sup id="cite_ref-IBP_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IBP-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> and power-sharing arrangement nearly 300 years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-meforum.org_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-meforum.org-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> The pact, which persists to this day,<sup id="cite_ref-IBP_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IBP-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> is based on the Al Saud maintaining the Al ash-Sheikh's authority in religious matters and upholding and propagating Wahhabi doctrine. In return, the Al ash-Sheikh support the Al Saud's political authority <sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> thereby using its religious-moral authority to legitimize the royal family's rule.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Although the Al ash Sheikh's domination of the ulama has diminished in recent decades,<sup id="cite_ref-Mattar_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mattar-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> they still hold the most important religious posts and are closely linked to the Al Saud by a high degree of intermarriage.<sup id="cite_ref-Hassner_49-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hassner-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Corruption">Corruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Corruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Corruption is widespread in Saudi Arabia, most prevalent in the form of <a href="/wiki/Nepotism" title="Nepotism">nepotism</a>, the use of middlemen, ‘<a href="/wiki/Wasta" title="Wasta">wasta</a>’, to do business as well as patronage systems.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> 
The Saudi government and the royal family have often, and over many years, been accused of corruption.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> In a country that is said to "belong" to the royal family and is <a href="/wiki/Etymology_of_Saudi_Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Etymology of Saudi Arabia">named after it</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Kamrava_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kamrava-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> the lines between state assets and the personal wealth of senior princes are blurred.<sup id="cite_ref-FT_Saud_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FT_Saud-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The corruption has been described as systemic<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> and endemic,<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> and its existence was acknowledged<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> and defended<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Bandar_bin_Sultan" class="mw-redirect" title="Bandar bin Sultan">Prince Bandar bin Sultan</a> (a senior member of the royal family)<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> in an interview in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-PBS_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PBS-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although corruption allegations have often been limited to broad undocumented accusations,<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> specific allegations were made in 2007, when it was claimed that the British defence contractor <a href="/wiki/BAE_Systems" title="BAE Systems">BAE Systems</a> had paid Prince Bandar US$2 billion in bribes relating to the <a href="/wiki/Al-Yamamah_arms_deal" title="Al-Yamamah arms deal">Al-Yamamah arms deal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> Prince Bandar denied the allegations.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> Investigations by both US and UK authorities resulted, in 2010, in <a href="/wiki/Plea_bargain" title="Plea bargain">plea bargain</a> agreements with the company, by which it paid $447 million in fines but did not admit to bribery.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Transparency_International" title="Transparency International">Transparency International</a> in its annual <a href="/wiki/Corruption_Perceptions_Index" title="Corruption Perceptions Index">Corruption Perceptions Index</a> for 2012 gave Saudi Arabia a score of 4.4 (on a scale from 0 to 10 where 0 is "highly corrupt" and 10 is "highly clean").<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 5 November <a href="/wiki/2017_Saudi_Arabian_anti-corruption_arrests" class="mw-redirect" title="2017 Saudi Arabian anti-corruption arrests">2017 Saudi Arabian anti-corruption arrests</a>, 11 princes and dozens of former ministers were detained in a new anti-corruption probe in Saudi Arabia. Among those detained include prominent billionaire investor Prince <a href="/wiki/Al-Waleed_bin_Talal" title="Al-Waleed bin Talal">Al-Waleed bin Talal</a>, National Guard Minister Miteb bin Abdullah and Economy and Planning Minister Adel Fakeih. The official line is that the purge was in response to corrupt practices by the accused and that the anti-corruption committee has the right to issue arrest warrants, impose travel restrictions and freeze bank accounts. It is also empowered to investigate financials and freeze assets until cases are decided on. The Royal proclamation further said "due to the propensity of some people for abuse, putting their personal interest above public interest, and stealing public funds."<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reform">Reform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since the <a href="/wiki/9/11" class="mw-redirect" title="9/11">9/11</a> attacks in 2001, there has been mounting pressure to reform and modernize the royal family's rule, an agenda championed by King Abdullah both before and after his accession in 2005. The creation of the Consultative Council in the early 1990s did not satisfy demands for political participation, and, in 2003, an annual <i>National Dialogue Forum</i> was announced that would allow selected professionals and intellectuals to publicly debate current national issues, within certain prescribed parameters. In 2005, the first municipal elections were held. In 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Allegiance_Council" title="Allegiance Council">Allegiance Council</a> was created to regulate the succession.<sup id="cite_ref-Al-Rasheed242_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Rasheed242-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> In 2009, the king made significant personnel changes to the government by appointing reformers to key positions and the first woman to a ministerial post.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> However, the changes have been criticized as being too slow or merely cosmetic,<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> and the royal family is reportedly divided on the speed and direction of reform.<sup id="cite_ref-Kostiner236_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kostiner236-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011, Abdullah announced that women will be able to be nominated to the <a href="/wiki/Consultative_Assembly_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Consultative Assembly of Saudi Arabia">Shura Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-oman_observer_electionday_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oman_observer_electionday-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics_outside_of_the_royal_family">Politics outside of the royal family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Politics outside of the royal family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Politics in Saudi Arabia, outside the royal family, can be examined in three contexts: the extent to which the royal family allows political participation by the wider Saudi society, opposition to the regime, and Islamist terrorism.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_participation">Political participation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Political participation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Outside the House of Al Saud, participation in the political process is limited to a relatively small segment of the population and takes the form of the royal family consulting with the ulama, tribal sheikhs and members of important commercial families on major decisions.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopædia_Britannica_Online:_Saudi_Arabia_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopædia_Britannica_Online:_Saudi_Arabia-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> This process is not reported by the Saudi media.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>  In theory, all males of the age of majority have a right to petition the king directly through the traditional tribal meeting known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Majlis" title="Majlis">majlis</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cavendish92_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish92-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> In many ways, the approach to government differs little from the traditional system of tribal rule. Tribal identity remains strong and, outside the royal family, political influence is frequently determined by tribal affiliation, with tribal sheikhs maintaining a considerable degree of influence over local and national events.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopædia_Britannica_Online:_Saudi_Arabia_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopædia_Britannica_Online:_Saudi_Arabia-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In recent years there have been limited steps to widen political participation, such as the establishment of the Consultative Council in the early 1990s and the National Dialogue Forum in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-Al-Rasheed242_76-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Rasheed242-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition_to_the_royal_family">Opposition to the royal family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Opposition to the royal family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Human_rights_in_Saudi_Arabia" title="Human rights in Saudi Arabia">Human rights in Saudi Arabia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Censorship_in_Saudi_Arabia" title="Censorship in Saudi Arabia">Censorship in Saudi Arabia</a></div>
<p>The rule of the Al Saud faces political opposition from four sources: <a href="/wiki/Sunni" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunni">Sunni</a> <a href="/wiki/Islamist" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamist">Islamist</a> activism, liberal critics, including an underground green party, the <a href="/wiki/Shia_Islam_in_Saudi_Arabia" title="Shia Islam in Saudi Arabia">Shia minority</a> – particularly in the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Province,_Saudi_Arabia" title="Eastern Province, Saudi Arabia">Eastern Province</a>; and long-standing tribal and regional particularistic opponents (for example in the <a href="/wiki/Hejaz" title="Hejaz">Hejaz</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> Of these, the Islamic activists have been the most prominent threat to the regime and have in recent years perpetrated a number of violent or <a href="/wiki/List_of_militant_incidents_in_Saudi_Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="List of militant incidents in Saudi Arabia">terrorist acts in the country</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cordesman_2009_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cordesman_2009-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>  However, open protest against the government, even if peaceful, is not tolerated. On 29 January 2011, hundreds of protesters gathered in the city of <a href="/wiki/Jeddah" title="Jeddah">Jeddah</a> in a rare display of protest against the city's poor infrastructure after deadly floods swept through the city, killing eleven people.<sup id="cite_ref-Montreal_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Montreal-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> Police stopped the demonstration after about 15 minutes and arrested 30 to 50 people.<sup id="cite_ref-Reuters_Dozens_detained_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters_Dozens_detained-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamist_terrorism">Islamist terrorism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Islamist terrorism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Islamist_terrorism" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamist terrorism">Islamist terrorism</a>, <a href="/wiki/State-sponsored_terrorism#Saudi_Arabia" title="State-sponsored terrorism">State-sponsored terrorism §&#160;Saudi Arabia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Terrorism_in_Saudi_Arabia" title="Terrorism in Saudi Arabia">Terrorism in Saudi Arabia</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Osama_bin_Laden" title="Osama bin Laden">Osama bin Laden</a> and 15 out of the 19 <a href="/wiki/9/11" class="mw-redirect" title="9/11">9/11</a> hijackers were Saudi nationals or used to be Saudi nationals <sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> and former <a href="/wiki/CIA" class="mw-redirect" title="CIA">CIA</a> director <a href="/wiki/James_Woolsey" class="mw-redirect" title="James Woolsey">James Woolsey</a> described Saudi Arabian <a href="/wiki/Wahhabism" title="Wahhabism">Wahhabism</a> as "the soil in which <a href="/wiki/Al-Qaeda" title="Al-Qaeda">Al-Qaeda</a> and its sister terrorist organizations are flourishing."<sup id="cite_ref-iags.org_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iags.org-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arab_Spring_protests">Arab Spring protests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Arab Spring protests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2011%E2%80%9313_Saudi_Arabian_protests" class="mw-redirect" title="2011–13 Saudi Arabian protests">2011–13 Saudi Arabian protests</a></div>
<p>Since 2011, Saudi Arabia has been affected by its own Arab Spring protests.<sup id="cite_ref-Fisk_troops_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fisk_troops-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> In response, King Abdullah announced on 22 February 2011 a series of benefits for citizens amounting to $36 billion, of which $10.7 billion was earmarked for <a href="/wiki/Housing" title="Housing">housing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> No political reforms were announced as part of the package, though some prisoners indicted for financial crimes were pardoned.<sup id="cite_ref-AlJazeeraEnglishBenefits_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AlJazeeraEnglishBenefits-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> On 18 March the same year, King Abdullah announced a package of $93 billion, which included 500,000 new homes to a cost of $67 billion, in addition to creating 60,000 new security jobs.<sup id="cite_ref-theguardian.com_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian.com-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-theguardian.com_93-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian.com-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The lack of critical thought in the education system has been cited by some as the reason why fewer protests occurred in the Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_government">Regional government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Regional government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The kingdom is divided into 13 regions (<i>manāṭiq</i>), which in turn are divided into numerous districts. Regional governors are appointed, usually from the royal family, and preside over one or more municipal councils, half of whose members are appointed and half elected. The governors are responsible for such functions as finance, health, education, agriculture, and municipalities. The consultative principle operates at all levels of government, including the government of villages and tribes.<sup id="cite_ref-Encyclopædia_Britannica_Online:_Saudi_Arabia_16-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encyclopædia_Britannica_Online:_Saudi_Arabia-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The governors act as regional "mini-kings", sitting in <a href="/wiki/Majlis" title="Majlis">majlises</a>, hearing grievances and settling disputes.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Municipal_elections">Municipal elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Municipal elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 2005, the first elections in Saudi Arabian history were held. The elections for "virtually powerless" municipal councils were for half the seats (the half of each council's seats were appointed). Women were not allowed to stand for office or to vote.<sup id="cite_ref-lacey-267_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lacey-267-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Riyadh" title="Riyadh">Riyadh</a>, the number of registered voters did not exceed 18% of those eligible to vote, representing only 2% of the city's population. There was evidence of much greater interest in the Shia community of the Eastern Province.<sup id="cite_ref-Al-Rasheed_248-250_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Rasheed_248-250-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> Women will be allowed to vote beginning in 2012, as King Abdullah announced in the opening speech of the new term of the Shura Council.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, candidates tended to be local businessmen, activists and professionals. Although political parties were not permitted, it was possible to identify candidates as having an <a href="/wiki/Islamist" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamist">Islamist</a> orientation, a liberal agenda or reliant on tribal status. The Islamist candidates tended to be backed by public figures and the religious establishment and won most of the seats in the Saudi cities such as Riyadh, <a href="/wiki/Jeddah" title="Jeddah">Jeddah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medina" title="Medina">Medina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tabuk,_Saudi_Arabia" title="Tabuk, Saudi Arabia">Tabuk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taif" class="mw-redirect" title="Taif">Taif</a>. Candidates with "Western sympathies or any suspicion of secularism" lost out heavily to "hardline conservatives who were endorsed by the local religious establishment."  This demonstrated to some that rather than being a conservative force holding back the country, the royal family was more progressive than the Saudi population as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-lacey-268_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lacey-268-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2007, a Saudi commentator noted that the municipal councils were proving to be powerless. Nevertheless, the elections represented an important step in modernizing the regime.<sup id="cite_ref-Al-Rasheed_248-250_98-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Rasheed_248-250-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although male-only municipal elections were <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabian_municipal_elections,_2011" title="Saudi Arabian municipal elections, 2011">held again on 29 September 2011</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-alawsat_details_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alawsat_details-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bloom_women_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bloom_women-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup> Abdullah announced that women will be able to vote and be elected in the <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabian_municipal_elections,_2015" title="Saudi Arabian municipal elections, 2015">2015 municipal elections</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-oman_observer_electionday_79-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oman_observer_electionday-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_reform">Political reform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Politics_of_Saudi_Arabia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Political reform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In March 1992, <a href="/wiki/Fahd_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Fahd of Saudi Arabia">King Fahd</a> issued several decrees outlining the basic statutes of government and codifying royal succession for the first time. The King's political reform program also provided for the establishment of a national <a href="/wiki/Consultative_Assembly_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Consultative Assembly of Saudi Arabia">Consultative Council</a>, with appointed members having advisory powers to review and give advice on issues of public interest. It also outlined a framework for councils at the provincial or emirate level.
</p><p>In September 1993, King Fahd issued additional reform decrees, appointing the members of the national Consultative Council and spelling out procedures for the new council's operations. He announced reforms to the Council of Ministers, including term limitations of 4 years and regulations to prohibit conflict of interest for ministers and other high-level officials. The members of 13 provincial councils and the councils' operating regulations were also announced.
</p><p>The membership of the Consultative Council was expanded from 60 to 90 members in July 1997, to 120 in May 2001, and to 150 members in 2005. Membership has changed significantly during each expansion of the council, as many members have not been reappointed. The role of the council is gradually expanding as it gains experience.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia_municipal_elections,_2005" class="mw-redirect" title="Saudi Arabia municipal elections, 2005">Saudi Municipal elections</a> took place in 2005 and some journalists saw this as a first tentative step towards the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Democratic_process" class="mw-redirect" title="Democratic process">democratic processes</a> in the Kingdom, including the legalization of political parties. Other analysts of the Saudi political scene were more skeptical.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> Islamist candidates, often businessmen, did well, but in practice had little real power.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup> In 2009, promised new elections and hopes for female suffrage in them were postponed for at least two years.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 15 February 2009, in a reshuffle <a href="/wiki/Abdullah_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Abdullah of Saudi Arabia">King Abdullah</a> removed <a href="/wiki/Sheikh_Ibrahim_Bin_Abdullah_Al-Ghaith" title="Sheikh Ibrahim Bin Abdullah Al-Ghaith">Sheikh Ibrahim Bin Abdullah Al-Ghaith</a> from his position as President of the <a href="/wiki/Commission_for_the_Promotion_of_Virtue_and_the_Prevention_of_Vice" class="mw-redirect" title="Commission for the Promotion of Virtue and the Prevention of Vice">Commission for the Promotion of Virtue and the Prevention of Vice</a>. He also removed Sheikh Saleh al-Luhaidan as head of the Supreme Judicial Council and appointed the first female minister.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In his first act as King, <a href="/wiki/Salman_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Salman of Saudi Arabia">Salman</a> removed <a href="/wiki/Khaled_al-Tuwaijri" title="Khaled al-Tuwaijri">Khaled al-Tuwaijri</a>, Abdullah's <i><a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">de facto</a></i> Prime Minister and <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%89minence_grise" title="Éminence grise">éminence grise</a></i>, replacing him with a Prince of the Blood.
</p>
