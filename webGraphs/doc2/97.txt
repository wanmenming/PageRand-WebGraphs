<p><b>Green accounting</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Accounting" title="Accounting">accounting</a> that attempts to factor <a href="/wiki/Environmental_cost" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental cost">environmental costs</a> into the financial results of operations. It has been argued that <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> ignores the environment and therefore policymakers need a revised model that incorporates green accounting.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The major purpose of <b>green accounting</b> is to help businesses understand and manage the potential <a href="/wiki/Quid_pro_quo" title="Quid pro quo">quid pro quo</a> between traditional economics goals and environmental goals. It also increases the important information available for analyzing policy issues, especially when those vital pieces of information are often overlooked.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Green accounting is said to only ensure <a href="/wiki/Weak_and_strong_sustainability" title="Weak and strong sustainability">weak sustainability</a>, which should be considered as a step toward ultimately a <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">strong sustainability</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is a controversial practice however, since <a href="/wiki/Depletion_(accounting)" title="Depletion (accounting)">depletion</a> may be already factored into accounting for the extraction industries and the accounting for externalities may be arbitrary. It is obvious therefore that a standard practice would need to be established in order for it to gain both credibility and use. Depletion is not the whole of environmental accounting however, with <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> being but one factor of business that is almost never accounted for specifically. <a href="/wiki/Julian_Lincoln_Simon" class="mw-redirect" title="Julian Lincoln Simon">Julian Lincoln Simon</a>, a professor of business administration at the University of Maryland and a Senior Fellow at the <a href="/wiki/Cato_Institute" title="Cato Institute">Cato Institute</a>, argued that use of <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> results in greater wealth, as evidenced by the falling prices over time of virtually all nonrenewable resources.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term was first brought into common usage by economist and professor Peter Wood in the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practice">Practice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Practice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Environmental responsibility is a potent issue among businesses in this modern age. It has become necessary for corporation to formulate methods of promoting green causes for the present and the future.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Green accounting helps promote a sustainable future for businesses as it brings green public procurement and green <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">research and development</a> into the big picture. Penalties for polluters and incentives (such as tax breaks, polluting permits, etc.) are also a crucial part of this type of accounting.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>
The <a href="/wiki/System_of_national_accounts" class="mw-redirect" title="System of national accounts">System of National Accounts (SNA)</a> defines <a href="/wiki/Net_domestic_product" title="Net domestic product">Net Domestic Product (NDP)</a> as:</p><blockquote><p><b>NDP = Net Exports + Final Consumption (C) + Net Investment (I)</b></p></blockquote><p>This is also a typical formula found in articles and texts about accounting.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Green Accounting, however, uses the <a href="/wiki/System_of_Environmental-Economic_Accounting" class="mw-redirect" title="System of Environmental-Economic Accounting">System of Environmental Economic Accounting (SEEA)</a>, which focuses on the depletion of scarce natural resources and measures the costs of environmental degradation along with its prevention.
</p><p>
Thus, the NDP is newly defined as Green NDP, or also known as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_Domestic_Product&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Environmental Domestic Product (page does not exist)">EDP</a>. The green accounting formula is:</p><blockquote><p><b>EDP = Net Exports + C + NAp. ec + (NAnp.ec - NAnp.n)</b></p></blockquote><p>Where:
</p><p><i>EDP = Environmental Domestic Product,</i>
</p><p><i>C = Final Consumption,</i>
</p><p><i>NApec = Net Accumulation of Produced Economic Assets,</i>
</p><p><i>NAnp.ec = Net Accumulation of Non-produced Economic Assets,</i>
</p><p><i>NAnp.n = Net Accumulation of Non-produced Natural Assets.</i><sup id="cite_ref-:3_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges">Challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_protection_and_economic_growth">Environmental protection and economic growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Environmental protection and economic growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The effect of environmental policies on the economy has always been a controversial topic. Many economists argue that sanctioned limits on pollution curtail economic growth.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> For instance, between 1973 and 1982, the United States imposed stricter regulations on pollution, which led to a 0.09% decrease per year in the national output growth.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> A study conducted in 1990 also analyzed the economic growth with during the time period between 1973 and 1980s. The result indicated that the government regulation reduced the annual <a href="/wiki/Gross_national_income" title="Gross national income">GNP</a> by 0.19% per year.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Other researchers argue that those number is insignificant compared to protecting and sustaining the priceless environment.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distributional_impacts_of_environmental_and_natural_resource_policies">Distributional impacts of environmental and natural resource policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distributional impacts of environmental and natural resource policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Not all industries pollute the same amount; chemical and paper manufacturing industries, for example, tend to pollute more than others. It is difficult to accurately measure the pollution level of each industry in order to categorize and to set up a fair set of policies. In particular, improved water quality might highly favor the higher income groups due to the fact that most improvements are done in the urban areas.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Links_between_trade_and_environmental_and_natural_resource_policies">Links between trade and environmental and natural resource policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Links between trade and environmental and natural resource policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the time of globalization and the rapid expansion of the international market, the US policymakers have come to realize the importance of what is happening in other countries. Before making any decision and submitting the final draft to <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a>, the policymakers were concerned about the effects of the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Free_Trade_Agreement" title="North American Free Trade Agreement">North American Free Trade Agreement</a> on the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> National accounting systems that include environmental and natural resources could provide useful information during negotiations over the nations' commitments to restore or maintain natural capital.
</p><p>Trade restrictions have not been used when a country's production and processing methods result in excessive discharges of pollutants (<a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbons" class="mw-redirect" title="Chlorofluorocarbons">chlorofluorocarbons</a>) across national boundaries. The difficulty comes in when determining the effects of trans-boundary pollutants on industry costs.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
