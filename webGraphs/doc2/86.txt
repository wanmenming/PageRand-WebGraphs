<p>The <b>Green Party of Switzerland</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Grüne Partei der Schweiz</i>; <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Les verts&#160;&#8211;&#32;Parti écologiste suisse</i>; <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <i lang="it">I Verdi&#160;&#8211;&#32;Partito ecologista svizzero</i>; <a href="/wiki/Romansh_language" title="Romansh language">Romansh</a>: <i lang="rm">La Verda&#160;&#8211;&#32;Partida ecologica svizra</i>; "The Greens&#160;&#8211;&#32;Swiss ecologist party") is the fifth-largest party in the <a href="/wiki/National_Council_of_Switzerland" class="mw-redirect" title="National Council of Switzerland">National Council of Switzerland</a>, and the largest party that is not represented on the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Federal_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Swiss Federal Council">Federal Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Switzerland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Green_party" title="Green party">Green party</a> in Switzerland was founded as a local party in 1971 in the town of <a href="/wiki/Neuch%C3%A2tel" title="Neuchâtel">Neuchâtel</a>. In 1979, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Br%C3%A9laz" title="Daniel Brélaz">Daniel Brélaz</a> was elected to the <a href="/wiki/National_Council_of_Switzerland" class="mw-redirect" title="National Council of Switzerland">National Council</a> as the first Green MP on the national level (in Switzerland and in the world). Local and regional Green parties and organisations were founded in many different towns and <a href="/wiki/Cantons_of_Switzerland" title="Cantons of Switzerland">cantons</a> in the following years.
</p><p>In 1983, two different national green party federations were created: in May, diverse local green groups came together in Fribourg to form the <i>Federation of Green Parties of Switzerland</i>, and in June, some left-alternative groups formed the <i>Green Alternative Party of Switzerland</i> in Bern. In 1990, an attempt to combine these organisations failed. Afterwards, some of the member groups from the <i>Green Alternative Party</i> joined the <i>Federation of Green Parties</i> which has become the <i>de facto</i> national Green party. In 1993, the <i>Federation of Green Parties</i> changed its name to the <i>Green Party of Switzerland</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1986, the first two Green members of a cantonal government become members of the <i>Regierungsrat</i> of Bern.
</p><p>In 1987, the Green Party of Switzerland joined the <a href="/wiki/European_Federation_of_Green_Parties" class="mw-redirect" title="European Federation of Green Parties">European Federation of Green Parties</a>.
</p><p>In the 1990s, members of the Green Party became town mayors, members of the high court and even president of a cantonal government (<a href="/wiki/Verena_Diener" title="Verena Diener">Verena Diener</a> in 1999).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policies">Policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_Party_of_Switzerland&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Swiss_party_politics_2007_en.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Swiss_party_politics_2007_en.png/300px-Swiss_party_politics_2007_en.png" width="300" height="325" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Swiss_party_politics_2007_en.png/450px-Swiss_party_politics_2007_en.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Swiss_party_politics_2007_en.png/600px-Swiss_party_politics_2007_en.png 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="1082" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Swiss_party_politics_2007_en.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Green Party's positions in the Swiss political spectrum (2007).</div></div></div>
<p>The traditional emphases of the party's policies lie in <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a> and green means of transportation. In terms of foreign policy, the greens set out on the course of openness and <a href="/wiki/Pacifism" title="Pacifism">pacifism</a>. In economic policy, the greens are <a href="/wiki/Center-left" class="mw-redirect" title="Center-left">center-left</a>. The majority of greens support an accession of <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> to the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. In immigration policy, the greens support further integration initiatives for immigrants. The greens support measures to increase energy efficiency, oppose <a href="/wiki/Anti-nuclear_movement" title="Anti-nuclear movement">nuclear power</a>, and support raising energy and fuel prices. According to their policy, the resulting revenues should be allocated to <a href="/wiki/Social_security" title="Social security">social security</a> spending.
</p>
