<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon trioxide</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For polyatomic ion CO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>, see <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">Carbonate</a>. For the UK postal district, see <a href="/wiki/CO_postcode_area" title="CO postcode area">CO postcode area</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Co3-geometries.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Co3-geometries.png/220px-Co3-geometries.png" width="220" height="89" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Co3-geometries.png/330px-Co3-geometries.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/40/Co3-geometries.png 2x" data-file-width="392" data-file-height="158" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Co3-geometries.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The C<sub>s</sub>, D<sub>3h</sub>, and C<sub>2v</sub> isomers of carbon trioxide.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Carbon trioxide</b> (<b>CO<sub>3</sub></b>) is an unstable <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> (an <a href="/wiki/Oxocarbon" title="Oxocarbon">oxocarbon</a>). Three possible <a href="/wiki/Isomer" title="Isomer">isomers</a> of carbon trioxide, with <a href="/wiki/Molecular_symmetry#Common_point_groups" title="Molecular symmetry">molecular symmetry point groups</a> <i>C<sub>s</sub></i>, <i>D<sub>3h</sub></i>, and <i>C<sub>2v</sub></i>, have been most studied by theoretical methods, and the <i>C<sub>2v</sub></i> state has been shown to be the <a href="/wiki/Ground_state" title="Ground state">ground state</a> of the molecule.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Carbon trioxide should not be confused with the stable <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> ion (CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup>).
</p><p>Carbon trioxide can be produced, for example, in the drift zone of a negative <a href="/wiki/Corona_discharge" title="Corona discharge">corona discharge</a> by reactions between <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) and the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_oxygen" class="mw-redirect" title="Atomic oxygen">atomic oxygen</a> (O) created from molecular oxygen by free electrons in the <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another reported method is photolysis of ozone O<sub>3</sub> dissolved in liquid CO<sub>2</sub>, or in CO<sub>2</sub>/<a href="/wiki/Sulfur_hexafluoride" title="Sulfur hexafluoride">SF<sub>6</sub></a> mixtures at -45&#160;°C, irradiated with light of 2537 <a href="/wiki/Angstrom" class="mw-redirect" title="Angstrom">Å</a>.  The formation of CO<sub>3</sub> is inferred but it appears to decay spontaneously by the route 2CO<sub>3</sub> → 2CO<sub>2</sub> + O<sub>2</sub> with a lifetime much shorter than 1 minute.<sup id="cite_ref-photozon_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-photozon-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Carbon trioxide can be made by blowing <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> at <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a> (solid CO<sub>2</sub>), and it has also been detected in reactions between <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> (CO) and <a href="/wiki/Molecular_oxygen" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular oxygen">molecular oxygen</a> (O<sub>2</sub>).
</p>
