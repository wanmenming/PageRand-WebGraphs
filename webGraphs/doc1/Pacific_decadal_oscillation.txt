<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PDO_Pattern.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/PDO_Pattern.png/300px-PDO_Pattern.png" width="300" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/PDO_Pattern.png/450px-PDO_Pattern.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/PDO_Pattern.png/600px-PDO_Pattern.png 2x" data-file-width="2814" data-file-height="1370" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PDO_Pattern.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>PDO positive phase global pattern</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Pacific Decadal Oscillation</b> (<b>PDO</b>) is a robust, recurring pattern of ocean-atmosphere climate variability centered over the mid-latitude Pacific basin. The PDO is detected as warm or cool surface waters in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, north of 20°N. Over the past century, the <a href="/wiki/Amplitude" title="Amplitude">amplitude</a> of this climate pattern has varied irregularly at interannual-to-interdecadal time scales (meaning time periods of a few years to as much as time periods of multiple decades). There is evidence of reversals in the prevailing polarity (meaning changes in cool surface waters versus warm surface waters within the region) of the oscillation occurring around 1925, 1947, and 1977; the last two reversals corresponded with dramatic shifts in salmon production regimes in the North Pacific Ocean. This climate pattern also affects coastal sea and continental surface air temperatures from <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> to California.
</p><p>During a "<a href="/wiki/Ocean_heat_content" title="Ocean heat content">warm</a>", or "positive", phase, the west Pacific becomes cooler and part of the eastern ocean warms; during a "cool" or "negative" phase, the opposite pattern occurs.  The Pacific Decadal Oscillation was named by Steven R. Hare, who noticed it while studying <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> production pattern results in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-Mantua1997_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mantua1997-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Pacific Decadal Oscillation <b>index</b> is the leading <a href="/wiki/Empirical_orthogonal_function" class="mw-redirect" title="Empirical orthogonal function">empirical orthogonal function</a> (EOF) of monthly <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperature</a> anomalies (<b>SST</b>-A) over the North Pacific (poleward of 20°N) after the global average sea surface temperature has been removed. This PDO index is the standardized <a href="/wiki/Principal_component" class="mw-redirect" title="Principal component">principal component</a> time series.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> A PDO 'signal' has been reconstructed as far back as 1661 through tree-ring chronologies in the <a href="/wiki/Baja_California" title="Baja California">Baja California</a> area.<sup id="cite_ref-Biondi2001_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Biondi2001-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanisms">Mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_decadal_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several studies have indicated that the PDO index can be reconstructed as the superimposition of tropical forcing and extra-tropical processes.<sup id="cite_ref-Newman_2003_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newman_2003-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Vimont_2005_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vimont_2005-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Schneider_2005_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schneider_2005-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Qiu_2007_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Qiu_2007-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Thus, unlike <a href="/wiki/ENSO" class="mw-redirect" title="ENSO">ENSO</a> (El Niño Southern Oscillation), the PDO is not a single physical mode of ocean variability, but rather the sum of several processes with different dynamic origins.
</p><p>At inter-annual time scales the PDO index is reconstructed as the sum of random and ENSO induced variability in the <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_low" class="mw-redirect" title="Aleutian low">Aleutian low</a>, whereas on decadal timescales ENSO teleconnections, stochastic atmospheric forcing and changes in the North Pacific oceanic <a href="/wiki/Gyre" class="mw-redirect" title="Gyre">gyre</a> circulation contribute approximately equally. Additionally sea surface temperature anomalies have some winter to winter persistence due to the reemergence mechanism.
</p><p><span id="ENSO_teleconnections,_the_atmospheric_bridge"></span><span id="ENSO"></span>
</p>
<dl><dt>ENSO teleconnections, the atmospheric bridge<sup id="cite_ref-Alenxander_2002_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alenxander_2002-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></dt></dl>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Atmospheric_bridge.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Atmospheric_bridge.png/250px-Atmospheric_bridge.png" width="250" height="129" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Atmospheric_bridge.png/375px-Atmospheric_bridge.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Atmospheric_bridge.png/500px-Atmospheric_bridge.png 2x" data-file-width="1256" data-file-height="650" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Atmospheric_bridge.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The atmospheric bridge during El Niño.</div></div></div>
<p>ENSO can influence the global circulation pattern thousands of kilometers away from the equatorial Pacific through the "atmospheric bridge". During <a href="/wiki/ENSO" class="mw-redirect" title="ENSO">El Nino</a> events, <a href="/wiki/Deep_convection" class="mw-redirect" title="Deep convection">deep convection</a> and heat transfer to the troposphere is enhanced over the anomalously warm <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperature</a>, this ENSO-related tropical forcing generates <a href="/wiki/Rossby_waves" class="mw-redirect" title="Rossby waves">Rossby waves</a> that propagate poleward and eastward and are subsequently refracted back from the pole to the tropics. The <a href="/wiki/Rossby_waves" class="mw-redirect" title="Rossby waves">planetary waves</a> form at preferred locations both in the North and South Pacific Ocean, and the teleconnection pattern is established within 2–6 weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-Liu_2007_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Liu_2007-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> ENSO driven patterns modify surface temperature, humidity, wind, and the distribution of clouds over the North Pacific that alter surface heat, momentum, and freshwater fluxes and thus induce sea surface temperature, salinity, and <a href="/wiki/Mixed_layer" title="Mixed layer">mixed layer</a> depth (MLD) anomalies.
</p><p>The atmospheric bridge is more effective during boreal winter when the deepened <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_low" class="mw-redirect" title="Aleutian low">Aleutian low</a> results in stronger and cold northwesterly winds over the central Pacific and warm/humid southerly winds along the North American west coast, the associated changes in the surface heat fluxes and to a lesser extent <a href="/wiki/Ekman_transport" title="Ekman transport">Ekman transport</a> creates negative sea surface temperature anomalies and a deepened MLD in the central pacific and warm the ocean from the Hawaii to the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Sea" title="Bering Sea">Bering Sea</a>.
</p><p><span id="SST_reemergence"></span><span id="SST"></span>
</p>
<dl><dt>SST reemergence<sup id="cite_ref-Deser_2003_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Deser_2003-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></dt></dl>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303569">@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .tmulti>.thumbinner{width:100%!important;max-width:none!important}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .tsingle{float:none!important;max-width:none!important;width:100%!important;text-align:center}}</style><div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:254px;max-width:254px"><div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:252px;max-width:252px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:Sst_reemergence.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Sst_reemergence.png/250px-Sst_reemergence.png" width="250" height="247" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Sst_reemergence.png/375px-Sst_reemergence.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Sst_reemergence.png/500px-Sst_reemergence.png 2x" data-file-width="838" data-file-height="828" /></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">Reemergence mechanism in the North Pacific.</div></div><div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:252px;max-width:252px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:Mixed_layer_depth_seasonal_cycle.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Mixed_layer_depth_seasonal_cycle.png/250px-Mixed_layer_depth_seasonal_cycle.png" width="250" height="138" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Mixed_layer_depth_seasonal_cycle.png/375px-Mixed_layer_depth_seasonal_cycle.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Mixed_layer_depth_seasonal_cycle.png/500px-Mixed_layer_depth_seasonal_cycle.png 2x" data-file-width="2721" data-file-height="1504" /></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">Mixed layer depth seasonal cycle.</div></div></div></div>
<p>Midlatitude <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">SST</a> anomaly patterns tend to recur from one winter to the next but not during the intervening summer, this process occurs because of the strong <a href="/wiki/Mixed_layer" title="Mixed layer">mixed layer</a> seasonal cycle. The mixed layer depth over the North Pacific is deeper, typically 100-200m, in winter than it is in summer and thus SST anomalies that form during winter and extend to the base of the mixed layer are sequestered beneath the shallow summer mixed layer when it reforms in late spring and are effectively insulated from the air-sea heat flux. When the mixed layer deepens again in the following autumn/early winter the anomalies may again influence the surface. This process has been named "reemergence mechanism" by Alexander and Deser<sup id="cite_ref-Alexander_1995_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alexander_1995-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and is observed over much of the North Pacific Ocean although it is more effective in the west where the winter mixed layer is deeper and the seasonal cycle greater.
</p><p><span id="Stochastic_atmospheric_forcing"></span><span id="Stochastic"></span>
</p>
<dl><dt>Stochastic atmospheric forcing<sup id="cite_ref-Alexander_1996_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alexander_1996-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></dt></dl>
<p>Long term sea surface temperature variation may be induced by random atmospheric forcings that are integrated and reddened into the ocean mixed layer. The stochastic climate model paradigm was proposed by Frankignoul and Hasselmann,<sup id="cite_ref-Frankignoul_1977_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frankignoul_1977-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> in this model a stochastic forcing represented by the passage of storms alter the ocean mixed layer temperature via surface energy fluxes and Ekman currents and the system is damped due to the enhanced (reduced) heat loss to the atmosphere over the anomalously warm (cold) SST via turbulent energy and <a href="/wiki/Longwave" title="Longwave">longwave</a> radiative fluxes, in the simple case of a linear negative <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">feedback</a> the model can be written as the separable <a href="/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equation" title="Ordinary differential equation">ordinary differential equation</a>:

</p><p><span id="Ocean_dynamics"></span><span id="Ocean"></span>
</p>
<dl><dt>Ocean dynamics</dt></dl>
<p>Several dynamic oceanic mechanisms and SST-air feedback may contribute to the observed decadal variability in the North Pacific Ocean. SST variability is stronger in the <a href="/wiki/Kuroshio_Current" title="Kuroshio Current">Kuroshio</a> <a href="/wiki/Oyashio_current" class="mw-redirect" title="Oyashio current">Oyashio</a> extension (KOE) region and is associated with changes in the KOE axis and strength,<sup id="cite_ref-Qiu_2007_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Qiu_2007-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> that generates decadal and longer time scales SST variance but without the observed magnitude of the spectral peak at ~10 years, and SST-air feedback. Remote reemergence occurs in regions of strong current such as the Kuroshio extension and the anomalies created near the Japan may reemerge the next winter in the central pacific.
</p><p><span id="Advective_resonance"></span><span id="Advective"></span>
</p>
<dl><dt>Advective resonance</dt></dl>
<p>Saravanan and McWilliams<sup id="cite_ref-Saravanan_1998_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saravanan_1998-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> have demonstrated that the interaction between spatially coherent atmospheric forcing patterns and an advective ocean shows periodicities at preferred time scales when non-local advective effects dominate over the local sea surface temperature damping. This "advective resonance" mechanism may generate decadal SST variability in the Eastern North Pacific associated with the anomalous Ekman advection and surface heat flux.<sup id="cite_ref-Wu_2003_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wu_2003-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><span id="North_Pacific_oceanic_gyre_circulation"></span><span id="North"></span>
</p>
<dl><dt>North Pacific oceanic gyre circulation</dt></dl>

</p><p>The propagation of h anomalies in the western pacific changes the KOE axis and strength<sup id="cite_ref-Qiu_2007_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Qiu_2007-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and impact SST due to the anomalous geostrophic heat transport. Recent studies<sup id="cite_ref-Qiu_2007_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Qiu_2007-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ceballos_2009_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ceballos_2009-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> suggest that Rossby waves excited by the Aleutian low propagate the PDO signal from the North Pacific to the KOE through changes in the KOE axis while Rossby waves associated with the <a href="/wiki/North_Pacific_Oscillation" title="North Pacific Oscillation">NPO</a> propagate the <a href="/wiki/North_Pacific_Gyre" title="North Pacific Gyre">North Pacific Gyre</a> oscillation signal through changes in the KOE strength.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impacts">Impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_decadal_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b> Temperature and precipitation </b>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303569"/><div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:308px;max-width:308px"><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:152px;max-width:152px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:PDO_Temperature.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/PDO_Temperature.png/150px-PDO_Temperature.png" width="150" height="82" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/PDO_Temperature.png/225px-PDO_Temperature.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/PDO_Temperature.png/300px-PDO_Temperature.png 2x" data-file-width="3021" data-file-height="1661" /></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">PDO DJFM temperature pattern.</div></div><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:152px;max-width:152px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:PDO_Precipitation.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/PDO_Precipitation.png/150px-PDO_Precipitation.png" width="150" height="82" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/PDO_Precipitation.png/225px-PDO_Precipitation.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/PDO_Precipitation.png/300px-PDO_Precipitation.png 2x" data-file-width="3021" data-file-height="1661" /></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">PDO DJFM precipitation pattern.</div></div><div style="clear:left"></div></div></div>
<p>The PDO spatial pattern and impacts are similar to those associated with <a href="/wiki/ENSO" class="mw-redirect" title="ENSO">ENSO</a> events. During the positive phase the wintertime <a href="/wiki/Aleutian_low" class="mw-redirect" title="Aleutian low">Aleutian low</a> is deepened and shifted southward, warm/humid air is advected along the North American west coast and temperatures are higher than usual from the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a> to Alaska but below normal in Mexico and the Southeastern United States.<sup id="cite_ref-Mantua2002_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mantua2002-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <br />
Winter precipitation is higher than usual in the Alaska Coast Range, Mexico and the Southwestern United States but reduced over Canada, Eastern Siberia and Australia<sup id="cite_ref-Mantua2002_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mantua2002-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-power_1998_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-power_1998-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> <br />
McCabe et al.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>  showed that the PDO along with the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Multidecadal_Oscillation" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation">AMO</a> strongly influence multidecadal droughts pattern in the United States, drought frequency is enhanced over much of the Northern United States during the positive PDO phase  and over the Southwest United States during the negative PDO phase in both cases if the PDO is associated with a positive AMO.<br />
The Asian Monsoon is also affected, increased rainfall and decreased summer temperature is observed over the Indian subcontinent during the negative phase.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconstructions_and_regime_shifts">Reconstructions and regime shifts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_decadal_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reconstructions and regime shifts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303569"/><div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:254px;max-width:254px"><div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:252px;max-width:252px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:PDO.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/PDO.svg/250px-PDO.svg.png" width="250" height="163" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/PDO.svg/375px-PDO.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/PDO.svg/500px-PDO.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="650" /></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">Observed monthly values for the PDO (1900–mar2014).</div></div><div class="tsingle" style="margin:1px;width:252px;max-width:252px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:PDO1000yr.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/PDO1000yr.svg/250px-PDO1000yr.svg.png" width="250" height="163" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/PDO1000yr.svg/375px-PDO1000yr.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/PDO1000yr.svg/500px-PDO1000yr.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="650" /></a></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">Reconstructed PDO Index (993-1996).</div></div></div></div>
<p>The PDO index has been reconstructed using <a href="/wiki/Tree_rings" class="mw-redirect" title="Tree rings">tree rings</a> and other hydrologically sensitive  <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(climate)" title="Proxy (climate)">proxies</a> from west North America and Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-Biondi2001_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Biondi2001-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Shen_2006_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shen_2006-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-D&#39;arrigo_2006_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D&#39;arrigo_2006-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>MacDonald and Case<sup id="cite_ref-MacDonald_2005_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacDonald_2005-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> reconstructed the PDO back to 993 using tree rings from <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>. The index shows a 50–70 year periodicity but is a strong mode of variability only after 1800, a persistent negative phase occurring during <a href="/wiki/Medieval_warm_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval warm period">medieval times</a> (993–1300) which is consistent with <a href="/wiki/La_Ni%C3%B1a" title="La Niña">La Niña</a> conditions reconstructed in the tropical Pacific<sup id="cite_ref-Rein_2004_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rein_2004-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and multi-century droughts in the South-West United States.<sup id="cite_ref-Seager_2007_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seager_2007-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several regime shifts are apparent both in the reconstructions and instrumental data, during the 20th century regime shifts associated with concurrent changes in <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">SST</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_pressure" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea level pressure">SLP</a>, land precipitation and ocean cloud cover occurred in 1924/1925, 1945/1946, and 1976/1977:<sup id="cite_ref-Deser_2004_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Deser_2004-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>1750: PDO displays an unusually strong oscillation.<sup id="cite_ref-Biondi2001_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Biondi2001-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>1924/1925: PDO changed to a "warm" phase.<sup id="cite_ref-Deser_2004_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Deser_2004-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>1945/1946: The PDO changed to a "cool" phase, the pattern of this regime shift is similar to the 1970s episode with maximum amplitude in the subarctic and subtropical front but with a greater signature near the Japan while the 1970s shift was stronger near the American west coast.<sup id="cite_ref-Deser_2004_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Deser_2004-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Minobe_2005_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Minobe_2005-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>1976/1977: PDO changed to a "warm" phase.<sup id="cite_ref-Hare2000_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hare2000-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>1988/1989: A weakening of the Aleutian low with associated SST changes was observed,<sup id="cite_ref-Trenberth_1994_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trenberth_1994-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> in contrast to others regime shifts this change appears to be related to concurrent extratropical oscillation in the North Pacific and North Atlantic rather than tropical processes.<sup id="cite_ref-Yasunaka_2003_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yasunaka_2003-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>1997/1998: Several changes in sea surface temperature and marine ecosystem occurred in the North Pacific after 1997/1998, in contrast to prevailing anomalies observed after the 1970s shift. The SST declined along the United States west coast and substantial changes in the populations of <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anchovy" title="Anchovy">anchovy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sardine" title="Sardine">sardine</a> were observed as the PDO changed back to a cool "anchovy" phase .<sup id="cite_ref-Chavez_2003_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chavez_2003-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> However the spatial pattern of the SST change was different with a meridional SST seesaw in the central and western Pacific that resembled a strong shift in the North Pacific Gyre Oscillation rather than the PDO structure. This pattern dominated much of the North Pacific SST variability after 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-Bond_2003_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bond_2003-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The 2014 flip from the cool PDO phase to the warm phase, which vaguely resembles a long and drawn out El Niño event, contributed to record-breaking surface temperatures across the planet in 2014.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Predictability">Predictability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_decadal_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Predictability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a> Earth System Research Laboratory produces official ENSO forecasts, and  Experimental statistical forecasts using a linear inverse modeling (LIM) method<sup id="cite_ref-Team_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Team-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Alexander_2008_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alexander_2008-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> to predict the PDO, LIM assumes that the PDO can be separated into a linear deterministic component and a non-linear component represented by random fluctuations.
</p><p>Much of the LIM PDO predictability arises from ENSO and the global trend rather than extra-tropical processes and is thus limited to ~4 seasons. The prediction is consistent with the seasonal footprinting mechanism<sup id="cite_ref-Vimont_2003_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vimont_2003-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> in which an optimal SST structure evolves into the ENSO mature phase 6–10 months later that subsequently impacts the North Pacific Ocean SST via the atmospheric bridge.
</p><p>Skills in predicting decadal PDO variability could arise from taking into account the impact of the externally forced<sup id="cite_ref-Meehl_2009_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meehl_2009-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> and internally generated<sup id="cite_ref-Mochizuki_2010_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mochizuki_2010-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Pacific variability.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_patterns">Related patterns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacific_decadal_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Related patterns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Interdecadal_Pacific_Oscillation" title="Interdecadal Pacific Oscillation">Interdecadal Pacific Oscillation</a> (IPO) is a similar but less localised phenomenon; it covers the Southern hemisphere as well (50°S to 50°N).</li>
<li>ENSO tends to lead PDO cycling.</li>
<li>Shifts in the IPO change the location and strength of ENSO activity.  The <a href="/wiki/South_Pacific_Convergence_Zone" class="mw-redirect" title="South Pacific Convergence Zone">South Pacific Convergence Zone</a> moves northeast during El Niño and southwest during La Niña events.  The same movement takes place during positive IPO and negative IPO phases respectively. (Folland et al., 2002)</li>
<li>Interdecadal temperature variations in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> are closely related to those of the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_oscillation" title="North Atlantic oscillation">NAO</a> and the NPO.</li>
<li>The amplitudes of the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_oscillation" title="North Atlantic oscillation">NAO</a> and NPO increased in the 1960s and interannual variation patterns changed from 3&#8211;4 years to 8&#8211;15 years.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">Sea level rise</a> is affected when large areas of water warm and expand, or cool and contract.</li></ul>
