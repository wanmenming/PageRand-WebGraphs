<p><b>Tin(II) oxide</b> (stannous oxide) is a compound with the formula SnO. It is composed of <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> where tin has the oxidation state of +2. There are two forms, a stable blue-black form and a <a href="/wiki/Metastable" class="mw-redirect" title="Metastable">metastable</a> red form.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation_and_reactions">Preparation and reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tin(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation and reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tin(II)_oxide_burning.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Tin%28II%29_oxide_burning.JPG/220px-Tin%28II%29_oxide_burning.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Tin%28II%29_oxide_burning.JPG/330px-Tin%28II%29_oxide_burning.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Tin%28II%29_oxide_burning.JPG/440px-Tin%28II%29_oxide_burning.JPG 2x" data-file-width="601" data-file-height="452" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tin(II)_oxide_burning.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tin(II) oxide burning</div></div></div>
<p>Blue-black SnO can be produced by heating the tin(II) oxide hydrate, SnO·xH<sub>2</sub>O (x&lt;1) precipitated when a tin(II) salt is reacted with an alkali hydroxide such as NaOH.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><br />
Metastable, red SnO can be prepared by gentle heating of the precipitate produced by the action of aqueous ammonia on a tin(II) salt.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><br />
SnO may be prepared as a pure substance in the laboratory, by controlled heating of tin(II) oxalate (stannous oxalate) in the absence of air or under a CO<sub>2</sub> atmosphere. This method is also applied to the production of <a href="/wiki/Ferrous_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferrous oxide">ferrous oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manganous_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Manganous oxide">manganous oxide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>SnC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O → SnO + CO<sub>2</sub> + CO + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Tin(II) oxide burns in air with a dim green flame to form  <a href="/wiki/Tin_dioxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Tin dioxide">SnO<sub>2</sub></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 SnO + O<sub>2</sub> → 2 SnO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>When heated in an inert atmosphere initially <a href="/wiki/Disproportionation" title="Disproportionation">disproportionation</a> occurs giving Sn metal and Sn<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> which further reacts to give SnO<sub>2</sub> and Sn metal.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>4SnO → Sn<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> + Sn</dd>
<dd>Sn<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> → 2SnO<sub>2</sub> + Sn</dd></dl>
<p>SnO is <a href="/wiki/Amphoteric" class="mw-redirect" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a>, dissolving in strong acid to give tin(II) salts and in strong base to give stannites containing Sn(OH)<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It can be dissolved in strong acid solutions to give the ionic complexes Sn(OH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>3</sub><sup>2+ </sup>and Sn(OH)(OH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub><sup>+</sup>, and in less acid solutions to give Sn<sub>3</sub>(OH)<sub>4</sub><sup>2+</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Note that anhydrous stannites, e.g. K<sub>2</sub>Sn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, K<sub>2</sub>SnO<sub>2</sub> are also known.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
SnO is a reducing agent and this appears to its role in the manufacture of so-called "copper ruby glass".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tin(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Black, α-SnO adopts the tetragonal <a href="/wiki/Lead(II)_oxide" title="Lead(II) oxide">PbO</a> layer structure containing four coordinate square pyramidal tin atoms.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> This form is found in nature as the rare mineral <a href="/w/index.php?title=Romarchite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Romarchite (page does not exist)">romarchite</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The asymmetry is usually simply ascribed to a sterically active lone pair; however, electron density calculations show that the asymmetry is caused by an antibonding interaction of the Sn(5s) and the O(2p) orbitals.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><br />
Non-stoichiometry has been observed in SnO.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The electronic band gap has been measured between 2.5<a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">eV</a> and 3eV.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tin(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The dominant use of stannous oxide is as a precursor in manufacturing of other, typically trivalent, tin compounds or salts. Stannous oxide  may also be employed as a reducing agent and in the creation of <a href="/wiki/Cranberry_glass" title="Cranberry glass">ruby glass</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> It has a minor use as an <a href="/wiki/Esterification" class="mw-redirect" title="Esterification">esterification</a> catalyst.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cerium(III)_oxide" title="Cerium(III) oxide">Cerium(III) oxide</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a> form, together with Tin(II) oxide (SnO) is used for illumination with UV light.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
