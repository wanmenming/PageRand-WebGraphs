<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Pig iron</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Pig_iron_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Pig iron (disambiguation)">Pig iron (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pig_iron.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Pig_iron.jpg/220px-Pig_iron.jpg" width="220" height="186" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Pig_iron.jpg/330px-Pig_iron.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/aa/Pig_iron.jpg 2x" data-file-width="361" data-file-height="306" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pig_iron.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pig iron of a type used to make ductile iron, stored in a bin</div></div></div>
<p><b>Pig iron</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Intermediate_product" title="Intermediate product">intermediate product</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ferrous_metallurgy" title="Ferrous metallurgy">iron industry</a>, also known as <b>crude iron</b>, which is first obtained from a <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a> furnace in the form of oblong blocks. Pig iron has a very high <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> content, typically 3.8–4.7%,<sup id="cite_ref-msts_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-msts-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> along with silica and other constituents of <a href="/wiki/Dross" title="Dross">dross</a>, which makes it very <a href="/wiki/Brittle" class="mw-redirect" title="Brittle">brittle</a> and not useful directly as a material except for limited applications. Pig iron is made by <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a> <a href="/wiki/Iron_ore" title="Iron ore">iron ore</a> into a transportable <a href="/wiki/Ingot" title="Ingot">ingot</a> of impure high carbon-content iron in a <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a> as an ingredient for further processing steps.<sup id="cite_ref-DThomasBio_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DThomasBio-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The traditional shape of the molds used for pig iron <a href="/wiki/Ingot" title="Ingot">ingots</a> was a branching structure formed in <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a>, with many individual ingots at right angles<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> to a central channel or <i>runner</i>, resembling a litter of <a href="/wiki/Domestic_pig" title="Domestic pig">piglets</a> being <a href="/wiki/Suckled" class="mw-redirect" title="Suckled">suckled</a> by a sow. When the metal had cooled and hardened, the smaller ingots (the <i>pigs</i>) were simply broken from the runner (the <i>sow</i>), hence the name <i>pig iron</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> As pig iron is intended for remelting, the uneven size of the ingots and the inclusion of small amounts of sand caused only insignificant problems considering the ease of casting and handling them.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pig_iron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_ferrous_metallurgy" class="mw-redirect" title="History of ferrous metallurgy">History of ferrous metallurgy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Casting_pig_iron,_Iroquois_smelter,_Chicago.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Casting_pig_iron%2C_Iroquois_smelter%2C_Chicago.jpg/220px-Casting_pig_iron%2C_Iroquois_smelter%2C_Chicago.jpg" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Casting_pig_iron%2C_Iroquois_smelter%2C_Chicago.jpg/330px-Casting_pig_iron%2C_Iroquois_smelter%2C_Chicago.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Casting_pig_iron%2C_Iroquois_smelter%2C_Chicago.jpg/440px-Casting_pig_iron%2C_Iroquois_smelter%2C_Chicago.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4456" data-file-height="3463" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Casting_pig_iron,_Iroquois_smelter,_Chicago.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Casting pig iron, Iroquois smelter, Chicago, between 1890 and 1901</div></div></div>
<p>Smelting and producing <a href="/wiki/Wrought_iron" title="Wrought iron">wrought iron</a> was known in ancient Europe and the Middle East, but iron was produced in <a href="/wiki/Bloomery" title="Bloomery">bloomeries</a> by direct reduction.  Pig iron was not produced in Europe before the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.  The <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> were also making pig iron by the later <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou dynasty</a> (which ended in 256 BC).<sup id="cite_ref-Wagner_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wagner-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Furnaces such as <a href="/wiki/Lapphyttan" title="Lapphyttan">Lapphyttan</a> in Sweden may date back to the 12th century; and some in the Mark, <a href="/wiki/Westfalen" class="mw-redirect" title="Westfalen">Westfalen</a>, Germany to the 13th.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  It remains to be established whether these northern European developments derive from Chinese ones.  Wagner<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (October 2018)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> has postulated a possible link via Persian contacts with China along the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">Silk Road</a> and Viking contacts with Persia,<sup id="cite_ref-Wagner_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wagner-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> but there is a chronological gap between the Viking period and Lapphyttan.  
</p><p>The phase transition of the iron into liquid in the furnace was an <i>avoided</i> phenomenon, as <a href="/wiki/Decarburization" title="Decarburization">decarburizing</a> the pig iron into steel was an extremely tedious process using medieval technology.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pig_iron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Traditionally, pig iron was worked into <a href="/wiki/Wrought_iron" title="Wrought iron">wrought iron</a> in <a href="/wiki/Finery_forge" title="Finery forge">finery forges</a>, later <a href="/wiki/Puddling_furnace" class="mw-redirect" title="Puddling furnace">puddling furnaces</a>, and more recently into <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In these processes, pig iron is melted and a strong current of air is directed over it while it is stirred or agitated. This causes the dissolved impurities (such as silicon) to be thoroughly oxidized. An intermediate product of puddling is known as <i>refined pig iron</i>, <i>finers metal</i>, or <i>refined iron</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-em_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-em-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pig iron can also be used to produce <a href="/wiki/Gray_iron" title="Gray iron">gray iron</a>. This is achieved by remelting pig iron, often along with substantial quantities of steel and <a href="/wiki/Scrap" title="Scrap">scrap</a> iron, removing undesirable contaminants, adding alloys, and adjusting the carbon content.  Some pig iron grades are suitable for producing <a href="/wiki/Ductile_iron" title="Ductile iron">ductile iron</a>.  These are high purity pig irons and depending on the grade of ductile iron being produced these pig irons may be low in the elements silicon, manganese, sulfur and phosphorus.  These types of pig iron are used to dilute all the elements (except carbon) in a ductile iron charge which may be harmful to the ductile iron process.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_uses">Modern uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pig_iron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Until recently, pig iron was typically poured directly out of the bottom of the <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a> through a <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/trough" class="extiw" title="wikt:trough">trough</a> into a <a href="/wiki/Ladle_(metallurgy)" title="Ladle (metallurgy)">ladle</a> car for transfer to the <a href="/wiki/Steel_mill" title="Steel mill">steel mill</a> in mostly liquid form; in this state, the pig iron was referred to as <i>hot metal</i>. The hot metal was then poured into a <a href="/wiki/Steelmaking" title="Steelmaking">steelmaking</a> vessel to produce steel, typically an <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc_furnace" title="Electric arc furnace">electric arc furnace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Induction_furnace" title="Induction furnace">induction furnace</a> or <a href="/wiki/Basic_oxygen_furnace" class="mw-redirect" title="Basic oxygen furnace">basic oxygen furnace</a>, where the excess carbon is burned off and the <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a> composition controlled. Earlier processes for this included the <a href="/wiki/Finery_forge" title="Finery forge">finery forge</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Puddling_furnace" class="mw-redirect" title="Puddling furnace">puddling furnace</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bessemer_process" title="Bessemer process">Bessemer process</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Open_hearth_furnace" title="Open hearth furnace">open hearth furnace</a>.
</p><p>Modern steel mills and <a href="/wiki/Direct_reduced_iron" title="Direct reduced iron">direct-reduction</a> iron plants transfer the molten iron to a ladle for immediate use in the steel making furnaces or cast it into pigs on a pig-casting machine for reuse or resale. Modern pig casting machines produce stick pigs, which break into smaller 4–10&#160;kg piglets at discharge.
</p>
