<p><b>Thorium monoxide</b> (<b>thorium(II) oxide</b>), is the <a href="/wiki/Binary_compound" class="mw-redirect" title="Binary compound">binary</a> <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> of <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a> having chemical formula ThO. The <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">covalent bond</a> in this <a href="/wiki/Diatomic_molecule" title="Diatomic molecule">diatomic molecule</a> is highly <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">polar</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">electric field</a> between the two atoms has been calculated to be 84&#160;gigavolts per centimeter, one of the largest known internal electric fields.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Simple <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> of thorium in air produces <a href="/wiki/Thorium_dioxide" title="Thorium dioxide">thorium dioxide</a>. However, <a href="/wiki/Laser_ablation" title="Laser ablation">laser ablation</a> of thorium in the presence of oxygen gives the monoxide.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, exposure of a thin film of thorium to low-pressure oxygen at medium temperature forms a rapidly growing layer of thorium monoxide under a more-stable surface coating of the dioxide.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At extremely high temperatures, thorium dioxide can convert to the monoxide either in by an <a href="/wiki/Disproportionation_reaction" class="mw-redirect" title="Disproportionation reaction">disproportionation reaction</a> (equilibrium with liquid thorium metal) above 1,850&#160;K (1,580&#160;°C; 2,870&#160;°F) or by simple dissociation (evolution of oxygen) above 2,500&#160;K (2,230&#160;°C; 4,040&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-Hoch1954_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoch1954-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
