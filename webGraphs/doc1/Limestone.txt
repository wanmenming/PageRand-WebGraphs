<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Limestone</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Limestone</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rock</a>, composed mainly of skeletal fragments of marine organisms such as <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">coral</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forams" class="mw-redirect" title="Forams">forams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Molluscs" class="mw-redirect" title="Molluscs">molluscs</a>. Its major materials are the <a href="/wiki/Minerals" class="mw-redirect" title="Minerals">minerals</a> <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a>, which are different <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">crystal forms</a> of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> (CaCO<sub>3</sub>).
</p><p>About 10% of sedimentary rocks are limestones.  The <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a> of limestone in water and weak acid solutions leads to <a href="/wiki/Karst" title="Karst">karst</a> landscapes, in which water erodes the limestone over thousands to millions of years. Most <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">cave</a> systems are through limestone bedrock.
</p><p>Limestone has numerous uses: as a <a href="/wiki/Building_material" title="Building material">building material</a>, an essential component of <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> (<a href="/wiki/Portland_cement" title="Portland cement">Portland cement</a>), as aggregate for the base of roads, as white pigment or filler in products such as <a href="/wiki/Toothpaste" title="Toothpaste">toothpaste</a> or <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">paints</a>, as a chemical <a href="/wiki/Feedstock" class="mw-redirect" title="Feedstock">feedstock</a> for the production of <a href="/wiki/Lime_(material)" title="Lime (material)">lime</a>, as a <a href="/wiki/Soil_conditioner" title="Soil conditioner">soil conditioner</a>, or as a popular decorative addition to rock gardens.
</p>

<p>Like most other sedimentary rocks, most limestone is composed of grains. Most grains in limestone are skeletal fragments of marine organisms such as <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">coral</a> or <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminifera</a>. These organisms secrete shells made of aragonite or calcite, and leave these shells behind when they die. Other <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> grains comprising limestones are <a href="/wiki/Ooids" class="mw-redirect" title="Ooids">ooids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peloids" class="mw-redirect" title="Peloids">peloids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intraclasts" title="Intraclasts">intraclasts</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Extraclasts&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Extraclasts (page does not exist)">extraclasts</a>.
</p><p>Limestone often contains variable amounts of <a href="/wiki/Silica" class="mw-redirect" title="Silica">silica</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Chert" title="Chert">chert</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chalcedony" title="Chalcedony">chalcedony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jasper" title="Jasper">jasper</a>, etc.) or siliceous skeletal fragment (sponge spicules, <a href="/wiki/Diatoms" class="mw-redirect" title="Diatoms">diatoms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radiolarians" class="mw-redirect" title="Radiolarians">radiolarians</a>), and varying amounts of <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a> (<a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">terrestrial</a> <a href="/wiki/Detritus" title="Detritus">detritus</a>) carried in by rivers.
</p><p>Some limestones do not consist of grains, and are formed by the chemical <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry)" title="Precipitation (chemistry)">precipitation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a>, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Travertine" title="Travertine">travertine</a>. Secondary calcite may be deposited by <a href="/wiki/Supersaturation" title="Supersaturation">supersaturated</a> <a href="/wiki/Meteoric_water" title="Meteoric water">meteoric waters</a> (<a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> that <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry)" title="Precipitation (chemistry)">precipitates</a> the material in caves). This produces <a href="/wiki/Speleothem" title="Speleothem">speleothems</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Stalagmite" title="Stalagmite">stalagmites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stalactite" title="Stalactite">stalactites</a>. Another form taken by calcite is oolitic limestone, which can be recognized by its granular (oolite) appearance.
</p><p>The primary source of the calcite in limestone is most commonly <a href="/wiki/Marine_biology" title="Marine biology">marine organisms</a>. Some of these organisms can construct mounds of rock known as reefs, building upon past generations. Below about 3,000 meters, water pressure and temperature conditions cause the dissolution of calcite to increase nonlinearly, so limestone typically does not form in deeper waters (see <a href="/wiki/Lysocline" title="Lysocline">lysocline</a>). Limestones may also form in <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lacustrine</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Evaporite" title="Evaporite">evaporite</a> <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_depositional_environment" class="mw-redirect" title="Sedimentary depositional environment">depositional environments</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Calcite can be <a href="/wiki/Solvation" title="Solvation">dissolved</a> or <a href="/wiki/Precipitate" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitate">precipitated</a> by groundwater, depending on several factors, including the water temperature, <a href="/wiki/Acidity" class="mw-redirect" title="Acidity">pH</a>, and dissolved <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ion</a> concentrations. Calcite exhibits an unusual characteristic called <a href="/wiki/Solubility#Temperature" title="Solubility">retrograde solubility</a>, in which it becomes less soluble in water as the temperature increases.
</p><p>Impurities (such as <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a>, sand, organic remains, <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxide</a>, and other materials) will cause limestones to exhibit different colors, especially with <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathered</a> surfaces.
</p><p>Limestone may be crystalline, <a href="/wiki/Clastic_rock" title="Clastic rock">clastic</a>, granular, or massive, depending on the method of formation. Crystals of calcite, <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">quartz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Barite" class="mw-redirect" title="Barite">barite</a> may line small cavities in the rock. When conditions are right for precipitation, calcite forms mineral coatings that cement the existing rock grains together, or it can fill fractures.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Travertine" title="Travertine">Travertine</a> is a banded, compact variety of limestone formed along streams, particularly where there are waterfalls and around hot or cold springs. Calcium carbonate is deposited where evaporation of the water leaves a solution supersaturated with the chemical constituents of calcite. <a href="/wiki/Tufa" title="Tufa">Tufa</a>, a porous or cellular variety of travertine, is found near waterfalls. <a href="/wiki/Coquina" title="Coquina">Coquina</a> is a poorly consolidated limestone composed of pieces of <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">coral</a> or <a href="/wiki/Animal_shell" class="mw-redirect" title="Animal shell">shells</a>.
</p><p>During regional <a href="/wiki/Metamorphism" title="Metamorphism">metamorphism</a> that occurs during the mountain building process (<a href="/wiki/Orogeny" title="Orogeny">orogeny</a>), limestone recrystallizes into <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a>.
</p><p>Limestone is a <a href="/wiki/Parent_material" title="Parent material">parent material</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mollisol" title="Mollisol">Mollisol</a> soil group.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_types_of_limestone" title="List of types of limestone">List of types of limestone</a></div>
<p>Two major classification schemes, the Folk and the Dunham, are used for identifying limestone and carbonate rocks.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Folk_classification">Folk classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Folk classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Folk_classification" title="Folk classification">Folk classification</a></div>
<p>Robert L. Folk developed a classification system that places primary emphasis on the detailed composition of grains and interstitial material in carbonate rocks. Based on composition, there are three main components: allochems (grains), matrix (mostly micrite), and cement (sparite). The Folk system uses two-part names; the first refers to the grains and the second is the root. It is helpful to have a petrographic microscope when using the Folk scheme, because it is easier to determine the components present in each sample.<sup id="cite_ref-Folk_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Folk-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dunham_classification">Dunham classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dunham classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dunham_classification" title="Dunham classification">Dunham classification</a></div>
<p>The Dunham scheme focuses on depositional textures. Each name is based upon the texture of the grains that make up the limestone. Robert J. Dunham published his system for limestone in 1962; it focuses on the depositional fabric of carbonate rocks. Dunham divides the rocks into four main groups based on relative proportions of coarser clastic particles. Dunham names are essentially for rock families. His efforts deal with the question of whether or not the grains were originally in mutual contact, and therefore self-supporting, or whether the rock is characterized by the presence of frame builders and algal mats. Unlike the Folk scheme, Dunham deals with the original porosity of the rock. The Dunham scheme is more useful for hand samples because it is based on texture, not the grains in the sample.<sup id="cite_ref-Dunham_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunham-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limestone_landscape">Limestone landscape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Limestone landscape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Karst_topography" class="mw-redirect" title="Karst topography">Karst topography</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Maczuga_Herkulesa_(background_Castle_Pieskowa_Ska%C5%82a).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Maczuga_Herkulesa_%28background_Castle_Pieskowa_Ska%C5%82a%29.jpg/170px-Maczuga_Herkulesa_%28background_Castle_Pieskowa_Ska%C5%82a%29.jpg" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Maczuga_Herkulesa_%28background_Castle_Pieskowa_Ska%C5%82a%29.jpg/255px-Maczuga_Herkulesa_%28background_Castle_Pieskowa_Ska%C5%82a%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Maczuga_Herkulesa_%28background_Castle_Pieskowa_Ska%C5%82a%29.jpg/340px-Maczuga_Herkulesa_%28background_Castle_Pieskowa_Ska%C5%82a%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1704" data-file-height="2272" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Maczuga_Herkulesa_(background_Castle_Pieskowa_Ska%C5%82a).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Maczuga_Herkulesa" title="Maczuga Herkulesa">The Cudgel of Hercules</a></i>, a tall limestone rock (<a href="/wiki/Pieskowa_Ska%C5%82a" title="Pieskowa Skała">Pieskowa Skała Castle</a> in the background)</div></div></div>
<p>About 10% of all sedimentary rocks are limestones.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Limestone is partially soluble, especially in acid, and therefore forms many erosional landforms.  These include <a href="/wiki/Limestone_pavement" title="Limestone pavement">limestone pavements</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pot_hole" class="mw-redirect" title="Pot hole">pot holes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cenote" title="Cenote">cenotes</a>, caves and gorges.  Such erosion landscapes are known as <a href="/wiki/Karsts" class="mw-redirect" title="Karsts">karsts</a>.  Limestone is less <a href="/wiki/Resistance_(geology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Resistance (geology)">resistant</a> than most <a href="/wiki/Igneous" class="mw-redirect" title="Igneous">igneous</a> rocks, but more resistant than most other <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a>.  It is therefore usually associated with hills and <a href="/wiki/Downland" title="Downland">downland</a>, and occurs in regions with other sedimentary rocks, typically clays.
</p><p>Karst <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a> and caves develop in limestone rocks due to their <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a> in dilute <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acidic</a> <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>.  The <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">solubility</a> of limestone in water and weak acid solutions leads to <a href="/wiki/Karst" title="Karst">karst</a> landscapes. Regions overlying limestone bedrock tend to have fewer visible above-ground sources (ponds and streams), as surface water easily drains downward through <a href="/wiki/Joint_(geology)" title="Joint (geology)">joints</a> in the limestone. While draining, water and organic acid from the soil slowly (over thousands or millions of years) enlarges these cracks, dissolving the calcium carbonate and carrying it away in <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a>. Most <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">cave</a> systems are through limestone bedrock.  Cooling groundwater or mixing of different groundwaters will also create conditions suitable for cave formation.
</p><p>Coastal limestones are often eroded by organisms which bore into the rock by various means. This process is known as <a href="/wiki/Bioerosion" title="Bioerosion">bioerosion</a>.  It is most common in the tropics, and it is known throughout the <a href="/wiki/Fossil_record" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil record">fossil record</a> (see Taylor and Wilson, 2003).
</p><p>Bands of limestone emerge from the Earth's surface in often spectacular rocky outcrops and islands. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/The_Burren" title="The Burren">Burren</a> in Co. Clare, Ireland; the <a href="/wiki/Verdon_Gorge" title="Verdon Gorge">Verdon Gorge</a> in France; <a href="/wiki/Malham_Cove" title="Malham Cove">Malham Cove</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Yorkshire" title="North Yorkshire">North Yorkshire</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> England; the <a href="/wiki/Great_Orme" title="Great Orme">Great Orme</a> in Wales&#160;; on <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A5r%C3%B6" title="Fårö">Fårö</a> near the Swedish island of <a href="/wiki/Gotland" title="Gotland">Gotland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Niagara_Escarpment" title="Niagara Escarpment">Niagara Escarpment</a> in Canada/United States, <a href="/wiki/Notch_Peak" title="Notch Peak">Notch Peak</a> in Utah, the <a href="/wiki/Ha_Long_Bay" class="mw-redirect" title="Ha Long Bay">Ha Long Bay</a> National Park in Vietnam and the hills around the <a href="/wiki/Lijiang_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Lijiang River">Lijiang River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guilin" title="Guilin">Guilin</a> city in China.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a>, islands off the south coast of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, are composed mainly of <a href="/wiki/Oolite" title="Oolite">oolitic</a> limestone (the Lower Keys) and the carbonate skeletons of <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">coral</a> reefs (the Upper Keys), which thrived in the area during interglacial periods when sea level was higher than at present.
</p><p>Unique habitats are found on <a href="/wiki/Alvar" title="Alvar">alvars</a>, extremely level expanses of limestone with thin soil mantles.  The largest such expanse in Europe is the <a href="/wiki/Stora_Alvaret" title="Stora Alvaret">Stora Alvaret</a> on the island of <a href="/wiki/%C3%96land" title="Öland">Öland</a>, Sweden. Another area with large quantities of limestone is the island of Gotland, Sweden. Huge quarries in northwestern Europe, such as those of Mount Saint Peter (Belgium/Netherlands), extend for more than a hundred kilometers.
</p><p>The world's largest limestone quarry is at <a href="/wiki/Michigan_Limestone_and_Chemical_Company" title="Michigan Limestone and Chemical Company">Michigan Limestone and Chemical Company</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rogers_City,_Michigan" title="Rogers City, Michigan">Rogers City, Michigan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Facade_Hagar_Qim.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Facade_Hagar_Qim.jpg/220px-Facade_Hagar_Qim.jpg" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Facade_Hagar_Qim.jpg/330px-Facade_Hagar_Qim.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Facade_Hagar_Qim.jpg/440px-Facade_Hagar_Qim.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1687" data-file-height="1106" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Facade_Hagar_Qim.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Megalithic_Temples_of_Malta" title="Megalithic Temples of Malta">Megalithic Temples of Malta</a> such as <a href="/wiki/%C4%A6a%C4%A1ar_Qim" title="Ħaġar Qim">Ħaġar Qim</a> are built entirely of limestone. They are among the oldest free-standing structures in existence.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Limestone is very common in architecture, especially in Europe and North America. Many landmarks across the world, including the <a href="/wiki/Great_Pyramid_of_Giza" title="Great Pyramid of Giza">Great Pyramid</a> and its associated <a href="/wiki/Giza_pyramid_complex" title="Giza pyramid complex">complex</a> in <a href="/wiki/Giza,_Egypt" class="mw-redirect" title="Giza, Egypt">Giza, Egypt</a>, were made of limestone. So many buildings in <a href="/wiki/Kingston,_Ontario" title="Kingston, Ontario">Kingston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, Canada were, and continue to be, constructed from it that it is nicknamed the 'Limestone City'.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> On the island of <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>, a variety of limestone called Globigerina limestone was, for a long time, the only building material available, and is still very frequently used on all types of buildings and sculptures. Limestone is readily available and relatively easy to cut into blocks or more elaborate carving.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Ancient American sculptors valued limestone because it was easy to work and good for fine detail. Going back to the Late Preclassic period (by 200–100 BCE), the <a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">Maya civilization</a> (Ancient Mexico) created refined sculpture using limestone because of these excellent carving properties. The Maya would decorate the ceilings of their sacred buildings (known as <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Maya_art" title="Ancient Maya art">lintels</a>) and cover the walls with carved limestone panels. Carved on these sculptures were political and social stories, and this helped communicate messages of the king to his people.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Limestone is long-lasting and stands up well to exposure, which explains why many limestone ruins survive. However, it is very heavy, making it impractical for tall buildings, and relatively expensive as a building material.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gizeh_Cheops_BW_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Gizeh_Cheops_BW_1.jpg/220px-Gizeh_Cheops_BW_1.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Gizeh_Cheops_BW_1.jpg/330px-Gizeh_Cheops_BW_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Gizeh_Cheops_BW_1.jpg/440px-Gizeh_Cheops_BW_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3443" data-file-height="2293" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gizeh_Cheops_BW_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Pyramid_of_Giza" title="Great Pyramid of Giza">Great Pyramid of Giza</a>, one of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Wonders_of_the_Ancient_World" title="Seven Wonders of the Ancient World">Seven Wonders of the Ancient World</a> had an outside cover made entirely from limestone.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Riley_(Kansas)_County_Courthouse_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Riley_%28Kansas%29_County_Courthouse_1.jpg/220px-Riley_%28Kansas%29_County_Courthouse_1.jpg" width="220" height="298" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Riley_%28Kansas%29_County_Courthouse_1.jpg/330px-Riley_%28Kansas%29_County_Courthouse_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Riley_%28Kansas%29_County_Courthouse_1.jpg/440px-Riley_%28Kansas%29_County_Courthouse_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1088" data-file-height="1472" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Riley_(Kansas)_County_Courthouse_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Riley_County" class="mw-redirect" title="Riley County">Riley County</a> Courthouse built of limestone in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan,_Kansas" title="Manhattan, Kansas">Manhattan, Kansas</a>, USA</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Litography_press_with_map_of_Moosburg_01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Litography_press_with_map_of_Moosburg_01.jpg/220px-Litography_press_with_map_of_Moosburg_01.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Litography_press_with_map_of_Moosburg_01.jpg/330px-Litography_press_with_map_of_Moosburg_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Litography_press_with_map_of_Moosburg_01.jpg/440px-Litography_press_with_map_of_Moosburg_01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Litography_press_with_map_of_Moosburg_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A limestone plate with a negative map of <a href="/wiki/Moosburg" title="Moosburg">Moosburg</a> in Bavaria is prepared for a <a href="/wiki/Lithography" title="Lithography">lithography</a> print.</div></div></div>
<p>Limestone was most popular in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Train stations, banks and other structures from that era are normally made of limestone. It is used as a facade on some skyscrapers, but only in thin plates for covering, rather than solid blocks. In the United States, Indiana, most notably the Bloomington area, has long been a source of high quality quarried limestone, called <a href="/wiki/Indiana_limestone" class="mw-redirect" title="Indiana limestone">Indiana limestone</a>. Many famous buildings in London are built from <a href="/wiki/Portland_limestone" class="mw-redirect" title="Portland limestone">Portland limestone</a>.
</p><p>Limestone was also a very popular building block in the Middle Ages in the areas where it occurred, since it is hard, durable, and commonly occurs in easily accessible surface exposures. Many medieval churches and castles in Europe are made of limestone.  <a href="/wiki/Beer_stone" class="mw-redirect" title="Beer stone">Beer stone</a> was a popular kind of limestone for medieval buildings in southern England.
</p><p>Limestone and (to a lesser extent) marble are reactive to acid solutions, making <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a> a significant problem to the preservation of artifacts made from this stone. Many limestone statues and building surfaces have suffered severe damage due to acid rain. Likewise limestone gravel has been used to protect lakes vulnerable to acid rain, acting as a pH buffering agent.  Acid-based cleaning chemicals can also etch limestone, which should only be cleaned with a neutral or mild alkaline-based cleaner.
</p><p>Other uses include:
</p>
<ul><li>It is the raw material for the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Quicklime" class="mw-redirect" title="Quicklime">quicklime</a> (calcium oxide), <a href="/wiki/Slaked_lime" class="mw-redirect" title="Slaked lime">slaked lime</a> (calcium hydroxide), <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(masonry)" title="Mortar (masonry)">mortar</a>.</li>
<li>Pulverized limestone is used as a soil conditioner to neutralize acidic soils (<a href="/wiki/Agricultural_lime" title="Agricultural lime">agricultural lime</a>).</li>
<li>Is crushed for use as <a href="/wiki/Construction_aggregate" title="Construction aggregate">aggregate</a>—the solid base for many roads as well as in <a href="/wiki/Asphalt_concrete" title="Asphalt concrete">asphalt concrete</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Geological_formation" title="Geological formation">Geological formations</a> of limestone are among the best <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_reservoir" title="Petroleum reservoir">petroleum reservoirs</a>;</li>
<li>As a <a href="/wiki/Reagent" title="Reagent">reagent</a> in <a href="/wiki/Flue-gas_desulfurization" title="Flue-gas desulfurization">flue-gas desulfurization</a>, it reacts with <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> for air pollution control.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Glass_making" class="mw-redirect" title="Glass making">Glass making</a>, in some circumstances, uses limestone.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>It is added to toothpaste, paper, plastics, paint, tiles, and other materials as both white pigment and a cheap filler.</li>
<li>It can suppress methane explosions in underground coal mines.</li>
<li>Purified, it is added to bread and cereals as a source of calcium.</li>
<li>Calcium levels in livestock feed are supplemented with it, such as for poultry (when ground up).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>It can be used for remineralizing and increasing the alkalinity of purified water to prevent pipe corrosion and to restore essential nutrient levels.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Used in <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnaces</a>, limestone binds with silica and other impurities to remove them from the iron.</li>
<li>It is used in sculptures because of its suitability for carving.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occupational_safety_and_health">Occupational safety and health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Occupational safety and health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>People can be exposed to limestone in the workplace by inhalation of and eye contact with the dust.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">Occupational Safety and Health Administration</a> (OSHA) has set the legal limit (<a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">permissible exposure limit</a>) for limestone exposure in the workplace as 15&#160;mg/m<sup>3</sup> total exposure and 5&#160;mg/m<sup>3</sup> respiratory exposure over an 8-hour workday. The <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health" title="National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health">National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health</a> (NIOSH) has set a <a href="/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit" title="Recommended exposure limit">recommended exposure limit</a> (REL) of 10&#160;mg/m<sup>3</sup> total exposure and 5&#160;mg/m<sup>3</sup> respiratory exposure over an 8-hour workday.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Degradation_by_organisms">Degradation by organisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Limestone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Degradation by organisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacterium" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyanobacterium">cyanobacterium</a> <i>Hyella balani</i> can bore through limestone; as can the <a href="/wiki/Green_alga" class="mw-redirect" title="Green alga">green alga</a> <i>Eugamantia sacculata</i> and the <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungus</a> <i>Ostracolaba implexa</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
