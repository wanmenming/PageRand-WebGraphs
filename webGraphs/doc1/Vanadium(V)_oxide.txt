<p><b>Vanadium(V) oxide</b>  (<i>vanadia</i>) is the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a>  V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>.  Commonly known as <b>vanadium pentoxide</b>, it is a brown/yellow solid, although when freshly precipitated from aqueous solution, its colour is deep orange.  Because of its high <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a>, it is both an <a href="/wiki/Amphoteric" class="mw-redirect" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a> oxide and an <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidizing agent</a>. From the industrial perspective, it is the most important compound of <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a>, being the principal precursor to alloys of vanadium and is a widely used industrial catalyst.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The mineral form of this compound, shcherbinaite, is extremely rare, almost always found among <a href="/wiki/Fumaroles" class="mw-redirect" title="Fumaroles">fumaroles</a>. A mineral <a href="/wiki/Hydrate" title="Hydrate">trihydrate</a>, V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O, is also known under the name of navajoite.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(V)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction_to_lower_oxides">Reduction to lower oxides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(V)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reduction to lower oxides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Upon heating a mixture of the pentoxide and V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, comproportionation occurs to give <a href="/wiki/Vanadium(IV)_oxide" title="Vanadium(IV) oxide">vanadium(IV) oxide</a>, as a deep-blue solid:<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>  +  V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>  →   4 VO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The reduction can also be effected by <a href="/wiki/Oxalic_acid" title="Oxalic acid">oxalic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>. Further reduction using <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> or excess CO can lead to complex mixtures of oxides such as V<sub>4</sub>O<sub>7</sub> and V<sub>5</sub>O<sub>9</sub> before black V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is reached. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acid-base_reactions">Acid-base reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(V)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Acid-base reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> it is an <a href="/wiki/Amphoteric" class="mw-redirect" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a> oxide. Unlike most metal oxides, it <a href="/wiki/Solubility" title="Solubility">dissolves</a> slightly in water to give a pale yellow, acidic solution. Thus V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> reacts with strong non-reducing acids to form solutions containing the pale yellow salts containing dioxovanadium(V) centers:
</p>
<dl><dd>V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> + 2 HNO<sub>3</sub>  →  2 VO<sub>2</sub>(NO<sub>3</sub>) + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>It also reacts with strong <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkali</a> to form polyoxovanadates, which have a complex structure that depends on <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-G&amp;E_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;E-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> If excess aqueous <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> is used, the product is a colourless <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_orthovanadate" title="Sodium orthovanadate">sodium orthovanadate</a>, Na<sub>3</sub>VO<sub>4</sub>. If acid is slowly added to a solution of Na<sub>3</sub>VO<sub>4</sub>, the colour gradually deepens through orange to red before brown hydrated V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> precipitates around pH&#160;2. These solutions contain mainly the ions HVO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup> and V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub><sup>4−</sup> between pH&#160;9 and pH&#160;13, but below pH&#160;9 more exotic species such as V<sub>4</sub>O<sub>12</sub><sup>4−</sup> and HV<sub>10</sub>O<sub>28</sub><sup>5−</sup> (<a href="/wiki/Decavanadate" class="mw-redirect" title="Decavanadate">decavanadate</a>) predominate.
</p><p>Upon treatment with <a href="/wiki/Thionyl_chloride" title="Thionyl chloride">thionyl chloride</a>, it converts to the volatile liquid <a href="/wiki/Vanadium_oxychloride" class="mw-redirect" title="Vanadium oxychloride">vanadium oxychloride</a>, VOCl<sub>3</sub>:<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> +  3 SOCl<sub>2</sub> → 2 VOCl<sub>3</sub> + 3 SO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_redox_reactions">Other redox reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(V)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other redox reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">Hydrochloric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrobromic_acid" title="Hydrobromic acid">hydrobromic acid</a> are oxidised to the corresponding <a href="/wiki/Halogen" title="Halogen">halogen</a>, e.g.,
</p>
<dl><dd>V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> +  6HCl + 7H<sub>2</sub>O → 2[VO(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>5</sub>]<sup>2+</sup>  +  4Cl<sup>−</sup> + Cl<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Vanadates or <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadyl(V)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Vanadyl(V) (page does not exist)">vanadyl(V)</a> compounds in acid solution are reduced by zinc amalgam through the colourful pathway:
</p>

<p>The ions are all hydrated to varying degrees.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(V)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a href="/wiki/File:V2O5powder.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/V2O5powder.jpg/140px-V2O5powder.jpg" width="140" height="101" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/V2O5powder.jpg/210px-V2O5powder.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/V2O5powder.jpg/280px-V2O5powder.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="505" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:V2O5powder.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The orange, partly hydrated form of V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a href="/wiki/File:V2O5_hydrous.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/V2O5_hydrous.jpg/140px-V2O5_hydrous.jpg" width="140" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/V2O5_hydrous.jpg/210px-V2O5_hydrous.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/V2O5_hydrous.jpg/280px-V2O5_hydrous.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:V2O5_hydrous.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Precipitate of "red cake", which is hydrous V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub></div></div></div>
<p>Technical grade V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is produced as a black powder used for the production of <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a> metal and <a href="/wiki/Ferrovanadium" title="Ferrovanadium">ferrovanadium</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-G&amp;E_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;E-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> A vanadium ore or vanadium-rich residue is treated with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Sodium_metavanadate" title="Sodium metavanadate">sodium metavanadate</a>, NaVO<sub>3</sub>. This material is then acidified to pH&#160;2–3 using <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub></a> to yield a precipitate of "red cake" (see <a href="#Chemical_properties">above</a>). The red cake is then melted at 690&#160;°C to produce the crude V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>.
</p><p>Vanadium(V) oxide is produced when <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a> metal is heated with excess <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, but this product is contaminated with other, lower oxides. A more satisfactory laboratory preparation involves the decomposition of <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_metavanadate" title="Ammonium metavanadate">ammonium metavanadate</a> at 500-550&#160;°C:<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 NH<sub>4</sub>VO<sub>3</sub>  →  V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> +  2 NH<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(V)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ferrovanadium_production">Ferrovanadium production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(V)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ferrovanadium production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In terms of quantity, the dominant use for vanadium(V) oxide is in the production of <a href="/wiki/Ferrovanadium" title="Ferrovanadium">ferrovanadium</a> (see <a href="#Preparation">above</a>). The oxide is heated with scrap <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ferrosilicon" title="Ferrosilicon">ferrosilicon</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Lime_(material)" title="Lime (material)">lime</a> added to form a <a href="/wiki/Calcium_silicate" title="Calcium silicate">calcium silicate</a> <a href="/wiki/Slag" title="Slag">slag</a>. <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">Aluminium</a> may also be used, producing the iron-vanadium alloy along with <a href="/wiki/Alumina" class="mw-redirect" title="Alumina">alumina</a> as a by-product.<sup id="cite_ref-G&amp;E_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;E-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sulfuric_acid_production">Sulfuric acid production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(V)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sulfuric acid production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another important use of vanadium(V) oxide is in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>, an important industrial chemical with an annual worldwide production of 165&#160;million metric tons in 2001, with an approximate value of US$8&#160;billion. Vanadium(V) oxide serves the crucial purpose of <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalyst">catalysing</a> the mildly <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">exothermic</a> <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidation</a> of sulfur dioxide to <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide" title="Sulfur trioxide">sulfur trioxide</a> by air in the <a href="/wiki/Contact_process" title="Contact process">contact process</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 SO<sub>2</sub>  +  O<sub>2</sub>  ⇌  2 SO<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The discovery of this simple reaction, for which V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is the most effective catalyst, allowed sulfuric acid to become the cheap commodity chemical it is today.  The reaction is performed between 400 and 620&#160;°C; below 400&#160;°C the V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is inactive as a catalyst, and above 620&#160;°C it begins to break down. Since it is known that V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> can be reduced to VO<sub>2</sub> by SO<sub>2</sub>, one likely catalytic cycle is as follows:
</p>
<dl><dd>SO<sub>2</sub>  +  V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>  →  SO<sub>3</sub>  +  2VO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>followed by
</p>
<dl><dd>2VO<sub>2</sub> +½O<sub>2</sub>  →  V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub></dd></dl>
<p>It is also used as catalyst in the <a href="/wiki/Selective_catalytic_reduction" title="Selective catalytic reduction">selective catalytic reduction</a> (SCR) of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide#NOx" title="Nitrogen oxide">NO<sub><i>x</i></sub></a> emissions in some <a href="/wiki/Power_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Power plant">power plants</a>.  Due to its effectiveness in converting sulfur dioxide into sulfur trioxide, and thereby sulfuric acid, special care must be taken with the operating temperatures and placement of a power plant's SCR unit when firing sulfur-containing fuels.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_oxidations">Other oxidations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(V)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other oxidations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Maleic_anhydride" title="Maleic anhydride">Maleic anhydride</a> is produced by the V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>-catalysed oxidation of butane with air:
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>4</sub>H<sub>10</sub>  +  4 O<sub>2</sub>   →   C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>2</sub>(CO)<sub>2</sub>O  +  8 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Maleic anhydride is used for the production of <a href="/wiki/Polyester" title="Polyester">polyester</a> resins and <a href="/wiki/Glycerin#Alkyd_resins_.28plastics.29_and_cellophane" class="mw-redirect" title="Glycerin">alkyd resins</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Phthalic_anhydride" title="Phthalic anhydride">Phthalic anhydride</a> is produced similarly by V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>-catalysed oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Ortho-xylene" class="mw-redirect" title="Ortho-xylene">ortho-xylene</a> or <a href="/wiki/Naphthalene" title="Naphthalene">naphthalene</a> at 350–400&#160;°C.  The equation is for the xylene oxidation:
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>(CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>  +  3 O<sub>2</sub>   →   C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>(CO)<sub>2</sub>O +  3 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Phthalic anhydride is a precursor to <a href="/wiki/Plasticiser" class="mw-redirect" title="Plasticiser">plasticisers</a>, used for conferring pliability to polymers.
</p><p>A variety of other industrial compounds are produced similarly, including <a href="/wiki/Adipic_acid" title="Adipic acid">adipic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acrylic_acid" title="Acrylic acid">acrylic acid</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Oxalic_acid" title="Oxalic acid">oxalic acid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anthraquinone" title="Anthraquinone">anthraquinone</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_applications">Other applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(V)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to its high coefficient of thermal resistance, vanadium(V) oxide finds use as a detector material in <a href="/wiki/Bolometer" title="Bolometer">bolometers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microbolometer" title="Microbolometer">microbolometer</a> arrays for <a href="/wiki/Thermal_imaging" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermal imaging">thermal imaging</a>. It also finds application as an ethanol sensor in ppm levels (up to 0.1&#160;ppm).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Vanadium_redox_battery" title="Vanadium redox battery">Vanadium redox batteries</a> are a type of <a href="/wiki/Flow_battery" title="Flow battery">flow battery</a> used for energy storage, including large power facilities such as <a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">wind farms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_activity">Biological activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(V)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Biological activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:V2o5label.jpg" class="image"><img alt="V2o5label.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/66/V2o5label.jpg/220px-V2o5label.jpg" width="220" height="186" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/66/V2o5label.jpg/330px-V2o5label.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/66/V2o5label.jpg/440px-V2o5label.jpg 2x" data-file-width="450" data-file-height="380" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:V2o5label.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>
<p>Vanadium(V) oxide exhibits very modest acute toxicity to humans, with an <a href="/wiki/LD50" class="mw-redirect" title="LD50">LD50</a> of about 470&#160;mg/kg. The greater hazard is with inhalation of the dust, where the <a href="/wiki/LD50" class="mw-redirect" title="LD50">LD50</a> ranges from 4–11&#160;mg/kg for a 14-day exposure.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Vanadate (<span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3−</sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>), formed by hydrolysis of V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> at high pH, appears to inhibit enzymes that process phosphate (PO<sub>4</sub><sup>3−</sup>). However the mode of action remains elusive.<sup id="cite_ref-G&amp;E_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;E-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
