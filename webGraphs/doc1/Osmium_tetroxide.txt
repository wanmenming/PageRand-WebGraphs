<p><b>Osmium tetroxide</b> (also <b>osmium(VIII) oxide</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> OsO<sub>4</sub>. The compound is noteworthy for its many uses, despite its toxicity and the rarity of <a href="/wiki/Osmium" title="Osmium">osmium</a>. It also has a number of interesting properties, one being that the solid is <a href="/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry)" title="Volatility (chemistry)">volatile</a>.  The compound is colourless, but most samples appear yellow.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This is most likely due to the presence of the impurity <a href="/wiki/Osmium_dioxide" title="Osmium dioxide">OsO<sub>2</sub></a>, which is yellow-brown in colour.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:OsO4structure.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/OsO4structure.png/220px-OsO4structure.png" width="220" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/OsO4structure.png/330px-OsO4structure.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/OsO4structure.png/440px-OsO4structure.png 2x" data-file-width="1160" data-file-height="702" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:OsO4structure.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Crystal structure of OsO<sub>4</sub><sup id="cite_ref-s1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-s1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Osmium(VIII) oxide forms <a href="/wiki/Monoclinic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> crystals.<sup id="cite_ref-s1_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-s1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-niosh_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-niosh-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It has a characteristic acrid <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>-like odor.  The <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">element</a> name osmium is derived from <i>osme</i>, <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> for <i>odor</i>. OsO<sub>4</sub> is volatile: it <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(chemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sublimation (chemistry)">sublimes</a> at <a href="/wiki/Room_temperature" title="Room temperature">room temperature</a>. It is soluble in a wide range of organic solvents.  It is also moderately soluble in water, with which it reacts reversibly to form osmic acid (see below).<sup id="cite_ref-thompson_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thompson-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <i>Pure</i> osmium(VIII) oxide is probably colourless<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and it has been suggested that its yellow hue is due to <a href="/wiki/Osmium_dioxide" title="Osmium dioxide">osmium dioxide</a> (OsO<sub>2</sub>) impurities.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The osmium tetroxide molecule is tetrahedral and therefore non-polar. This nonpolarity helps OsO<sub>4</sub> penetrate charged cell membranes. OsO<sub>4</sub> is 518 times more soluble in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a> than in water.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_electron_configuration">Structure and electron configuration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure and electron configuration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Osmium" title="Osmium">osmium</a> of OsO<sub>4</sub> has an <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_number" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxidation number">oxidation number</a> of VIII, however the metal does not possess a corresponding 8+ charge as the bonding in the compound is largely <a href="/wiki/Covalent" class="mw-redirect" title="Covalent">covalent</a> in character (the <a href="/wiki/Ionization_energy" title="Ionization energy">ionization energy</a> required to produce a formal 8+ charge also far exceeds the energies available in normal chemical reactions). The osmium atom has eight <a href="/wiki/Valence_shell" class="mw-redirect" title="Valence shell">valence</a> electrons (6s<sup>2</sup>, 5d<sup>6</sup>) with double bonds to the four <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> <a href="/wiki/Ligands" class="mw-redirect" title="Ligands">ligands</a> resulting in a <a href="/wiki/16-Electron_Complexes#16e_complexes" class="mw-redirect" title="16-Electron Complexes">16 electron complex</a>. This is isoelectronic with <a href="/wiki/Permanganate" title="Permanganate">permanganate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chromate" class="mw-redirect" title="Chromate">chromate</a> ions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>OsO<sub>4</sub> is formed slowly when osmium powder reacts with O<sub>2</sub> at ambient temperature. Reaction of bulk solid requires heating to 400&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-h&amp;s_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-h&amp;s-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidation_of_alkenes">Oxidation of alkenes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Oxidation of alkenes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alkenes add to OsO<sub>4</sub> to give <a href="/wiki/Alkoxide" title="Alkoxide">diolate</a> species that hydrolyze to <i>cis</i>-diols.  The net process is called dihydroxylation. This proceeds via a [3 + 2] <a href="/wiki/Cycloaddition" title="Cycloaddition">cycloaddition</a> reaction between the OsO<sub>4</sub> and alkene to form an intermediate osmate ester which rapidly hydrolyses to yield the <a href="/wiki/Diol#Vicinal_diols" title="Diol">vicinal diol</a>. As the oxygen atoms are added in a concerted step the resulting stereochemistry is <i><a href="/wiki/Cis-trans_isomerism" class="mw-redirect" title="Cis-trans isomerism">cis</a></i>.
</p>
<dl><dd><div class="center"><div class="thumb tnone"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:602px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dihydroxylation_with_OsO4.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Dihydroxylation_with_OsO4.png/600px-Dihydroxylation_with_OsO4.png" width="600" height="113" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Dihydroxylation_with_OsO4.png/900px-Dihydroxylation_with_OsO4.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Dihydroxylation_with_OsO4.png/1200px-Dihydroxylation_with_OsO4.png 2x" data-file-width="1872" data-file-height="354" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dihydroxylation_with_OsO4.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Idealized depiction of the cis-dihydroxylation of alkenes.</div></div></div></div></dd></dl>
<p>OsO<sub>4</sub> is expensive and highly toxic, making it an unappealing reagent to use in <a href="/wiki/Stoichiometric" class="mw-redirect" title="Stoichiometric">stoichiometric</a> amounts. However its reactions are made  <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalytic</a> by adding reagents to reoxidise the Os(VI) by-product back to Os(VIII).  Typical reagents include <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub></a> (<a href="/wiki/Milas_hydroxylation" title="Milas hydroxylation">Milas hydroxylation</a>), <a href="/wiki/N-Methylmorpholine_N-oxide" title="N-Methylmorpholine N-oxide">N-methylmorpholine N-oxide</a> (<a href="/wiki/Upjohn_dihydroxylation" title="Upjohn dihydroxylation">Upjohn dihydroxylation</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Potassium_ferricyanide" title="Potassium ferricyanide">K<sub>3</sub>Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub></a>, as these will not react with the alkenes on their own. Other osmium compounds can be used as catalysts, including osmate(VI) salts ([OsO<sub>2</sub>(OH)<sub>4</sub>)]<sup>2−</sup>, and osmium trichloride hydrate (OsCl<sub>3</sub>·<i>x</i>H<sub>2</sub>O). These species oxidise to osmium(VIII) in the presence of such oxidants.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lewis bases such as tertiary <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pyridine" title="Pyridine">pyridines</a> increase the rate of dihydroxylation. This "ligand-acceleration" arises via the formation of <a href="/wiki/Adduct" title="Adduct">adduct</a> OsO<sub>4</sub>L, which adds more rapidly to the alkene. If the amine is chiral, then the dihydroxylation can proceed with enantioselectivity (see <a href="/wiki/Sharpless_asymmetric_dihydroxylation" title="Sharpless asymmetric dihydroxylation">Sharpless asymmetric dihydroxylation</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-catalysis_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-catalysis-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> OsO<sub>4</sub> does not react with most carbohydrates.<sup id="cite_ref-stain_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stain-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The process can be extended to give to <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehydes</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Lemieux%E2%80%93Johnson_oxidation" title="Lemieux–Johnson oxidation">Lemieux–Johnson oxidation</a>, which uses <a href="/wiki/Periodate" title="Periodate">periodate</a> to achieve <a href="/wiki/Diol_cleavage" class="mw-redirect" title="Diol cleavage">diol cleavage</a> and to regenerate the catalytic loading of OsO<sub>4</sub>. This process is equivalent to that of <a href="/wiki/Ozonolysis" title="Ozonolysis">ozonolysis</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/File:Lemieux%E2%80%93Johnson_oxidation.svg" class="image"><img alt="Lemieux–Johnson oxidation.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Lemieux%E2%80%93Johnson_oxidation.svg/600px-Lemieux%E2%80%93Johnson_oxidation.svg.png" width="600" height="164" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Lemieux%E2%80%93Johnson_oxidation.svg/900px-Lemieux%E2%80%93Johnson_oxidation.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Lemieux%E2%80%93Johnson_oxidation.svg/1200px-Lemieux%E2%80%93Johnson_oxidation.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="140" /></a></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coordination_chemistry">Coordination chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Coordination chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>OsO<sub>4</sub> is a <a href="/wiki/Lewis_acid" class="mw-redirect" title="Lewis acid">Lewis acid</a> and a mild oxidant. It reacts with alkaline aqueous solution to give the perosmate anion <span class="chemf nowrap">OsO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>(OH)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This species is easily reduced to <a href="/wiki/Potassium_osmate" title="Potassium osmate">osmate</a> anion, <span class="chemf nowrap">OsO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>(OH)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4−</sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>.
</p><p>When the <a href="/wiki/Lewis_base" class="mw-redirect" title="Lewis base">Lewis base</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Amine" title="Amine">amine</a>, adducts are also formed.  Thus OsO<sub>4</sub> can be stored in the form of <b>osmeth</b>, in which OsO<sub>4</sub> is <a href="/wiki/Complexation" class="mw-redirect" title="Complexation">complexed</a> with <a href="/wiki/Hexamine" class="mw-redirect" title="Hexamine">hexamine</a>. Osmeth can be dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Tetrahydrofuran" title="Tetrahydrofuran">tetrahydrofuran</a> (THF) and diluted in an aqueous <a href="/wiki/Buffer_solution" title="Buffer solution">buffer solution</a> to make a dilute (0.25%) working <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a> of OsO<sub>4</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With <a href="/wiki/Tert-Butylamine" title="Tert-Butylamine">tert-BuNH<sub>2</sub></a>, the <a href="/wiki/Imido" class="mw-redirect" title="Imido">imido</a> derivative is produced:
</p>
<dl><dd>OsO<sub>4</sub>  +  Me<sub>3</sub>CNH<sub>2</sub>   →  OsO<sub>3</sub>(NCMe<sub>3</sub>) + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Similarly, with <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">NH<sub>3</sub></a> one obtains the <a href="/wiki/Nitrido_complex" class="mw-redirect" title="Nitrido complex">nitrido complex</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>OsO<sub>4</sub> + NH<sub>3</sub> + KOH → K[Os(N)O<sub>3</sub>] + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>The [Os(N)O<sub>3</sub>]<sup>−</sup> anion is isoelectronic and isostructural with OsO<sub>4</sub>.
</p><p>OsO<sub>4</sub> is very soluble in <a href="/wiki/Tert-butyl_alcohol" class="mw-redirect" title="Tert-butyl alcohol">tert-butyl alcohol</a>. In solution, it is readily reduced by hydrogen to osmium metal. The suspended osmium metal can be used to <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalyze</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a> of a wide variety of organic chemicals containing double or triple bonds.
</p>
<dl><dd>OsO<sub>4</sub> + 4 H<sub>2</sub> → Os  + 4 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>OsO<sub>4</sub> undergoes "reductive carbonylation" with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> in methanol at 400&#160;K and 200&#160;sbar to produce the triangular cluster <a href="/wiki/Triosmium_dodecacarbonyl" title="Triosmium dodecacarbonyl">Os<sub>3</sub>(CO)<sub>12</sub></a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>3 OsO<sub>4</sub>  +  24 CO   →   Os<sub>3</sub>(CO)<sub>12</sub>  +  12 CO<sub>2</sub><sup id="cite_ref-h&amp;s_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-h&amp;s-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxofluorides">Oxofluorides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Oxofluorides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Osmium forms several oxofluorides, all of which are very sensitive to moisture.
Purple <i>cis</i>-OsO<sub>2</sub>F<sub>4</sub> forms at 77&#160;K in an anhydrous <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">HF</a> solution:<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>OsO<sub>4</sub> + 2 KrF<sub>2</sub> → <i>cis</i>-OsO<sub>2</sub>F<sub>4</sub> + 2 Kr + O<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>OsO<sub>4</sub> also reacts with F<sub>2</sub> to form yellow OsO<sub>3</sub>F<sub>2</sub>:<sup id="cite_ref-chem_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chem-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 OsO<sub>4</sub> + 2 F<sub>2</sub> → 2 OsO<sub>3</sub>F<sub>2</sub> + O<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>OsO<sub>4</sub> reacts with one equivalent of [Me<sub>4</sub>N]F at 298&#160;K and 2 equivalents at 253&#160;K:<sup id="cite_ref-h&amp;s_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-h&amp;s-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>OsO<sub>4</sub> + [Me<sub>4</sub>N]F → [Me<sub>4</sub>N][OsO<sub>4</sub>F]</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>OsO<sub>4</sub> + 2 [Me<sub>4</sub>N]F → [Me<sub>4</sub>N]<sub>2</sub>[<i>cis</i>-OsO<sub>4</sub>F<sub>2</sub>]</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_synthesis">Organic synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Organic synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In organic synthesis OsO<sub>4</sub> is widely used to oxidise <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Vicinal_(chemistry)" title="Vicinal (chemistry)">vicinal</a> diols, adding two <a href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> groups at the same side (<a href="/wiki/Syn_addition" class="mw-redirect" title="Syn addition">syn addition</a>). See reaction and mechanism above. This reaction has been made both catalytic (<a href="/wiki/Upjohn_dihydroxylation" title="Upjohn dihydroxylation">Upjohn dihydroxylation</a>) and asymmetric (<a href="/wiki/Sharpless_asymmetric_dihydroxylation" title="Sharpless asymmetric dihydroxylation">Sharpless asymmetric dihydroxylation</a>).
</p><p>Osmium(VIII) oxide is also used in catalytic amounts in the <a href="/wiki/Sharpless_oxyamination" title="Sharpless oxyamination">Sharpless oxyamination</a> to give <a href="/wiki/Vicinal_(chemistry)" title="Vicinal (chemistry)">vicinal</a> amino-alcohols.
</p><p>In combination with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_periodate" title="Sodium periodate">sodium periodate</a>, OsO<sub>4</sub> is used for the oxidative cleavage of <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Lemieux-Johnson_oxidation" class="mw-redirect" title="Lemieux-Johnson oxidation">Lemieux-Johnson oxidation</a>) when the periodate serves both to cleave the diol formed by dihydroxylation, and to reoxidize the OsO<sub>3</sub> back to OsO<sub>4</sub>. The net transformation is identical to that produced by <a href="/wiki/Ozonolysis" title="Ozonolysis">ozonolysis</a>. Below an example from the total synthesis of Isosteviol.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/File:Isosteviol-OsO4.svg" class="image"><img alt="Isosteviol-OsO4.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Isosteviol-OsO4.svg/700px-Isosteviol-OsO4.svg.png" width="700" height="153" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Isosteviol-OsO4.svg/1050px-Isosteviol-OsO4.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Isosteviol-OsO4.svg/1400px-Isosteviol-OsO4.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="3428" data-file-height="748" /></a>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_staining">Biological staining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Biological staining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>OsO<sub>4</sub> is a widely used <a href="/wiki/Staining" title="Staining">staining</a> agent used in <a href="/wiki/Transmission_electron_microscopy" title="Transmission electron microscopy">transmission electron microscopy</a> (TEM) to provide contrast to the image.<sup id="cite_ref-Bozzola_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bozzola-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> As a <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipid</a> stain, it is also useful in <a href="/wiki/Scanning_electron_microscopy" class="mw-redirect" title="Scanning electron microscopy">scanning electron microscopy</a> (SEM) as an alternative to <a href="/wiki/Sputter_coating" class="mw-redirect" title="Sputter coating">sputter coating</a>. It embeds a heavy metal directly into cell membranes, creating a high electron scattering rate without the need for coating the membrane with a layer of metal, which can obscure details of the cell membrane. In the staining of the <a href="/wiki/Plasma_membrane" class="mw-redirect" title="Plasma membrane">plasma membrane</a>, osmium(VIII) oxide binds <a href="/wiki/Phospholipid" title="Phospholipid">phospholipid</a> head regions, thus creating contrast with the neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Protoplasm" title="Protoplasm">protoplasm</a> (cytoplasm). Additionally, osmium(VIII) oxide is also used for fixing biological samples in conjunction with HgCl<sub>2</sub>. Its rapid killing abilities are used to quickly kill live specimens such as protozoa. OsO<sub>4</sub> stabilizes many proteins by transforming them into gels without destroying structural features. Tissue proteins that are stabilized by OsO<sub>4</sub> are not coagulated by alcohols during dehydration.<sup id="cite_ref-stain_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stain-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Osmium(VIII) oxide is also used as a stain for lipids in optical microscopy.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> OsO<sub>4</sub> also stains the human cornea (see <a href="/wiki/Osmium_tetroxide#Safety_considerations" title="Osmium tetroxide">safety considerations</a>).
</p>

The cells are black as a result of the effects of osmium tetroxide.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polymer_staining">Polymer staining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Polymer staining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is also used to stain <a href="/wiki/Copolymers" class="mw-redirect" title="Copolymers">copolymers</a> preferentially, the best known example being block copolymers where one phase can be stained so as to show the <a href="/wiki/Microstructure" title="Microstructure">microstructure</a> of the material. For example, styrene-butadiene block copolymers have a central <a href="/wiki/Polybutadiene" title="Polybutadiene">polybutadiene</a> chain with polystyrene end caps. When treated with OsO<sub>4</sub>, the butadiene matrix reacts preferentially and so absorbs the oxide. The presence of a heavy metal is sufficient to block the electron beam, so the polystyrene domains are seen clearly in thin films in <a href="/wiki/Transmission_electron_microscopy" title="Transmission electron microscopy">TEM</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Osmium_ore_refining">Osmium ore refining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Osmium ore refining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>OsO<sub>4</sub> is an intermediate in the extraction of osmium from its ores. Osmium-containing residues are treated with sodium peroxide (Na<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) forming Na<sub>2</sub>[OsO<sub>4</sub>(OH)<sub>2</sub>], which is soluble.  When exposed to <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>, this salt gives OsO<sub>4</sub>. In the final stages of refining, crude OsO<sub>4</sub> is dissolved in alcoholic <a href="/wiki/NaOH" class="mw-redirect" title="NaOH">NaOH</a> forming Na<sub>2</sub>[OsO<sub>2</sub>(OH)<sub>4</sub>], which, when treated with <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">NH<sub>4</sub>Cl</a>, to give (NH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>4</sub>[OsO<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>2</sub>]. This salt is reduced under <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> to give osmium.<sup id="cite_ref-thompson_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thompson-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buckminsterfullerene_adduct">Buckminsterfullerene adduct</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Buckminsterfullerene adduct">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>OsO<sub>4</sub> allowed for the confirmation of the soccer ball model of <a href="/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene" title="Buckminsterfullerene">buckminsterfullerene</a>, a 60 atom <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> <a href="/wiki/Allotrope" class="mw-redirect" title="Allotrope">allotrope</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Adduct" title="Adduct">adduct</a>, formed from a derivative of OsO<sub>4</sub>, was C<sub>60</sub>(OsO<sub>4</sub>)(4-<i>tert</i>-<a href="/wiki/Butyl" class="mw-redirect" title="Butyl">butyl</a><a href="/wiki/Pyridine" title="Pyridine">pyridine</a>)<sub>2</sub>. The adduct broke the fullerene's symmetry allowing for crystallization and confirmation of the structure of C<sub>60</sub> by <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">X-ray crystallography</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_considerations">Safety considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Safety considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Label_for_ampoules_of_OsO4.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Label_for_ampoules_of_OsO4.jpg/300px-Label_for_ampoules_of_OsO4.jpg" width="300" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Label_for_ampoules_of_OsO4.jpg/450px-Label_for_ampoules_of_OsO4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7a/Label_for_ampoules_of_OsO4.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="350" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Label_for_ampoules_of_OsO4.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Label with poison warning</div></div></div><p>OsO<sub>4</sub> is highly poisonous, even at low exposure levels, and must be handled with appropriate precautions. In particular, inhalation at concentrations well below those at which a smell can be perceived can lead to <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_edema" title="Pulmonary edema">pulmonary edema</a> and subsequent death. Noticeable symptoms can take hours to appear after exposure.
</p><p>OsO<sub>4</sub> also stains the human <a href="/wiki/Cornea" title="Cornea">cornea</a>, which can lead to blindness if proper safety precautions are not observed. The permissible exposure limit for osmium(VIII) oxide (8 hour time-weighted average) is 200&#160;µg/m<sup>3</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-niosh_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-niosh-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Osmium(VIII) oxide can penetrate plastics and therefore is stored in glass under refrigeration.<sup id="cite_ref-stain_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stain-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On April 6, 2004 British intelligence sources believed they had foiled a plot to detonate a bomb involving OsO<sub>4</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Experts interviewed by <i><a href="/wiki/New_Scientist" title="New Scientist">New Scientist</a></i> affirmed osmium(VIII) oxide's toxicity, though some highlighted the difficulties of using it in a weapon: osmium(VIII) oxide is very expensive. The osmium(VIII) oxide may be destroyed by the blast; remaining toxic fumes may also be dispersed by the blast.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
