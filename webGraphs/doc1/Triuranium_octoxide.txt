<p><b>Triuranium octoxide</b> (U<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub>) is a compound of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>. It is present as an olive green to black, odorless solid. It is one of the more popular forms of <a href="/wiki/Yellowcake" title="Yellowcake">yellowcake</a> and is shipped between mills and refineries in this form.
</p><p>U<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub> has potential long-term stability in a <a href="/wiki/Deep_geological_repository" title="Deep geological repository">geologic environment</a>. In the presence of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (O<sub>2</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Uranium_dioxide" title="Uranium dioxide">uranium dioxide</a> (UO<sub>2</sub>) is <a href="/wiki/Oxidized" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxidized">oxidized</a> to U<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub>, whereas <a href="/wiki/Uranium_trioxide" title="Uranium trioxide">uranium trioxide</a> (UO<sub>3</sub>) loses oxygen at temperatures above 500&#160;°C and is <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduced</a> to U<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub>. The compound can be produced by any one of three primary chemical conversion processes, involving either <a href="/wiki/Uranium_tetrafluoride" title="Uranium tetrafluoride">uranium tetrafluoride</a> (UF<sub>4</sub>) or <a href="/wiki/Uranyl_fluoride" title="Uranyl fluoride">uranyl fluoride</a> (UO<sub>2</sub>F<sub>2</sub>) as intermediates. It is generally considered to be the more attractive form for disposal purposes because, under normal environmental conditions, U<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub> is one of the most kinetically and thermodynamically stable forms of uranium. Its particle density is 8.3 g cm<sup>−3</sup>.
</p><p>Triuranium octoxide is converted to <a href="/wiki/Uranium_hexafluoride" title="Uranium hexafluoride">uranium hexafluoride</a> for the purpose of <a href="/wiki/Uranium_enrichment" class="mw-redirect" title="Uranium enrichment">uranium enrichment</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solid_state_structure">Solid state structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triuranium_octoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Solid state structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The solid is a layered structure where the layers are bridged by <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> atoms, each layer contains uranium atoms which are in different coordination environments.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bond_valence_study">Bond valence study</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Triuranium_octoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Bond valence study">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Using a 6Å x 6Å x 6Å box with the uranium atom in the centre, the bond valence calculation was performed for both U1 and U2 in solid.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It was found, using the parameters for U(VI), that the calculated oxidation states for U1 and U2 are 5.11 and 5.10. Using the parameters for U(IV), the calculated oxidation states are 5.78 and 5.77 for U1 and U2, respectively. These studies suggests that all the uranium atoms have the same oxidation state, so that the oxidation states are disordered through the lattice.
</p>
