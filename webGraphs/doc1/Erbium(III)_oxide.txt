<p><b>Erbium(III) oxide</b>, is  synthesized from the lanthanide metal <a href="/wiki/Erbium" title="Erbium">erbium</a>. It was partially isolated by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustaf_Mosander" title="Carl Gustaf Mosander">Carl Gustaf Mosander</a> in 1843, and first obtained in pure form in 1905 by <a href="/wiki/Georges_Urbain" title="Georges Urbain">Georges Urbain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_James_(chemist)" title="Charles James (chemist)">Charles James</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It has a pink color with a cubic crystal structure. Under certain conditions erbium oxide can also have a hexagonal form.<sup id="cite_ref-Singh_2003_2889_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Singh_2003_2889-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> 
Erbium oxide is toxic when inhaled, taken orally, or injected into the blood stream in massive amounts. The effect of erbium oxides in low concentrations on humans over long periods of time has not been determined.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erbium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Erbium metal tarnishes slowly in air. Erbium burns readily to form erbium (III) oxide:
</p><p>The formation of erbium oxide is accomplished via the reaction 4 Er + 3 O<sub>2</sub> → 2 Er<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Erbium oxide is insoluble in water and soluble in mineral acids. Er<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> readily absorb moisture and carbon dioxide from the atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-Singh_2003_2889_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Singh_2003_2889-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  It can react with acids to form the corresponding erbium(III) salts.
</p><p>For example, with hydrochloric acid, the oxide follows the reaction Er<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 6 HCl → 2 ErCl<sub>3</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O to form <a href="/wiki/Erbium_chloride" class="mw-redirect" title="Erbium chloride">erbium chloride</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erbium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One interesting property of erbium oxides is their ability to up convert energy. Energy up conversion takes place when infrared or visible radiation, low energy light, is converted to ultraviolet or violet radiation higher energy light via multiple transfer or absorption of energy.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  Erbium oxide nanoparticles also possess photoluminescence properties. Erbium oxide nanoparticles can be formed by applying ultrasound (20&#160;kHz, 29 W·cm<sup>−2</sup>) in the presence of multiwall carbon nanotubes. The erbium oxide nanoparticles that have been successfully made by employing ultrasound are erbium carboxioxide, hexagonal and spherical geometry erbium oxide. Each ultrasonically formed erbium oxide is photoluminescence in the visible region of the electromagnetic spectrum under excitation of 379&#160;nm in water. Hexagonal erbium oxide photoluminescence is long lived and allows higher energy transitions (<sup>4</sup>S<sub>3/2</sub> - <sup>4</sup>I<sub>15/2</sub>). Spherical erbium oxide does not experience <sup>4</sup>S<sub>3/2</sub> - <sup>4</sup>I<sub>15/2</sub> energy transitions.<sup id="cite_ref-Radziuk_2011_14472–14480_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Radziuk_2011_14472–14480-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Erbium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The applications of Er<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> are varied due to their electrical, optical and photoluminescence properties. Nanoscale materials doped with Er<sup>+3</sup> are of much interest because they have special particle-size-dependent optical and electrical properties.<sup id="cite_ref-Richard_1996_271–358_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richard_1996_271–358-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  Erbium oxide doped nanoparticle materials can be dispersed in glass or plastic for display purposes, such as display monitors. The spectroscopy of Er+3 electronic transitions in host crystals lattices of nanoparticles combined with ultrasonically formed geometries in aqueous solution of carbon nanotubes is of great interest for synthesis of photoluminescence nanoparticles in ‘green’ chemistry.<sup id="cite_ref-Radziuk_2011_14472–14480_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Radziuk_2011_14472–14480-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Erbium oxide is among the most important rare earth metals used in biomedicine.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  The photoluminescence property of erbium oxide nanoparticles on carbon nanotubes makes them useful in biomedical applications. For example, erbium oxide nanoparticles can be surface modified for distribution into aqueous and non-aqueous media for bioimaging.<sup id="cite_ref-Richard_1996_271–358_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Richard_1996_271–358-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  Erbium oxides are also used as gate dielectrics in semi conductor devices since it has a high dielectric constant (10-14) and a large band gap.  <a href="/wiki/Erbium" title="Erbium">Erbium</a> is sometimes used as a coloring for <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glasses</a><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  and erbium oxide can also be used as a burnable <a href="/wiki/Neutron_poison" title="Neutron poison">neutron poison</a> for <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuel</a>.
</p>
