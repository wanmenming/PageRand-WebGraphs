<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Land reclamation</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Perth1964.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/76/Perth1964.jpg/220px-Perth1964.jpg" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/76/Perth1964.jpg/330px-Perth1964.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/76/Perth1964.jpg/440px-Perth1964.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1526" data-file-height="1026" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Perth1964.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reclaiming in Perth, Australia 1964</div></div></div>
<p><b>Land reclamation</b>, usually known as <b>reclamation</b>, and also known as <b>land fill</b> (not to be confused with a <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfill</a>), is the process of creating new <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_ecoregion" class="mw-redirect" title="Terrestrial ecoregion">land</a> from <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stream_bed" title="Stream bed">riverbeds</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Lake_beds" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake beds">lake beds</a>. The land reclaimed is known as <b>reclamation ground</b> or <b>land fill</b>.
</p><p>In a number of other jurisdictions, including parts of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> the term "reclamation" can refer to returning disturbed lands to an improved state. In <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, Canada, for example, reclamation is defined by the provincial government as "The process of reconverting disturbed land to its former or other productive uses."<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceania</a> it is frequently referred to as <a href="/wiki/Land_rehabilitation" title="Land rehabilitation">land rehabilitation</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">Methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_reclamation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Land reclamation can be achieved with a number of different methods. The most simple method involves simply filling the area with large amounts of heavy rock and/or <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">cement</a>, then filling with clay and dirt until the desired height is reached. The process is called "infilling"<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and the material used to fill the space is generally called "infill".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Draining of submerged <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a> is often used to reclaim land for <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> use. <a href="/wiki/Deep_cement_mixing" title="Deep cement mixing">Deep cement mixing</a> is used typically in situations in which the material displaced by either <a href="/wiki/Dredging" title="Dredging">dredging</a> or draining may be contaminated and hence needs to be contained. Land dredging is also another method of land reclamation. It is the removal of sediments and debris from the bottom of a body of water. It is commonly used for maintaining reclaimed land masses as sedimentation, a natural process, fills channels and harbors naturally.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_reclamation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Some of the coastlines of <a href="/wiki/Saadiyat_Island" title="Saadiyat Island">Saadiyat Island</a>, in the UAE. Used for commercial purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Much of the coastlines of <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. It took over 150 years to join the original <a href="/wiki/Seven_Islands_of_Bombay" title="Seven Islands of Bombay">Seven Islands of Bombay</a>. These seven islands were lush, green, thickly wooded, and dotted with 22 hills, with the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>  washing through them at high tide. The original <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Bombay" title="Isle of Bombay">Isle of Bombay</a> was only 24&#160;km long and 4&#160;km wide from Dongri to <a href="/wiki/Malabar_Hill" title="Malabar Hill">Malabar Hill</a> (at its broadest point) and the other six were  <a href="/wiki/Colaba" title="Colaba">Colaba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Woman%27s_Island" title="Old Woman&#39;s Island">Old Woman's Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mahim" title="Mahim">Mahim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parel" title="Parel">Parel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worli" title="Worli">Worli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mazgaon" class="mw-redirect" title="Mazgaon">Mazgaon</a>. (See also <a href="/wiki/Hornby_Vellard" title="Hornby Vellard">Hornby Vellard</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Mumbai_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mumbai-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Much of the coastlines of <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">Mainland China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>. It is estimated that nearly 65% of <a href="/wiki/Mudflat" title="Mudflat">tidal flats</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Yellow_Sea" title="Yellow Sea">Yellow Sea</a> have been reclaimed.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Inland lowlands in the <a href="/wiki/Yangtze" title="Yangtze">Yangtze</a> valley, China, including the areas of important cities like <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wuhan" title="Wuhan">Wuhan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Much of the coastline of <a href="/wiki/Karachi" title="Karachi">Karachi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>.</li>
<li>The shore of <a href="/wiki/Jakarta_Bay" title="Jakarta Bay">Jakarta Bay</a>. Land is usually reclaimed to create new housing areas and real estate properties, for the rapidly expanding city of <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a>. <p>One of the earliest large scale projects was the <a href="/wiki/Beemster" title="Beemster">Beemster Polder</a> in the Netherlands, realized in 1612 adding 70 square kilometres (27&#160;sq&#160;mi) of land. In Hong Kong the <a href="/wiki/Praya_Reclamation_Scheme" title="Praya Reclamation Scheme">Praya Reclamation Scheme</a> added 20 to 24 hectares (50 to 60 acres) of land in 1890 during the second phase of construction. It was one of the most ambitious projects ever taken during the <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Hong_Kong" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonial Hong Kong">Colonial Hong Kong</a> era.<sup id="cite_ref-Bard_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bard-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  Some 20% of land in the <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Bay" title="Tokyo Bay">Tokyo Bay</a> area has been reclaimed,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> most notably <a href="/wiki/Odaiba" title="Odaiba">Odaiba</a> artificial island.	<a href="/wiki/Fontvieille,_Monaco" title="Fontvieille, Monaco">Le Portier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">Monaco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a> are also expanding due to land reclamation. The city of <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> was largely built on reclaimed land, as was <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">Wellington</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Artificial_island" title="Artificial island">Artificial islands</a> are an example of land reclamation. Creating an artificial island is an expensive and risky undertaking. It is often considered in places with high population density and a scarcity of flat land. <a href="/wiki/Kansai_International_Airport" title="Kansai International Airport">Kansai International Airport</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Osaka" title="Osaka">Osaka</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_International_Airport" title="Hong Kong International Airport">Hong Kong International Airport</a> are examples where this process was deemed necessary. The <a href="/wiki/Palm_Islands" title="Palm Islands">Palm Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_World_(archipelago)" title="The World (archipelago)">The World</a> and hotel <a href="/wiki/Burj_al-Arab" class="mw-redirect" title="Burj al-Arab">Burj al-Arab</a> off <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a> are other examples of artificial islands (although there is yet no real "scarcity of land" in Dubai), as well as the <a href="/wiki/Flevopolder" title="Flevopolder">Flevopolder</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> which is the largest artificial island in the world.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_reclamation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bingzhou_Peninsula_area_-_land_reclamation_-_DSCF9204.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Bingzhou_Peninsula_area_-_land_reclamation_-_DSCF9204.JPG/220px-Bingzhou_Peninsula_area_-_land_reclamation_-_DSCF9204.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Bingzhou_Peninsula_area_-_land_reclamation_-_DSCF9204.JPG/330px-Bingzhou_Peninsula_area_-_land_reclamation_-_DSCF9204.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Bingzhou_Peninsula_area_-_land_reclamation_-_DSCF9204.JPG/440px-Bingzhou_Peninsula_area_-_land_reclamation_-_DSCF9204.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bingzhou_Peninsula_area_-_land_reclamation_-_DSCF9204.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Land reclamation in progress in Bingzhou (丙州) Peninsula (formerly, island) of the Dongzui Bay (东咀港). <a href="/wiki/Tong%27an_District" title="Tong&#39;an District">Tong'an District</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xiamen" title="Xiamen">Xiamen</a>, China</div></div></div>
<p>Agriculture was a drive for land reclamation before <a href="/wiki/Industrialisation" title="Industrialisation">industrialisation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  In South China, farmers reclaimed <a href="/wiki/Paddy_field" title="Paddy field">paddy fields</a> by enclosing an area with a stone wall on the <a href="/wiki/Sea_shore" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea shore">sea shore</a> near a <a href="/wiki/River_mouth" title="River mouth">river mouth</a> or <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">river delta</a>.  The species of rice that grow on these grounds are more <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> tolerant.  Another use of such enclosed land is the creation of <a href="/wiki/Fish_pond" title="Fish pond">fish ponds</a>.  It is commonly seen on the <a href="/wiki/Pearl_River_Delta" title="Pearl River Delta">Pearl River Delta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>.  These reclaimed areas also attract species of <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migrating birds</a>.
</p><p>A related practice is the <a href="/wiki/Drainage" title="Drainage">draining</a> of swampy or seasonally submerged <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a> to convert them to <a href="/wiki/Farmland_(farming)" class="mw-redirect" title="Farmland (farming)">farmland</a>. While this does not create new land exactly, it allows commercially productive use of land that would otherwise be restricted to <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a> <a href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Habitat (ecology)">habitat.</a> It is also an important method of <a href="/wiki/Mosquito_control" title="Mosquito control">mosquito control</a>.
</p><p>Even in the post-industrial age, there have been land reclamation projects intended for increasing available agricultural land.  For example, the village of <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Cgata,_Akita" title="Ōgata, Akita">Ogata</a> in <a href="/wiki/Akita,_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="Akita, Japan">Akita, Japan</a>, was established on land reclaimed from <a href="/wiki/Lake_Hachirogata" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake Hachirogata">Lake Hachirogata</a> (Japan's second largest lake at the time) starting in 1957. By 1977, the amount of land reclaimed totalled 172.03 square kilometres (66.42&#160;sq&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beach_restoration">Beach restoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_reclamation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Beach restoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Beach_nourishment" title="Beach nourishment">Beach nourishment</a></div>
<p>Beach rebuilding is the process of repairing <a href="/wiki/Beach" title="Beach">beaches</a> using materials such as <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sand</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mud" title="Mud">mud</a> from inland. This can be used to build up beaches suffering from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Beach_starvation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Beach starvation (page does not exist)">beach starvation</a> or erosion from <a href="/wiki/Longshore_drift" title="Longshore drift">longshore drift</a>. It stops the movement of the original beach material through longshore drift and retains a natural look to the beach. Although it is not a long-lasting solution, it is cheap compared to other types of <a href="/wiki/Coastal_management" title="Coastal management">coastal defences</a>. An example of this is the city of Mumbai.<sup id="cite_ref-Mumbai_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mumbai-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Landfill">Landfill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_reclamation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Landfill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">human overcrowding of developed areas</a> intensified during the 20th century, it has become important to develop land re-use strategies for completed <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfills</a>.  Some of the most common usages are for parks, <a href="/wiki/Golf_course" title="Golf course">golf courses</a> and other sports fields. Increasingly, however, office buildings and industrial uses are made on a completed landfill. In these latter uses, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> capture is customarily carried out to minimize explosive hazard within the building.
</p><p>An example of a <a href="/wiki/Class_A_office_space" class="mw-redirect" title="Class A office space">Class A office building</a> constructed over a landfill is the <a href="/wiki/Dakin_Building" title="Dakin Building">Dakin Building</a> at <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Point,_Brisbane,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Sierra Point, Brisbane, California">Sierra Point</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brisbane,_California" title="Brisbane, California">Brisbane, California</a>. The underlying fill was deposited from 1965 to 1985, mostly consisting of construction debris from <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> and some municipal wastes. <a href="/wiki/Aerial_photograph" class="mw-redirect" title="Aerial photograph">Aerial photographs</a> prior to 1965 show this area to be <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tideland" class="extiw" title="wikt:tideland">tidelands</a> of the San Francisco Bay. A clay cap was constructed over the debris prior to building approval.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A notable example is <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Olympic_Park" title="Sydney Olympic Park">Sydney Olympic Park</a>, the primary venue for the 2000 Summer Olympic Games, which was built atop an industrial wasteland that included landfills.
</p><p>Another strategy for landfill is the incineration of landfill trash at high temperature via the <a href="/wiki/Plasma_arc_waste_disposal" class="mw-redirect" title="Plasma arc waste disposal">plasma-arc gasification process</a>, which is currently used at two facilities in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, and will be used at a planned facility in <a href="/wiki/St._Lucie_County" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Lucie County">St. Lucie County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact">Environmental impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_reclamation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Environmental impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bay_area_fill.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c9/Bay_area_fill.jpg/220px-Bay_area_fill.jpg" width="220" height="265" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c9/Bay_area_fill.jpg/330px-Bay_area_fill.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c9/Bay_area_fill.jpg/440px-Bay_area_fill.jpg 2x" data-file-width="560" data-file-height="675" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bay_area_fill.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Parts (highlighted in brown) of the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay" title="San Francisco Bay">San Francisco Bay</a> were reclaimed from wetlands for urban use.</div></div></div>
<p>Draining wetlands for ploughing, for example, is a form of <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">habitat destruction</a>. In some parts of the world, new reclamation projects are restricted or no longer allowed, due to <a href="/wiki/Environmental_movement" title="Environmental movement">environmental protection</a> laws. Reclamation projects have strong negative impacts on coastal populations, although some species can take advantage of the newly created area.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_legislation">Environmental legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_reclamation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Environmental legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hong_Kong_reclamation.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Hong_Kong_reclamation.png/220px-Hong_Kong_reclamation.png" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Hong_Kong_reclamation.png/330px-Hong_Kong_reclamation.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Hong_Kong_reclamation.png/440px-Hong_Kong_reclamation.png 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hong_Kong_reclamation.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A map of reclaimed land in Hong Kong: Grey (built), red (proposed or under development). Many of the urban areas of Hong Kong are on reclaimed land.</div></div></div>
<p>The State of California created a state commission, the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Conservation_and_Development_Commission" title="San Francisco Bay Conservation and Development Commission">San Francisco Bay Conservation and Development Commission</a>, in 1965 to protect San Francisco Bay and regulate development near its shores.  The commission was created in response to growing concern over the shrinking size of the bay.
</p><p>Hong Kong legislators passed the <a href="/wiki/Protection_of_the_Harbour_Ordinance" title="Protection of the Harbour Ordinance">Protection of the Harbour Ordinance</a>, proposed by the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Protection_of_the_Harbour" title="Society for Protection of the Harbour">Society for Protection of the Harbour</a>, in 1997 in an effort to safeguard the increasingly threatened <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Harbour" title="Victoria Harbour">Victoria Harbour</a> against encroaching land development.<sup id="cite_ref-bill_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bill-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Several large reclamation schemes at Green Island, West Kowloon, and <a href="/wiki/Kowloon_Bay" title="Kowloon Bay">Kowloon Bay</a> were subsequently shelved, and others reduced in size.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dangers">Dangers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_reclamation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Dangers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Reclaimed land is highly susceptible to <a href="/wiki/Soil_liquefaction" title="Soil liquefaction">soil liquefaction</a> during earthquakes,<sup id="cite_ref-abag_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abag-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> which can amplify the amount of damage that occurs to buildings and infrastructure. <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">Subsidence</a> is another issue, both from <a href="/wiki/Soil_compaction" title="Soil compaction">soil compaction</a> on filled land, and also when wetlands are enclosed by <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">levees</a> and drained to create <a href="/wiki/Polder" title="Polder">Polders</a>. Drained marshes will eventually sink below the surrounding water level, increasing the danger from <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_amounts_added">Land amounts added</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Land_reclamation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Land amounts added">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a></b> – about 110 square kilometres (42&#160;sq&#160;mi) in total and has 12,000 square kilometres (4,600&#160;sq&#160;mi) potential (8% of total area) up to 12 metres (39&#160;ft) depth in the territorial sea area.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a></b> – about 1/6 (almost 17%) of the entire country, or about 7,000 square kilometres (2,700&#160;sq&#160;mi) in total, has been reclaimed from the sea, lakes, marshes and swamps. The province of <a href="/wiki/Flevoland" title="Flevoland">Flevoland</a> has almost completely been reclaimed from the <a href="/wiki/Zuiderzee" title="Zuiderzee">Zuiderzee</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a></b> – As of 2006, 38 percent or 1,550 square kilometres (600&#160;sq&#160;mi) of coastal wetlands reclaimed, including 400 square kilometres (150&#160;sq&#160;mi) at <a href="/wiki/Saemangeum_Seawall" title="Saemangeum Seawall">Saemangeum</a>. <a href="/wiki/Songdo_International_Business_District" title="Songdo International Business District">Songdo International Business district</a>, the largest private development in history, is a large-scale reclamation project built entirely on tidal mudflats.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a></b> – 20% of the original size or 135 square kilometres (52&#160;sq&#160;mi). As of  2003<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Land_reclamation&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, plans for 99 square kilometres (38&#160;sq&#160;mi) more are to go ahead,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> despite the fact that disputes persist with Malaysia over Singapore's extensive land reclamation works.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a></b> – (<i>Main article: <a href="/wiki/Land_reclamation_in_Hong_Kong" title="Land reclamation in Hong Kong">Land reclamation in Hong Kong</a></i>)</li></ul>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/Praya_Reclamation_Scheme" title="Praya Reclamation Scheme">Praya Reclamation Scheme</a> began in the late 1860s and consisted of two stages totaling 20 to 24 hectares (50 to 60 acres).<sup id="cite_ref-Bard_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bard-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Disneyland" title="Hong Kong Disneyland">Hong Kong Disneyland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_International_Airport" title="Hong Kong International Airport">Hong Kong International Airport</a>, and its predecessor, <a href="/wiki/Kai_Tak_Airport" title="Kai Tak Airport">Kai Tak Airport</a>, were all built on reclaimed land. In addition, much reclamation has taken place in prime locations on the waterfront on both sides of <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Harbour" title="Victoria Harbour">Victoria Harbour</a>. This has raised <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmental</a> issues of the protection of the harbour which was once the source of prosperity of Hong Kong, traffic congestion in the <a href="/wiki/Central,_Hong_Kong" title="Central, Hong Kong">Central district</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-court_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-court-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> as well as the collusion of the Hong Kong Government with the <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real estate</a> developers in the territory.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></dd>
<dd>In addition, as the city expands, new towns in different decades were mostly built on reclaimed land, such as <a href="/wiki/Tuen_Mun" title="Tuen Mun">Tuen Mun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tai_Po" title="Tai Po">Tai Po</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shatin" class="mw-redirect" title="Shatin">Shatin</a>-<a href="/wiki/Ma_On_Shan_(area)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ma On Shan (area)">Ma On Shan</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Kowloon" title="West Kowloon">West Kowloon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kwun_Tong" title="Kwun Tong">Kwun Tong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tseung_Kwan_O" title="Tseung Kwan O">Tseung Kwan O</a>.</dd></dl>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a></b> – 170% of the original size or 17 square kilometres (6.6&#160;sq&#160;mi)<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a></b>, <b><a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a></b> – <a href="/wiki/Giant_Sea_Wall_Jakarta" title="Giant Sea Wall Jakarta">Giant Sea Wall Jakarta</a> is part of a massive coastal development project at Jakarta Bay, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Manila_Bay" title="Manila Bay">Manila Bay</a></b>, <b><a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a></b> – additional 626 hectares along the eastern coast of Manila Bay created in the 1990s<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> to the 88-hectare <a href="/wiki/Cultural_Center_of_the_Philippines_Complex" title="Cultural Center of the Philippines Complex">Cultural Center of the Philippines</a> Complex. The shore road of <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">Manila</a> (<a href="/wiki/Roxas_Boulevard" title="Roxas Boulevard">Roxas Boulevard</a>) is actually reclaimed land, as well as its extension road to Cavite (<a href="/wiki/Manila%E2%80%93Cavite_Expressway" title="Manila–Cavite Expressway">Manila-Cavite Expressway / Aguinaldo Boulevard</a>).</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a></b>, <b><a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a></b> – An archipelago of originally <a href="/wiki/Seven_islands_of_Bombay" class="mw-redirect" title="Seven islands of Bombay">seven separate islands</a> were joined together by land reclamation over a span of five centuries. This was done to develop Mumbai as a harbour city.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">Monaco</a></b> – 0.41 square kilometres (0.16&#160;sq&#160;mi) out of 2.05 square kilometres (0.79&#160;sq&#160;mi), or one fifth of Monaco comes from land taken from the sea, mainly in the neighborhoods of <a href="/wiki/Fontvieille,_Monaco" title="Fontvieille, Monaco">Fontvieille</a>, <a href="/wiki/La_Condamine" title="La Condamine">La Condamine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Larvotto" title="Larvotto">Larvotto/Bas Moulins</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Bay" title="Tokyo Bay">Tokyo Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a></b> – 249 square kilometres (96&#160;sq&#160;mi)<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> including the entirety of <a href="/wiki/Odaiba" title="Odaiba">Odaiba</a> <a href="/wiki/Artificial_island" title="Artificial island">artificial island</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Kobe,_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="Kobe, Japan">Kobe, Japan</a></b> – 23 square kilometres (8.9&#160;sq&#160;mi) (1995).</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Bahrain" title="Bahrain">Bahrain</a></b> – 76.3% of original size of 410 square kilometres (160&#160;sq&#160;mi) (1931–2007).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a></b> – significant areas of land totalling several hundred hectares have been reclaimed along the harbourfront of <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">Auckland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">Wellington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dunedin" title="Dunedin">Dunedin</a>. In Dunedin – which in its early days was nicknamed "Mudedin" – around 2.5 square kilometres (0.97&#160;sq&#160;mi), including much of the inner city and suburbs of Dunedin North, South Dunedin and Andersons Bay is reclaimed from the Otago Harbour, and a similar area in the suburbs of St Clair and St Kilda is reclaimed swampland.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a></b> – Dubai has a total of four reclaimed islands (the <a href="/wiki/Palm_Jumeirah" title="Palm Jumeirah">Palm Jumeirah</a>,  Jebal Ali, The <a href="/wiki/Burj_al_Arab" class="mw-redirect" title="Burj al Arab">Burj al Arab</a> Island, and <a href="/wiki/The_World_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="The World Islands">The World Islands</a>), with a fifth under construction (the <a href="/wiki/Palm_Deira" class="mw-redirect" title="Palm Deira">Palm Deira</a>). There are several man made islands in <a href="/wiki/Abu_Dhabi" title="Abu Dhabi">Abu Dhabi</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Yas_Island" title="Yas Island">Yas Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/Al_Lulu_Island" title="Al Lulu Island">Al Lulu Island</a>.</li></ul>
