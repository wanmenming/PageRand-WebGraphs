<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Greenhouse debt</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Greenhouse debt</b> is the measure to which an individual person, incorporated association, business enterprise, government instrumentality or / [and] (per Neb., USA) geographic community exceeds its permitted <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse</a> <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">footprint</a> and contributes <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> that contribute to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The concept makes no sense without a clear numerical value for the permitted <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse</a> <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">footprint</a>. It is not clear what this value is.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Friends_of_the_Earth" title="Friends of the Earth">Friends of the Earth</a> and similar organisations put forward the concept to define specifically the environmental harm caused by developed countries' past and present policies.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Some governments, at least the Australian <a href="/wiki/Australian_Labor_Party" title="Australian Labor Party">Labor</a> leadership, have a tendency to accept such a line of reasoning.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The greenhouse debt assessment thus forms an <a href="/wiki/Ecological_footprint" title="Ecological footprint">ecological footprint</a> analysis but can be used separately. Taken conjointly with a 'water debt' <a href="/wiki/Needs_assessment" title="Needs assessment">analysis</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_assessment" title="Environmental impact assessment">ecological impact assessment</a>, greenhouse debt analysis is basic to giving individuals, organisations, governments and communities an understanding of the effects they are having on <a href="/wiki/Gaia_hypothesis" title="Gaia hypothesis">Gaia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.  
</p><p>Ensuring that the greenhouse debt is zero is essential towards achieving <a href="/wiki/Ecologically_sustainable_development" title="Ecologically sustainable development">ecologically sustainable development</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_retreat" class="mw-redirect" title="Sustainable retreat">sustainable retreat</a>. Any greenhouse debt incurred will contribute to making life harder for future generations of humans and <a href="/wiki/Non-human" title="Non-human">non-human</a> lifeforms.
</p><p>There are three possible consequences that occur as a result of a greenhouse debt.
</p>
<ol><li><i><a href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitigation of global warming">Mitigation</a>:</i> finding compensatory ways of reducing the greenhouse debt so its effects are neutralised</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">Adaptation</a>:</i> finding ways of adjusting to the resulting global warming or climate change</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Suffering" title="Suffering">Suffering</a>:</i> having one's quality of life reduced as a result of the consequences</li></ol>
