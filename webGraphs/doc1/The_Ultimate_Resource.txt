<p><i><b>The Ultimate Resource</b></i> is a 1981 book written by <a href="/wiki/Julian_Lincoln_Simon" class="mw-redirect" title="Julian Lincoln Simon">Julian Lincoln Simon</a> challenging the notion that humanity was running out of <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Simon-1981_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simon-1981-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It was revised in 1996 as <i>The Ultimate Resource 2</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Simon-1996_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simon-1996-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ultimate_Resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The overarching <a href="/wiki/Thesis" title="Thesis">thesis</a> on why there is no resource <a href="/wiki/Crisis" title="Crisis">crisis</a> is that as a particular resource becomes more <a href="/wiki/Scarce" class="mw-redirect" title="Scarce">scarce</a>, its <a href="/wiki/Price" title="Price">price</a> rises. This price rise creates an incentive for people to discover more of the resource, ration and recycle it, and eventually, develop substitutes. The "ultimate resource" is not any particular physical object but the capacity for humans to invent and adapt.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scarcity">Scarcity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ultimate_Resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scarcity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The work opens with an explanation of scarcity, noting its relation to price; high prices denote relative scarcity and low prices indicate <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/abundance" class="extiw" title="wikt:abundance">abundance</a>. Simon usually measures prices in wage-adjusted terms, since this is a measure of how much labor is required to purchase a fixed amount of a particular resource. Since prices for most raw materials (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>) have fallen between 1800 and 1990 (adjusting for <a href="/wiki/Wages" class="mw-redirect" title="Wages">wages</a> and adjusting for <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation</a>), Simon argues that this indicates that those materials have become less scarce.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Westmine.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/20/Westmine.jpg/220px-Westmine.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/20/Westmine.jpg/330px-Westmine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/20/Westmine.jpg/440px-Westmine.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Westmine.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Simon contends that resources, such as copper, become less scarce as demand for them drives recycling, development of alternatives, and new extraction techniques, which are all reflected in the drop in their wage-adjusted prices.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forecasting">Forecasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ultimate_Resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Forecasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Simon makes a distinction between "engineering” and "economic" forecasting. Engineering forecasting consists of estimating the amount of known physical amount of resources, <a href="/wiki/Extrapolate" class="mw-redirect" title="Extrapolate">extrapolates</a> the rate of use from current use and subtracts one from the other. Simon argues that these simple analyses are often wrong. While focusing only on proven resources is helpful in a <a href="/wiki/Business" title="Business">business</a> context, it is not appropriate for economy-wide forecasting. There exist undiscovered sources, sources not yet economically feasible to extract, sources not yet <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technologically</a> feasible to extract, and ignored resources that could prove useful but are not yet worth trying to discover.
</p><p>To counter the problems of engineering forecasting, Simon proposes economic forecasting, which proceeds in three steps in order to capture, in part, the unknowns the engineering method leaves out (p 27):
</p>
<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tbody><tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“
</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
<ol><li>Ask whether there is any convincing reason to think that the period for which you are forecasting will be different from the past, going back as far as the data will allow</li>
<li>If there is no good reason to reject the past trend as representative of the future as well, ask whether there is a reasonable explanation for the observed trend</li>
<li>If there is no reason to believe that the future will be different from the past, and if you have solid explanation for the trend—or even if you lack a solid theory, but the data are overwhelming—project the trend into the future</li></ol>
</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”
</td></tr>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infinite_resources">Infinite resources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ultimate_Resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Infinite resources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Perhaps the most controversial claim in the book is that natural resources are infinite. Simon argues not that there is an infinite <i>physical</i> amount of, say, copper, but for human purposes that amount should be treated as infinite because it is not <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bounded" class="extiw" title="wikt:bounded">bounded</a> or limited in any <i>economic</i> sense, because:
</p>
<ul><li>known reserves are of uncertain quantity</li>
<li>new reserves may become available, either through discovery or via the development of new extraction techniques</li>
<li>recycling</li>
<li>more efficient utilization of existing reserves (e.g., "It takes much less copper now to pass a given message than a hundred years ago." [<i>The Ultimate Resource 2</i>, 1996, footnote, page 62])</li>
<li>development of economic equivalents, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Optic_fibre" class="mw-redirect" title="Optic fibre">optic fibre</a> in the case of copper for <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications" class="mw-redirect" title="Telecommunications">telecommunications</a></li></ul>
<p>The ever-decreasing prices, in <a href="/wiki/Wage_inflation" class="mw-redirect" title="Wage inflation">wage-adjusted terms</a>, indicate decreasing scarcity, in that it takes less time for the average worker to earn the money required to purchase a set amount of some commodity. This suggests, Simon claims, an enduring trend of increased availability that will not cease in the foreseeable future, despite continued population growth.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evidence">Evidence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ultimate_Resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Evidence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A plurality of the book consists of chapters showcasing the economics of one resource or another and proposing why this resource is, for human purposes, infinite.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_precedent">Historical precedent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ultimate_Resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Historical precedent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Simon argues that for thousands of years, people have always worried about the end of <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">civilization</a> brought on by a crisis of resources. Simon lists several past unfounded <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmental</a> fears in order to back his claim that modern fears are nothing new and will also be disproven.
</p><p>Some of the "crises" he notes are a shortage of <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a> in the 13th century <a href="/wiki/BCE" class="mw-redirect" title="BCE">BCE</a>; <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">disappearing forests</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> in 550 BCE and in England in the 16th century to 18th century <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">CE</a>; <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> in 1798; <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> in Great Britain in the 19th century; <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">oil</a> since the 1850s; and various <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a> since the 1970s.
</p>
<h2><span id="Simon.E2.80.93Ehrlich_wager"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Simon–Ehrlich_wager">Simon–Ehrlich wager</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ultimate_Resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Simon–Ehrlich wager">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Simon%E2%80%93Ehrlich_wager" title="Simon–Ehrlich wager">Simon–Ehrlich wager</a></div>
<p>Based on preliminary research for <i>The Ultimate Resource</i>, Simon and Paul Ehrlich made a famous wager in 1980, betting on a mutually agreed upon measure of resource scarcity over the decade leading up to 1990.
</p><p>Ehrlich was the author of a popular book, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Population_Bomb" title="The Population Bomb">The Population Bomb</a></i>, which argued that mankind was facing a demographic catastrophe with the rate of population growth quickly outstripping growth in the supply of food and resources. Simon was highly skeptical of such claims.
</p><p>Simon had Ehrlich choose five of several commodity metals. Ehrlich chose five metals: copper, chromium, nickel, tin, and tungsten. Simon bet that their prices would go down. Ehrlich bet they would go up.
</p><p>The basket of goods, costing US$1,000 in 1980, fell in price by over 57 percent over the following decade. As a result, in October 1990, Paul Ehrlich mailed Julian Simon a check for US$576.07 to settle the wager in Simon's favor.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Ultimate_Resource&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A large section of the book is dedicated to showing how population growth ultimately creates more resources. The basic argument echoes the overarching thesis: as resources become more scarce, the price rises, creating an incentive to adapt. It suggests that the more a society has to <a href="/wiki/Invent" class="mw-redirect" title="Invent">invent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Innovate" class="mw-redirect" title="Innovate">innovate</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ceteris_paribus" title="Ceteris paribus">ceteris paribus</a></i>, the more easily the society will raise its living standards and lower resource scarcity.
</p>
