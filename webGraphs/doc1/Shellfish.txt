<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Shellfish</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Oysters_p1040741.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Oysters_p1040741.jpg/220px-Oysters_p1040741.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Oysters_p1040741.jpg/330px-Oysters_p1040741.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Oysters_p1040741.jpg/440px-Oysters_p1040741.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1949" data-file-height="1462" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Oysters_p1040741.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Raw oysters, opened, and presented on a plate</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cocktail_1_bg_060702.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Cocktail_1_bg_060702.jpg/220px-Cocktail_1_bg_060702.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Cocktail_1_bg_060702.jpg/330px-Cocktail_1_bg_060702.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Cocktail_1_bg_060702.jpg/440px-Cocktail_1_bg_060702.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cocktail_1_bg_060702.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Shrimp_cocktail" class="mw-redirect" title="Shrimp cocktail">shrimp cocktail</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Shellfish</b> is a food source and <a href="/wiki/Fisheries" class="mw-redirect" title="Fisheries">fisheries</a> term for <a href="/wiki/Exoskeleton" title="Exoskeleton">exoskeleton</a>-bearing aquatic <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> used as <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>, including various species of <a href="/wiki/Mollusca" title="Mollusca">molluscs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Echinoderm" title="Echinoderm">echinoderms</a>. Although most kinds of shellfish are harvested from <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">saltwater</a> environments, some kinds are found in <a href="/wiki/Freshwater" class="mw-redirect" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a>. In addition, a few species of land crabs are eaten, for example <i><a href="/wiki/Cardisoma_guanhumi" title="Cardisoma guanhumi">Cardisoma guanhumi</a></i> in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>.
</p><p>Despite the name, shellfish are not a kind of <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, but are simply water-dwelling animals.  Many varieties of shellfish (<a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> in particular) are actually closely related to insects and arachnids, making up one of the main classes of the phylum <a href="/wiki/Arthropoda" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthropoda">Arthropoda</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cephalopods" class="mw-redirect" title="Cephalopods">Cephalopods</a> (squids, octopuses, cuttlefish) and <a href="/wiki/Bivalves" class="mw-redirect" title="Bivalves">bivalves</a> (clams, oysters) are <a href="/wiki/Mollusc" class="mw-redirect" title="Mollusc">molluscs</a>, as are <a href="/wiki/Gastropods" class="mw-redirect" title="Gastropods">Gastropods</a> (aquatic species such as whelks and winkles; also land species such as snails and slugs).
</p><p>Shellfish used as a food source by humans include many <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Clam" title="Clam">clams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mussel" title="Mussel">mussels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oyster" title="Oyster">oysters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Periwinkle_(mollusc)" class="mw-redirect" title="Periwinkle (mollusc)">winkles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scallop" title="Scallop">scallops</a>. Some crustaceans that are commonly eaten are  <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">lobsters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crayfish" title="Crayfish">crayfish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Echinoderm" title="Echinoderm">Echinoderms</a> are not as frequently harvested for food as molluscs and crustaceans; however, <a href="/wiki/Sea_urchin" title="Sea urchin">sea urchin</a> <a href="/wiki/Roe" title="Roe">roe</a> is quite popular in many parts of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most shellfish eat a diet composed primarily of <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zooplankton" title="Zooplankton">zooplankton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Shellfish are among the most common <a href="/wiki/Food_allergy" title="Food allergy">food allergens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shellfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rheinische_Muscheln.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Rheinische_Muscheln.jpg/180px-Rheinische_Muscheln.jpg" width="180" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Rheinische_Muscheln.jpg/270px-Rheinische_Muscheln.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Rheinische_Muscheln.jpg/360px-Rheinische_Muscheln.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1278" data-file-height="1116" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rheinische_Muscheln.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cooked <a href="/wiki/Mussel" title="Mussel">mussels</a></div></div></div><p>The term <i>shellfish</i> is used both broadly and specifically. In common parlance, as in having "shellfish" for dinner, it can refer to anything from clams and oysters to lobster and shrimp. For regulatory purposes it is often narrowly defined as <i>filter-feeding <a href="/wiki/Molluscs" class="mw-redirect" title="Molluscs">molluscs</a></i> such as clams, mussels, and oyster to the exclusion of <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> and all else.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the term is primarily applied to <a href="/wiki/Marine_(ocean)" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine (ocean)">marine</a> species, edible <a href="/wiki/Freshwater" class="mw-redirect" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> invertebrates such as <a href="/wiki/Crayfish" title="Crayfish">crayfish</a> and river <a href="/wiki/Mussels" class="mw-redirect" title="Mussels">mussels</a> are also sometimes grouped under the umbrella term "shellfish".
</p><p>Although their shells may differ, all shellfish are invertebrates.  As non-mammalian animals that spend their entire lives in water they are "fish" in an informal sense; however the term <i>finfish</i> is sometimes used to distinguish <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> as animals <i>defined by having <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrae</a></i> from shellfish in modern terminology.
</p><p>The word "shellfish" is both singular and plural; the rarely used "shellfishes" is sometimes employed to distinguish among various types of shellfish.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shellfish_in_various_cuisines">Shellfish in various cuisines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shellfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Shellfish in various cuisines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Archaeological finds have shown that humans have been making use of shellfish as a food item for hundreds of thousands of years. In the present, shellfish dishes are a feature of almost all the <a href="/wiki/Cuisine" title="Cuisine">cuisines</a> of the world,  providing an important source of protein in many cuisines around the world, especially in the countries with coastal areas.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sakura_ebi.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Sakura_ebi.jpg/180px-Sakura_ebi.jpg" width="180" height="137" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Sakura_ebi.jpg/270px-Sakura_ebi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Sakura_ebi.jpg/360px-Sakura_ebi.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1349" data-file-height="1030" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sakura_ebi.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sakura ebi</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Japan">In Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shellfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cuisine" title="Japanese cuisine">Japanese cuisine</a>, chefs often use shellfish and their <a href="/wiki/Roe" title="Roe">roe</a> in different dishes. <a href="/wiki/Sushi" title="Sushi">Sushi</a> (vinegared rice, topped with other ingredients, including shellfish, fish, meat and vegetables) features both raw and cooked shellfish.  <a href="/wiki/Sashimi" title="Sashimi">Sashimi</a> primarily consists of very fresh raw seafood, sliced into thin pieces. Both sushi and sashimi are served with soy sauce and <a href="/wiki/Wasabi" title="Wasabi">wasabi</a> paste (a Japanese <a href="/wiki/Horseradish" title="Horseradish">horseradish</a> root, a spice with extremely strong, hot flavor), thinly sliced pickled ginger root, and a simple garnish such as <a href="/wiki/Perilla" title="Perilla">shiso</a> (a kitchen herb, member of the <a href="/wiki/Mentha" title="Mentha">mint</a> family) or finely shredded daikon radish, or both.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_United_States">In the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shellfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Boiled_Maine_Lobster.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Boiled_Maine_Lobster.jpg/180px-Boiled_Maine_Lobster.jpg" width="180" height="180" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Boiled_Maine_Lobster.jpg/270px-Boiled_Maine_Lobster.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Boiled_Maine_Lobster.jpg/360px-Boiled_Maine_Lobster.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1331" data-file-height="1331" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Boiled_Maine_Lobster.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Boiled <a href="/wiki/American_lobster" title="American lobster">Maine lobster</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">Lobster</a> in particular is a great delicacy in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, where families in the Northeast region make them into the centerpiece of a <a href="/wiki/New_England_clam_bake" class="mw-redirect" title="New England clam bake">clam bake</a>, usually for special occasions. Lobsters are eaten on much of the East Coast; the <a href="/wiki/American_lobster" title="American lobster">American lobster</a> ranges from <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a> down to about the <a href="/wiki/Carolinas" class="mw-redirect" title="Carolinas">Carolinas</a>, but is most often associated with <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>. A typical meal involves boiling the lobster with some slight seasoning and then serving it with drawn butter, <a href="/wiki/Baked_potato" title="Baked potato">baked potato</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Corn_on_the_cob" title="Corn on the cob">corn on the cob</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Clam_digging" title="Clam digging">Clamming</a> is done both commercially and recreationally along the Northeast coastline of the US. Various type of clams are incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/Cuisine_of_New_England" title="Cuisine of New England">cuisine of New England</a>. The soft-shelled clam is eaten either <a href="/wiki/Fried_clam" class="mw-redirect" title="Fried clam">fried</a> or <a href="/wiki/New_England_Clam_Boil" class="mw-redirect" title="New England Clam Boil">steamed</a> (and then called "<a href="/wiki/Soft-shell_Clam" class="mw-redirect" title="Soft-shell Clam">steamers</a>"). Many types of clams can be used for <a href="/wiki/Clam_chowder" title="Clam chowder">clam chowder</a>, but the <a href="/wiki/Quahog" class="mw-redirect" title="Quahog">quahog</a>, a hard shelled clam also known as a chowder clam, is often used because the long cooking time softens its tougher meat.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> region has generally been associated more with crabs, but in recent years the area has been trying to reduce its catch of <a href="/wiki/Callinectes_sapidus" title="Callinectes sapidus">blue crabs</a>, as wild populations have been depleted.  This has not, however, stemmed the demand: Maryland-style <a href="/wiki/Crabcake" class="mw-redirect" title="Crabcake">crabcakes</a> are still a well known treat in crabhouses all over the bay, though the catch now comes from points farther south.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Scallop_sandwich_(1).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Scallop_sandwich_%281%29.jpg/220px-Scallop_sandwich_%281%29.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Scallop_sandwich_%281%29.jpg/330px-Scallop_sandwich_%281%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Scallop_sandwich_%281%29.jpg/440px-Scallop_sandwich_%281%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Scallop_sandwich_(1).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Scallop" title="Scallop">Scallop</a> sandwich served in San Diego</div></div></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">Southeast</a>, and particularly the gulf states, <a href="/wiki/Shrimping" class="mw-redirect" title="Shrimping">shrimping</a> is an important industry. Copious amounts of shrimp are harvested each year in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> to satisfy a national demand for shrimp. Locally, prawns and shrimp are often deep fried; in the Cajun and Creole kitchens of <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, shrimp and prawns are a common addition to traditional recipes like <a href="/wiki/Jambalaya" title="Jambalaya">jambalaya</a> and certain stews. <a href="/wiki/Crawfish" class="mw-redirect" title="Crawfish">Crawfish</a><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> are a well known and much eaten delicacy there, often boiled in huge pots and heavily spiced.
</p><p>In many major cities with active fishing ports, raw oyster bars are also a feature of shellfish consumption. When served freshly shucked (opened) and iced, one may find a liquid inside the shell, called the liquor. Some believe that oysters have the properties of an <a href="/wiki/Aphrodisiac" title="Aphrodisiac">aphrodisiac</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Inter-tidal herbivorous shellfish such as mussels and clams can help people reach a healthy balance of <a href="/wiki/Omega-3_fatty_acid" title="Omega-3 fatty acid">omega-3</a> and omega-6 fats in their diets, instead of the current <a href="/wiki/Western_diets" class="mw-redirect" title="Western diets">Western diets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> For this reason, the eating of shellfish is often encouraged by dietitians.
Shellfish are also a rich source of the amino acid <a href="/wiki/Taurine" title="Taurine">taurine</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_dietary_restrictions">Religious dietary restrictions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shellfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Religious dietary restrictions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jewish_dietary_laws" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish dietary laws">Jewish dietary laws</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Islamic_dietary_laws" title="Islamic dietary laws">Islamic dietary laws</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a> forbids the consumption of shellfish (i.e. the only permitted seafood is fish with fins and scales), in the books of <a href="/wiki/Leviticus" class="mw-redirect" title="Leviticus">Leviticus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deuteronomy" class="mw-redirect" title="Deuteronomy">Deuteronomy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Jews (of all religious traditions) who fully observe the dietary laws thus do not eat shellfish.  Neither do <a href="/wiki/Seventh-day_Adventist_Church" title="Seventh-day Adventist Church">Seventh-day Adventists</a>, who follow Jewish dietary law because the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a> is part of their <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Islamic_dietary_laws" title="Islamic dietary laws">Islamic dietary laws</a> are divided on the question.  Most <a href="/wiki/Schools_of_Islamic_theology" title="Schools of Islamic theology">Muslim</a> traditions consider some or all shellfish to be <a href="/wiki/Halal" title="Halal">halal</a> while some others classify all shellfish as <a href="/wiki/Haram" title="Haram">haram</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Allergy">Allergy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shellfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Allergy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While estimates vary from shellfish, approximately 1% of the population is estimated to suffer from <a href="/wiki/Seafood" title="Seafood">seafood</a> allergy, which is more common in teenage and adult life than very early childhood. An estimated 20% will grow out of their <a href="/wiki/Allergy" title="Allergy">allergy</a> with time.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxic_content">Toxic content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shellfish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Toxic content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some shellfish, such as <a href="/wiki/Whelk" title="Whelk">whelk</a>, contain <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>. A sample of whelk was found to have a total content of arsenic at <span class="nowrap">15.42 mg/kg</span> of which 1% is inorganic arsenic.<sup id="cite_ref-gov_fsis8205_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gov_fsis8205-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
