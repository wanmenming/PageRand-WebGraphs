<p><b>Arsenic pentoxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the formula As<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  This glassy, white, <a href="/wiki/Deliquescent" class="mw-redirect" title="Deliquescent">deliquescent</a> solid is relatively unstable, consistent with the rarity of the As(V) oxidation state.  More common, and far more important commercially, is <a href="/wiki/Arsenic(III)_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Arsenic(III) oxide">arsenic(III) oxide</a> (As<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>).  All arsenic compounds are highly toxic and thus find only limited commercial applications.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The structure consists of tetrahedral {AsO<sub>4</sub>} and octahedral {AsO<sub>6</sub>} centers linked by sharing corners.<sup id="cite_ref-Jansen_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jansen-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>    The structure differs from that of the corresponding phosphorus(V) oxide; as a result, although there is still a solid solution with that oxide, it only progresses to the equimolar point, at which point phosphorus has substituted for arsenic in all of its tetrahedral sites. Likewise, arsenic pentoxide can also dissolve up to an equimolar amount of <a href="/wiki/Antimony_pentoxide" title="Antimony pentoxide">antimony pentoxide</a>, as antimony substitutes for arsenic only in its octahedral sites.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical">Historical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Historical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Paracelsus" title="Paracelsus">Paracelsus</a> Macquer found a crystallizable salt which he called ‘sel neutre arsenical’. This salt was the obtaining residue after distilling <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> from a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Potassium_nitrate" title="Potassium nitrate">potassium nitrate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_trioxide" title="Arsenic trioxide">arsenic trioxide</a>. Previously Paracelsus heated a mixture of arsenic trioxide and potassium nitrate. He applied the term ‘arsenicum fixum’ to the product. A. <a href="/wiki/Libavius" class="mw-redirect" title="Libavius">Libavius</a> called the same  product ‘butyrum arsenici’ (butter of arsenic), although this term was actually used for arsenic tricholoride. The products that Paracelsus and Libavius found were all impure alkali arsenates.<sup id="cite_ref-Mellor_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mellor-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Scheele" title="Scheele">Scheele</a> prepared a number of arsenates by the action of arsenic acid on the alkalies. One of the arsenates that he prepared, was arsenic pentoxide.<sup id="cite_ref-Zenger_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zenger-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  The water in the alkalies evaporated at 180˚C, and the arsenic pentoxide was stable below 400˚C .<sup id="cite_ref-Mellor_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mellor-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_methods">Modern methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Arsenic pentoxide can be crystallized by heating As<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> under oxygen. This reaction is reversible:<sup id="cite_ref-Jansen_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jansen-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>As<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>  ⇌  As<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + O<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Strong <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agents" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxidizing agents">oxidizing agents</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> convert arsenic trioxide to the pentoxide.
</p><p>Arsenic acid can be generated via routine processing of arsenic compounds including the oxidation of arsenic and arsenic-containing minerals in air. Illustrative is the roasting of <a href="/wiki/Orpiment" title="Orpiment">orpiment</a>, a typical arsenic sulfide ore:<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 As<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>  +  11 O<sub>2</sub>   →  2 As<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> + 6 SO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_toxicity" class="mw-redirect" title="Arsenic toxicity">arsenic toxicity</a></div>
<p>Like all arsenic compounds, the pentoxide is highly toxic.  Its reduced derivative arsenite, which is an As(III) compound, is even more toxic since it has a high affinity for thiol groups of <a href="/wiki/Cysteine" title="Cysteine">cysteine</a> residues in proteins.
</p><p>It is classified as an <a href="/wiki/List_of_extremely_hazardous_substances" title="List of extremely hazardous substances">extremely hazardous substance</a> in the United States as defined in Section 302 of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Emergency_Planning_and_Community_Right-to-Know_Act" title="Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act">Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act</a> (42 U.S.C. 11002), and is subject to strict reporting requirements by facilities which produce, store, or use it in significant quantities.<sup id="cite_ref-gov-right-know_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gov-right-know-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
