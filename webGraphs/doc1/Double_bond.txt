<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Double bond</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>double bond</b> in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bond</a> between two <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical elements</a> involving four <a href="/wiki/Bonding_electron" class="mw-redirect" title="Bonding electron">bonding electrons</a> instead of the usual two. The most common double bond, that is between two carbon atoms, can be found in <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a>. Many types of double bonds exist between two different elements. For example, in a <a href="/wiki/Carbonyl" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> group with a carbon atom and an oxygen atom. Other common double bonds are found in <a href="/wiki/Azo_compound" title="Azo compound">azo compounds</a> (N=N), <a href="/wiki/Imine" title="Imine">imines</a> (C=N) and <a href="/wiki/Sulfoxide" title="Sulfoxide">sulfoxides</a> (S=O). In <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_formula" title="Skeletal formula">skeletal formula</a> the double bond is drawn as two parallel lines (=) between the two connected atoms; typographically, the <a href="/wiki/Equals_sign" title="Equals sign">equals sign</a> is used for this.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Double bonds were first introduced in chemical notation by <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> chemist <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Butlerov" title="Alexander Butlerov">Alexander Butlerov</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Double bonds involving carbon are stronger than <a href="/wiki/Covalent_bond" title="Covalent bond">single bonds</a> and are also shorter. The <a href="/wiki/Bond_order" title="Bond order">bond order</a> is two. Double bonds are also electron-rich, which makes them potentially more reactive in the presence of a strong electron acceptor (as in addition reactions of the halogens).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Double_bonds_in_alkenes">Double bonds in alkenes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double_bond&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Double bonds in alkenes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ethylene-CRC-MW-dimensions-2D.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Ethylene-CRC-MW-dimensions-2D.png/220px-Ethylene-CRC-MW-dimensions-2D.png" width="220" height="137" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Ethylene-CRC-MW-dimensions-2D.png/330px-Ethylene-CRC-MW-dimensions-2D.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Ethylene-CRC-MW-dimensions-2D.png/440px-Ethylene-CRC-MW-dimensions-2D.png 2x" data-file-width="1100" data-file-height="687" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ethylene-CRC-MW-dimensions-2D.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Geometry of ethylene</div></div></div>
<p>The type of bonding can be explained in terms of <a href="/wiki/Orbital_hybridisation" title="Orbital hybridisation">orbital hybridisation</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a> each carbon atom has three <a href="/wiki/Orbital_hybridization#sp2_hybrids" class="mw-redirect" title="Orbital hybridization">sp<sup>2</sup></a> orbitals and one <a href="/wiki/Atomic_orbital" title="Atomic orbital">p-orbital</a>. The three sp<sup>2</sup> orbitals lie in a plane with ~120° angles. The p-orbital is perpendicular to this plane. When the carbon atoms approach each other, two of the sp<sup>2</sup> orbitals overlap to form a <a href="/wiki/Sigma_bond" title="Sigma bond">sigma bond</a>. At the same time, the two p-orbitals approach (again in the same plane) and together they form a <a href="/wiki/Pi-bond" class="mw-redirect" title="Pi-bond">pi-bond</a>. For maximum overlap, the p-orbitals have to remain parallel, and, therefore, rotation around the central bond is not possible. This property gives rise to <a href="/wiki/Cis-trans_isomerism" class="mw-redirect" title="Cis-trans isomerism">cis-trans isomerism</a>. Double bonds are shorter than single bonds because p-orbital overlap is maximized.
</p>

<p>With 133&#160;pm, the ethylene <a href="/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93carbon_bond" title="Carbon–carbon bond">C=C</a> <a href="/wiki/Bond_length" title="Bond length">bond length</a> is shorter than the C&#8722;C length in <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a> with 154&#160;pm. The double bond is also stronger, 636&#160;<a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">kJ</a>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mol</a><sup>&#8722;1</sup> versus 368&#160;kJ&#160;mol<sup>&#8722;1</sup> but not twice as much as the pi-bond is weaker than the sigma bond due to less effective pi-overlap.
</p><p>In an alternative representation, the double bond results from two overlapping sp<sup>3</sup> orbitals as in a <a href="/wiki/Bent_bond" title="Bent bond">bent bond</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">Variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double_bond&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In molecules, with alternating double bonds and single bonds, p-orbital overlap can exist over multiple atoms in a chain, giving rise to a <a href="/wiki/Conjugated_system" title="Conjugated system">conjugated system</a>. Conjugation can be found in systems such as <a href="/wiki/Diene" title="Diene">dienes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Enone" title="Enone">enones</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Cyclic_compound" title="Cyclic compound">cyclic molecules</a>, conjugation can lead to <a href="/wiki/Aromaticity" title="Aromaticity">aromaticity</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Cumulene" title="Cumulene">cumulenes</a>, two double bonds are adjacent.
</p><p>Double bonds are common for <a href="/wiki/Period_2_element" title="Period 2 element">period 2 elements</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, and less common with <a href="/wiki/Double_bond_rule" title="Double bond rule">elements of higher periods</a>. Metals, too, can engage in multiple bonding in a <a href="/wiki/Metal_ligand_multiple_bond" class="mw-redirect" title="Metal ligand multiple bond">metal ligand multiple bond</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Group_14_alkene_homologs">Group 14 alkene homologs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double_bond&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Group 14 alkene homologs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Double bonded compounds, <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkene</a> homologs, R<sub>2</sub>E=ER<sub>2</sub> are now known for all of the heavier <a href="/wiki/Carbon_group" title="Carbon group">group 14</a> elements. Unlike the alkenes these compounds are not planar but adopt twisted and/or trans bent structures. These effects become more pronounced for the heavier elements. The distannene (Me<sub>3</sub>Si)<sub>2</sub>CHSn=SnCH(SiMe<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> has a tin-tin bond length just a little shorter than a single bond, a trans bent structure with pyramidal coordination at each tin atom, and readily dissociates in solution to form (Me<sub>3</sub>Si)<sub>2</sub>CHSn: (stannanediyl, a carbene analog). The bonding comprises two weak donor acceptor bonds, the lone pair on each tin atom overlapping with the empty p orbital on the other.<sup id="cite_ref-Power1999_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Power1999-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wang&amp;Robinson2009_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wang&amp;Robinson2009-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  In contrast, in disilenes each silicon atom has planar coordination but the substituents are twisted so that the molecule as a whole is not planar. In diplumbenes the Pb=Pb bond length can be longer than that of many corresponding single bonds<sup id="cite_ref-Wang&amp;Robinson2009_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wang&amp;Robinson2009-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Plumbenes and stannenes generally dissociate in solution into monomers with bond enthalpies that are just a fraction of the corresponding single bonds. Some double bonds plumbenes and stannenes are similar in strength to hydrogen bonds.<sup id="cite_ref-Power1999_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Power1999-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Carter-Goddard-Malrieu-Trinquier_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Carter-Goddard-Malrieu-Trinquier model">Carter-Goddard-Malrieu-Trinquier model</a> can be used to predict the nature of the bonding.<sup id="cite_ref-Power1999_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Power1999-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
