<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Malthusian growth model</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>Malthusian growth model</b>, sometimes called a <b>simple exponential</b> growth model, is essentially <a href="/wiki/Exponential_growth" title="Exponential growth">exponential growth</a> based on the idea of the function being proportional to the speed to which the function grows. The model is named after <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Robert_Malthus" title="Thomas Robert Malthus">Thomas Robert Malthus</a>, who wrote <i><a href="/wiki/An_Essay_on_the_Principle_of_Population" title="An Essay on the Principle of Population">An Essay on the Principle of Population</a></i> (1798), one of the earliest and most influential books on <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-malthus_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-malthus-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Malthusian models have the following form:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"  alttext="{\displaystyle P(t)=P_{0}e^{rt}}">
  <semantics>
    <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
      <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
        <mi>P</mi>
        <mo stretchy="false">(</mo>
        <mi>t</mi>
        <mo stretchy="false">)</mo>
        <mo>=</mo>
        <msub>
          <mi>P</mi>
          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
            <mn>0</mn>
          </mrow>
        </msub>
        <msup>
          <mi>e</mi>
          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
            <mi>r</mi>
            <mi>t</mi>
          </mrow>
        </msup>
      </mstyle>
    </mrow>
    <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle P(t)=P_{0}e^{rt}}</annotation>
  </semantics>
</math></span><img src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/614cb210863245b47b246c78f0246122c938566e" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" aria-hidden="true" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:12.69ex; height:3.009ex;" alt="{\displaystyle P(t)=P_{0}e^{rt}}"/></span></dd></dl>
<p>where
</p>
<ul><li><i>P</i><sub>0</sub> = <i>P</i>(0) is the initial population size,</li>
<li><i>r</i> = the population growth rate, sometimes called <i>Malthusian parameter</i>,</li>
<li><i>t</i> = time.</li></ul>
<p>The model can also been written in the form of a differential equation:
</p><p>dP/dt = rP
</p><p>with initial condition:
P(0)= P<sub>0</sub>
</p><p>This model is often referred to as the <i>exponential law</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is widely regarded in the field of <a href="/wiki/Population_ecology" title="Population ecology">population ecology</a> as the <a href="/wiki/First_principle" title="First principle">first principle</a> of <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics" title="Population dynamics">population dynamics</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Malthus" class="mw-redirect" title="Malthus">Malthus</a> as the founder. The exponential law is therefore also sometimes referred to as the <i>Malthusian Law</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> By now, it is a widely accepted view to analogize Malthusian growth in Ecology to <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_First_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Newton&#39;s First Law">Newton's First Law of uniform motion</a> in physics.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Malthus wrote that all life forms, including humans, have a propensity to exponential population growth when resources are abundant but that actual growth is limited by available resources:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Through the animal and vegetable kingdoms, nature has scattered the seeds of life abroad with the most profuse and liberal hand.... The germs of existence contained in this spot of earth, with ample food, and ample room to expand in, would fill millions of worlds in the course of a few thousand years. Necessity, that imperious all pervading law of nature, restrains them within the prescribed bounds. The race of plants, and the race of animals shrink under this great restrictive law. And the race of man cannot, by any efforts of reason, escape from it. Among plants and animals its effects are waste of seed, sickness, and premature death. Among mankind, misery and vice. </p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite>Thomas Malthus, 1798. <i><a href="/wiki/An_Essay_on_the_Principle_of_Population" title="An Essay on the Principle of Population">An Essay on the Principle of Population</a></i>. Chapter I.</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>A model of population growth bounded by resource limitations was developed by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Francois_Verhulst" class="mw-redirect" title="Pierre Francois Verhulst">Pierre Francois Verhulst</a> in 1838, after he had read Malthus' essay. Verhulst named the model a <a href="/wiki/Logistic_function" title="Logistic function">logistic function</a>.
</p>
