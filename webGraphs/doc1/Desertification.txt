<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Desertification</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

The lake has shrunk by 94% since the 1960s.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p><b>Desertification</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Land_degradation" title="Land degradation">land degradation</a> in which a relatively <a href="/wiki/Drylands" title="Drylands">dry</a> area of land becomes increasingly arid, typically losing its bodies of water as well as vegetation and wildlife .<sup id="cite_ref-Geist-2005-p2_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Geist-2005-p2-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is caused by a variety of factors, such as through <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> (particularly the current <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-Zeng_L17401_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zeng_L17401-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and through the <a href="/wiki/Overexploitation" title="Overexploitation">overexploitation</a> of soil through human activity.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> When deserts appear automatically over the natural course of a planet's life cycle, then it can be called a natural phenomenon; however, when deserts emerge due to the rampant and unchecked depletion of nutrients in soil that are essential for it to remain arable, then a virtual "soil death" can be spoken of,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> which traces its cause back to human overexploitation. Desertification is a significant global ecological and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_problems" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental problems">environmental problem</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Geist-2005-p4_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Geist-2005-p4-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desertification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Considerable controversy exists over the proper definition of the term "desertification" for which Helmut Geist (2005) has identified more than 100 formal definitions. The most widely accepted<sup id="cite_ref-Geist-2005-p2_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Geist-2005-p2-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> of these is that of the <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a> Dictionary which defines it as "the process of fertile land transforming into desert typically as a result of <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, drought or improper/inappropriate agriculture".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><br />
Desertification has been neatly defined in the text of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_to_Combat_Desertification" title="United Nations Convention to Combat Desertification">United Nations Convention to Combat Desertification</a> (UNCCD) as "land degradation in arid, semi-arid and dry sub-humid regions resulting from various factors, including climatic variations and human activities."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another major contribution to the controversy comes from the sub-grouping of types of desertification. Spanning from the very vague yet shortsighted view as the "man-made-desert" to the more broad yet less focused type as the "Non-pattern-Desert"<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest known discussion of the topic arose soon after the <a href="/wiki/French_colonization" class="mw-redirect" title="French colonization">French colonization</a> of <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>, when the Comité d'Etudes commissioned a study on <i>desséchement progressif</i> to explore the prehistoric expansion of the Sahara Desert.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desertification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The world's most noted <a href="/wiki/Deserts" class="mw-redirect" title="Deserts">deserts</a> have been formed by natural processes interacting over long intervals of time. During most of these times, deserts have grown and shrunk independent of human activities. Paleodeserts are large <a href="/wiki/Sand_sea" class="mw-redirect" title="Sand sea">sand seas</a> now inactive because they are stabilized by vegetation, some extending beyond the present margins of core deserts, such as the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>, the largest hot desert.<sup id="cite_ref-Desertification_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Desertification-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Desertification has played a significant role in human history, contributing to the collapse of several large empires, such as Carthage, Greece, and the Roman Empire, as well as causing displacement of local populations.<sup id="cite_ref-Geist-2005-p4_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Geist-2005-p4-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LOWDERMILK_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LOWDERMILK-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Historical evidence shows that the serious and extensive land deterioration occurring several centuries ago in arid regions had three epicenters: the Mediterranean, the Mesopotamian Valley, and the <a href="/wiki/Loess_Plateau" title="Loess Plateau">Loess Plateau</a> of China, where population was dense.<sup id="cite_ref-LOWDERMILK_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LOWDERMILK-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Areas_affected">Areas affected</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desertification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Areas affected">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sun,_Moon_and_Telescopes_above_the_Desert_(ESO).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Sun%2C_Moon_and_Telescopes_above_the_Desert_%28ESO%29.jpg/220px-Sun%2C_Moon_and_Telescopes_above_the_Desert_%28ESO%29.jpg" width="220" height="70" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Sun%2C_Moon_and_Telescopes_above_the_Desert_%28ESO%29.jpg/330px-Sun%2C_Moon_and_Telescopes_above_the_Desert_%28ESO%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Sun%2C_Moon_and_Telescopes_above_the_Desert_%28ESO%29.jpg/440px-Sun%2C_Moon_and_Telescopes_above_the_Desert_%28ESO%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="16726" data-file-height="5336" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sun,_Moon_and_Telescopes_above_the_Desert_(ESO).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope" title="Very Large Telescope">large telescopes</a> above the desert<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Drylands occupy approximately 40–41% of Earth’s land area<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-johnson-1_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-johnson-1-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and are home to more than 2 billion people.<sup id="cite_ref-johnson-1_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-johnson-1-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> It has been estimated that some 10–20% of drylands are already degraded, the total area affected by desertification being between 6 and 12 million square kilometres, that about 1–6% of the inhabitants of drylands live in desertified areas, and that a billion people are under threat from further desertification.<sup id="cite_ref-holtz_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holtz-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 1998, the then-current degree of southward expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> was not well known, due to a lack of recent, measurable expansion of the desert into the Sahel at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The impact of global warming and human activities are presented in the Sahel. In this area, the level of desertification is very high compared to other areas in the world. All areas situated in the eastern part of Africa (i.e. in the Sahel region) are characterized by a dry climate, hot temperatures, and low rainfall (300–750&#160;mm rainfall per year). So, droughts are the rule in the Sahel region.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Some studies have shown that Africa has lost approximately 650,000&#160;km² of its productive agricultural land over the past 50 years. The propagation of desertification in this area is considerable.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some statistics have shown that since 1900 the Sahara has expanded by 250&#160;km to the south over a stretch of land from west to east 6,000&#160;km long.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The survey, done by the research institute for development, had demonstrated that this means dryness is spreading fast in the Sahelian countries. 70% of the arid area has deteriorated and water resources have disappeared, leading to soil degradation. The loss of topsoil means that plants cannot take root firmly and can be uprooted by torrential water or strong winds.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The United Nations Convention (UNC) says that about six million Sahelian citizens would have to give up the desertified zones of sub-Saharan Africa for North Africa and Europe between 1997 and 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another major area that is being impacted by desertification is the <a href="/wiki/Gobi_Desert" title="Gobi Desert">Gobi Desert</a>. Currently, the Gobi desert is the fastest moving desert on Earth; according to some researchers, the Gobi Desert swallows up over 1,300 square miles (3,370&#160;km²) of land annually. This has destroyed many villages in its path. Currently, photos show that the Gobi Desert has expanded to the point the entire nation of Croatia could fit inside its area.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
This is causing a major problem for the people of China. They will soon have to deal with the desert as it creeps closer. Although the Gobi Desert itself is still a distance away from Beijing, reports from field studies state there are large sand dunes forming only 70&#160;km (43.5&#160;mi) outside the city.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vegetation_patterning">Vegetation patterning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desertification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vegetation patterning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As the desertification takes place, the landscape may progress through different stages and continuously transform in appearance. On gradually sloped terrain, desertification can create increasingly larger empty spaces over a large strip of land, a phenomenon known as "<a href="/wiki/Tiger_bush" title="Tiger bush">Brousse tigrée</a>". A mathematical model of this phenomenon proposed by C. Klausmeier attributes this patterning to dynamics in plant-water interaction.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>  One outcome of this observation suggests an optimal planting strategy for agriculture in arid environments.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desertification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Overgrazing is not caused by nomadic <a href="/wiki/Grazing" title="Grazing">grazers</a> in huge populations of travel <a href="/wiki/Herd" title="Herd">herds</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Duke_University_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duke_University-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> nor by <a href="/wiki/Holistic_management_(agriculture)" title="Holistic management (agriculture)">holistic</a> planned grazing.<sup id="cite_ref-Forbes-AS_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forbes-AS-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></div></div><div style="clear:left"></div></div></div>
<p>The immediate cause is the loss of most vegetation. This is driven by a number of factors, alone or in combination, such as drought, climatic shifts, tillage for agriculture, overgrazing and deforestation for fuel or construction materials. Vegetation plays a major role in determining the biological composition of the soil. Studies have shown that, in many environments, the rate of erosion and runoff decreases exponentially with increased vegetation cover.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Unprotected, dry soil surfaces blow away with the wind or are washed away by flash floods, leaving infertile lower soil layers that bake in the sun and become an unproductive hardpan.
</p><p>Many scientists think that one of the most common causes is <a href="/wiki/Overgrazing" title="Overgrazing">overgrazing</a>, too much consumption of vegetation by cattle. Controversially, <a href="/wiki/Allan_Savory" title="Allan Savory">Allan Savory</a> has claimed that the controlled movement of herds of livestock, mimicking herds of grazing wildlife, can reverse desertification.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Morroco-arid-climate.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Morroco-arid-climate.jpg/220px-Morroco-arid-climate.jpg" width="220" height="152" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Morroco-arid-climate.jpg/330px-Morroco-arid-climate.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Morroco-arid-climate.jpg/440px-Morroco-arid-climate.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1108" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Morroco-arid-climate.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A shepherd guiding his sheep through the high desert outside <a href="/wiki/Marrakech" class="mw-redirect" title="Marrakech">Marrakech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a></div></div></div>
<p>Scientists agree, that there is a natural cause for the existence of desert in the place where is now the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara desert</a>: a natural climate cycle cause a lack of water in this area from time to time. There is a suggestion that the last time that the Sahara was converted from savanna to desert it was partially due to overgrazing by the cattle of the local population.<sup id="cite_ref-Humans_as_Agents_in_the_Termination_of_the_African_Humid_Period_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Humans_as_Agents_in_the_Termination_of_the_African_Humid_Period-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the causes of desertification in the 20-21 centuries is probably <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">climate change</a><sup id="cite_ref-Zeng_L17401_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zeng_L17401-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Poverty">Poverty</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desertification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Poverty">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At least 90% of the inhabitants of drylands live in <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing countries</a>, where they also suffer from poor economic and social conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-holtz_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holtz-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> This situation is exacerbated by land degradation because of the reduction in productivity, the precariousness of living conditions and the difficulty of access to resources and opportunities.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A downward spiral is created in many underdeveloped countries by <a href="/wiki/Overgrazing" title="Overgrazing">overgrazing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Land_exhaustion" class="mw-redirect" title="Land exhaustion">land exhaustion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Overdrafting" title="Overdrafting">overdrafting</a> of groundwater in many of the marginally productive world regions due to <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">overpopulation</a> pressures to exploit marginal drylands for farming. Decision-makers are understandably averse to invest in arid zones with low potential. This absence of investment contributes to the marginalisation of these zones. When unfavourable agro-climatic conditions are combined with an absence of infrastructure and access to markets, as well as poorly adapted production techniques and an underfed and undereducated population, most such zones are excluded from development.<sup id="cite_ref-csf-desertification.org_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-csf-desertification.org-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Desertification often causes rural lands to become unable to support the same sized populations that previously lived there. This results in mass <a href="/wiki/Human_migration" title="Human migration">migrations</a> out of rural areas and into urban areas, particularly in Africa. These migrations into the cities often cause large numbers of unemployed people, who end up living in <a href="/wiki/Slums" class="mw-redirect" title="Slums">slums</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Agriculture is a main source of income for many desert communities. The increase in desertification in theses regions has degraded the land enough where people can no longer productively farm and make a profit. This has negatively impacted the economy and increased poverty rates.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Countermeasures_and_prevention">Countermeasures and prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desertification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Countermeasures and prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:North_Sahara._Anti-sand_shields.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/North_Sahara._Anti-sand_shields.jpg/220px-North_Sahara._Anti-sand_shields.jpg" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/North_Sahara._Anti-sand_shields.jpg/330px-North_Sahara._Anti-sand_shields.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/North_Sahara._Anti-sand_shields.jpg/440px-North_Sahara._Anti-sand_shields.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2709" data-file-height="1916" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:North_Sahara._Anti-sand_shields.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Anti-sand shields in north <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GreeningdesertTharIndia.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/GreeningdesertTharIndia.jpg/220px-GreeningdesertTharIndia.jpg" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/GreeningdesertTharIndia.jpg/330px-GreeningdesertTharIndia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/GreeningdesertTharIndia.jpg/440px-GreeningdesertTharIndia.jpg 2x" data-file-width="873" data-file-height="617" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GreeningdesertTharIndia.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Jojoba" title="Jojoba">Jojoba</a> plantations, such as those shown, have played a role in combating edge effects of desertification in the <a href="/wiki/Thar_Desert" title="Thar Desert">Thar Desert</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Techniques and countermeasures exist for mitigating or reversing the effects of desertification, and some possess varying levels of difficulty. For some, there are numerous barriers to their implementation. Yet for others, the solution simply requires the exercise of human reason.
</p><p>One less difficult solution that has been proposed,<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> however controversial it may be, is to bring about a cap on the population growth, and in fact to turn this into a population decay, so that each year there will gradually exist fewer and fewer humans who require the land to be depleted even further in order to grow their food.
</p><p>One proposed barrier is that the costs of adopting sustainable agricultural practices sometimes exceed the benefits for individual farmers, even while they are socially and environmentally beneficial.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Another issue is a lack of political will, and lack of funding to support <a href="/wiki/Land_reclamation" title="Land reclamation">land reclamation</a> and anti-desertification programs.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Desertification is recognized as a major threat to <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>. Some countries have developed <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_Action_Plan" class="mw-redirect" title="Biodiversity Action Plan">Biodiversity Action Plans</a> to counter its effects, particularly in relation to the protection of endangered <a href="/wiki/Flora" title="Flora">flora</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">fauna</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Reforestation" title="Reforestation">Reforestation</a> gets at one of the root causes of desertification and is not just a treatment of the symptoms. Environmental organizations<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> work in places where deforestation and desertification are contributing to extreme <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>. There they focus primarily on educating the local population about the dangers of deforestation and sometimes employ them to grow seedlings, which they transfer to severely deforested areas during the rainy season.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization_of_the_United_Nations" class="mw-redirect" title="Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations">Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations</a> launched the FAO <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.fao.org/forestry/aridzone/restoration/en/">Drylands Restoration Initiative</a> in 2012 to draw together knowledge and experience on dryland restoration.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>  In 2015, FAO published global guidelines for the restoration of degraded forests and landscapes in drylands, in collaboration with the Turkish Ministry of Forestry and Water Affairs and the Turkish Cooperation and Coordination Agency.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Green_Wall_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Wall of China">Green Wall of China</a></i> is a high-profile example of one method that has been finding success in this battle with desertification.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2018)">according to whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. This wall is a much larger-scale version of what American farmers did in the 1930s to stop the great Midwest dust bowl. This plan was proposed in the late 1970s, and has become a major ecological engineering project that is not predicted to end until the year 2055. According to Chinese reports, there have been nearly 66,000,000,000 trees planted in China's great green wall.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
Due to the success that China has been finding in stopping the spread of desertification, plans are currently be made in Africa to start a "wall" along the borders of the Sahara desert as well.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Techniques focus on two aspects: provisioning of water, and fixation and hyper-fertilizing soil.
</p><p>Fixating the soil is often done through the use of <a href="/wiki/Windbreak" title="Windbreak">shelter belts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Woodlot" title="Woodlot">woodlots</a> and <a href="/wiki/Windbreak" title="Windbreak">windbreaks</a>. Windbreaks are made from trees and bushes and are used to reduce <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a>. They were widely encouraged by development agencies from the middle of the 1980s in the <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a> area of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.
</p><p>Some soils (for example, clay), due to lack of water can become <a href="/wiki/Consolidation_(soil)" title="Consolidation (soil)">consolidated</a> rather than porous (as in the case of sandy soils). Some techniques as zaï or <a href="/wiki/Tillage" title="Tillage">tillage</a> are then used to still allow the planting of crops.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another technique that is useful is <a href="/wiki/Contour_trenching" title="Contour trenching">contour trenching</a>. This involves the digging of 150&#160;m long, 1&#160;m deep trenches in the soil. The trenches are made parallel to the height lines of the landscape, preventing the water from flowing within the trenches and causing erosion. Stone walls are placed around the trenches to prevent the trenches from closing up again. The method was invented by Peter Westerveld.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Enriching of the soil and restoration of its fertility is often done by plants. Of these, <a href="/wiki/Legume" title="Legume">leguminous plants</a> which extract <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> from the air and fix it in the soil, and food crops/trees as <a href="/wiki/Grain" title="Grain">grains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barley" title="Barley">barley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beans" class="mw-redirect" title="Beans">beans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_dactylifera" class="mw-redirect" title="Phoenix dactylifera">dates</a> are the most important. <a href="/wiki/Sand_fence" title="Sand fence">Sand fences</a> can also be used to control drifting of soil and sand erosion.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some research centra (such as Bel-Air Research Center IRD/ISRA/UCAD) are also experimenting with the inoculation of tree species with <a href="/wiki/Mycorrhiza" title="Mycorrhiza">mycorrhiza</a> in arid zones. The mycorrhiza are basically fungi attaching themselves to the roots of the plants. They hereby create a symbiotic relation with the trees, increasing the surface area of the tree's roots greatly (allowing the tree to gather much more nutrients from the soil).<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As there are many different types of deserts, there are also different types of desert reclamation methodologies. An example for this is the salt-flats in the  <a href="/wiki/Rub%27_al_Khali" title="Rub&#39; al Khali">Rub' al Khali</a> desert in  <a href="/wiki/Saudi-Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Saudi-Arabia">Saudi-Arabia</a>. These salt-flats are one of the most promising desert areas for seawater agriculture and could be revitalized without the use of freshwater or much energy.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Farmer-managed_natural_regeneration" title="Farmer-managed natural regeneration">Farmer-managed natural regeneration</a> (FMNR) is another technique that has produced successful results for desert reclamation. Since 1980, this method to reforest degraded landscape has been applied with some success in Niger. This simple and low-cost method has enabled farmers to regenerate some 30,000 square kilometers in Niger. The process involves enabling native sprouting tree growth through selective pruning of shrub shoots. The residue from pruned trees can be used to provide mulching for fields thus increasing soil water retention and reducing evaporation. Additionally, properly spaced and pruned trees can increase crop yields. The Humbo Assisted Regeneration Project which uses FMNR techniques in Ethiopia has received money from The World Bank’s BioCarbon Fund, which supports projects that sequester or conserve carbon in forests or agricultural ecosystems.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Managed_grazing">Managed grazing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desertification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Managed grazing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Restoring grasslands store CO<sub>2</sub> from the air into plant material. Grazing livestock, usually not left to wander, would eat the grass and would minimize any grass growth while grass left alone would eventually grow to cover its own growing buds, preventing them from photosynthesizing and killing the plant.<sup id="cite_ref-Tlovell_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tlovell-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>  A method proposed to restore grasslands uses fences with many small paddocks and moving herds from one paddock to another after a day or two in order to mimic natural grazers and allowing the grass to grow optimally.<sup id="cite_ref-Tlovell_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tlovell-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> It is estimated that increasing the carbon content of the soils in the world’s 3.5 billion hectares of agricultural grassland would offset nearly 12 years of CO<sub>2</sub> emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-Tlovell_66-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tlovell-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Allan_Savory" title="Allan Savory">Allan Savory</a>, as part of <a href="/wiki/Holistic_management" class="mw-redirect" title="Holistic management">holistic management</a>, claims that while large herds are often blamed for desertification, prehistoric lands used to support large or larger herds and areas where herds were removed in the United States are still desertifying;<sup id="cite_ref-ASavory_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASavory-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Range_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Range science">range scientists</a> have however not been able to experimentally confirm his claims.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
