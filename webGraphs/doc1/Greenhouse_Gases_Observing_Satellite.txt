<p>The <b>Greenhouse Gases Observing Satellite</b> (<b>GOSat</b>), also known as <b>Ibuki</b><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>:&#32;<span lang="ja">いぶき</span><span style="display:none">,</span>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Hepburn_romanization" title="Hepburn romanization">Hepburn</a>:&#32;<i>Ibuki</i>, meaning "breath"<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>)</span>, is an <a href="/wiki/Earth_observation_satellite" title="Earth observation satellite">Earth observation satellite</a> and the world's first satellite dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse-gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse-gas">greenhouse-gas</a>-monitoring.<sup id="cite_ref-AP_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AP-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It measures the densities of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> from 56,000 locations on the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth&#39;s atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Reuters_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The GOSAT was developed by the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (<a href="/wiki/JAXA" title="JAXA">JAXA</a>) and launched on 23 January 2009, from the <a href="/wiki/Tanegashima_Space_Center" title="Tanegashima Space Center">Tanegashima Space Center</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Reuters_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_the_Environment_(Japan)" title="Ministry of the Environment (Japan)">Ministry of the Environment</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Environmental_Studies" title="National Institute for Environmental Studies">National Institute for Environmental Studies</a> (NIES) <sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> use the data to track gases causing the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>, and share the data with <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> and other international scientific organizations.<sup id="cite_ref-AP_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AP-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Launch">Launch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_Gases_Observing_Satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Launch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>GOSAT was launched along with seven other <a href="/wiki/Piggy-back_(transportation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Piggy-back (transportation)">piggyback</a> probes using the <a href="/wiki/H-IIA" title="H-IIA">H-IIA</a>, Japan's primary large-scale <a href="/wiki/Expendable_launch_system" title="Expendable launch system">expendable launch system</a>, at 3:54&#160;am on 23 January 2009 UTC on <a href="/wiki/Tanegashima" title="Tanegashima">Tanegashima</a>, a small island in southern Japan, after a two-day delay due to unfavourable weather.<sup id="cite_ref-Reuters_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AP_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AP-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> At approximately 16 minutes after liftoff, the separation of Ibuki from the launch rocket was confirmed.<sup id="cite_ref-JAXA_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAXA-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Instruments">Instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greenhouse_Gases_Observing_Satellite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to JAXA, the Ibuki satellite is equipped with a greenhouse gas observation sensor (TANSO-FTS) and a cloud/aerosol sensor (TANSO-CAI) that supplements TANSO-FTS. The greenhouse gas observation sensor of Ibuki observes a wide range of wavelengths (near-<a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> region–thermal infrared region) within the infrared band to enhance observation accuracy.<sup id="cite_ref-JAXA_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JAXA-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The satellite uses a <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">spectrometer</a> to measure different elements and compounds based on their response to certain types of light. This technology allows the satellite to measure "the concentration of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere at a super-high resolution."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
