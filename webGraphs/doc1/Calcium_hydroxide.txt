<p><b>Calcium hydroxide</b> (traditionally called <b>slaked lime</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the chemical formula <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">Ca</a>(<a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">OH</a>)<sub>2</sub>. It is a colorless crystal or white powder and is obtained when <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a> (called <i>lime</i> or <i>quicklime</i>) is mixed, or <a href="/wiki/Slaking_(geology)" title="Slaking (geology)">slaked</a> with <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>. It has many names including <b>hydrated lime</b>, <b>caustic lime</b>, <b>builders' lime</b>, <b>slack lime</b>, <b>cal</b>, or <b>pickling lime</b>. Calcium hydroxide is used in many applications, including food preparation. <b><a href="/wiki/Limewater" title="Limewater">Limewater</a></b> is the common name for a <a href="/wiki/Saturated_solution" class="mw-redirect" title="Saturated solution">saturated solution</a> of calcium hydroxide.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Calcium hydroxide is relatively insoluble in water, with a <a href="/wiki/Solubility_product" class="mw-redirect" title="Solubility product">solubility product</a> <a href="/wiki/Solubility_product" class="mw-redirect" title="Solubility product">K<sub>sp</sub></a> of 5.5&#160;×&#160;10<sup>−6</sup>. It is large enough that its solutions are basic according to the following reaction:
</p>
<dl><dd>Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub>  → Ca<sup>2+</sup> + 2 OH<sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>At ambient temperature. Calcium hydroxide (<a href="/wiki/Portlandite" title="Portlandite">portlandite</a>) dissolves in pure water to produce an alkaline solution with a pH of about 12.4. Calcium hydroxide solutions can cause chemical burns. At high pH value (see <a href="/wiki/Common_ion_effect" class="mw-redirect" title="Common ion effect">common ion effect</a>), its solubility drastically decreases.  This behavior is relevant to cement pastes. Aqueous solutions of calcium hydroxide are called <a href="/wiki/Limewater" title="Limewater">limewater</a> and are medium strength <a href="/wiki/Basic_(chemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Basic (chemistry)">bases</a> that reacts with <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> and can attack some <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> (amphoteric hydroxide dissolving at high pH) while protecting other metals from corrosion such as <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> by <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">passivation</a> of their surface. Limewater turns milky in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> due to formation of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>, a process called <a href="/wiki/Carbonatation" title="Carbonatation">carbonatation</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub>   →   CaCO<sub>3</sub>  + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>When heated to 512&#160;°C, the <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial pressure</a> of water in equilibrium with calcium hydroxide reaches 101<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>kPa (normal atmospheric pressure), which <a href="/wiki/Chemical_decomposition" title="Chemical decomposition">decomposes</a> calcium hydroxide into calcium oxide and water.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub>   →   CaO  + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<h2><span id="Structure.2C_preparation.2C_occurrence"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure,_preparation,_occurrence">Structure, preparation, occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure, preparation, occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CaOH2SEM.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/CaOH2SEM.jpg/170px-CaOH2SEM.jpg" width="170" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/CaOH2SEM.jpg/255px-CaOH2SEM.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/CaOH2SEM.jpg/340px-CaOH2SEM.jpg 2x" data-file-width="406" data-file-height="344" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CaOH2SEM.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Scanning_electron_microscopy" class="mw-redirect" title="Scanning electron microscopy">SEM</a> image of fractured hardened cement paste, showing plates of calcium hydroxide       and needles of <a href="/wiki/Ettringite" title="Ettringite">ettringite</a> (micron scale)</div></div></div>
<p>Calcium hydroxide adopts a polymeric structure, as do all metal hydroxides.  The structure is identical to that of Mg(OH)<sub>2</sub> (<i>brucite structure</i>); i.e., the <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_iodide" title="Cadmium iodide">cadmium iodide</a> motif. Strong <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_bond" title="Hydrogen bond">hydrogen bonds</a> exist between the layers.<sup id="cite_ref-G&amp;W_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;W-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Calcium hydroxide is produced commercially by treating lime with water:
</p>
<dl><dd>CaO + H<sub>2</sub>O → Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>In the laboratory it can be prepared by mixing <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/aqueous" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:aqueous">aqueous</a> solutions of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_chloride" title="Calcium chloride">calcium chloride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>. The mineral form, <a href="/wiki/Portlandite" title="Portlandite">portlandite</a>, is relatively rare but can be found in some volcanic, <a href="/wiki/Plutonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Plutonic">plutonic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rocks" class="mw-redirect" title="Metamorphic rocks">metamorphic rocks</a>. It has also been known to arise in burning coal dumps.
</p><p>The positively charged ionized species CaOH<sup>+</sup> has been detected in the atmosphere of <a href="/wiki/S-type_star" title="S-type star">S-type stars</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retrograde_solubility">Retrograde solubility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Retrograde solubility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The solubility of calcium hydroxide (<a href="/wiki/Portlandite" title="Portlandite">portlandite</a>) at 70&#160;°C is about half of its value at 25&#160;°C. The reason for this rather uncommon phenomenon is that the dissolution of calcium hydroxide in water is an exothermic process, and also adheres to <a href="/wiki/Le_Chatelier%27s_principle" title="Le Chatelier&#39;s principle">Le Chatelier's principle</a>. A lowering of temperature thus favours the elimination of the heat liberated through the process of dissolution and increases the equilibrium constant of dissolution of Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub>, and so increase its solubility at low temperature. This counter-intuitive temperature dependence of the solubility is referred to as "retrograde" or "inverse" solubility. The variably hydrated phases of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_sulfate" title="Calcium sulfate">calcium sulfate</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bassanite" title="Bassanite">bassanite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anhydrite" title="Anhydrite">anhydrite</a>) also exhibit a retrograde solubility for the same reason because their dissolution reactions are exothermic.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One significant application of calcium hydroxide is as a <a href="/wiki/Flocculation" title="Flocculation">flocculant</a>, in water and <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">sewage treatment</a>. It forms a fluffy charged solid that aids in the removal of smaller particles from water, resulting in a clearer product. This application is enabled by the low cost and low toxicity of calcium hydroxide. It is also used in fresh water treatment for raising the pH of the water so that pipes will not corrode where the base water is acidic, because it is self-regulating and does not raise the pH too much.
</p><p>It is also used in the preparation of ammonia gas (NH<sub>3</sub>), using the following reaction:
</p>
<dl><dd>Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub> + <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">2NH<sub>4</sub>Cl</a> → <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">2NH<sub>3</sub></a> + <a href="/wiki/Calcium_Chloride" class="mw-redirect" title="Calcium Chloride">CaCl<sub>2</sub></a> + 2H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Another large application is in the paper industry, where it is an intermediate in the reaction in the production of sodium hydroxide. This conversion is part of the <i>causticizing</i> step in the <a href="/wiki/Kraft_process#Recovery_process" title="Kraft process">Kraft process</a> for making pulp.<sup id="cite_ref-G&amp;W_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;W-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  In the causticizing operation burned lime is added to <i>green liquor</i> which is a solution primarily of sodium carbonate and sodium sulfate produced by dissolving <i>smelt</i>, which is the molten form of these chemicals from the recovery furnace.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_industry">Food industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Food industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because of its low <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxicity</a> and the mildness of its basic properties, slaked lime is widely used in the <a href="/wiki/Food_industry" title="Food industry">food industry</a> to:
</p>
<ul><li>clarify raw juice from <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sugar_beet" title="Sugar beet">sugar beets</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Sugar_industry" title="Sugar industry">sugar industry</a>, (see <a href="/wiki/Carbonatation" title="Carbonatation">carbonatation</a>)</li>
<li>process water for alcoholic beverages and soft drinks</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pickling" title="Pickling">pickle</a> cucumbers and other foods</li>
<li>make Chinese <a href="/wiki/Century_egg" title="Century egg">century eggs</a></li>
<li>in maize preparation: removes the cellulose hull of maize kernals (see <a href="/wiki/Nixtamalization" title="Nixtamalization">nixtamalization</a>)</li>
<li>clear a <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">brine</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonates</a> of calcium and <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> in the manufacture of salt for food and pharmaceutical uses</li>
<li>fortify (Ca supplement) fruit drinks, such as orange juice, and <a href="/wiki/Infant_formula" title="Infant formula">infant formula</a></li>
<li>aid digestion (called Choona, used in India in <i><a href="/wiki/Paan" title="Paan">paan</a></i>, a mixture of <a href="/wiki/Areca_nuts" class="mw-redirect" title="Areca nuts">areca nuts</a>, calcium hydroxide and a variety of seeds wrapped in betel leaves)</li>
<li>substitute for <a href="/wiki/Baking_soda" class="mw-redirect" title="Baking soda">baking soda</a> in making <i><a href="/wiki/Papadam" class="mw-redirect" title="Papadam">papadam</a></i>.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Native_American_uses">Native American uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Native American uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nixtamalized_Corn_maize_El_Salvador_recipe.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Nixtamalized_Corn_maize_El_Salvador_recipe.jpg/220px-Nixtamalized_Corn_maize_El_Salvador_recipe.jpg" width="220" height="132" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Nixtamalized_Corn_maize_El_Salvador_recipe.jpg/330px-Nixtamalized_Corn_maize_El_Salvador_recipe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Nixtamalized_Corn_maize_El_Salvador_recipe.jpg/440px-Nixtamalized_Corn_maize_El_Salvador_recipe.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2700" data-file-height="1624" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nixtamalized_Corn_maize_El_Salvador_recipe.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dry untreated maize (left), and treated maize (right) after boiling in water with calcium hydroxide (15<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>ml, or 1<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>Tbsp, lime for 500<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>g of corn) for 15 minutes.</div></div></div>
<p>In Spanish, calcium hydroxide is called <i>cal</i>. Maize cooked with <i>cal</i> (<a href="/wiki/Nixtamalization" title="Nixtamalization">nixtamalization</a>) becomes <a href="/wiki/Hominy" title="Hominy">hominy</a> (<i>nixtamal</i>), which significantly increases the bioavailability of niacin, and it is also considered tastier and easier to digest.
</p><p>In chewing <a href="/wiki/Coca_leaves" class="mw-redirect" title="Coca leaves">coca leaves</a>, calcium hydroxide is usually chewed alongside to keep the <a href="/wiki/Alkaloid" title="Alkaloid">alkaloid</a> <a href="/wiki/Stimulant" title="Stimulant">stimulants</a> chemically available for <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(small_intestine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Absorption (small intestine)">absorption</a> by the body. Similarly, Native Americans traditionally chewed tobacco leaves with calcium hydroxide derived from burnt mollusc shells to enhance the effects. It has also been used by some indigenous American tribes as an ingredient in <i><a href="/wiki/Yopo" class="mw-redirect" title="Yopo">yopo</a></i>, a psychedelic snuff prepared from the beans of some <i><a href="/wiki/Anadenanthera" title="Anadenanthera">Anadenanthera</a></i> species.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Asian_uses">Asian uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Asian uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Calcium hydroxide is typically added to a bundle of <a href="/wiki/Areca_nut" title="Areca nut">areca nut</a> and <a href="/wiki/Betel" title="Betel">betel</a> leaf to keep the <a href="/wiki/Alkaloid" title="Alkaloid">alkaloid</a> <a href="/wiki/Stimulant" title="Stimulant">stimulants</a> chemically available to enter the bloodstream via <a href="/wiki/Sublingual" class="mw-redirect" title="Sublingual">sublingual</a> absorption.
</p><p>It is used in making <i><a href="/wiki/Naswar" title="Naswar">naswar</a></i> (also known as <i>nass</i> or <i>niswar</i>), a type of dipping tobacco made from fresh tobacco leaves, calcium hydroxide (<i>chuna</i>), and wood ash.  It is consumed most in the <a href="/wiki/Pashtun_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Pashtun people">Pathan</a> diaspora, <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>. Villagers also use calcium hydroxide to paint their mud houses in Afghanistan, Pakistan and India.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_risks">Health risks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcium_hydroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Health risks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Unprotected exposure to Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub> can cause severe skin irritation, chemical burns, blindness or lung damage.
