<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Zero population growth</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Zero_population_growth_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Zero population growth (disambiguation)">Zero population growth (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Zero population growth</b>, sometimes abbreviated <b>ZPG</b> (also called the <a href="/wiki/Total_fertility_rate#Replacement_rates" title="Total fertility rate">replacement level of fertility</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-Ref&#95;_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ref_-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is a condition of <a href="/wiki/Demographic" class="mw-redirect" title="Demographic">demographic</a> balance where the number of people in a specified population neither <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">grows</a> nor <a href="/wiki/Population_decline" title="Population decline">declines</a>, considered as a social aim by some.<sup id="cite_ref-Ref_a_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ref_a-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
According to some, zero population growth, perhaps after stabilizing at some <a href="/wiki/Optimum_population" title="Optimum population">optimum population</a>, is the ideal towards which countries and the whole world should aspire in the interests of accomplishing long-term environmental <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ref_b_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ref_b-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> What it means by ‘the number of people neither grows nor declines’ is that births plus in-migrants equal deaths plus out-migrants.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero_population_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A loosely defined goal of ZPG is to match the <a href="/wiki/Total_fertility_rate#Replacement_rates" title="Total fertility rate">replacement fertility rate</a>, which is the average number of children per woman which would hold the population constant. This replacement fertility will depend on <a href="/wiki/Mortality_rates" class="mw-redirect" title="Mortality rates">mortality rates</a> and the sex ratio at birth, and varies from around 2.1 in developed countries to over 3.0 in some developing countries.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The American <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociologist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Demography" title="Demography">demographer</a> <a href="/wiki/Kingsley_Davis" title="Kingsley Davis">Kingsley Davis</a> is credited with coining the term<sup id="cite_ref-Davis1967_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davis1967-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ref_c_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ref_c-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  but it was used earlier by George Stolnitz, who stated that the concept of a <a href="/wiki/Demography#Common_Rates_and_Ratios" title="Demography">stationary population</a> dated back to 1693.<sup id="cite_ref-Stolnitz1955_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stolnitz1955-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  A mathematical description was given by <a href="/wiki/James_Mirrlees" title="James Mirrlees">James Mirrlees</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mirrlees1967_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mirrlees1967-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the late 1960s ZPG became a prominent political movement in the U.S. and parts of Europe, with strong links to <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminism</a>. <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> was a stronghold of the ZPG activists who believed “that a constantly increasing population is responsible for many of our problems: pollution, violence, loss of values and of individual privacy.”<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Founding fathers of the movement were <a href="/wiki/Paul_R._Ehrlich" title="Paul R. Ehrlich">Paul Ehrlich</a>, author of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Population_Bomb" title="The Population Bomb">The Population Bomb</a></i>, Richard Bowers, a Connecticut lawyer, and Professor <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lee_Remington" title="Charles Lee Remington">Charles Lee Remington</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Ehrlich stated: “The mother of the year should be a sterilized woman with two adopted children.”
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero_population_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the long term, zero population growth can be achieved when the <a href="/wiki/Birth_rate" title="Birth rate">birth rate</a> of a population equals the <a href="/wiki/Death_rate" class="mw-redirect" title="Death rate">death rate</a>, i.e. fertility is at replacement level and birth and death rates are stable, a condition also called demographic equilibrium. Unstable rates can lead to drastic changes in population levels. This analysis is valid for the planet as a whole (assuming that <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_spaceflight" title="Interplanetary spaceflight">interplanetary travel</a> remains at zero or negligible levels), but not necessarily for a region or country as it ignores <a href="/wiki/Human_migration" title="Human migration">migration</a>. A population that has been growing in the past will have a higher proportion of young people.  As it is younger people who have children, there is large time lag between the point at which the fertility rate (mean total number of children each woman has during her childbearing years) falls to the replacement level (the fertility rate which would result in equal birth and death rates for a population at equilibrium) and the point at which the population stops rising.<sup id="cite_ref-Ref_d_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ref_d-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The reason for this is that even though the fertility rate has dropped to replacement level, people already within a population continue to live for some time. Therefore, equilibrium, with a static population, will not be reached until the first "replacement level" birth cohorts reach old age and die. The related calculations are complex because the population's overall death rate can vary over time, and mortality also varies with age (being highest among the old).
</p><p>Conversely, with fertility below replacement, a large elderly generation eventually results (as in an aging “<a href="/wiki/Baby_boom" title="Baby boom">baby boom</a>”); but since that generation failed to replace itself during its <a href="/wiki/Fertility" title="Fertility">fertile</a> years, a subsequent “population bust”, or decrease in population, will occur when the older generation dies off. This effect has been termed <a href="/wiki/Birth_dearth" title="Birth dearth">birth dearth</a>. In addition, if a country's fertility is at replacement level, and has been that way for at least several decades (to stabilize its age distribution), then that country's population could still experience coincident growth due to continuously increasing <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">life expectancy</a>, even though the population growth is likely to be smaller than it would be from natural population increase.
</p><p>Zero population growth is often a goal of demographic planners and <a href="/wiki/Environmentalist" title="Environmentalist">environmentalists</a> who believe that reducing population growth is essential for the health of the <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>. Preserving cultural traditions and <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_diversity" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethnic diversity">ethnic diversity</a> is a factor for not allowing human populations levels or rates to fall too low. Achieving ZPG is difficult because a country's population growth is often determined by economic factors, incidence of poverty, natural disasters, disease, etc.
</p><p>However, even if there is zero population growth, there may be changes in demographics of great <a href="/wiki/Demographic_gift" class="mw-redirect" title="Demographic gift">importance to economic factors</a>, such as changes in <a href="/wiki/Population_pyramid" title="Population pyramid">age distribution</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="How_ZPG_can_be_achieved">How ZPG can be achieved</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero_population_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: How ZPG can be achieved">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Number of demographic experts have suggested a few ways to reach zero population growth.
</p><p>Biologist Alan D. Thornhill and Author <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Quinn" title="Daniel Quinn">Daniel Quinn</a> argue that human population growth is a function of the human food supply<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and that human population growth can only be achieved by an expanded food supply to support the growing population. In 1998, they produced a video entitled "<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AAqWUxCjisE">Food Production and Human Population Growth</a>" where they explain the theory and answer audience questions.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Albert_Allen_Bartlett" title="Albert Allen Bartlett">Albert Bartlett</a>, an emeritus professor of physics at University of Colorado at Boulder in his lifetime, suggested that the population have following choices to be at ZPG:
</p>
<ol><li>Voluntarily limit births and immigration to achieve zero population growth;</li>
<li>Continue on the present path until our population is so large that draconian measures become necessary to stop the growth of population;</li>
<li>Do nothing and let nature stop the growth through disease, starvation, war and pestilence. If humans do not solve the problem, nature will.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>Similarly, Jason Brent, another demographic expert, argues that there are three ways to achieve zero population growth. His argument is as follows:
</p>
<ol><li>By war, with or without <a href="/wiki/Weapons_of_mass_destruction" class="mw-redirect" title="Weapons of mass destruction">weapons of mass destruction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Starvation" title="Starvation">starvation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">rape</a>, <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_cleansing" title="Ethnic cleansing">ethnic cleansing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Internment" title="Internment">concentration camps</a>, and other horrors beyond the imagination, when humanity has exceeded the <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a> of the Earth.</li>
<li>By the voluntary action of all of humanity prior to the human population exceeding the carrying capacity of the Earth. If any group or even if a single-family failed to control its population the entire program would fail.</li>
<li>By coercive population control prior to the human population exceeding the carrying capacity of the Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_China">In China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zero_population_growth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/China#Demographics" title="China">China</a> is the largest country by population in the world, being home to some 1.4 billion people. China is expected to witness a zero population growth rate by 2030. China's population growth has slowed since the beginning of this century. This was mostly the result of China's economic growth and increasing living standards which led to the decline. However, many demographers also credit China's <a href="/wiki/One-child_policy" title="One-child policy">family planning policy</a>, which was formulated in the early 1970s, encourages late marriages, late childbearing, and the use of contraceptives, and since 1980 has limited most urban couples to one child and most rural couples to two children. According to government projections, the work-age population will then drop to 870 million.  It was said, in 2009, that the Chinese government was hoping to see zero population growth in the future<sup id="cite_ref-Xiang2009_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Xiang2009-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> but, in November 2013, a relaxation of the one-child policy was announced amid unpopularity, reduced labour pool and support for an ageing population.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_News_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_News-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
