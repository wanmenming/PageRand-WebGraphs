<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Sustainable fashion</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Organiccotton.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Organiccotton.jpg/220px-Organiccotton.jpg" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Organiccotton.jpg/330px-Organiccotton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Organiccotton.jpg/440px-Organiccotton.jpg 2x" data-file-width="698" data-file-height="519" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Organiccotton.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Organic_cotton" title="Organic cotton">Organic cotton</a> yarn</div></div></div>
<p><b>Sustainable fashion</b>, also called <b>eco fashion</b>, is a part of the growing design philosophy and trend of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>, the goal of which is to create a system which can be supported indefinitely in terms of <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">human impact on the environment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_responsibility" title="Social responsibility">social responsibility</a>. It can be seen as an alternative trend against <a href="/wiki/Fast_fashion" title="Fast fashion">fast fashion</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sustainable <a href="/wiki/Fashion" title="Fashion">fashion</a> came into the public foray in the late 1980s and early 1990s as well-known companies such as <a href="/wiki/Patagonia_(clothing)" title="Patagonia (clothing)">Patagonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Esprit_Holdings" title="Esprit Holdings">ESPRIT</a> brought "sustainability" into their businesses. The owners of those companies at that time, <a href="/wiki/Yvon_Chouinard" title="Yvon Chouinard">Yvon Chouinard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Doug_Tompkins" class="mw-redirect" title="Doug Tompkins">Doug Tompkins</a> were outdoorsmen and witnessed the environment being harmed by over consumption. They commissioned research into the impacts of fibers used in their companies. For Patagonia, this resulted in a lifecycle assessment for four fibers, cotton, wool, nylon and polyester. For ESPRIT the focus was on cotton, which represented 90% of their business at that time.
</p><p>The principles of sustainable fashion as put forward by these two companies was based on the philosophy of the deep ecologists <a href="/wiki/Arne_N%C3%A6ss" title="Arne Næss">Arne Næss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fritjof_Capra" title="Fritjof Capra">Fritjof Capra</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Callenbach" title="Ernest Callenbach">Ernest Callenbach</a>.
</p><p>The work of these companies influenced a whole movement in fashion and sustainability. They co-funded the first organic cotton conference held in 1991 in Visalia, California. ESPRIT ecollection, developed by head designer <a href="/wiki/Lynda_Grose" title="Lynda Grose">Lynda Grose</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> was launched at retail in 1992 and was based on the Eco Audit Guide, published by the Elmwood Institute. It comprised organic cotton, recycled wool, naturally processed wool, "low impact" dyes (focusing on water energy and toxicity), naturally colored cotton, non electroplated hard wear. Patagonia made a commitment to recycled polyester in 1992 and a company wide commitment to organic cotton in 1996. Both communicated their action for "sustainability" through <a href="/wiki/Point-of-sale" class="mw-redirect" title="Point-of-sale">point-of-sale</a> materials, catalogues and <a href="/wiki/Public_relations" title="Public relations">PR</a>. Both supported the work of The Sustainable Cotton Project, which ran farm tours for fashion industry professionals to meet directly with farmers growing organic and <a href="/wiki/Integrated_pest_management" title="Integrated pest management">IPM</a> cotton in California. Both companies contributed to the US <a href="/wiki/National_Organic_Standards_Board" title="National Organic Standards Board">NOSB</a> standards to include organic fiber as well as food.
</p><p>Throughout the 1990s and early 2000s, the movement in sustainable fashion broadened to include many brands. Though the primary focus has remained on improving the impacts of products through fiber and fabric processing and material provenance, Doug Tompkins and Yvon Chouinard were early to note the fundamental cause of unsustainability: exponential growth and consumption. ESPRIT placed and ad in <i><a href="/wiki/Utne_Reader" title="Utne Reader">Utne Reader</a></i> in 1990 making a plea for responsible consumption. Patagonia has since made headlines with its "Don't buy this Jacket" ad in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>.
</p><p>With the average American throwing away nearly 70 pounds of clothing per year,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> the fashion industry is the second largest cause of pollution worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Sustainable fashion approaches this issue with an ethical response to potential environmental and occupational hazards.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Purpose">Purpose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Purpose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The fashion industry has a clear opportunity to act differently, pursuing profit and growth while also creating new value for society and therefore for the world economy. It comes with an urgent need to place environmental, social, and ethical improvements on management’s agenda.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The goal of sustainable fashion is to increase the value of local production and products, to prolong the lifecycle of materials, to increase the value of timeless garments, to reduce the amount of waste, and to reduce the harm to the environment. It aims to educate people to practice environmentally friendly consumption by promoting the "green consumer".<sup id="cite_ref-:1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Representation">Representation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Representation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sustainable fashion resists fast fashion. It represents one of the forms of anti-consumption or alternative consumption, such as eco-consumption, <a href="/wiki/Green_consumption" title="Green consumption">green consumption</a>, ethical consumption, and political consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Characteristics of sustainable fashion match the philosophies of <a href="/wiki/Slow_fashion" title="Slow fashion">"slow fashion"</a>:  buying vintage clothes, redesigning old clothes, shopping from smaller producers, making clothes and accessories at home and buying garments that last longer. New ideas and product innovations are constantly redefining slow fashion, so using a static, single definition would ignore the evolving nature of the concept.
</p><p>The empirical definition of sustainable fashion is as follows: a fashion production which is locally embedded, slow in terms of production cycles, with a quantity of produced goods that corresponds to demand and without overproduction. It utilizes eco-friendly and qualitative materials, yet it is not cheap, the idea being to stimulate prolonged use of the clothing.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethics">Ethics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ethics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The clothing industry has an impact on the environment. Globalization, consumerism, and recycling are all a part of a clothing life cycle. Globalization has made it possible to produce clothing at increasingly lower prices, prices so low that many consumers consider this clothing to be disposable.<sup><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_fashion#cite_note-:2-6" title="Sustainable fashion">[6]</a></sup> Disposable clothing appears popular throughout many malls in America and Europe. This is a key characteristic of fast fashion. However, fast fashion adds to pollution and generates potential environmental and occupational hazards. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occupational_Hazards">Occupational Hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Occupational Hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>China has emerged as the largest exporter of fast fashion, accounting for 30% of world apparel exports.<sup><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_fashion#cite_note-7" title="Sustainable fashion">[7]</a></sup> However, some Chinese workers make as little as 12–18 cents per hour working in poor conditions.<sup><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_fashion#cite_note-:2-6" title="Sustainable fashion">[6]</a></sup> Each year Americans purchase approximately 1 billion garments made in China. Fierce global competition in the garment industry translates into poor working conditions for many laborers in developing nations. Developing countries aim to become a part of the world’s apparel market despite poor working conditions and low pay. Countries such as Honduras and Bangladesh import large amounts of clothing into the United States every year.<sup><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_fashion#cite_note-:2-6" title="Sustainable fashion">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_Hazards">Environmental Hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Environmental Hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The clothing industry has one of the highest impacts on the planet. High water usage, pollution from chemical treatments used in dyeing and preparation and the disposal of large amounts of unsold clothing through incineration or landfill deposits are hazardous to the environment.<sup><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_fashion#cite_note-8" title="Sustainable fashion">[8]</a></sup> There is a growing water <a href="/wiki/Scarcity" title="Scarcity">scarcity</a>, the current usage level of fashion materials (79 billion cubic meters annually) is very concerning, because textile production mostly takes place in areas of fresh water stress.<sup><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_fashion#cite_note-:0-4" title="Sustainable fashion">[4]</a></sup> Only around 20% of clothing is recycled or reused, huge amounts of fashion product end up as waste in landfills or is incinerated.<sup><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_fashion#cite_note-9" title="Sustainable fashion">[9]</a></sup> It has been estimated that in the UK alone around 350,000 tons of clothing ends up as landfill every year. According to Earth Pledge, a <a href="/wiki/Non-profit_organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-profit organization">non-profit organization</a> committed to promoting and supporting <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>, "At least 8,000 chemicals are used to turn raw materials into textiles and 25% of the world's <a href="/wiki/Pesticides" class="mw-redirect" title="Pesticides">pesticides</a> are used to grow non-organic cotton. This causes irreversible damage to people and the <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a>, and still two thirds of a garment's <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">carbon footprint</a> will occur after it is purchased."<sup><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_fashion#cite_note-10" title="Sustainable fashion">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials">Materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are many factors when considering the <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> of a material. The renewability and source of a <a href="/wiki/Fiber" title="Fiber">fiber</a>, the process of how a raw fiber is turned into a <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textile</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Working_conditions" class="mw-redirect" title="Working conditions">working conditions</a> of the people producing the materials, and the material's total <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">carbon footprint</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_fibers">Natural fibers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Natural fibers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Natural fibers are fibers which are found in <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">nature</a> and are not petroleum-based. Natural fibers can be categorized into two main groups, <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> or <a href="/wiki/Plant_fiber" class="mw-redirect" title="Plant fiber">plant fiber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> or <a href="/wiki/Animal_fiber" title="Animal fiber">animal fiber</a>. Uses of these fibers can be anything from buttons to eyewear such as sunglasses.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cellulose">Cellulose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cellulose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">Cotton</a> is one of the most widely grown and chemical-intensive crops in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Conventionally grown cotton uses approximately 25% of the world's <a href="/wiki/Insecticides" class="mw-redirect" title="Insecticides">insecticides</a> and more than 10% of the world's <a href="/wiki/Pesticides" class="mw-redirect" title="Pesticides">pesticides</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Other <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> fibers include: <a href="/wiki/Jute" title="Jute">jute</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flax" title="Flax">flax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemp" title="Hemp">hemp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ramie" title="Ramie">ramie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abaca" class="mw-redirect" title="Abaca">abaca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bamboo" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a> (used for <a href="/wiki/Viscose" title="Viscose">viscose</a>), <a href="/wiki/Soy" class="mw-redirect" title="Soy">soy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corn" class="mw-redirect" title="Corn">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banana" title="Banana">banana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pineapple" title="Pineapple">pineapple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beechwood" class="mw-redirect" title="Beechwood">beechwood</a> (used for <a href="/wiki/Rayon" title="Rayon">rayon</a>). Alternative fibers such as bamboo (in yarn) and hemp (of a variety that produces only a tiny amount of the psychoactive component found in cannabis) are coming into greater use in so-called eco-fashions.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Protein">Protein</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Protein">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Protein fibers originate from animal sources and are made up of protein molecules. The basic elements in these protein molecules being carbon, hydrogen oxygen and nitrogen.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Natural protein fibers include: <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angora_wool" title="Angora wool">angora</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camel" title="Camel">camel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alpaca" title="Alpaca">alpaca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Llama" title="Llama">llama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vicuna" class="mw-redirect" title="Vicuna">vicuna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cashmere_wool" title="Cashmere wool">cashmere</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mohair" title="Mohair">mohair</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufactured">Manufactured</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Manufactured">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Fibers manufactured from natural materials include: <a href="/wiki/Lyocell" title="Lyocell">Lyocell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polylactic_acid" title="Polylactic acid">polylactic acid</a> (PLA).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recycled_fibers">Recycled fibers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Recycled fibers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Recycled or reclaimed fibers are made from scraps of fabrics collected from clothing factories, which are processed back into short fibers for spinning into a new yarn. There are only a few facilities globally that are able to process the clippings.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Reliable source needed for the whole sentence (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Variations range from a blend of recycled cotton fibers with added RePET yarns for strength to recycled cotton fibers with virgin acrylic fibers which are added for color consistency and strength.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Upcycled_fibers">Upcycled fibers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Upcycled fibers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Upcycling" title="Upcycling">Upcycling</a> is the practice of converting materials into something with greater value in a second life.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Upcycling is a form of recycling, which refers to the reuse of material for the purpose of waste reduction. Upcycled fibers are made from materials that are not originally used to make fibers. This includes fibers made of <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gillnet" class="mw-redirect" title="Gillnet">gillnets</a>. An example of the use of this type of fiber can be seen in the shoe <a href="/wiki/Adidas" title="Adidas">Adidas</a> made with Parley for the Oceans.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another example is <a href="/wiki/Fish_leather" class="mw-redirect" title="Fish leather">fish leather</a> made from fish skins that are a by-product of the <a href="/wiki/Food_industry" title="Food industry">food industry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Fish leather tanning is less harmful on the environment due to no hair-removal being required, leading to less solid waste and organic pollutants in the wastewater from the process.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Also, no poisonous, explosive <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide" title="Hydrogen sulfide">hydrogen sulfide</a> gas is released in the process.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Upcycling also pre-supposes that designers invest creativity in their products and act as “entrepreneurs of taste” by adding design ideas for the purpose of creating a new garment from an old one.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Designer Conny Groenewegen focuses on using fishing wire and old fleece sweaters to re-design form and function in fashion.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Producers">Producers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Producers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Golden_Book_Gown_by_Ryan_Jude_Novelline.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A mannequin wears a multicolored gown with a golden bodice, full skirt, and flowing train." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/The_Golden_Book_Gown_by_Ryan_Jude_Novelline.jpg/220px-The_Golden_Book_Gown_by_Ryan_Jude_Novelline.jpg" width="220" height="205" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/The_Golden_Book_Gown_by_Ryan_Jude_Novelline.jpg/330px-The_Golden_Book_Gown_by_Ryan_Jude_Novelline.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/The_Golden_Book_Gown_by_Ryan_Jude_Novelline.jpg/440px-The_Golden_Book_Gown_by_Ryan_Jude_Novelline.jpg 2x" data-file-width="689" data-file-height="641" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Golden_Book_Gown_by_Ryan_Jude_Novelline.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ryan_Jude_Novelline#The_Golden_Book_Gown" title="Ryan Jude Novelline"><i>The Golden Book Gown</i></a> made of recycled book pages.</div></div></div><p>Due to the efforts taken to minimize harm in the growth, manufacturing, and shipping of the products, sustainable fashion is typically more expensive than clothing produced by conventional methods.<sup id="cite_ref-e2f_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-e2f-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> However, various celebrities, models, and designers have recently drawn attention to socially conscious and environmentally friendly fashion. More innovative eco-fashions are being developed and made available to consumers at different levels of the fashion spectrum, from casual clothing to <a href="/wiki/Haute_couture" title="Haute couture">haute couture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><h3><span id="Designers.2C_retailers.2C_and_labels"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Designers,_retailers,_and_labels">Designers, retailers, and labels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Designers, retailers, and labels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Eastern European prisoners are designing sustainable prison fashion in Latvia and Estonia under the <a href="/wiki/Heavy_Eco" title="Heavy Eco">Heavy Eco</a> label,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> part of a trend called "prison couture".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ryan_Jude_Novelline" title="Ryan Jude Novelline">Ryan Jude Novelline</a> created a ballroom gown constructed entirely from the pages of recycled and discarded children's books known as <i>The Golden Book Gown</i> that "prove[d] that green fashion can provide as rich a fantasia as can be imagined."<sup id="cite_ref-chevrolet_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chevrolet-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Eco-couture designer <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Tammam" title="Lucy Tammam">Lucy Tammam</a> uses <a href="/wiki/Eri_silk" title="Eri silk">eri silk</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ahimsa_silk" title="Ahimsa silk">ahimsa/peace silk</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Organic_cotton" title="Organic cotton">organic cotton</a> to create her eco friendly couture evening and bridal wear collections.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Other sustainable fashion labels include Elena Garcia, Nancy Dee, By Stamo, Outsider Fashion, Beyond Skin, Oliberté, Hetty Rose, DaRousso, KSkye the Label,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and Eva Cassis.<sup id="cite_ref-vildamagazine.com_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vildamagazine.com-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The brand Boll &amp; Branch make all of their <a href="/wiki/Bedding" title="Bedding">bedding</a> products from <a href="/wiki/Organic_cotton" title="Organic cotton">organic cotton</a> and have been certified by <a href="/wiki/Fair_Trade_USA" title="Fair Trade USA">Fair Trade USA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Hemp_Trading_Company" title="The Hemp Trading Company">The Hemp Trading Company</a> is an ethically driven underground clothing label, specializing in environmentally friendly, politically conscious street wear made of hemp, bamboo, organic cotton and other sustainable fabrics.<sup id="cite_ref-roberts_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-roberts-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Patagonia, a major retailer in casual wear, has been selling fleece clothing made from post-consumer plastic soda bottles since 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>There is no certain stable model among the designers for how to be sustainable in practice, and the understanding of sustainability is always a process or a work-in-progress.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Companies_and_Organizations">Companies and Organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Companies and Organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some companies and organizations are working to combat clothing pollution, increase sustainable design opportunities, and increase the visibility of the sustainable fashion movement. 
</p>
<ul><li>The National Association of Sustainable Fashion Designers is an organization aimed to assist entrepreneurs with growing fashion businesses that create social change and respect the environment. They provide specialized education, training and programs that can transform the fashion industry by cultivating collaboration, sustainability and economic growth.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Red Carpet Green Dress, founded by <a href="/wiki/Suzy_Amis_Cameron" title="Suzy Amis Cameron">Suzy Amis Cameron</a>, is a global initiative showcasing sustainable fashion on the <a href="/wiki/Red_carpet" title="Red carpet">red carpet</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Oscars</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Talent supporting the project includes <a href="/wiki/Naomie_Harris" title="Naomie Harris">Naomie Harris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missi_Pyle" title="Missi Pyle">Missi Pyle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kellan_Lutz" title="Kellan Lutz">Kellan Lutz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Olga_Kurylenko" title="Olga Kurylenko">Olga Kurylenko</a>.</li>
<li>Undress Brisbane is an Australian fashion show that sheds light on sustainable designers in Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li>Eco Age, a consultancy company specializing in enabling businesses to achieve growth and add value through sustainability is one of the most recognizable organizations that promote sustainable fashion. Its creative director, Livia Firth, is also the founder of the Green Carpet Challenge which aims to promote ethically made outfits from fashion designers.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li>Ecoluxe London, a not-for-profit platform, supports luxury with ethos through hosting a biannual exhibition during <a href="/wiki/London_Fashion_Week" title="London Fashion Week">London Fashion Week</a> and showcasing eco-sustainable and ethical designers.<sup id="cite_ref-vildamagazine.com_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vildamagazine.com-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li>Fashion Takes Action formed in 2007 and received a non-profit status in 2011. It is an organization that promotes social justice, fair trade and sustainable clothing production as well as advances sustainability in the fashion system through education, awareness and collaboration. FTA promotes sustainable fashion via social media, PR, hosting fashion shows, public talks, school lectures and conferences.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li>The Ethical Fashion Initiative, a flagship program of the <a href="/wiki/International_Trade_Centre" title="International Trade Centre">International Trade Centre</a>, a joint agency of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_Trade_and_Development" title="United Nations Conference on Trade and Development">United Nations Conference on Trade and Development</a> (UNCTAD) and <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">World Trade Organization</a>, enables artisans living in urban and rural poverty to connect with the global fashion chain.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The Initiative also works with the rising generation of fashion talent from Africa, encouraging the forging sustainable and fulfilling creative collaborations with artisans on the continent.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The Ethical Fashion Initiative is headed by <a href="/wiki/Simone_Cipriani" title="Simone Cipriani">Simone Cipriani</a>.</li></ul>
<ul><li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://tranclo.com/">Trans-America Trading Company</a> is one of the biggest of about 3,000 textile recycler's in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Trans-America has processed more than 12 million pounds of post consumer textiles per year since 1942.  At its 80,000-square-foot sorting facility, workers separate used clothing into 300 different categories by type of item, size, and fiber content. About 30% of the textiles are turned into absorbent wiping rags for industrial uses, and another 25–30% are recycled into fiber for use as stuffing for upholstery, insulation, and the manufacture of paper products.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials_Controversy">Materials Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Materials Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though <a href="/wiki/Organic_cotton" title="Organic cotton">organic cotton</a> is considered a more sustainable choice for fabric, as it uses fewer <a href="/wiki/Pesticides" class="mw-redirect" title="Pesticides">pesticides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_fertilizer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical fertilizer">chemical fertilizers</a>, it remains less than 1% global cotton production. Hurdles to growth include cost of hand labor for hand weeding, reduced yields in comparison to conventional cotton and absence of fiber commitments from brands to farmers before planting seed. The up front financial risks and costs are therefore shouldered by the farmers, many of whom struggle to compete with economies of scale of corporate farms.
</p><p>Though some designers have marketed <a href="/wiki/Bamboo_fiber" class="mw-redirect" title="Bamboo fiber">bamboo fiber</a>, as an alternative to conventional cotton, citing that it absorbs greenhouse gases during its life cycle and grows quickly and plentifully without pesticides, the conversion of bamboo fiber to fabric is the same as rayon and is highly toxic. The FTC ruled that labeling of bamboo fiber should read "rayon from bamboo". Bamboo fabric can cause environmental harm in production due to the chemicals used to create a soft viscose from hard bamboo.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Impacts regarding production of new materials make recycled, reclaimed, surplus, and vintage fabric arguably the most sustainable choice, as the raw material requires no agriculture and no manufacturing to produce.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> However, it must be noted that these are indicative of a system of production and consumption that creates excessive volumes of waste.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second-Hand_Controversy">Second-Hand Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Second-Hand Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Used clothing is sold in more than 100 countries. In Tanzania, used clothing is sold at the <i>mitumba  (Swahili for "secondhand")</i> markets. Most of the clothing is imported from the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> However, there are concerns that trade in secondhand clothing in African countries decreases development of local industries even as it creates employment in these countries.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> And the authors of <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://randd.defra.gov.uk/Document.aspx?Document=WR0201_6352_FRP.pdf">Recycling of Low Grade Clothing Waste</a></i> warn that in the long run, as prices and quality of new clothing continue to decline, the demand for used clothing will also diminish.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing_Controversy">Marketing Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Marketing Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The increase in western consumers’ environmental interest is motivating companies to use sustainable and environmental arguments solely to increase sales. And because environmental and sustainability issues are complex, it is also easy to mislead consumers. Companies can use sustainability as a “marketing ploy” something that can be seen as <a href="/wiki/Greenwashing" title="Greenwashing">greenwashing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Greenwashing is the deceptive use of an eco-agenda in marketing strategies.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It refers mostly to corporations that make efforts to clean up their reputation because of social pressure or for the purpose of financial gain. 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_of_fashion_sustainability">Future of fashion sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_fashion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Future of fashion sustainability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the European Union, the Registration, Evaluation, Authorization and Restriction of Chemicals (REACH) regulations required in 2007 that clothing manufacturers and importers identified and quantified the chemicals used in their products.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On May 3, 2012, the world's largest summit on fashion sustainability was held in Copenhagen, gathering more than 1,000 key stakeholders in the industry to discuss the importance of making the fashion industry sustainable. Copenhagen Fashion Summit has since then gathered thousands of people from the fashion industry in their effort to create a movement within the industry.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2012, the Sustainable Apparel Coalition launched the <a href="/wiki/Higg_Index" title="Higg Index">Higg Index</a>, a self-assessment standard designed to measure and promote sustainable supply chains in the apparel and footwear industries.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Founded in 2011, the Sustainable Apparel Coalition is a nonprofit organization whose members include brands producing apparel or footwear, retailers, industry affiliates and trade associations, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Environmental_Protection_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a>, academic institutions and environmental nonprofits.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Global Change Award, is a innovation challenge created by the H&amp;M foundation.<sup id="cite_ref-:02_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:02-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> It created a trend report in 2017 to look at the future of sustainable fashion.  Five mega trends are identified by the organization that will lead the future of sustainable fashion.  The first mega trend is "Power of Nature" which is the industry looking into materials that have always been looked at as waste as a more sustainable method to making new clothing.<sup id="cite_ref-:02_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:02-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The materials that will mitigate negative impacts from the industry include vegan materials from the earth and recycling old fabric into new clothing.  The second mega trend is "Rent a Closet" this initiative has been around for a while. This trend ultimately lowers the new purchase of clothing and disposal of clothing, which means less waste.<sup id="cite_ref-:02_53-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:02-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Rent_the_Runway" title="Rent the Runway">Rent the Runway</a> is an example of the "Rent a Closet" trend.  Rent the Runway started as a company that would give luxury brands like <a href="/wiki/Herv%C3%A9_Leger" title="Hervé Leger">Hervé Leger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vera_Wang" title="Vera Wang">Vera Wang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Etro" title="Etro">Etro</a> to people who may not be able to afford the clothing at regular retail price.  Renting and sharing clothing is also known as CFC (collaborative fashion consumption) a sustainable fashion trend consumers are getting involved in.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>  The third trend is "Long Live Fashion" is the revival of <a href="/wiki/Vintage_clothing" title="Vintage clothing">Vintage clothing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:02_53-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:02-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Vintage clothing is a way to lower the amount of clothing that gets disposed of and ends up in landfills.  Companies like RE/DONE, Vintage Twin and Frankie Collective sell re-paired vintage clothing. Repairing and reselling clothing has less negative impact than creating new clothing does.   The fourth mega trend is  "Innovative Recycling" which is looking at waste as value.  The industry is starting to create incentives for consumers to participate in the recycling of clothing.<sup id="cite_ref-:02_53-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:02-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
