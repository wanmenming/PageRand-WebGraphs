<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Extinction risk from global warming</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Update" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Ambox current red.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/42px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png" width="42" height="34" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/63px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/84px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="360" data-file-height="290" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article needs to be <b>updated</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please update this article to reflect recent events or newly available information.</span>  <small><i>(November 2017)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The <b>extinction risk of global warming</b> is the risk of <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> becoming <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinct</a> due to the <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">effects of global warming</a>.  This may be Earth's <a href="/wiki/The_Sixth_Extinction:_An_Unnatural_History" title="The Sixth Extinction: An Unnatural History">  sixth major extinction</a>, often called the <a href="/wiki/Anthropocene" title="Anthropocene">Anthropocene</a> or <a href="/wiki/Holocene_extinction" title="Holocene extinction">Holocene extinction</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_projections">Current projections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Extinction_risk_from_global_warming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Current projections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><p>
The scientific consensus in the 2007 <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">IPCC Fourth Assessment Report</a> is that </p><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"Anthropogenic warming could lead to some impacts that are abrupt or irreversible, depending upon the rate and magnitude of the climate change."
</p><p>"There is medium confidence that approximately 20-30% of species assessed so far are likely to be at increased risk of extinction if increases in global average warming exceed 1.5-2.5 °C (relative to 1980-1999). As global average temperature increase exceeds about 3.5 °C, model projections suggest significant extinctions (40-70% of species assessed) around the globe."
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In one study published in <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> in 2004, between 15 and 37% of 1103 endemic or near-endemic known plant and animal species will be "committed to extinction" by 2050.<sup id="cite_ref-Thomas2004_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomas2004-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> More properly, changes in habitat by 2050 will put them outside the survival range for the inhabitants, thus committing the species to extinction.
</p><p>Other researchers, such as Thuiller <i>et al.</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Thuiller2004_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thuiller2004-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Araújo <i>et al.</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Araujo2004_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Araujo2004-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Person <i>et al.</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Buckley and <a href="/wiki/Joan_Roughgarden" title="Joan Roughgarden">Roughgarden</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and Harte <i>et al.</i><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> have raised concern regarding uncertainty in Thomas <i>et al.</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s projections; some of these studies believe it is an overestimate, others believe the risk could be greater. Thomas <i>et al.</i> replied in Nature <sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> addressing criticisms and concluding "Although further investigation is needed into each of these areas, it is unlikely to result in substantially reduced estimates of extinction.  <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">Anthropogenic</a> climate change seems set to generate very large numbers of species-level extinctions." On the other hand, Daniel Botkin <i>et al.</i> state "... global estimates of extinctions due to climate change (Thomas et al. 2004) may have greatly overestimated the probability of extinction..."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mechanistic studies are documenting extinctions due to recent <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>: McLaughlin <i>et al.</i> documented two populations of <a href="/wiki/Bay_checkerspot_butterfly" title="Bay checkerspot butterfly">Bay checkerspot butterfly</a> being threatened by <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> change.<sup id="cite_ref-McLaughlin_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLaughlin-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Parmesan states, "Few studies have been conducted at a scale that encompasses an entire species"<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and McLaughlin <i>et al.</i> agreed "few mechanistic studies have linked extinctions to recent climate change."<sup id="cite_ref-McLaughlin_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLaughlin-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Lemur-like_Ringtail_Possum" class="mw-redirect" title="Lemur-like Ringtail Possum">white lemuroid possum</a> was reported to be the first known <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammal</a> species to be driven extinct by man-made <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. However, these reports were based on a misunderstanding. One population of these possums in the mountain forests of <a href="/wiki/North_Queensland" title="North Queensland">northern Queensland</a> is severely threatened by climate change as the animals cannot survive extended temperatures over 30&#160;°C. However, another population 100 kilometres south remains in good health.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to research published in the January 4, 2012 <i><a href="/wiki/Proceedings_of_the_Royal_Society_B" class="mw-redirect" title="Proceedings of the Royal Society B">Proceedings of the Royal Society B</a></i> current climate models may be flawed because they overlook two important factors: the differences in how quickly species relocate and competition among species. According to the researchers, led by <a href="/wiki/Mark_C._Urban" title="Mark C. Urban">Mark C. Urban</a>, an ecologist at the University of Connecticut, diversity decreased when they took these factors into account, and that new communities of organisms, which do not exist today, emerged. As a result the rate of extinctions may be higher than previously projected.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to research published in the 30 May 2014 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a>,</i> most known species have small ranges, and the numbers of small-ranged species are increasing quickly. They are geographically concentrated and are disproportionately likely to be threatened or already extinct. According to the research, current rates of extinction are three orders of magnitude higher than the <a href="/wiki/Background_extinction_rate" title="Background extinction rate">background extinction rate</a>, and future rates, which depend on many factors, are poised to increase. Although there has been rapid progress in developing protected areas, such efforts are not ecologically representative, nor do they optimally protect biodiversity. In the researchers' view, human activity tends to destroy critical habitats where species live, warms the planet, and tends to move species around the planet to places where they don’t belong and where they can come into conflict with human needs (e.g. causing species to become pests).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016 the <a href="/wiki/Bramble_Cay_melomys" title="Bramble Cay melomys">Bramble Cay melomys</a>, which lived on a <a href="/wiki/Great_Barrier_Reef" title="Great Barrier Reef">Great Barrier Reef</a> island, was reported to probably be the first mammal to become extinct because of sea level rises due to human-made climate change.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
