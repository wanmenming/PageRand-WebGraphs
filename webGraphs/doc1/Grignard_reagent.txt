<p>The <b>Grignard reaction</b> (pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɡriɲar/</span>) is an <a href="/wiki/Organometallic_chemistry" title="Organometallic chemistry">organometallic</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reaction</a> in which <a href="/wiki/Alkyl" title="Alkyl">alkyl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_group" title="Vinyl group">vinyl</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Aryl" title="Aryl">aryl</a>-<a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> <a href="/wiki/Halide" title="Halide">halides</a> (<b>Grignard reagents</b>) add to  a <a href="/wiki/Carbonyl" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> group in an <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehyde</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketone</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  This reaction is an important tool for the formation of <a href="/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93carbon_bond" title="Carbon–carbon bond">carbon–carbon bonds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The reaction of an organic halide with magnesium is <i>not</i> a Grignard reaction, but provides a Grignard reagent.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/File:Grignard_Reaction_Scheme.png" class="image" title="An example of a Grignard reaction"><img alt="An example of a Grignard reaction" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Grignard_Reaction_Scheme.png/500px-Grignard_Reaction_Scheme.png" width="500" height="86" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Grignard_Reaction_Scheme.png/750px-Grignard_Reaction_Scheme.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Grignard_Reaction_Scheme.png/1000px-Grignard_Reaction_Scheme.png 2x" data-file-width="3250" data-file-height="559" /></a></dd></dl>
<p>Grignard reactions and reagents were discovered by and are named after the French chemist <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Auguste_Victor_Grignard" class="mw-redirect" title="François Auguste Victor Grignard">François Auguste Victor Grignard</a> (<a href="/wiki/University_of_Nancy" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Nancy">University of Nancy</a>, France), who published it in 1900 and was awarded the 1912 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry" title="Nobel Prize in Chemistry">Nobel Prize in Chemistry</a> for this work.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  Grignard reagents are similar to <a href="/wiki/Organolithium_reagent" title="Organolithium reagent">organolithium reagents</a> because both are strong nucleophiles that can form new carbon–carbon bonds. The nucleophilicity increases if the alkyl substituent is replaced by an <a href="/wiki/Metal_amides" title="Metal amides">amido</a> group. These amido magnesium halides are called <a href="/wiki/Hauser_base" title="Hauser base">Hauser bases</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_mechanism">Reaction mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Reaction mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Grignard reagent functions as a <a href="/wiki/Nucleophile" title="Nucleophile">nucleophile</a>, attacking the <a href="/wiki/Electrophilic" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrophilic">electrophilic</a> carbon atom that is present within the <a href="/wiki/Polar_bond" class="mw-redirect" title="Polar bond">polar bond</a> of a carbonyl group. 
The addition of the Grignard reagent to the carbonyl typically proceeds through a six-membered ring transition state.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a href="/wiki/File:Grignard-Reaction_Mechanism.png" class="image" title="The mechanism of the Grignard reaction."><img alt="The mechanism of the Grignard reaction." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Grignard-Reaction_Mechanism.png/600px-Grignard-Reaction_Mechanism.png" width="600" height="120" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Grignard-Reaction_Mechanism.png/900px-Grignard-Reaction_Mechanism.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Grignard-Reaction_Mechanism.png/1200px-Grignard-Reaction_Mechanism.png 2x" data-file-width="1909" data-file-height="382" /></a></div></div>
<p>However, with hindered Grignard reagents, the reaction may proceed by single-electron transfer.  Similar pathways are assumed for other reactions of Grignard reagents, e.g., in the formation of carbon–<a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, carbon–<a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, carbon–<a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a>, carbon–<a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a> and other carbon–<a href="/wiki/Heteroatom" title="Heteroatom">heteroatom</a> bonds.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation_of_Grignard_reagent">Preparation of Grignard reagent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation of Grignard reagent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Grignard reagents form via the reaction of an alkyl or aryl halide with <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>. The reaction is conducted by adding the organic halide to a suspension of magnesium in an <a href="/wiki/Ether" title="Ether">etherial</a> solvent, which provides <a href="/wiki/Ligands" class="mw-redirect" title="Ligands">ligands</a> required to stabilize the <a href="/wiki/Organomagnesium_compound" class="mw-redirect" title="Organomagnesium compound">organomagnesium compound</a>.   Empirical evidence suggests that the reaction takes place on the surface of the metal.  The reaction proceeds through <a href="/wiki/Single_electron_transfer" class="mw-redirect" title="Single electron transfer">single electron transfer</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In the Grignard formation reaction, radicals may be converted into carbanions through a second electron transfer.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>R−X + Mg → R−X<sup>•−</sup> + Mg<sup>•+</sup></dd>
<dd>R−X<sup>•−</sup> → R<sup>•</sup> + X<sup>−</sup></dd>
<dd>R<sup>•</sup> + Mg<sup>•+</sup> → RMg<sup>+</sup></dd>
<dd>RMg<sup>+</sup> + X<sup>−</sup> → RMgX</dd></dl>
<p>A limitation of Grignard reagents is that they do not readily react with alkyl halides via an S<sub>N</sub>2 mechanism.  On the other hand, they readily participate in <a href="/wiki/Transmetalation" title="Transmetalation">transmetalation</a> reactions:
</p>
<dl><dd>RMgX  +  AlX  →   AlR  +  MgX<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>For this purpose, commercially available Grignard reagents are especially useful because this route avoids the problem with initiation.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_conditions">Reaction conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reaction conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In reactions involving Grignard reagents, it is important to exclude water and air, which rapidly destroy the reagent by <a href="/wiki/Protonolysis" title="Protonolysis">protonolysis</a> or oxidation.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  Since most Grignard reactions are conducted in anhydrous <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">diethyl ether</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tetrahydrofuran" title="Tetrahydrofuran">tetrahydrofuran</a>, side-reactions with air are limited by the protective blanket provided by solvent vapors.  Small-scale or quantitative preparations should be conducted under <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> or <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a> atmospheres, using <a href="/wiki/Air-free_technique" title="Air-free technique">air-free techniques</a>.  Although the reagents still need to be dry, ultrasound can allow Grignard reagents to form in wet solvents by activating the magnesium such that it consumes the water.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_organic_halide">The organic halide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The organic halide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Grignard reactions often start slowly.  As is common for reactions involving solids and solution, initiation follows an <a href="/wiki/Induction_period" title="Induction period">induction period</a> during which reactive magnesium becomes exposed to the organic reagents.  After this induction period, the reactions can be highly <a href="/wiki/Exothermic" class="mw-redirect" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a>.  Alkyl and aryl <a href="/wiki/Bromide" title="Bromide">bromides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iodide" title="Iodide">iodides</a> are common, with <a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">chlorides</a> being seen as well. However, <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluorides</a> are generally unreactive, except with specially activated magnesium (through <a href="/wiki/Rieke_metal" class="mw-redirect" title="Rieke metal">Rieke metals</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnesium">Magnesium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Magnesium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Typical Grignard reactions involve the use of magnesium ribbon.  All magnesium is coated with a  <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">passivating</a>  layer of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">magnesium oxide</a>, which inhibits reactions with the organic halide.  Specially activated magnesium, such as <a href="/wiki/Rieke_metals" title="Rieke metals">Rieke magnesium</a>, circumvents this problem.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The oxide layer can also be broken up using ultrasound,  using a stirring rod to scratch the oxidized layer off,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> or by adding a few drops of iodine or <a href="/wiki/1,2-Diiodoethane" title="1,2-Diiodoethane">1,2-Diiodoethane</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solvent">Solvent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Solvent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Methylmagnesium-chloride-THF-3D-balls.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Methylmagnesium-chloride-THF-3D-balls.png/220px-Methylmagnesium-chloride-THF-3D-balls.png" width="220" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Methylmagnesium-chloride-THF-3D-balls.png/330px-Methylmagnesium-chloride-THF-3D-balls.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Methylmagnesium-chloride-THF-3D-balls.png/440px-Methylmagnesium-chloride-THF-3D-balls.png 2x" data-file-width="1100" data-file-height="846" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Methylmagnesium-chloride-THF-3D-balls.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Usually Grignard reagents are written as RMgX, but in fact the magnesium(II) centre is tetrahedral when dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Lewis_basic" class="mw-redirect" title="Lewis basic">Lewis basic</a> solvents, as shown here for the bis-adduct of methylmagnesium chloride and THF.</div></div></div>
<p>Most Grignard reactions are conducted in ethereal solvents, especially <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">diethyl ether</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tetrahydrofuran" title="Tetrahydrofuran">THF</a>. With the chelating diether <a href="/wiki/Dioxane" class="mw-redirect" title="Dioxane">dioxane</a>, some Grignard reagents undergo a <a href="/wiki/Redistribution_reaction" class="mw-redirect" title="Redistribution reaction">redistribution reaction</a> to give diorganomagnesium compounds (R = organic group, X = halide):
</p>
<dl><dd>2 RMgX  +  dioxane  ⇌  R<sub>2</sub>Mg  +  MgX<sub>2</sub>(dioxane)</dd></dl>
<p>This reaction is known as the <a href="/wiki/Schlenk_equilibrium" title="Schlenk equilibrium">Schlenk equilibrium</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Testing_Grignard_reagents">Testing Grignard reagents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Testing Grignard reagents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because Grignard reagents are so sensitive to moisture and oxygen, many methods have been developed to test the quality of a batch.  Typical tests involve titrations with weighable, anhydrous protic reagents, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Menthol" title="Menthol">menthol</a> in the presence of a color-indicator.  The interaction of the Grignard reagent with <a href="/wiki/Phenanthroline" title="Phenanthroline">phenanthroline</a><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/2,2%27-bipyridine" class="mw-redirect" title="2,2&#39;-bipyridine">2,2'-bipyridine</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> causes a color change.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Initiation">Initiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Initiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many methods have been developed to initiate sluggish Grignard reactions.  These methods weaken the <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">passivating</a> layer of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">MgO</a>, thereby exposing highly reactive magnesium to the organic halide.  Mechanical methods include crushing of the Mg pieces in situ, rapid stirring, and <a href="/wiki/Sonication" title="Sonication">sonication</a><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> of the suspension.  <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">Iodine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methyl_iodide" title="Methyl iodide">methyl iodide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/1,2-Dibromoethane" title="1,2-Dibromoethane">1,2-Dibromoethane</a> are common activating agents.  The use of 1,2-dibromoethane is particularly advantageous as its action can be monitored by the observation of bubbles of <a href="/wiki/Ethylene" title="Ethylene">ethylene</a>.  Furthermore, the side-products are innocuous:
</p>
<dl><dd>Mg  +  BrC<sub>2</sub>H<sub>4</sub>Br →  C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>4</sub>  +  MgBr<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The amount of Mg consumed by these activating agents is usually insignificant. A small amount of <a href="/wiki/Mercuric_chloride" class="mw-redirect" title="Mercuric chloride">mercuric chloride</a> will <a href="/wiki/Amalgam_(chemistry)" title="Amalgam (chemistry)">amalgamate</a> the surface of the metal, allowing it to react.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_production">Industrial production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Industrial production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Grignard reagents are produced in industry for use <i>in situ</i>, or for sale. As with a bench-scale, the main problem is that of initiation; a portion of a previous batch of Grignard reagent is often used as the initiator. Grignard reactions are exothermic, and this exothermicity must be considered when a reaction is scaled-up from laboratory to production plant.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many Grignard reagents such as <a href="/wiki/Methylmagnesium_bromide" class="mw-redirect" title="Methylmagnesium bromide">methylmagnesium bromide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methylmagnesium_chloride" title="Methylmagnesium chloride">methylmagnesium chloride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phenylmagnesium_bromide" title="Phenylmagnesium bromide">phenylmagnesium bromide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Allylmagnesium_bromide" title="Allylmagnesium bromide">allylmagnesium bromide</a> are available commercially as <a href="/wiki/Tetrahydrofuran" title="Tetrahydrofuran">tetrahydrofuran</a> or <a href="/wiki/Diethyl_ether" title="Diethyl ether">diethyl ether</a> solutions.
</p>
<h3><span id="Mg_transfer_reaction_.28halogen.E2.80.93Mg_exchange.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Mg_transfer_reaction_(halogen–Mg_exchange)">Mg transfer reaction (halogen–Mg exchange)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mg transfer reaction (halogen–Mg exchange)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An alternative preparation of Grignard reagents involves transfer of Mg from a preformed Grignard reagent to an organic halide.  This method offers the advantage that the Mg transfer tolerates many functional groups.  A typical reaction involves isopropylmagnesium chloride and aryl bromide or iodides.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions_of_Grignard_reagents">Reactions of Grignard reagents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reactions of Grignard reagents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="With_carbonyl_compounds">With carbonyl compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: With carbonyl compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Grignard reagents react with a variety of <a href="/wiki/Carbonyl" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> derivatives.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a href="/wiki/File:Grignard_with_carbonyl.png" class="image" title="Reactions of Grignard reagents with carbonyls"><img alt="Reactions of Grignard reagents with carbonyls" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Grignard_with_carbonyl.png/600px-Grignard_with_carbonyl.png" width="600" height="403" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Grignard_with_carbonyl.png/900px-Grignard_with_carbonyl.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Grignard_with_carbonyl.png/1200px-Grignard_with_carbonyl.png 2x" data-file-width="1730" data-file-height="1163" /></a></div></div>
<p>The most common application of Grignard reagents is the alkylation of aldehydes and ketones, i.e. the Grignard reaction:<sup id="cite_ref-Haugan1997_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haugan1997-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a href="/wiki/File:GrignardReactionVinylation.png" class="image" title="Reaction of CH3C(=O)CH(OCH3)2 with H2C=CHMgBr"><img alt="Reaction of CH3C(=O)CH(OCH3)2 with H2C=CHMgBr" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/GrignardReactionVinylation.png/400px-GrignardReactionVinylation.png" width="400" height="106" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/GrignardReactionVinylation.png/600px-GrignardReactionVinylation.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/GrignardReactionVinylation.png/800px-GrignardReactionVinylation.png 2x" data-file-width="1614" data-file-height="426" /></a></div></div>
<p>Note that the <a href="/wiki/Acetal" title="Acetal">acetal</a> function (a protected carbonyl) does not react.
</p><p>Such reactions usually involve an aqueous acidic workup, though this step is rarely shown in reaction schemes.  In cases where the Grignard reagent is adding to an aldehyde or a <a href="/wiki/Prochiral" class="mw-redirect" title="Prochiral">prochiral</a> ketone, the <a href="/wiki/Asymmetric_induction" title="Asymmetric induction">Felkin-Anh model or Cram's Rule</a> can usually predict which stereoisomer will be formed.  With easily deprotonated 1,3-<a href="/wiki/Diketone" title="Diketone">diketones</a> and related acidic substrates, the Grignard reagent RMgX functions merely as a base, giving the <a href="/wiki/Enolate" class="mw-redirect" title="Enolate">enolate</a> anion and liberating the alkane RH.
</p><p>Grignard reagents are <a href="/wiki/Nucleophile" title="Nucleophile">nucleophiles</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nucleophilic_aliphatic_substitution" class="mw-redirect" title="Nucleophilic aliphatic substitution">nucleophilic aliphatic substitutions</a> for instance with <a href="/wiki/Alkyl_halide" class="mw-redirect" title="Alkyl halide">alkyl halides</a> in a key step in industrial <a href="/wiki/Naproxen" title="Naproxen">Naproxen</a> production:
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a href="/wiki/File:Naproxen_synthesis.png" class="image" title="Naproxen synthesis"><img alt="Naproxen synthesis" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Naproxen_synthesis.png/550px-Naproxen_synthesis.png" width="550" height="81" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/95/Naproxen_synthesis.png 1.5x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="118" /></a></div></div>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a href="/wiki/File:Grignard_with_others.png" class="image" title="Reactions of Grignard reagents with various electrophiles"><img alt="Reactions of Grignard reagents with various electrophiles" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Grignard_with_others.png/500px-Grignard_with_others.png" width="500" height="397" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Grignard_with_others.png/750px-Grignard_with_others.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Grignard_with_others.png/1000px-Grignard_with_others.png 2x" data-file-width="1470" data-file-height="1166" /></a></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions_as_a_base">Reactions as a base</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Reactions as a base">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Grignard reagents serve as a base for protic substrates (this scheme does not show workup conditions, which typically includes water).  Grignard reagents are basic and react with alcohols, phenols, etc. to give <a href="/wiki/Alkoxide" title="Alkoxide">alkoxides</a> (ROMgBr). The phenoxide derivative is susceptible to formylation paraformaldehyde to give <a href="/wiki/Salicylaldehyde" title="Salicylaldehyde">salicylaldehyde</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Formation_of_bonds_to_B.2C_Si.2C_P.2C_Sn"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_of_bonds_to_B,_Si,_P,_Sn">Formation of bonds to B, Si, P, Sn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Formation of bonds to B, Si, P, Sn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Like <a href="/wiki/Organolithium_compound" class="mw-redirect" title="Organolithium compound">organolithium compounds</a>, Grignard reagents are useful for forming carbon–heteroatom bonds.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction_with_transition_metal_halides">Reaction with transition metal halides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Reaction with transition metal halides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Grignard reagents react with many metal-based electrophiles.  For example, they undergo <a href="/wiki/Transmetallation" class="mw-redirect" title="Transmetallation">transmetallation</a> with <a href="/wiki/Cadmium_chloride" title="Cadmium chloride">cadmium chloride</a> (CdCl<sub>2</sub>) to give <a href="/wiki/Organocadmium_compound" title="Organocadmium compound">dialkylcadmium</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 RMgX + CdCl<sub>2</sub> → R<sub>2</sub>Cd + 2 Mg(X)Cl</dd></dl>
<p>Dialkylcadmium reagents are used for preparation of ketones from <a href="/wiki/Acyl_halides" class="mw-redirect" title="Acyl halides">acyl halides</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 R'C(O)Cl + R<sub>2</sub>Cd  →   2 R'C(O)R  +  CdCl<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="With_organic_halides">With organic halides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: With organic halides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Grignard reagents do <i>not</i> typically react with organic halides, in contrast with their high reactivity with other main group halides.  In the presence of metal catalysts, however, Grignard reagents participate in C-C <a href="/wiki/Coupling_reaction" title="Coupling reaction">coupling reactions</a>. For example, nonylmagnesium bromide reacts with methyl <i>p</i>-chlorobenzoate to give <i>p</i>-nonylbenzoic acid, in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Tris(acetylacetonato)iron(III)" title="Tris(acetylacetonato)iron(III)">Tris(acetylacetonato)iron(III)</a> (Fe(acac)<sub>3</sub>), after workup with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">NaOH</a> to <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolyze</a> the <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">ester</a>, shown as follows. Without the Fe(acac)<sub>3</sub>, the Grignard reagent would attack the <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">ester</a> <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">group</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Aryl_halide" title="Aryl halide">aryl halide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a href="/wiki/File:4nonylbenzoicacidSynthesis.svg" class="image" title="4-nonylbenzoicacid synthesis using a grignard reagent"><img alt="4-nonylbenzoicacid synthesis using a grignard reagent" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/4nonylbenzoicacidSynthesis.svg/521px-4nonylbenzoicacidSynthesis.svg.png" width="521" height="154" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/4nonylbenzoicacidSynthesis.svg/782px-4nonylbenzoicacidSynthesis.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/4nonylbenzoicacidSynthesis.svg/1042px-4nonylbenzoicacidSynthesis.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="521" data-file-height="154" /></a></div></div>
<p>For the coupling of aryl halides with aryl Grignard reagents, <a href="/wiki/Nickel_chloride" class="mw-redirect" title="Nickel chloride">nickel chloride</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tetrahydrofuran" title="Tetrahydrofuran">tetrahydrofuran</a> (THF) is also a good catalyst. Additionally, an effective catalyst for the couplings of alkyl halides is <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dilithium_tetrachlorocuprate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dilithium tetrachlorocuprate (page does not exist)">dilithium tetrachlorocuprate</a> (Li<sub>2</sub>CuCl<sub>4</sub>), prepared by mixing <a href="/wiki/Lithium_chloride" title="Lithium chloride">lithium chloride</a> (LiCl) and <a href="/wiki/Copper(II)_chloride" title="Copper(II) chloride">copper(II) chloride</a> (CuCl<sub>2</sub>) in THF. The <a href="/wiki/Kumada-Corriu_coupling" class="mw-redirect" title="Kumada-Corriu coupling">Kumada-Corriu coupling</a> gives access to [substituted] <a href="/wiki/Styrene" title="Styrene">styrenes</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidation">Oxidation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Oxidation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Treatment of a Grignard reagent with oxygen gives the magnesium organoperoxide. Hydrolysis of this material yields <a href="/wiki/Hydroperoxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroperoxide">hydroperoxides</a> or alcohol.  These reactions involve <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">radical</a> intermediates.

<p>The simple oxidation of Grignard reagents to give alcohols is of little practical import as yields are generally poor. In contrast, two-step sequence via a borane (<i>vide supra</i>) that is subsequently oxidized to the alcohol with hydrogen peroxide is of synthetic utility.
</p><p>The synthetic utility of Grignard oxidations can be increased by a reaction of Grignard reagents with oxygen in presence of an <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkene</a> to an ethylene extended <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>  This modification requires <a href="/wiki/Aryl" title="Aryl">aryl</a> or <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_group" title="Vinyl group">vinyl</a> Grignards. Adding just the Grignard and the alkene does not result in a reaction demonstrating that the presence of oxygen is essential. The only drawback is the requirement of at least two equivalents of Grignard although this can partly be circumvented by the use of a dual Grignard system with a cheap reducing Grignard such as n-butylmagnesium bromide.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elimination">Elimination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Elimination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Boord_olefin_synthesis" title="Boord olefin synthesis">Boord olefin synthesis</a>, the addition of magnesium to certain β-haloethers results in an <a href="/wiki/Elimination_reaction" title="Elimination reaction">elimination reaction</a> to the alkene. This reaction can limit the utility of Grignard reactions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Degradation_of_Grignard_reagents">Degradation of Grignard reagents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Degradation of Grignard reagents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At one time, the formation and hydrolysis of Grignard reagents was used in the determination of the number of halogen atoms in an <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compound</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In modern usage Grignard degradation is used in the chemical analysis of certain triacylglycerols.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_use">Industrial use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Grignard_reaction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Industrial use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An example of the Grignard reaction is a key step in the (non-stereoselective) industrial production of <a href="/wiki/Tamoxifen" title="Tamoxifen">Tamoxifen</a><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> (currently used for the treatment of estrogen receptor positive breast cancer in women):<sup id="cite_ref-Jordan_1993_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jordan_1993-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
