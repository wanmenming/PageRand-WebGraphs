<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Weather modification</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dszpics1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Dszpics1.jpg/290px-Dszpics1.jpg" width="290" height="192" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Dszpics1.jpg/435px-Dszpics1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Dszpics1.jpg/580px-Dszpics1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="679" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dszpics1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a> in central <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>. Weather researchers may aspire to eliminate or control dangerous types of weather such as this.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Weather modification</b> is the act of intentionally manipulating or altering the <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a>. The most common form of weather modification is <a href="/wiki/Cloud_seeding" title="Cloud seeding">cloud seeding</a>, which increases rain or snow, usually for the purpose of increasing the local <a href="/wiki/Water_supply" title="Water supply">water supply</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-azwater_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-azwater-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Weather modification can also have the goal of preventing damaging weather, such as <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hurricanes" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricanes">hurricanes</a>, from occurring; or of provoking damaging weather against the enemy, as a tactic of military or <a href="/wiki/Economic_warfare" title="Economic warfare">economic warfare</a> like <a href="/wiki/Operation_Popeye" title="Operation Popeye">Operation Popeye</a>, where clouds were <a href="/wiki/Cloud_seeding" title="Cloud seeding">seeded</a> to prolong the monsoon in Vietnam. Weather modification in warfare has been banned by the United Nations.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_modification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A popular belief in northern Europe that shooting prevents hail caused many agricultural towns to fire cannons without ammunition. Veterans of the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years&#39; War">Seven Years' War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_wars" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleonic wars">Napoleonic wars</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> reported that rain fell after every large battle. After their stories were collected in <i>War and Weather</i>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_War" title="United States Department of War">United States Department of War</a> in the late 19th century purchased $9,000 of gunpowder and explosives to detonate them in Texas. The results of the test, supervised by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Dyrenforth" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Dyrenforth">Robert Dyrenforth</a>, were inconclusive.<sup id="cite_ref-ley196102_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ley196102-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Reich" title="Wilhelm Reich">Wilhelm Reich</a> performed <a href="/wiki/Cloudbuster" title="Cloudbuster">cloudbusting</a> experiments in the 1950s, the results of which are controversial and not widely accepted by mainstream science.
</p><p>In January 2011, several newspapers and magazines, including the UK's <i>Sunday Times</i> and <i>Arabian Business</i>, reported that scientists backed by the government of <a href="/wiki/Abu_Dhabi" title="Abu Dhabi">Abu Dhabi</a>, the capital of the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>, had created over 50 artificial rainstorms between July and August 2010 near <a href="/wiki/Al_Ain" title="Al Ain">Al Ain</a>, a city which lies close to the country's border with <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a> and is the second-largest city in the <a href="/wiki/Abu_Dhabi_Emirate" class="mw-redirect" title="Abu Dhabi Emirate">Abu Dhabi Emirate</a>. The artificial rainstorms were said to have sometimes caused hail, gales and thunderstorms, baffling local residents.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The scientists reportedly used ionizers to create the rainstorms, and although the results are disputed, the large number of times it is recorded to have rained right after the ionizers were switched on during a usually dry season is encouraging to those who support the experiment.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cloud_seeding">Cloud seeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_modification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cloud seeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cloud_Seeding.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Cloud_Seeding.svg/290px-Cloud_Seeding.svg.png" width="290" height="152" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Cloud_Seeding.svg/435px-Cloud_Seeding.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Cloud_Seeding.svg/580px-Cloud_Seeding.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1001" data-file-height="523" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cloud_Seeding.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cloud seeding</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cloud_seeding" title="Cloud seeding">Cloud seeding</a></div><p>
Cloud seeding is a common technique to enhance precipitation. Cloud seeding entails spraying small particles, such as <a href="/wiki/Silver_iodide" title="Silver iodide">silver iodide</a>, onto clouds to affect their development, usually with the goal of increasing precipitation. Cloud seeding only works to the extent that there is already water vapor present in the air. Critics generally contend that claimed successes occur in conditions which were going to lead to rain anyway. It is used in a variety of drought-prone countries, including the United States, the People's Republic of China, India, and the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian Federation">Russian Federation</a>. In the People's Republic of China there is a perceived dependency upon it in dry regions, and there is a strong suspicion it is used to "wash the air" in dry and heavily polluted places, such as Beijing. In mountainous areas of the United States such as the Rocky Mountains and Sierra Nevada,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> cloud seeding has been employed since the 1950s.</p><div style="clear:both;"></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Project_stormfury_hypothesis.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Project_stormfury_hypothesis.png/250px-Project_stormfury_hypothesis.png" width="250" height="320" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/15/Project_stormfury_hypothesis.png 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="384" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Project_stormfury_hypothesis.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Project_Stormfury" title="Project Stormfury">Project Stormfury</a></div></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Storm_prevention">Storm prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_modification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Storm prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:International_congress_on_hail_shooting.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/International_congress_on_hail_shooting.jpg/250px-International_congress_on_hail_shooting.jpg" width="250" height="179" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/International_congress_on_hail_shooting.jpg/375px-International_congress_on_hail_shooting.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/International_congress_on_hail_shooting.jpg/500px-International_congress_on_hail_shooting.jpg 2x" data-file-width="704" data-file-height="503" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:International_congress_on_hail_shooting.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hail_cannon" title="Hail cannon">Hail cannons</a> at an international congress on hail shooting held in 1901</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Project_Stormfury" title="Project Stormfury">Project Stormfury</a> was an attempt to weaken <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> by flying aircraft into storms and seeding the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eyewall</a> with silver iodide. The project was run by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">United States Government</a> from 1962 to 1983. A similar project using soot was run in 1958, with inconclusive results.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Various methods have been proposed to reduce the harmful effects of hurricanes. Moshe Alamaro of the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> proposed using barges with upward-pointing jet engines to trigger smaller storms to disrupt the progress of an incoming hurricane; critics doubt the jets would be powerful enough to make any noticeable difference.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Alexandre Chorin of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>, proposed dropping large amounts of environmentally friendly oils on the sea surface to prevent droplet formation.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Experiments by Kerry Emanuel<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> of MIT in 2002 suggested that hurricane-force winds would disrupt the oil slick, making it ineffective.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Other scientists disputed the factual basis of the theoretical mechanism assumed by this approach.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Florida company Dyn-O-Mat and its CEO, Peter Cordani, proposed the use of a patented product it developed, called Dyn-O-Gel, to reduce the strength of hurricanes. The substance is a <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a> in powder form (a <a href="/wiki/Polyacrylic_acid" title="Polyacrylic acid">polyacrylic acid</a> derivative) which reportedly has the ability to absorb 1,500 times its own weight in water. The theory is that the polymer is dropped into clouds to remove their moisture and force the storm to use more energy to move the heavier water drops, thus helping to dissipate the storm. When the gel reaches the ocean surface, it is reportedly dissolved. Peter Cordani teamed up with Mark Daniels and Victor Miller, the owners of a government contracting aviation firm <a href="/wiki/AeroGroup" title="AeroGroup">AeroGroup</a> which operated ex-military aircraft commercially. Using a high altitude B-57 Bomber, <a href="/wiki/AeroGroup" title="AeroGroup">AeroGroup</a> tested the substance dropping 9,000 pounds from the <a href="/wiki/Martin_B-57_Canberra" title="Martin B-57 Canberra">B-57</a> aircraft's large bomb bay disbursing it into a large thunderstorm cell just off the east coast of Florida. The tests were documented on film and made international news showing the storms were successfully removed on monitored Doppler radar. In 2003, the program was shut down because of political pressure through <a href="/wiki/NOAA" class="mw-redirect" title="NOAA">NOAA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Numerical simulations performed by <a href="/wiki/NOAA" class="mw-redirect" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> showed however that it would not be a practical solution for large systems like a tropical cyclone.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Hail_cannon" title="Hail cannon">Hail cannons</a> have been used by some farmers since the 19th century in an attempt to ward off <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>, but there is no reliable scientific evidence to confirm their effectiveness. Another new anti-hurricane technology<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> is a method for the reduction of tropical cyclones' destructive force – pumping sea water into and diffusing it in the wind at the bottom of such tropical cyclone in its eye wall.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_modification">Hurricane modification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_modification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hurricane modification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>NOAA published a page addressing various ideas in regards to tropical cyclone manipulation.
</p><p>In 2007, "How to stop a hurricane"<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> explored various ideas such as:
</p>
<ul><li>Using lasers to discharge <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> in storms which are likely to become hurricanes</li>
<li>Pouring <a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">liquid nitrogen</a> onto the sea to deprive the hurricane of <a href="/wiki/Internal_energy" title="Internal energy">heat energy</a>.</li>
<li>Creating <a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">soot</a> to absorb sunlight and change <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">air temperature</a> and create <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection currents</a> in the outer wall.</li></ul>
<p>Researchers from NOAA's hurricane research division addressed hurricane control based ideas.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Later ideas (2017) include laser inversion along the same lines as laser cooling (normally used at cryogenic temperatures) but intended to cool the top 1mm of water. If enough power was used then it might be enough combined with computer modeling to form an interference pattern able to inhibit a hurricane or significantly reduce its strength by depriving it of heat energy.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_military">In the military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_modification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In the military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In "Benign Weather Modification" published March 1997, Air Force Major Barry B. Coble superficially documents the existence of weather modification science where he traces the developments that have occurred, notably, in the hands of the Pentagon and CIA's staunchest ideological enemies.
</p>
<ul><li>The first scientifically controlled and monitored effort generally recognized by the meteorological community as constituting weather modification occurred in 1948. When Dr. Irving Langmuir first experimented with artificially seeding clouds to produce rain, his experiments showed positive results – sparking tremendous interest in the field nearly overnight.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Many countries throughout the world practice weather modification. The Russians have long been interested in using weather modification as a way to control hail.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The Chinese recognize the value of weather modification and believe that the US military continues to use weather as a weapon.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>In the 1990s a directive from the chief of staff of the Air Force Ronald R. Fogleman was issued to examine the concepts, capabilities, and technologies the United States would require to remain the dominant air and space force in the future.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_law">In law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_modification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="US_and_Canada_agreement">US and Canada agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_modification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: US and Canada agreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1975, the US and Canada entered into an agreement under the auspices of the United Nations for the exchange of information on weather modification activity.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1977_UN_Environmental_Modification_Convention">1977 UN Environmental Modification Convention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_modification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1977 UN Environmental Modification Convention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Modification_Convention" title="Environmental Modification Convention">Environmental Modification Convention</a></div>
<p>Weather modification, particularly hostile <a href="/wiki/Weather_warfare" title="Weather warfare">weather warfare</a>, was addressed by the "United Nations General Assembly Resolution 31/72, TIAS 9614 Convention<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> on the Prohibition of Military or Any Other Hostile Use of Environmental Modification Techniques." The Convention was signed in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a> on May 18, 1977; entered into force on October 5, 1978; ratified by U.S. President Jimmy Carter on December 13, 1979; and the U.S. ratification deposited at New York January 17, 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-dos_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dos-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="US_National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration">US National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_modification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: US National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the US, the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> keeps records of weather modification projects on behalf of the Secretary of Commerce, under authority of Public Law 92-205, 15 USC § 330B, enacted in 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_US_legislation">Proposed US legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_modification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Proposed US legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>2005 U.S. Senate Bill 517 and U.S. House Bill 2995</b>
U.S. Senate Bill 517<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> and U.S. House Bill 2995<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> were two bills proposed in 2005 that would have expanded experimental weather modification, to establish a <a href="/wiki/Weather_Modification_Operations_and_Research_Board" title="Weather Modification Operations and Research Board">Weather Modification Operations and Research Board</a>, and implemented a national weather modification policy. Neither were made into law. Former <a href="/wiki/Texas_State_Senate" class="mw-redirect" title="Texas State Senate">Texas State Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/John_N._Leedom" title="John N. Leedom">John N. Leedom</a> was the key <a href="/wiki/Lobbyist" class="mw-redirect" title="Lobbyist">lobbyist</a> on behalf of the weather modification bills.
</p><p><b>2007 U.S. Senate Bill 1807 &amp; U.S. House Bill 3445</b>
Senate Bill 1807 and House Bill 3445, identical bills introduced July 17, 2007, proposed to establish a Weather Mitigation Advisory and Research Board to fund weather modification research<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_religion_and_mythology">In religion and mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Weather_modification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In religion and mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Mythopoeic_thought" title="Mythopoeic thought">Mythopoeic thought</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Witches_add_ingredients_to_a_cauldron.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Witches_add_ingredients_to_a_cauldron.JPG/220px-Witches_add_ingredients_to_a_cauldron.JPG" width="220" height="262" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Witches_add_ingredients_to_a_cauldron.JPG/330px-Witches_add_ingredients_to_a_cauldron.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Witches_add_ingredients_to_a_cauldron.JPG/440px-Witches_add_ingredients_to_a_cauldron.JPG 2x" data-file-width="444" data-file-height="528" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Witches_add_ingredients_to_a_cauldron.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Witchcraft" title="Witchcraft">Witches</a> concoct a <a href="/wiki/Potion" title="Potion">brew</a> to summon a <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hailstorm</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Magic_(paranormal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Magic (paranormal)">Magical</a> and religious practices to control the weather are attested in a variety of cultures. In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient India">ancient India</a> it is said that yajna or vedic rituals of chanting manthras and offering were performed by <a href="/wiki/Rishis" class="mw-redirect" title="Rishis">rishis</a> to bring sudden bursts of rain fall in rain starved regions. Some <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Indigenous Americans</a>, like some Europeans, had rituals which they believed could induce rain. The <a href="/wiki/Finns" title="Finns">Finnish people</a>, on the other hand, were believed by others to be able to control weather. As a result, <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Vikings</a> refused to take Finns on their oceangoing raids. Remnants of this superstition lasted into the twentieth century, with some ship crews being reluctant to accept Finnish sailors.
</p><p>The early modern era saw people observe that during battles the firing of <a href="/wiki/Cannon" title="Cannon">cannons</a> and other firearms often initiated precipitation.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iphigenia" title="Iphigenia">Iphigenia</a> was offered as a <a href="/wiki/Human_sacrifice" title="Human sacrifice">human sacrifice</a> to appease the wrath of the goddess <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">Artemis</a>, who had becalmed the <a href="/wiki/Achaeans_(Homer)" title="Achaeans (Homer)">Achaean</a> fleet at <a href="/wiki/Avlida" title="Avlida">Aulis</a> at the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War" title="Trojan War">Trojan War</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">Odyssey</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Aeolus" title="Aeolus">Aeolus</a>, keeper of the winds, bestowed <a href="/wiki/Odysseus" title="Odysseus">Odysseus</a> and his crew with a gift of the four winds in a bag. However, the sailors opened the bag while Odysseus slept, looking for booty (money), and as a result were blown off course by the resulting gale.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In ancient Rome, the <i><a href="/wiki/Lapis_manalis" title="Lapis manalis">lapis manalis</a></i> was a sacred stone kept outside the <a href="/wiki/Servian_Wall" title="Servian Wall">walls of Rome</a> in a temple of <a href="/wiki/Mars_(mythology)" title="Mars (mythology)">Mars</a>. When Rome suffered from drought, the stone was dragged into the city.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
The <a href="/wiki/North_Berwick_witch_trials" title="North Berwick witch trials">Berwick witches</a> of Scotland were found guilty of using <a href="/wiki/Black_magic" title="Black magic">black magic</a> to summon storms to murder <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">King James VI of Scotland</a> by seeking to sink the ship upon which he travelled.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Scandinavian witches allegedly claimed to sell the wind in bags or magically confined into wooden staves; they sold the bags to seamen who could release them when becalmed.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> In various towns of <a href="/wiki/Navarre" title="Navarre">Navarre</a>, prayers petitioned <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Saint Peter</a> to grant rain in time of drought. If the rain was not forthcoming, the statue of St Peter was removed from the church and tossed into a river.
</p>
