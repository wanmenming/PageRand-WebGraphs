<p><b>Local food</b> (<b>local food movement</b> or <b>locavore</b>) is a movement of people who prefer to eat foods which are grown or farmed relatively close to the places of sale and preparation. 
</p><p>Local food movements aim to connect <a href="/wiki/Food_producer" class="mw-redirect" title="Food producer">food producers</a> and food consumers in the same geographic region, in order to develop more <a href="/wiki/Self-reliant" class="mw-redirect" title="Self-reliant">self-reliant</a> and resilient <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food networks</a>; improve local economies; or to affect the <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">health</a>, environment, community, or society of a particular place.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The term has also been extended to include not only the geographic location of supplier and consumer but can also be "defined in terms of social and <a href="/wiki/Supply_chain" title="Supply chain">supply chain</a> characteristics."<sup id="cite_ref-Martinez_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martinez-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> For example, local food initiatives often promote <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable</a> and <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">organic farming</a> practices, although these are not explicitly related to the geographic proximity of producer and consumer.
</p><p>Local food represents an alternative to the <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">global</a> food model, a model which often sees food <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">traveling long distances</a> before it reaches the consumer. A local food network involves relationships between food producers, distributors, retailers, and consumers in a particular place, where they work together to increase food security and ensure economic, ecological and social sustainability of a community.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the USA, the local food movement has been traced to the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_Adjustment_Act" title="Agricultural Adjustment Act">Agricultural Adjustment Act</a> (AAA) of 1933, which spawned today's controversial <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_subsidies" class="mw-redirect" title="Agricultural subsidies">agricultural subsidies</a> and price supports.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The contemporary American movement associated with the term can be traced back to proposed resolutions to the <a href="/wiki/Society_for_Nutrition_Education" class="mw-redirect" title="Society for Nutrition Education">Society for Nutrition Education</a>'s 1981 guidelines. These largely unsuccessful resolutions encouraged increased local production to slow farmland loss. The program described "sustainable diets" - a term then new to the American public. At the time, the resolutions were met with strong criticism from pro-business institutions, but have had a strong resurgence of backing since 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008, revisions were made to the United States <a href="/wiki/United_States_farm_bill" title="United States farm bill">Farm Bill</a> which put an emphasis on nutrition: "it provides low-income seniors with vouchers for use at local produce markets, and it added more than $1 billion to the fresh fruit and vegetable program, which serves healthy snacks to 3 million low-income children in schools".<sup id="cite_ref-search.ebscohost.com_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-search.ebscohost.com-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Definitions_of_.22local.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions_of_&quot;local&quot;">Definitions of "local"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definitions of &quot;local&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Goats_and_cheesemaking_workshop,_Maker_Faire_2011.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Goats_and_cheesemaking_workshop%2C_Maker_Faire_2011.jpg/220px-Goats_and_cheesemaking_workshop%2C_Maker_Faire_2011.jpg" width="220" height="132" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Goats_and_cheesemaking_workshop%2C_Maker_Faire_2011.jpg/330px-Goats_and_cheesemaking_workshop%2C_Maker_Faire_2011.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Goats_and_cheesemaking_workshop%2C_Maker_Faire_2011.jpg/440px-Goats_and_cheesemaking_workshop%2C_Maker_Faire_2011.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="1952" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Goats_and_cheesemaking_workshop,_Maker_Faire_2011.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Cheesemaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Cheesemaker">cheesemaking</a> <a href="/wiki/Workshop" title="Workshop">workshop</a> with goats at <a href="/wiki/Maker_Faire" title="Maker Faire">Maker Faire</a> 2011. The sign declares, "Eat your <a href="/wiki/ZIP_code" class="mw-redirect" title="ZIP code">Zipcode</a>!"</div></div></div>
<p>No single definition of "local" or "local food systems" exists. The geographic distances between production and consumption varies within the movement. However, the general public recognizes that "local" describes the marketing arrangement (e.g. farmers selling directly to consumers at regional <a href="/wiki/Farmers%27_market" title="Farmers&#39; market">farmers' markets</a> or to schools).<sup id="cite_ref-Martinez_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martinez-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> There are "a number of different definitions for local [that] have been used or recorded by researchers assessing local food systems [and] most [are] informed by political or geographic boundaries. Among the more widely circulated and popular defining parameters is the concept of <a href="/wiki/Food_miles" title="Food miles">food miles</a>, which has been suggested for policy recommendations."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Food,_Conservation,_and_Energy_Act_of_2008" title="Food, Conservation, and Energy Act of 2008">Food, Conservation, and Energy Act of 2008</a> includes a definition, with "locally" and "regionally" grouped together and defined as:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>‘‘(I) the locality or region in which the final product is marketed, so that the total distance that the product is transported is less than 400 miles from the origin of the product; or<br />‘‘(II) the State in which the product is produced.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.gpo.gov/fdsys/pkg/BILLS-110hr2419enr/pdf/BILLS-110hr2419enr.pdf">H. R. 2419</a></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>In May 2010 the USDA acknowledged this definition in an informational leaflet.<sup id="cite_ref-Martinez_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martinez-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The concept of "local" is also seen in terms of <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, where food production is considered from the perspective of a basic ecological unit defined by its climate, soil, <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">watershed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> and local <a href="/w/index.php?title=Agrisystem&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Agrisystem (page does not exist)">agrisystems</a>, a unit also called an <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregion</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Foodshed" title="Foodshed">food shed</a>. Similar to watersheds, food sheds follow the process of where food comes from and where it ends up.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "local" is widely understood by the general public as a description of regional distribution of food, though that does not involve a regulation of distance between the farmer, their food and the consumer. It is the consumer's responsibility to conclude how "local" the food is.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_local_food_market">Contemporary local food market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Contemporary local food market">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The USDA included statistics about the growing local food market in the leaflet released in May 2010. The statistics are as follows: "Direct-to-consumer marketing amounted to $1.2 billion in current dollar sales in 2007, according to the 2007 Census of Agriculture, compared with $551 million in 1997. Direct-to-consumer sales accounted for 0.4 percent of total agricultural sales in 2007, up from 0.3 percent in 1997. If non-edible products are excluded from total agricultural sales, direct-to-consumer sales accounted for 0.8 percent of agricultural sales in 2007. The number of farmers' markets rose to 5,274 in 2009, up from 2,756 in 1998 and 1,755 in 1994, according to USDA's Agricultural Marketing Service. In 2005, there were 1,144 community-supported agriculture organizations (CSAs) in operation, up from 400 in 2001 and 2 in 1986, according to a study by the nonprofit, nongovernmental organization National Center for Appropriate Technology. In early 2010, estimates exceeded 1,400, but the number could be much larger. The number of farm to school programs, which use local farms as food suppliers for school meals programs, increased to 2,095 in 2009, up from 400 in 2004 and 2 in the 1996-97 school year, according to the National Farm to School Network. Data from the 2005 School Nutrition and Dietary Assessment Survey, sponsored by USDA's Food and Nutrition Service, showed that 14 percent of school districts participated in Farm to School programs, and 16 percent reported having guidelines for <a href="/wiki/Local_purchasing" title="Local purchasing">purchasing locally grown produce</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-Martinez_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martinez-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Using metrics including some of those cited above, a Vermont-based farm and food advocacy organization, <a href="/wiki/Strolling_of_the_Heifers" title="Strolling of the Heifers">Strolling of the Heifers</a>, publishes the annual Locavore Index, a ranking of the 50 U.S. states plus <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> and the <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="District of Columbia">District of Columbia</a>. In the 2016 Index, the three top-ranking states were Vermont, Maine and Oregon, while the three lowest-ranking states were Nevada, Texas and Florida.<sup id="cite_ref-strollingoftheheifers.com_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-strollingoftheheifers.com-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Networks of local farmers and producers are now collaborating in the UK, Canada and the US to provide online farmers' markets to consumers. This technological change enables more consumers to participate in farmers' markets. This development also allows local farmers and producers to harvest and prepare produce according to orders, and means that farmers are also able to spread the website costs.  Consumers have access to a huge inventory of farms and their products, without having to be locked into buying whatever a CSA provides.
</p><p>Websites now exist that aim to connect people to local food growers. They often include a map where fruit and vegetable growers can pinpoint their location and advertise their produce.
</p><p>Supermarket chains also participate in the local food scene. In 2008 <a href="/wiki/Walmart" title="Walmart">Walmart</a> announced plans to invest $400 million in locally grown produce.<sup id="cite_ref-burros_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-burros-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Other chains, like Wegman's (a 71-store chain across the northeast), have a long and cooperative history with the local food movement. In this chain's case, each store's produce manager oversees the influx of local foods.<sup id="cite_ref-burros_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-burros-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> A recent study led by Miguel Gomez, a professor of Applied Economics and Management at <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>, in cooperation with the <a href="/wiki/Atkinson_Center_for_a_Sustainable_Future" title="Atkinson Center for a Sustainable Future">Atkinson Center for a Sustainable Future</a>, found that in many instances, the supermarket supply chain did much better in terms of food miles and fuel consumption for each pound compared to farmers markets. The study suggests that selling locally grown foods through supermarkets may be more economically viable and sustainable than through farmers' markets.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Locavore_and_invasivore">Locavore and invasivore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Locavore and invasivore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A "locavore" or "localvore" (the term is a <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologism</a>) is a person interested in eating food that is locally produced, not moved long distances to market. One common – but not universal – definition of "local" food is food grown within 100 miles (160&#160;km) of its point of purchase or consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Lure_of_the_100-Mile_Diet_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Lure_of_the_100-Mile_Diet-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The locavore movement in the United States and elsewhere was spawned as a result of interest in <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> and eco-consciousness becoming more prevalent.<sup id="cite_ref-Locavores_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Locavores-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The word "locavore" was the word of the year for 2007 in the <i><a href="/wiki/New_Oxford_American_Dictionary" title="New Oxford American Dictionary">Oxford American Dictionary</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The suffix "vore" comes from the Latin word <i>vorare</i> (as in "devour"), and is used to form nouns indicating what kind of a diet an animal has. This word was the creation of <a href="/wiki/Jessica_Prentice" title="Jessica Prentice">Jessica Prentice</a> of the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">San Francisco Bay Area</a> at the time of <a href="/wiki/UN_World_Environment_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="UN World Environment Day">World Environment Day</a> 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It may be rendered "localvore", depending on regional differences.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bennett_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>More recently, an "invasivore" movement has emerged as a subset of the locavore movement, which encourages the consumption of nonindigenous <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> with the intent of controlling harmful populations.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_food_campaigns">Local food campaigns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Local food campaigns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Locavores are interested in affecting their community by supporting the local farmers. The locavore movement has been successful in supporting small local farmers. After declining for more than a century, the number of small farms has increased 20% in the past six years, to 1.2 million, according to the Agriculture Department.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>

<p>In the city of <a href="/wiki/Graz" title="Graz">Graz</a> (<a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>), several restaurants display a sign with a "Genuss Region" logo, which refers to the restaurant using ingredients from local sources and a commitment to the traditions of cultivating regional foods.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="North_Carolina_10.25_campaign"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Carolina_10%_campaign">North Carolina 10% campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: North Carolina 10% campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Launched in late 2009,  North Carolina's 10% campaign is aimed at stimulating economic development, creating jobs and promoting the state's agricultural offerings.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>  The campaign is a partnership between The Center for Environmental Farming Systems (CEFS), with support from N.C. Cooperative Extension and the <a href="/wiki/Golden_LEAF_Foundation" title="Golden LEAF Foundation">Golden LEAF Foundation</a>. More than 4,600 individuals and 543 businesses, including 76 restaurants, have signed on to the campaign through the website nc10percent.com, pledging to spend 10 percent of their food budget on locally sourced foods. Participants receive weekly emails prompting them to record how much they have spent on local food that week. Currently the campaign reports that more than $14 million has been recorded by participants. "The $10 million mark is a true testament to the commitment of our agricultural community and the quality of North Carolina-grown products."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Center for Environmental Farming Systems estimates that if all North Carolinians allocated 10% of their food expenditures to locally produced food, $3.5 billion would be generated for the state's economy. <a href="/wiki/Brunswick_County,_North_Carolina" title="Brunswick County, North Carolina">Brunswick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cabarrus_County,_North_Carolina" title="Cabarrus County, North Carolina">Cabarrus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chatham_County,_North_Carolina" title="Chatham County, North Carolina">Chatham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guilford_County,_North_Carolina" title="Guilford County, North Carolina">Guilford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forsyth_County,_North_Carolina" title="Forsyth County, North Carolina">Forsyth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Onslow_County,_North_Carolina" title="Onslow County, North Carolina">Onslow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rockingham_County,_North_Carolina" title="Rockingham County, North Carolina">Rockingham</a> counties have adopted resolutions in support of the campaign. Stores are advertising local products with buy-local food labels. CEFS' co-director Nancy Creamer explains: "North Carolina is uniquely positioned to capitalize on the increased consumer demand for locally produced foods
...Agriculture is the backbone of our economy. The state's climate, soils and coastal resources support production of a wide variety of produce, meats, fish and seafood."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Growing_Power.2C_Inc."></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Growing_Power,_Inc.">Growing Power, Inc.</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Growing Power, Inc.">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Urban_environment" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban environment">Urban environments</a> are known for their food deserts in areas of <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a>, and most of the food available is shipped in. Growing Power, Inc. has a mission of "helping to provide equal access to healthy, high-quality, safe and affordable food for people in all communities".<sup id="cite_ref-Growing_Power_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Growing_Power-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Several farms in the Madison, Milwaukee, and Chicago area are <a href="/wiki/Good_agricultural_practice" title="Good agricultural practice">good agricultural practice</a> (GAP) certified.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Motivations_for_eating_local">Motivations for eating local</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Motivations for eating local">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are a number of reasons why people choose to participate in the locavore lifestyle. Motivations include healthier food, environmental benefits, and economic or community benefits. Many local farmers whom locavores turn to for their source of food use the <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">crop rotation</a> method when producing their organic crops. This method not only aids in reducing the use of pesticides and pollutants, but also keeps the soil in good condition rather than depleting it.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Locavores seek out farmers close to where they live, and this significantly reduces the amount of travel time taken for the food to get from the farm to the table. Reducing the travel time makes it possible to transport the crops while they are still fresh, without using chemical preservatives.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The combination of local farming techniques and short travel distances makes the food consumed more likely to be organic and fresh, an added benefit.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits_of_eating_local">Benefits of eating local</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Benefits of eating local">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Community_benefits">Community benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Community benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A community supported agriculture system is extremely beneficial to a community because it "enables consumers to support local farmers, obtain food that might be fresher than store-bought food, and learn more information from farmers about how the food is grown." Furthermore, local eating can support public objectives. It can promote community interaction by fostering relationships between farmers and consumers. Even shopping experiences and interaction at local farmers' markets have public benefits such as "bonus-incentive or gleaning programs, the hosting of health sessions and dissemination of informational materials, and establishment of an organized central location that facilitates community engagement." In fact, farmers' markets inspire more sociable behavior. Studies show that 75% of shoppers at farmers' markets arrived in groups while only 16% of shoppers at supermarkets arrive in groups. Only 9% of customers in chain supermarkets had a social interaction with another customer, and 14% had an interaction with an employee, but at farmers' markets, 63% had an interaction with a fellow shopper, and 42% had an interaction with an employee or farmer.<sup id="cite_ref-UCS_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UCS-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Local food builds community vibrancy and retains local traditions while establishing a local identity through a unique sense of community. <a href="/wiki/Urban_agriculture" title="Urban agriculture">Urban gardens</a> as shown in the documentary, "Urban Roots" are another solution to creating local food that greatly benefits the community as a whole. These urban gardens create local produce as well as educational and social opportunities.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="User_groups">User groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: User groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Food accessibility is a topic that affects everyone in America. Supermarkets tend to carry foods that have been shipped halfway around the world, chemically ripened, and off-season. However, more affluent areas tend to have at least some access to local, <a href="/wiki/Organic_food" title="Organic food">organic food</a>. Low-income communities are especially vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Food_desert" title="Food desert">food deserts</a>, areas in which there is little to no access to healthy food. These neighborhoods not only lack healthy food, but are overrun with unhealthy options; "disadvantaged neighborhoods are often replete with calorie-dense, low-quality food options", which adds to the obesity crisis rampant in America.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In America, many low-income areas correlate to highly African-American and Hispanic populations, so in many ways, food deserts tend to continually marginalize these races. These groups are then continually categorized as vulnerable populations. The study conducted by Taylor Eagle et al. gives a strong example between the correlation between socioeconomic class and accessibility to fruits and vegetables. It also exemplifies the prevalence of unhealthy food in more impoverished areas of Michigan cities.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This study focused on a particular demographic: children in school. The test subjects were 6th grade children and although the focus was on mean income correlating with food consumption, the test site was the cafeteria. <a href="/wiki/Childhood_obesity" title="Childhood obesity">Childhood obesity</a> is highly linked to adult obesity so in order to be effective one must target the younger subset. Targeting a younger subset is the best plan because to achieve full growth and reduce their risk of diseases, children must eat healthy into their adolescent years.<sup id="cite_ref-Shannon,_C._2002_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shannon,_C._2002-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>  A cafeteria creates an atmosphere of little choice;. According to Terry Huang, "schools play a vital and visible role in their communities".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Why is there no better model for food accessibility in these arenas? Children can become a user group for food accessibility, as they are models for the future health of this country. What children choose to eat will affect their food choices at an older age.<sup id="cite_ref-Shannon,_C._2002_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shannon,_C._2002-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> In other words, what we eat as children is what we will eat as adults unless change is forced at schools. This trend continues even into college, where students play an active role in changing the face of food. College students are of one of the worst demographics of unhealthy eaters and this becomes a target for providing local, natural food access.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> In all, people in low-income areas and school children are undernourished and overfed. Low-income populations should focus on joining together as a community to ask for these local sources of food as well as educate their peers about the dangers of fast food. School children should also have access to education to begin lifelong healthy-eating habits. This opens up a pocket of opportunity that will not only aid health, but also help build community in diverse areas.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_benefits">Environmental benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Environmental benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Local foods are sometimes considered the most climate friendly because the energy needed to store and transport the food is removed from the equation. There is a decrease in <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> emitted because locally grown goods do not need to be transported across the country, or constantly cooled in large refrigerators. Another benefit of locally grown food is its lower concentration of pollution sources. According to the USDA, more than 335 million tons of <a href="/wiki/Manure" title="Manure">manure</a> are produced annually in American farms. In <a href="/wiki/Intensive_animal_farming" title="Intensive animal farming">factory farms</a>, this waste is extremely concentrated, and without proper regulation and disposal, the waste pollutes the surrounding areas. The Natural Resource Defense Council even remarks that factory farms have reached a point in which the farms threaten public health.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Pollutants from the manure and urine of overcrowded factory farms lead to water and air pollution. Some of these pollutants, such as hydrogen sulfide and various nitrates, are dangerous even at low levels. Factory farms are also considered unsanitary because they place animals in overcrowded conditions in fully enclosed rooms that often become the perfect breeding grounds for diseases.  Locally grown foods support free-range or pasture-grazing farming methods, decreasing the need for large factory farms. With fewer factory farms, waste will not be so concentrated and will thus not have such profound effects on the immediate surrounding areas.
</p><p>Growing and selling foods locally saves the environment from serious detriments. With local farms, "food miles" can essentially be eliminated, which includes the accompanying pollution. There would be no need to establish more expansive industrial farms that contaminate the soil, whereas local farmers are able to preserve soil for sustainability.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_benefits">Economic benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Economic benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A critical objective for any community is to promote investments that serve to increase the economic and social opportunities available for residents. If the United States wishes to sustain current agricultural production in the future, there must be a market for emerging farmers to counter the effects of a collectively aging farmer population. The introduction of farmers' markets into the local economy can directly benefit the lives of all citizens within the community. In a study conducted in the state of Iowa (Hood 2010), it was concluded that the introduction of 152 farmers' markets into the state economy led to the creation of 576 jobs, a $59.4 million increase in output, and a $17.8 million increase in income UCSUSA report.<sup id="cite_ref-UCS_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UCS-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While this is just one state, other studies conducted in different regions have produced similar results on the economic benefit of more local farming on a specific community. Otto's study further reported that each individual farmers' market produced 3.8 new jobs per market. However, these economic developments are not limited to local food markets. Surveys of towns in Oregon, Lev, Brewer, and Stephenson (2003), found that farmers' markets were the primary reason that tourists visited local towns on the weekend. The gross economic effect can be calculated, as in the case of the Crescent City Farmers Market in New Orleans, where this single market contributed over $10 million to the local economy. The potential reauthorization of the Federal Farmers Market Promotion Program led to the creation of thousands of jobs within local economies, and further collective economic growth. The logical conclusion is that with the increase in economic benefits due to local farming, room is created in this ever-expanding industry.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_miles">Food miles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Food miles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Critics of the local foods movement question the fundamental principles behind the push to eat locally. For example, the concept that fewer "food miles" translates to a more sustainable meal has not been supported by major scientific studies. According to a study conducted at <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_University_(New_Zealand)" title="Lincoln University (New Zealand)">Lincoln University</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>: "As a concept, food miles has gained some traction with the popular press and certain groups overseas. However, this debate which only includes the distance food travels is spurious as it does not consider total energy use especially in the production of the product."<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The locavore movement has been criticized by Dr. Vasile Stănescu, the co-senior editor of the <i><a href="/wiki/Critical_Animal_Studies" class="mw-redirect" title="Critical Animal Studies">Critical Animal Studies</a></i> book series, as being idealistic and for not actually achieving the environmental benefits of the claim that the reduced food miles decreases the amount of gasses emitted.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Studies have shown that the amount of gasses saved by local transportation, while existing, does not have a significant enough impact to consider it a benefit.
</p><p>The only study to date that directly focuses on whether or not a local diet is more helpful in reducing greenhouse gases was conducted by Christopher L. Weber and H. Scott Matthews at <a href="/wiki/Carnegie-Mellon" class="mw-redirect" title="Carnegie-Mellon">Carnegie-Mellon</a>. They concluded that "dietary shift can be a more effective means of lowering an average household's food-related climate footprint than ‘buying local’".<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact">Environmental impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Environmental impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Numerous studies have shown that locally and sustainably grown foods actually release more greenhouse gases than food made in factory farms. The "Land Degradation" section of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> report <i>Livestock's Long Shadow</i> concludes that "<a href="/wiki/Intensive_farming" title="Intensive farming">Intensification</a> - in terms of increased productivity both in livestock production and in feed crop agriculture - can reduce greenhouse gas emissions from <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Nathan Pelletier of <a href="/wiki/Dalhousie_University" title="Dalhousie University">Dalhousie University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Halifax,_Nova_Scotia" title="Halifax, Nova Scotia">Halifax, Nova Scotia</a> found that cattle raised on open pastures release 50% more greenhouse gas emissions than cattle raised in factory farms.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Williams" title="Adrian Williams">Adrian Williams</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cranfield_University" title="Cranfield University">Cranfield University</a> in England found that <a href="/wiki/Free_range" title="Free range">free range</a> and organic raised chickens have a 20% greater impact on global warming than chickens raised in factory farm conditions, and organic egg production had a 14% higher impact on the climate than factory farm egg production.   Studies such as Christopher Weber's report on food miles have shown that the total amount of greenhouse gas emissions in production far outweighs those in transportation, which implies that locally grown food is actually worse for the environment than food made in factory farms.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_feasibility">Economic feasibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_food&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Economic feasibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While locavorism has been promoted as a feasible alternative to modern food production, some believe it might negatively affect the efficiency of production.<sup id="cite_ref-independent.org_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent.org-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> As technological advances have influenced the amount of output of farms, the productivity of farmers has skyrocketed in the last 70 years. These latter criticisms combine with deeper concerns of food safety, cited on the lines of the historical pattern of economic or food safety inefficiencies of <a href="/wiki/Subsistence_farming" class="mw-redirect" title="Subsistence farming">subsistence farming</a> which form the topic of the book <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Locavore%27s_Dilemma&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Locavore&#39;s Dilemma (page does not exist)">The Locavore's Dilemma</a></i> by geographer <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pierre_Desrochers_(geographer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pierre Desrochers (geographer) (page does not exist)">Pierre Desrochers</a> and public policy scholar <a href="/wiki/Hiroko_Shimizu" class="mw-redirect" title="Hiroko Shimizu">Hiroko Shimizu</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-independent.org_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent.org-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
