<p><b>Lead(II,IV) oxide</b>, also called <b>red lead</b> is the inorganic compound with the formula Pb<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>. A bright red or orange solid, it is used as <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a>, in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Lead%E2%80%93acid_battery" title="Lead–acid battery">batteries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead_glass" title="Lead glass">lead glass</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">rustproof</a> <a href="/wiki/Primer_(paint)" title="Primer (paint)">primer paints</a>.  It is an example of a mixed valence compound, being composed of both Pb(II) and Pb(IV).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead(II,IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lead(II,IV) oxide has a tetragonal crystal structure at <a href="/wiki/Room_temperature" title="Room temperature">room temperature</a>, which transforms to an <a href="/wiki/Orthorhombic" class="mw-redirect" title="Orthorhombic">orthorhombic</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pearson_symbol" title="Pearson symbol">Pearson symbol</a> oP28, <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">Space group</a> = Pbam, No 55) form at temperature 170&#160;K (−103&#160;°C). This phase transition only changes the symmetry of the crystal and slightly modifies the interatomic distances and angles.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead(II,IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lead(II,IV) oxide is prepared by <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcination</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lead(II)_oxide" title="Lead(II) oxide">lead(II) oxide</a> (PbO; also called <a href="/wiki/Litharge" title="Litharge">litharge</a>) in air at about 450-480&#160;°C:<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>6&#160;PbO + O<sub>2</sub> → 2&#160;Pb<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The resulting material is contaminated with PbO. If a pure compound is desired, PbO can be removed by a <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a> solution:
</p>
<dl><dd>PbO + KOH + H<sub>2</sub>O → K[Pb(OH)<sub>3</sub>]</dd></dl>
<p>Another method of preparation relies on <a href="/wiki/Annealing_(metallurgy)" title="Annealing (metallurgy)">annealing</a> of <a href="/wiki/Lead_carbonate" title="Lead carbonate">lead(II) carbonate</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cerussite" title="Cerussite">cerussite</a>) in air:
</p>
<dl><dd>6&#160;PbCO<sub>3</sub> + O<sub>2</sub> → 2&#160;Pb<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> + 6&#160;CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Yet another method is oxidative annealing of <a href="/wiki/White_lead" title="White lead">white lead</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>3&#160;Pb<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>(OH)<sub>2</sub> + O<sub>2</sub> → 2&#160;Pb<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> + 3&#160;CO<sub>2</sub> + 3&#160;H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>In solution, lead(II,IV) oxide can be prepared by reaction of potassium plumbate with <a href="/wiki/Lead_acetate" title="Lead acetate">lead(II) acetate</a>, yielding yellow insoluble lead(II,IV) oxide <a href="/wiki/Monohydrate" class="mw-redirect" title="Monohydrate">monohydrate</a>, Pb<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>·H<sub>2</sub>O, which can be turned into the anhydrous form by gentle heating:
</p>
<dl><dd>K<sub>2</sub>PbO<sub>3</sub> + 2&#160;Pb(OCOCH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → Pb<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> + 2&#160;KOCOCH<sub>3</sub> + 2 CH<sub>3</sub>COOH</dd></dl>
<p>Natural <a href="/wiki/Minium_(mineral)" title="Minium (mineral)">minium</a> is uncommon, forming only in extreme oxidizing conditions of <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> ore bodies. The best known natural specimens come from <a href="/wiki/Broken_Hill" title="Broken Hill">Broken Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, Australia, where they formed as the result of a mine fire.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead(II,IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Red lead is virtually insoluble in water and in <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>. However, it is soluble in <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> present in the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a>, and is therefore <a href="/wiki/Toxic" class="mw-redirect" title="Toxic">toxic</a> when ingested. It also dissolves in <a href="/wiki/Glacial_acetic_acid" class="mw-redirect" title="Glacial acetic acid">glacial acetic acid</a> and a diluted mixture of <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a>.
</p><p>When heated to 500&#160;°C, it decomposes to lead(II) oxide and oxygen. At 580&#160;°C, the reaction is complete.
</p>
<dl><dd>2Pb<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> → 6&#160;PbO + O<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">Nitric acid</a> dissolves the lead(II) oxide component, leaving behind the insoluble <a href="/wiki/Lead(IV)_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Lead(IV) oxide">lead(IV) oxide</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>Pb<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> + 4&#160;HNO<sub>3</sub> → PbO<sub>2</sub> + 2&#160;Pb(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>With iron oxides and with elemental <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, lead(II,IV) oxide forms insoluble iron(II) and iron(III) <a href="/wiki/Plumbate" title="Plumbate">plumbates</a>, which is the basis of the anti-<a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosive</a> properties of lead-based paints applied to iron objects.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use">Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead(II,IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lead tetraoxide is most often used as a <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a> for <a href="/wiki/Primer_(paint)" title="Primer (paint)">primer</a> <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">paints</a> for <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> objects. Due to its <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxicity</a>, its use is being limited. In the past, it was used in combination with <a href="/wiki/Linseed_oil" title="Linseed oil">linseed oil</a> as a thick, long-lasting anti-corrosive paint. The combination of minium and linen fibres was also used for <a href="/wiki/Plumbing" title="Plumbing">plumbing</a>, now replaced with <a href="/wiki/PTFE" class="mw-redirect" title="PTFE">PTFE</a> tape. Currently it is mostly used for manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Lead_glass" title="Lead glass">lead crystal glass</a>. It finds limited use in some amateur <a href="/wiki/Pyrotechnics" title="Pyrotechnics">pyrotechnics</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Delay_charge" class="mw-redirect" title="Delay charge">delay charge</a> and was used in the past in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Dragon%27s_egg" title="Dragon&#39;s egg">dragon's egg</a> <a href="/wiki/Pyrotechnic_stars" class="mw-redirect" title="Pyrotechnic stars">pyrotechnic stars</a>.
</p><p>Red lead is used as a curing agent in some <a href="/wiki/Neoprene" title="Neoprene">polychloroprene</a> rubber compounds.  It is used in place of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">magnesium oxide</a> to provide better water resistance properties.
</p><p>Red lead was used for engineer's <a href="/wiki/Hand_scraper" title="Hand scraper">scraping</a>, before being supplanted by <a href="/wiki/Engineer%27s_blue" title="Engineer&#39;s blue">engineer's blue</a>. 
</p><p>It is also used as an adultering agent in <a href="/wiki/Turmeric" title="Turmeric">turmeric powder</a> .
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiological_effects">Physiological effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead(II,IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Physiological effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lead_poisoning" title="Lead poisoning">Lead poisoning</a></div>
<p>When inhaled, lead(II,IV) oxide irritates lungs. In case of high dose, the victim experiences a metallic taste, chest pain, and abdominal pain. When ingested, it is dissolved in the <a href="/wiki/Gastric_acid" title="Gastric acid">gastric acid</a> and absorbed, leading to <a href="/wiki/Lead_poisoning" title="Lead poisoning">lead poisoning</a>. High concentrations can be absorbed through skin as well, and it is important to follow safety precautions when working with lead-based paint.
</p><p>Long-term contact with lead(II,IV) oxide may lead to accumulation of lead compounds in organisms, with development of symptoms of acute lead poisoning. Chronic poisoning displays as agitation, irritability, vision disorders, <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a>, and also a grayish facial hue.
</p><p>Lead(II,IV) oxide was shown to be <a href="/wiki/Carcinogenic" class="mw-redirect" title="Carcinogenic">carcinogenic</a> for <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_animals" class="mw-redirect" title="Laboratory animals">laboratory animals</a>. Its carcinogenicity for humans was not proven.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lead(II,IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This compound's <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> name <i>minium</i> originates from the <a href="/wiki/Minho_(river)" title="Minho (river)">Minius</a>, a river in northwest <a href="/wiki/Iberia" class="mw-redirect" title="Iberia">Iberia</a> where it was first mined.
</p><p>Lead(II,IV) oxide was used as a red pigment in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Rome</a>, where it was prepared by <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcination</a> of <a href="/wiki/White_lead" title="White lead">white lead</a>. In the ancient and medieval periods it was used as a pigment in the production of <a href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscript" title="Illuminated manuscript">illuminated manuscripts</a>, and gave its name to the <i>minium</i> or <a href="/wiki/Miniature_(illuminated_manuscript)" title="Miniature (illuminated manuscript)">miniature</a>, a style of picture painted with the colour. As a finely divided powder, it was also sprinkled on <a href="/wiki/Dielectric" title="Dielectric">dielectric</a> surfaces to study <a href="/wiki/Lichtenberg_figures" class="mw-redirect" title="Lichtenberg figures">Lichtenberg figures</a>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_medicine" title="Traditional Chinese medicine">traditional Chinese medicine</a>, red lead is used to treat <a href="/wiki/Ringworms" class="mw-redirect" title="Ringworms">ringworms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ulceration" class="mw-redirect" title="Ulceration">ulcerations</a>, though the practice is limited due to its toxicity. Also, <i>azarcón</i>, a Mexican folk remedy for gastrointestinal disorders, contains up to 95% lead(II,IV) oxide.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
