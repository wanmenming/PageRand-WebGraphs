<p>The <b>New European Driving Cycle</b> (<b>NEDC</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Driving_cycle" title="Driving cycle">driving cycle</a>, last updated in 1997, designed to assess the emission levels of car engines and <a href="/wiki/Fuel_economy_in_automobiles#Europe" title="Fuel economy in automobiles">fuel economy</a> in <a href="/wiki/Automobile" class="mw-redirect" title="Automobile">passenger cars</a> (which excludes <a href="/wiki/Light_truck" title="Light truck">light trucks</a> and commercial vehicles).  It is also referred to as <b>MVEG</b> cycle (Motor Vehicle Emissions Group).
</p><p>The NEDC, which is supposed to represent the typical usage of a car in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, is repeatedly criticised for delivering economy-figures which are unachievable in reality. It consists of four repeated <b>ECE-15</b> urban driving cycles (<b>UDC</b>) and one Extra-Urban driving cycle (<b>EUDC</b>).
</p><p>The test procedure is defined in UNECE R101<sup id="cite_ref-UNECE_R101_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNECE_R101-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> for the measurement of CO<sub>2</sub> and fuel consumption and/or the measurement of electric
energy consumption and electric range in hybrid and fully electric M1 and N1 vehicles, and UNECE R83<sup id="cite_ref-UNECE_R83_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNECE_R83-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> for the measurement of emission of pollutants of M, N1 and M2 vehicles. It is maintained by the <a href="/wiki/UNECE" class="mw-redirect" title="UNECE">UNECE</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Forum_for_Harmonization_of_Vehicle_Regulations" title="World Forum for Harmonization of Vehicle Regulations">World Forum for Harmonization of Vehicle Regulations</a> (WP.29),<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> which also works on its successor, the <a href="/wiki/Worldwide_harmonized_Light_vehicles_Test_Procedures" class="mw-redirect" title="Worldwide harmonized Light vehicles Test Procedures">Worldwide harmonized Light vehicles Test Procedures</a> (WLTP).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although originally designed for petrol-based road vehicles, the driving cycle is now also used for diesel vehicles and to estimate the electric power consumption and driving range of <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_electric_vehicle" title="Hybrid electric vehicle">hybrid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battery_electric_vehicles" class="mw-redirect" title="Battery electric vehicles">battery electric vehicles</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurements">Measurements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_European_Driving_Cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Measurements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UN_Regulation_101">UN Regulation 101</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_European_Driving_Cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: UN Regulation 101">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several measurements are usually performed along the cycle. The figures made available to the general public are:
</p>
<ul><li>Urban fuel economy (first 780 seconds)</li>
<li>Extra-Urban fuel economy (780 to 1180&#160;s)</li>
<li>Overall fuel economy (complete cycle)</li>
<li>CO<sub>2</sub> emission (complete cycle)</li></ul>
<p>The following parameters are also generally measured to validate the compliance to <a href="/wiki/European_emission_standards" title="European emission standards">European emission standards</a>:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">Carbon monoxide</a></li>
<li>Unburnt <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbons" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrocarbons">hydrocarbons</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxides" class="mw-redirect" title="Nitrogen oxides">Nitrogen oxides</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">Particulate matter</a></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="UN_Regulation_83">UN Regulation 83</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_European_Driving_Cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: UN Regulation 83">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some or all of the following parameters are measured depending upon the requirements of the region implementing the test:
</p>
<ul><li>Mass of carbon monoxide (CO)</li>
<li>Mass of total hydrocarbons (THC)</li>
<li>Mass of nonmethane hydrocarbons (NMHC)</li>
<li>Mass of oxides of nitrogen (NO<sub><var>x</var></sub>)</li>
<li>Combined mass of hydrocarbons and oxides of nitrogen (THC + NO<sub><var>x</var></sub>)</li>
<li>Mass of particulate matter (PM)</li>
<li>Number of particulates (PN)</li></ul>
<p>The region implementing the test defines limits for each of the pollutants, for instance the <a href="/wiki/European_emission_standards" title="European emission standards">Euro</a> level within the EU.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Test_procedure">Test procedure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_European_Driving_Cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Test procedure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The cycle must be performed on a cold vehicle at 20–30&#160;°C (typically run at 25&#160;°C). The cycles may be performed on a flat road, in the absence of wind. However, to improve <a href="/wiki/Repeatability" title="Repeatability">repeatability</a>, they are generally  performed on a roller test bench. This type of bench is equipped with an <a href="/wiki/Electrical_machine" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical machine">electrical machine</a> to emulate resistance due to <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_drag" class="mw-redirect" title="Aerodynamic drag">aerodynamic drag</a> and vehicle <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> (<a href="/wiki/Inertia" title="Inertia">inertia</a>).
</p><p>For each vehicle configuration, a look-up table is applied: each speed corresponds to a certain value of resistance (reverse <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">torque</a> applied to the drive wheels). This arrangement enables the use of a single physical vehicle to test all vehicle body styles (Sedan, hatchback, MPV etc.) by simply changing the look-up table. A fan is coupled to the roller bench to provide the vehicle air intakes with an airflow matching the current speed. Many more tests can be performed during vehicle development with this arrangement than with conventional road tests.
</p><p>The test is conducted with all ancillary loads turned off (Air conditioning compressor and fan, lights, heated rear window, etc.)
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_driving_Cycle">Urban driving Cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_European_Driving_Cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Urban driving Cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:New_European_Driving_Cycle.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/New_European_Driving_Cycle.svg/400px-New_European_Driving_Cycle.svg.png" width="400" height="320" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/New_European_Driving_Cycle.svg/600px-New_European_Driving_Cycle.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/New_European_Driving_Cycle.svg/800px-New_European_Driving_Cycle.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:New_European_Driving_Cycle.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The NEDC is composed of two parts: ECE-15 (Urban Driving Cycle), repeated 4 times, is plotted from 0&#160;s  to 780&#160;s; EUDC cycle is plotted from 780&#160;s to 1180&#160;s</div></div></div>
<p>The Urban Driving Cycle <b>ECE-15</b> (or just <b>UDC</b>) was introduced first in 1970 as part of <a href="/wiki/ECE_vehicle_regulations" class="mw-redirect" title="ECE vehicle regulations">ECE vehicle regulations</a>; the recent version is defined by ECE R83, R84 and R101.<sup id="cite_ref-UNECE_R101_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNECE_R101-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-UNECE_R83_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNECE_R83-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-UNECE_R84_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNECE_R84-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The cycle has been designed to represent typical driving conditions of busy European cities, and is characterized by low engine load, low exhaust gas temperature, and a maximum speed of 50&#160;km/h.<sup id="cite_ref-NEDC_DieselNet_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NEDC_DieselNet-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When the engine starts, the car pauses for 11&#160;s   - if equipped with a <a href="/wiki/Manual_gearbox" class="mw-redirect" title="Manual gearbox">manual gearbox</a>, 6&#160;s  in neutral (with clutch engaged) and 5&#160;s  in the 1st gear (with clutch disengaged) - then slowly accelerates to 15&#160;km/h  in 4&#160;s, cruises at constant speed for 8&#160;s, brakes to a full stop in 5&#160;s  (manual: last 3&#160;s  with <a href="/wiki/Clutch" title="Clutch">clutch</a> disengaged), then stops for 21&#160;s  (manual: 16&#160;s  in neutral, then  5&#160;s  in the 1st gear).
</p><p>At 49&#160;s, the car slowly accelerates to 32&#160;km/h in 12&#160;s  (manual: 5&#160;s  in 1st gear, 2&#160;s  gear change, then 5&#160;s  in the 2nd gear), cruises for 24&#160;s, slowly brakes to a full stop in 11&#160;s  (manual: last 3&#160;s  with clutch disengaged), then pauses for another 21&#160;s  (manual: 16&#160;s  in neutral, 5&#160;s  in the 1st gear).
</p><p>At 117&#160;s, the car slowly accelerates to 50&#160;km/h in 26&#160;s  (manual: 5&#160;s, 9&#160;s  and 8&#160;s  in the 1st, 2nd and 3rd gears, with additional 2 × 2&#160;s  for gear changes), cruises for 12&#160;s, decelerates to 35&#160;km/h in 8&#160;s, cruises for another 13&#160;s, brakes to a full stop in 12&#160;s  (manual: 2&#160;s  change to the 2nd gear, 7&#160;s  in the 2nd gear, last 3&#160;s  with clutch disengaged), then pauses for 7&#160;s  (manual: in neutral with clutch engaged).
</p><p>The cycle ends on 195&#160;s  after a theoretical distance of 994.03 meters, then it repeats four consecutive times. Total duration is 780&#160;s  (13 minutes) over a theoretical distance of 3976.1 meters, with an average speed of 18.35&#160;km/h.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extra-urban_driving_Cycle">Extra-urban driving Cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_European_Driving_Cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Extra-urban driving Cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Extra-Urban Driving Cycle <b>EUDC</b>, introduced by ECE R101 in 1990,<sup id="cite_ref-UNECE_R101_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNECE_R101-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> has been designed to represent more aggressive, high speed driving modes. The maximum speed of the EUDC cycle is 120&#160;km/h; low-powered vehicles are limited to 90&#160;km/h.<sup id="cite_ref-NEDC_DieselNet_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NEDC_DieselNet-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After a 20&#160;s  stop - if equipped with manual gearbox, in the 1st gear with clutch disengaged - the car slowly accelerates to 70&#160;km/h in 41&#160;s  (manual: 5&#160;s, 9&#160;s, 8&#160;s  and 13&#160;s  in the 1st, 2nd, 3rd and 4th gears, with additional 3 × 2&#160;s  for gear changes), cruises for 50&#160;s  (manual: in the 5th gear [sic]), decelerates to 50&#160;km/h in 8&#160;s  (manual: 4&#160;s  in the 5th and 4&#160;s  in the 4th gear [sic]) and cruises for 69&#160;s, then slowly accelerates to 70&#160;km/h in 13&#160;s .
</p><p>At 201&#160;s, the car cruises at 70&#160;km/h for 50&#160;s  (manual: in the 5th gear), then slowly accelerates to 100&#160;km/h in 35&#160;s  and cruises for 30&#160;s  (manual: in the 5th or 6th gear).
</p><p>Finally, at 316&#160;s  the car slowly accelerates to 120&#160;km/h in 20&#160;s, cruises for 10&#160;s, then slowly brakes to a full stop in 34&#160;s  (manual: in the 5th or 6th gear, last 10&#160;s  with clutch disengaged), and idles for another 20&#160;s  (manual: in neutral).
</p><p>Total duration is 400&#160;s  (6 minutes 40&#160;s econds) and theoretical distance is 6956 meters, with an average speed of 62.6&#160;km/h.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combined">Combined</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_European_Driving_Cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Combined">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The combined fuel economy is calculated by a total consumption of urban and extra-urban cycles over the total distance (theoretical 10932 meters). The total test time amounts to 1180&#160;s  with an average speed of 33.35&#160;km/h. Sometimes the NEDC is also quoted at 1220&#160;s, which includes the initial 40&#160;s with the vehicle at standstill and combustion engine off.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_European_Driving_Cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inability_to_represent_real-life_driving">Inability to represent real-life driving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_European_Driving_Cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Inability to represent real-life driving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The NEDC was conceived when European vehicles were lighter and less powerful. The test offers a stylized driving speed pattern with low accelerations, constant speed cruises, and many idling events. However, accelerations are much steeper and variable in practice,<sup id="cite_ref-ICCT-2012-02_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ICCT-2012-02-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> which is in part caused by the power surplus of modern engines as the 0–100&#160;km/h (0–62&#160;mph) average-time decreased from 14 seconds in 1981 to 9 seconds in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In 1998, a Swedish researcher criticized the NEDC standard for allowing large emission differences between test and reality.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The UK consumer group <a href="/wiki/Which%3F" title="Which?">Which?</a>, criticized the NEDC test procedure as being out-of-date as its most recent update was made in 1997<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>; before hybrid cars and stop-start technology was generally available. Which? claimed the test did not replicate real-world driving conditions and had numerous loopholes which cause the results to be unachievable in practice It was also claimed that no official body polices the tests and the car-companies can arbitrarily reduce their results by 4% at the end of the cycle. Weaknesses noted are: (i) that tests are not necessarily repeatable and comparable; (ii) the test-cycle does not include sustained motorway driving; (iii) test-cycles can be performed using optional economy settings which will not typically be selected by drivers; (iv) the test-cycle is performed with ancillary equipment such an air-conditioning and heated windows switched off; (v) the tests can be conducted at 2 km/h (1.2 mph) below the required speed thus using less fuel; (vi) roof-rails and passenger door-mirror can be removed for the test, to reduce <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_drag" class="mw-redirect" title="Aerodynamic drag">drag</a>; (vii) tyre inflation for the test can be set above the recommended pressure values to artificially reduce <a href="/wiki/Rolling_resistance" title="Rolling resistance">rolling resistance</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cycle_beating">Cycle beating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_European_Driving_Cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cycle beating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/European_emission_standards#&quot;Cycle_beating&quot;_controversy" title="European emission standards">European emission standards §&#160;"Cycle beating" controversy</a></div>
<p>For the emission standards to deliver real emission reductions it is crucial to use a test cycle that reflects real-world driving style. However the fixed speeds, gear shift points and accelerations of the NEDC offer possibilities for manufacturers to engage in what is called 'cycle beating' to optimise engine emission performance to the corresponding operating points of the test cycle, while emissions from typical driving conditions would be much higher than expected, undermining the standards and public health.<sup id="cite_ref-ICCT-2012-02_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ICCT-2012-02-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In one particular instance, research from two German technology institutes found that for diesel cars no 'real' NO<sub><var>x</var></sub> reductions have been achieved after 13 years of stricter standards.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_deceptions">Other deceptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_European_Driving_Cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other deceptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is alleged that, under NEDC, some automakers overinflate tyres, adjusting or disconnecting brakes to reduce friction, and taping cracks between body panels and windows to reduce air resistance, some go as far as removing wing mirrors, to inflate measured fuel economy and lower measured carbon emission.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Successors">Successors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_European_Driving_Cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Successors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/UNECE" class="mw-redirect" title="UNECE">UNECE</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Forum_for_Harmonization_of_Vehicle_Regulations" title="World Forum for Harmonization of Vehicle Regulations">WP29</a> is currently developing a new global harmonized driving cycle, the <a href="/wiki/World_Light_Test_Procedure" class="mw-redirect" title="World Light Test Procedure">World Light Test Procedure</a> (WLTP) with participation of experts from the European Union, India, and Japan; it will apply to light duty vehicles (i.e. passenger cars and light commercial vans).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The European Commission intends to introduce a type approval procedure to measure the energy efficiency of mobile air conditioning systems in vehicles, as well as its effect on fuel consumption and emissions, and display an efficiency label on the vehicle; the work is being performed by <a href="/wiki/Graz_University_of_Technology" title="Graz University of Technology">TU Graz</a> (Institute for Internal combustion engines and Thermodynamics), <a href="/wiki/Aristotle_University_of_Thessaloniki" title="Aristotle University of Thessaloniki">Aristotle University of Thessaloniki</a> (Laboratory of Applied Thermodynamics), <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Organisation_for_Applied_Scientific_Research" title="Netherlands Organisation for Applied Scientific Research">TNO</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_Automobile_Manufacturers_Association" title="European Automobile Manufacturers Association">ACEA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
