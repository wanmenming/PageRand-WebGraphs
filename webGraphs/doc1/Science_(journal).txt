<p><i><b>Science</b></i>, also widely referred to as <i><b>Science Magazine</b></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> is the <a href="/wiki/Peer-reviewed" class="mw-redirect" title="Peer-reviewed">peer-reviewed</a> <a href="/wiki/Academic_journal" title="Academic journal">academic journal</a> of the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="American Association for the Advancement of Science">American Association for the Advancement of Science</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> (AAAS) and one of the world's top academic journals.<sup id="cite_ref-Lemonick_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lemonick-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It was first published in 1880, is currently circulated weekly and has a print subscriber base of around 130,000. Because institutional subscriptions and online access serve a larger audience, its estimated readership is 570,400 people.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The major focus of the journal is publishing important original scientific research and research reviews, but <i>Science</i> also publishes science-related news, opinions on <a href="/wiki/Science_policy" title="Science policy">science policy</a> and other matters of interest to scientists and others who are concerned with the wide implications of science and technology. Unlike most <a href="/wiki/Scientific_journals" class="mw-redirect" title="Scientific journals">scientific journals</a>, which focus on a specific field, <i>Science</i> and its rival <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> cover the full range of <a href="/wiki/List_of_academic_disciplines#Natural_sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="List of academic disciplines">scientific disciplines</a>. According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_Citation_Reports" title="Journal Citation Reports">Journal Citation Reports</a></i>, <i>Science</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s 2015 <a href="/wiki/Impact_factor" title="Impact factor">impact factor</a> was 34.661.<sup id="cite_ref-WoS_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WoS-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although it is the journal of the AAAS, membership in the AAAS is not required to publish in <i>Science</i>. Papers are accepted from authors around the world. Competition to publish in <i>Science</i> is very intense, as an article published in such a highly cited journal can lead to attention and career advancement for the authors. Fewer than 7% of articles submitted are accepted for publication.
</p><p><i>Science</i> is based in Washington, D.C., United States, with a second office in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, England.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_(journal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Science_1883_Cover.png" class="image"><img alt="cover of the first volume of the resurrected journal (February–June 1883)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Science_1883_Cover.png/220px-Science_1883_Cover.png" width="220" height="307" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Science_1883_Cover.png/330px-Science_1883_Cover.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Science_1883_Cover.png/440px-Science_1883_Cover.png 2x" data-file-width="1020" data-file-height="1425" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Science_1883_Cover.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cover of the first volume of the resurrected journal (February–June 1883)</div></div></div>
<p><i>Science</i> was founded by New York journalist John Michels in 1880 with financial support from <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a> and later from <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Graham_Bell" title="Alexander Graham Bell">Alexander Graham Bell</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> (Edison received favorable editorial treatment in return, without disclosure of the financial relationship, at a time when his reputation was suffering due to delays producing the promised commercially viable light bulb.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>) However, the journal never gained enough subscribers to succeed and ended publication in March 1882. Entomologist <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Hubbard_Scudder" title="Samuel Hubbard Scudder">Samuel H. Scudder</a> resurrected the journal one year later and had some success while covering the meetings of prominent American scientific societies, including the AAAS.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> However, by 1894, <i>Science</i> was again in financial difficulty and was sold to psychologist <a href="/wiki/James_McKeen_Cattell" title="James McKeen Cattell">James McKeen Cattell</a> for $500.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> 
</p><p>In an agreement worked out by Cattell and AAAS secretary <a href="/wiki/Leland_Ossian_Howard" title="Leland Ossian Howard">Leland O. Howard</a>, <i>Science</i> became the journal of the American Association for the Advancement of Science in 1900.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> During the early part of the 20th century important articles published in <i>Science</i> included papers on <a href="/wiki/Drosophila_melanogaster" title="Drosophila melanogaster">fruit fly</a> <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a> by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hunt_Morgan" title="Thomas Hunt Morgan">Thomas Hunt Morgan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_lens" title="Gravitational lens">gravitational lensing</a> by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spiral_nebulae" class="mw-redirect" title="Spiral nebulae">spiral nebulae</a> by <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">Edwin Hubble</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> After Cattell died in 1944, the ownership of the journal was transferred to the AAAS.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After Cattell's death in 1944, the journal lacked a consistent editorial presence until Graham DuShane became editor in 1956. In 1958, under DuShane's leadership, <i>Science</i> absorbed <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scientific_Monthly" title="The Scientific Monthly">The Scientific Monthly</a></i>, thus increasing the journal's circulation by over 60% from 38,000 to more than 61,000.<sup id="cite_ref-maturing3_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maturing3-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Physicist <a href="/wiki/Philip_Abelson" title="Philip Abelson">Philip Abelson</a>, a co-discoverer of <a href="/wiki/Neptunium" title="Neptunium">neptunium</a>, served as editor from 1962 to 1984. Under Abelson the efficiency of the review process was improved and the publication practices were brought up to date.<sup id="cite_ref-maturing3_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maturing3-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> During this time, papers on the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a> missions and some of the earliest reports on AIDS were published.<sup id="cite_ref-change3_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-change3-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Biochemist <a href="/wiki/Daniel_E._Koshland,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Daniel E. Koshland, Jr.">Daniel E. Koshland, Jr.</a> served as editor from 1985 until 1995. From 1995 until 2000, neuroscientist <a href="/wiki/Floyd_E._Bloom" title="Floyd E. Bloom">Floyd E. Bloom</a> held that position.<sup id="cite_ref-change3_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-change3-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Biologist <a href="/wiki/Donald_Kennedy" title="Donald Kennedy">Donald Kennedy</a> became the editor of <i>Science</i> in 2000. Biochemist <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Alberts" title="Bruce Alberts">Bruce Alberts</a> took his place in March 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Geophysicist <a href="/wiki/Marcia_McNutt" title="Marcia McNutt">Marcia McNutt</a> became editor-in-chief in June 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> During her tenure the family of journals expanded to include <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_Robotics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Science Robotics (page does not exist)">Science Robotics</a></i> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_Immunology&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Science Immunology (page does not exist)">Science Immunology</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Open_access" title="Open access">open access</a> publishing with <i><a href="/wiki/Science_Advances" title="Science Advances">Science Advances</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_M._Berg" title="Jeremy M. Berg">Jeremy M. Berg</a> became editor-in-chief on July 1, 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2001, draft results of the <a href="/wiki/Human_genome" title="Human genome">human genome</a> were simultaneously published by <i>Nature</i> and <i>Science</i> with <i>Science</i> publishing the <a href="/wiki/Celera_Corporation" title="Celera Corporation">Celera Genomics</a> paper and <i>Nature</i> publishing the publicly funded <a href="/wiki/Human_Genome_Project" title="Human Genome Project">Human Genome Project</a>. In 2007 <i>Science </i>(together with <a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)"><i>Nature</i></a>) received the <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_Asturias_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince of Asturias Awards">Prince of Asturias Award</a> for Communications and Humanity.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In 2015 <a href="/wiki/Rush_D._Holt,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Rush D. Holt, Jr.">Rush D. Holt, Jr.</a>, chief executive officer of the AAAS and executive publisher of <i>Science</i>, stated that the journal was becoming increasingly international: "[I]nternationally co-authored papers are now the norm—they represent almost 60 percent of the papers. In 1992, it was slightly less than 20 percent."<sup id="cite_ref-Science_and_Diplomacy._23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science_and_Diplomacy.-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_of_journals">Family of journals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_(journal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Family of journals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Science family of journals includes <i>Science</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Science_Translational_Medicine" title="Science Translational Medicine">Science Translational Medicine</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Science_Signaling" title="Science Signaling">Science Signaling</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Science_Advances" title="Science Advances">Science Advances</a></i>. In 2015, Holt announced another expansion: <i>Science Robotics</i> and <i>Science Immunology</i> would begin publication in mid-2016.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Availability">Availability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Science_(journal)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Availability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The latest editions of the journal are available online, through the main journal website, only to subscribers, AAAS members, and for delivery to <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">IP addresses</a> at institutions that subscribe; students, K–12 teachers, and some others can subscribe at a reduced fee. However, research articles published after 1997 are available for free (with online registration) one year after they are published i.e. <a href="/wiki/Delayed_open_access" class="mw-redirect" title="Delayed open access">delayed open access</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-delayed_oa_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-delayed_oa-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Significant public-health related articles are also available for free, sometimes immediately after publication. AAAS members may also access the pre-1997 Science archives at the <i>Science</i> website, where it is called "Science Classic". Institutions can opt to add Science Classic to their subscriptions for an additional fee. Some older articles can also be accessed via <a href="/wiki/JSTOR" title="JSTOR">JSTOR</a> and <a href="/wiki/ProQuest" title="ProQuest">ProQuest</a>.
</p><p>The journal also participates in initiatives that provide free or low-cost access to readers in developing countries, including <a href="/wiki/HINARI" title="HINARI">HINARI</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=OARE&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="OARE (page does not exist)">OARE</a>, <a href="/wiki/AGORA" title="AGORA">AGORA</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scidev.net" class="mw-redirect" title="Scidev.net">Scidev.net</a>.
</p><p>Other features of the <i>Science</i> website include the free "ScienceNow" section with "up to the minute news from science",<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and "ScienceCareers", which provides free career resources for scientists and engineers.
</p>
