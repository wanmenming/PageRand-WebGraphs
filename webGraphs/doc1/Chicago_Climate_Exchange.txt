<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Chicago Climate Exchange</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>The <b>Chicago Climate Exchange</b> (CCX) was North America’s only voluntary, legally binding <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> (GHG) <a href="/wiki/Emissions_trading" title="Emissions trading">reduction and trading system</a> for emission sources and offset projects in North America and Brazil.
</p><p>CCX employed independent verification, included six greenhouse gases, and traded greenhouse gas emission allowances from 2003 to 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-ng2010_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ng2010-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ft2010_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ft2010-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The companies joining the exchange committed to reducing their aggregate emissions by 6% by 2010.  CCX had an aggregate baseline of 680 million metric tons of <a href="/wiki/CO2_equivalent" class="mw-redirect" title="CO2 equivalent">CO<sub>2</sub> equivalent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>CCX ceased trading carbon credits at the end of 2010 due to inactivity in the U.S. carbon markets,<sup id="cite_ref-reuters-2010_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-2010-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> although carbon exchanges were intended to still be facilitated.<sup id="cite_ref-ft2010_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ft2010-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ng2010_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ng2010-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_Exchange_PLC">Climate Exchange PLC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chicago_Climate_Exchange&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Climate Exchange PLC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Until July 2010, CCX was operated by the public company <a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_Exchange_PLC&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Climate Exchange PLC (page does not exist)">Climate Exchange PLC</a>, which also owned the <a href="/wiki/European_Climate_Exchange" title="European Climate Exchange">European Climate Exchange</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Richard_L._Sandor" title="Richard L. Sandor">Richard Sandor</a>, creator of the Sustainable Performance Group, founded the exchange and has been a spokesman for it.  The exchange traded in emissions of six gases: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_hexafluoride" title="Sulfur hexafluoride">sulfur hexafluoride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Perfluorocarbons" class="mw-redirect" title="Perfluorocarbons">perfluorocarbons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluorocarbons" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrofluorocarbons">hydrofluorocarbons</a>. CCX started trading in October 2003, prior to the commencement of trading in the European Union through the <a href="/wiki/European_Union_Emission_Trading_Scheme" title="European Union Emission Trading Scheme">ETS system</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/IntercontinentalExchange" class="mw-redirect" title="IntercontinentalExchange">IntercontinentalExchange</a> (<a href="/wiki/New_York_Stock_Exchange" title="New York Stock Exchange">NYSE</a>:&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nyse.com/quote/XNYS:ICE">ICE</a>), a leading operator of regulated global futures exchanges, clearing houses and over-the-counter (OTC) markets, announced in April 2010 that it had agreed on terms to acquire Climate Exchange plc.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> That acquisition was completed in July 2010<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and was followed by an announcement that half of the company's Chicago-based workforce would be laid off due to inactivity in the U.S. carbon markets.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters-2010_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters-2010-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In November 2010, the Climate Exchange stated that it would cease trading carbon credits at the end of 2010, although carbon exchanges will still be facilitated.<sup id="cite_ref-ng2010_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ng2010-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ft2010_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ft2010-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_Trading_Position">Final Trading Position</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chicago_Climate_Exchange&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Final Trading Position">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The effective final CFI position was reached in November 2010 when the carbon credit price per metric ton of CO2 was between 10 and 5 US Cents, down from its highest value of 750 US Cents in May 2008. Trading reached zero monthly volume in February 2010 and remained at zero for the next 9 months when the decision to close the exchange was announced.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_Sequestration_Atlas_of_the_United_States_and_Canada">Carbon Sequestration Atlas of the United States and Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chicago_Climate_Exchange&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Carbon Sequestration Atlas of the United States and Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Carbon Sequestration Atlas of the United States and Canada</i> detailed some of CCX's activity in various regions of North America.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The Valley Wood Carbon Sequestration Project, the first such project to be verified through the Chicago Climate Exchange, was the recipient of offset funding generated through a unique partnership, developed in 2008 by Verus Carbon Neutral, that brought together 17 merchants of Atlanta's Virginia-Highland shopping and dining neighborhood retail district to establish the first Carbon Neutral Zone in the united States.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Components_of_the_Trading_System">Components of the Trading System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chicago_Climate_Exchange&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Components of the Trading System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Trading System had the following three parts.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>The <i>Trading Platform</i> was a marketplace for executing trades among Registry Account Holders. For instance, National Farmers Union’s Carbon Credit Program was a multi-state program that allowed farmers and landowners to earn income by storing carbon in their soil through no-till crop production and long-term grass seeding practices. Farmers Union had earned approval from the Chicago Climate Exchange to aggregate carbon credits. Farmers Union was enrolling producer areas of carbon into blocks of credits that were traded on the Exchange, much like other agricultural commodities are traded.</li>
<li>The <i>Clearing and Settlement Platform</i> processed all transaction information.</li>
<li>The <i>Registry</i> was the official database for Carbon Financial Instruments owned by Registry Account Holders.</li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Participants">Participants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chicago_Climate_Exchange&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Participants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The exchange had more than 400 members ranging from corporations like <a href="/wiki/Ford" class="mw-redirect" title="Ford">Ford</a>, <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">DuPont</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Motorola" title="Motorola">Motorola</a>, to state and municipalities such as <a href="/wiki/Oakland" class="mw-redirect" title="Oakland">Oakland</a> and Chicago, to educational institutions such as University of California, San Diego, <a href="/wiki/Tufts_University" title="Tufts University">Tufts University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michigan_State_University" title="Michigan State University">Michigan State University</a> and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota" title="University of Minnesota">University of Minnesota</a>, to farmers and their organizations, such as the <a href="/wiki/National_Farmers_Union_(United_States)" title="National Farmers Union (United States)">National Farmers Union</a> and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iowa_Farm_Bureau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Iowa Farm Bureau (page does not exist)">Iowa Farm Bureau</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The government run passenger rail corporation, <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a>, was also a member of the exchange and was trying to reduce its emissions even though the railroad planned to dramatically increase its annual ridership totals.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
