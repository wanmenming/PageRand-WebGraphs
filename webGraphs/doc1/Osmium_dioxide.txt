<p><b>Osmium dioxide</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> OsO<sub>2</sub>. It exists as brown to black crystalline powder, but single crystals are golden and exhibit metallic conductivity.  The compound crystallizes in the <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a> structural motif, i.e. the connectivity is very similar to that in the mineral rutile.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>OsO<sub>2</sub> can be obtained by the reaction of <a href="/wiki/Osmium" title="Osmium">osmium</a> with a variety of oxidizing agents, including, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chlorate" title="Sodium chlorate">sodium chlorate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osmium_tetroxide" title="Osmium tetroxide">osmium tetroxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a> at about 600&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Using <a href="/wiki/Chemical_transport_reaction" title="Chemical transport reaction">chemical transport</a>, one can obtain large crystals of OsO<sub>2</sub>, sized up to 7x5x3 mm<sup>3</sup>. Single crystals show metallic resistivity of ~15 μΩ cm. A typical transport agent is <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> via the reversible formation of volatile OsO<sub>4</sub>:<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>OsO<sub>2</sub>  +  O<sub>2</sub> ⇌ OsO<sub>4</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Osmium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>OsO<sub>2</sub> does not dissolve in water but is attacked by dilute <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The crystals have <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a> structure.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Unlike <a href="/wiki/Osmium_tetroxide" title="Osmium tetroxide">osmium tetroxide</a>, OsO<sub>2</sub> is not toxic.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
