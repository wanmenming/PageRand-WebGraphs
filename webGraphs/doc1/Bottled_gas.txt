<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Bottled gas</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Bottled gas</b> is a term used for substances which are gaseous at <a href="/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard temperature and pressure">standard temperature and pressure</a> (STP) and have been compressed and stored in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_steel" title="Carbon steel">carbon steel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aluminum" class="mw-redirect" title="Aluminum">aluminum</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite</a> bottles known as <a href="/wiki/Gas_cylinder" title="Gas cylinder">gas cylinders</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_state_in_cylinders">Gas state in cylinders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bottled_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Gas state in cylinders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are four cases: either the substance remains a gas at standard temperature but increased pressure, the substance liquefies at standard temperature but increased pressure, the substance is dissolved in a <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a>, or the substance is liquefied at reduced temperature and increased pressure. In the last case the bottle is constructed with an inner and outer shell separated by a vacuum (<a href="/wiki/Dewar_flask" class="mw-redirect" title="Dewar flask">dewar flask</a>) so that the low temperature can be maintained by <a href="/wiki/Evaporative_cooling" class="mw-redirect" title="Evaporative cooling">evaporative cooling</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Case_I">Case I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bottled_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Case I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The substance remains a <i>gas</i> at <i>standard temperature</i> and <i>increased pressure</i>, its <a href="/wiki/Critical_temperature" class="mw-redirect" title="Critical temperature">critical temperature</a> being below standard temperature.  Examples include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Air" class="mw-redirect" title="Air">air</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Krypton" title="Krypton">krypton</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Case_II">Case II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bottled_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Case II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ButaneGasCylinder_WhiteBack.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/ButaneGasCylinder_WhiteBack.jpg/220px-ButaneGasCylinder_WhiteBack.jpg" width="220" height="291" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/ButaneGasCylinder_WhiteBack.jpg/330px-ButaneGasCylinder_WhiteBack.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/ButaneGasCylinder_WhiteBack.jpg/440px-ButaneGasCylinder_WhiteBack.jpg 2x" data-file-width="776" data-file-height="1028" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ButaneGasCylinder_WhiteBack.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Butane gas cylinder</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tea_beauty_in_simplicity.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Tea_beauty_in_simplicity.jpg/220px-Tea_beauty_in_simplicity.jpg" width="220" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Tea_beauty_in_simplicity.jpg/330px-Tea_beauty_in_simplicity.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Tea_beauty_in_simplicity.jpg/440px-Tea_beauty_in_simplicity.jpg 2x" data-file-width="636" data-file-height="638" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tea_beauty_in_simplicity.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>modest Tea <a href="/wiki/Kettle" title="Kettle">Kettle</a> boiling <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> over small Bottled gas at a <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">tea</a> house.</div></div></div>
<p>The substance <i><a href="/wiki/Liquefaction_of_gases" title="Liquefaction of gases">liquefies</a></i> at <i>standard temperature</i> but <i>increased pressure</i>.  Examples include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Butane" title="Butane">butane</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (also packaged as a cryogenic gas, Case IV)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Case_III">Case III</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bottled_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Case III">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The substance is <i>dissolved</i> at <i>standard temperature</i> in a solvent.  Examples include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Acetylene" title="Acetylene">acetylene</a>
<ul><li>Note: Acetylene cylinders contain an inert packing material, which may be <a href="/wiki/Agamassan" title="Agamassan">agamassan</a>, and are filled with a solvent such as <a href="/wiki/Acetone" title="Acetone">acetone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dimethylformamide" title="Dimethylformamide">dimethylformamide</a>.  The acetylene is pumped into the cylinder and it dissolves in the solvent.  When the cylinder is opened the acetylene comes back out of solution, much like a carbonated beverage bubbles when opened. This is a workaround to acetylene's property to explode when pressurized above 200&#160;kPa or liquified.</li></ul></li>
<li>carbon dioxide in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Soft_drink" title="Soft drink">soft drink</a></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Case_IV">Case IV</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bottled_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Case IV">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The substance is <i>liquefied</i> at <i>reduced temperature</i> and <i>increased pressure</i>.  These are also referred to as <a href="/wiki/Cryogenic" class="mw-redirect" title="Cryogenic">cryogenic</a> gases.  Examples include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen" title="Liquid nitrogen">liquid nitrogen</a> (LN<sub>2</sub>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen" title="Liquid hydrogen">liquid hydrogen</a> (LH<sub>2</sub>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Liquid_oxygen" title="Liquid oxygen">liquid oxygen</a> (LO<sub>X</sub>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (also packaged as a liquefied gas, Case II)</li></ul>
<dl><dd>Note: cryogenic gases are typically equipped with some type of 'bleed' device to prevent overpressure from rupturing the bottle and to allow <a href="/wiki/Evaporative_cooling" class="mw-redirect" title="Evaporative cooling">evaporative cooling</a> to continue.</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion_and_volume">Expansion and volume</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bottled_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Expansion and volume">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The general rule is that one unit volume of liquid will expand to approximately 800 unit volumes of gas at <a href="/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure" class="mw-redirect" title="Standard temperature and pressure">Standard temperature and pressure</a> with some variation due to <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_force" title="Intermolecular force">intermolecular force</a> and molecule size compared to an <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">ideal gas</a>. Normal high pressure gas cylinders or bottles will hold from 200 to 400 <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atmosphere (unit)s</a>. The atmosphere units pressure held by the bottle is equivalent to the number of volumes of standard temperature and pressure of the gas held by the bottle for an ideal gas.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Special_handling_considerations">Special handling considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bottled_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Special handling considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because the contents are under high pressure and are sometimes hazardous, there are special <a href="/wiki/Safety" title="Safety">safety</a> regulations for handling bottled gases. These include chaining bottles to prevent falling and breaking, proper ventilation to prevent injury or death in case of leaks and signage to indicate the potential hazards.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Compressed_Gas_Association" title="Compressed Gas Association">Compressed Gas Association</a> (CGA) sells a number of booklets and pamphlets on safe handling and use of bottled gases.  (Members of the CGA can get the pamphlets for free.) The <a href="/w/index.php?title=European_Industrial_Gases_Association&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="European Industrial Gases Association (page does not exist)">European Industrial Gases Association</a> and the <a href="/wiki/British_Compressed_Gases_Association" title="British Compressed Gases Association">British Compressed Gases Association</a> provide similar facilities in Europe and the United Kingdom.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature_differences">Nomenclature differences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bottled_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nomenclature differences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the United States, 'bottled gas' typically refers to <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">liquefied petroleum gas</a>. 'Bottled gas' is sometimes used in medical supply, especially for portable <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_tank" title="Oxygen tank">oxygen tanks</a>. Packaged industrial gases are frequently called 'cylinder gas', though 'bottled gas' is sometimes used.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and other parts of Europe more commonly refer to 'bottled gas' when discussing any usage whether industrial, medical or liquefied petroleum. However, in contrast, what the United States calls liquefied petroleum gas is known generically in the United Kingdom as 'LPG'; and it may be ordered using by one of several <a href="/wiki/Trade_name" title="Trade name">Trade names</a>, or specifically as <a href="/wiki/Butane" title="Butane">butane</a> or <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a> depending on the required heat output.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Colour_coding">Colour coding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bottled_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Colour coding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Different countries have different gas colour codes but attempts are being made to standardise the colours of cylinder shoulders:
</p>
<ul><li>Colours of cylinders for Medical gases are covered by an <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a> (ISO) standard, ISO 32; but not all countries use this standard.</li>
<li>Within Europe gas cylinders colours are being standardised according to EN 1089-3, the standard colours applying to the cylinder shoulder only; i.e., the top of the cylinder close to the pillar valve.</li>
<li>In the United States, colour-coding is not regulated by law.</li></ul>
<p>The user should not rely on the colour of a cylinder to indicate what it contains. The label or decal should always be checked for product identification.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_cylinder_colours">European cylinder colours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bottled_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: European cylinder colours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h4><span id="Gas_mixtures.2C_mostly_for_diving"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_mixtures,_mostly_for_diving">Gas mixtures, mostly for diving</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bottled_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Gas mixtures, mostly for diving">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Diving_cylinder" title="Diving cylinder">Diving cylinders</a> are left unpainted (for aluminium), or painted to prevent corrosion (for steel), often in bright colors, most often <a href="/wiki/Fluorescent" class="mw-redirect" title="Fluorescent">fluorescent</a> yellow, to increase visibility. This should not be confused with industrial gases, where a yellow shoulder means chlorine.
