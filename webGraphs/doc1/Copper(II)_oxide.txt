<p><b>Copper(II) oxide</b> or <b>cupric oxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the formula CuO.  A black solid, it is one of the two stable <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxides</a> of <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, the other being Cu<sub>2</sub>O or <a href="/wiki/Copper(I)_oxide" title="Copper(I) oxide">cuprous oxide</a>.  As a mineral, it is known as <a href="/wiki/Tenorite" title="Tenorite">tenorite</a>.  It is a product of copper mining and the precursor to many other copper-containing products and chemical compounds.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is produced on a large scale by <a href="/wiki/Pyrometallurgy" title="Pyrometallurgy">pyrometallurgy</a> used to extract copper from ores.  The ores are treated with an aqueous mixture of ammonium carbonate, ammonia, and oxygen to give copper(I) and copper(II) <a href="/wiki/Ammine_complex" class="mw-redirect" title="Ammine complex">ammine complexes</a>, which are extracted from the solids.  These complexes are decomposed with steam to give CuO.
</p><p>It can be formed by heating copper in air at around 300 – 800°C:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Cu + O<sub>2</sub> → 2 CuO</dd></dl>
<p>For laboratory uses, pure copper(II) oxide is better prepared by heating <a href="/wiki/Copper(II)_nitrate" title="Copper(II) nitrate">copper(II) nitrate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Copper(II)_hydroxide" title="Copper(II) hydroxide">copper(II) hydroxide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Copper(II)_carbonate" title="Copper(II) carbonate">copper(II) carbonate</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Cu(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> → 2 CuO + 4 NO<sub>2</sub> + O<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd>Cu(OH)<sub>2</sub> (s) → CuO (s) + H<sub>2</sub>O (l)</dd>
<dd>CuCO<sub>3</sub> → CuO + CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p><br />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Copper(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span>  <small><i>(September 2018)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Copper(II) oxide dissolves in <a href="/wiki/Mineral_acid" title="Mineral acid">mineral acids</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> to give the corresponding copper(II) salts:
</p>
<dl><dd>CuO + 2 HNO<sub>3</sub> → Cu(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
<dd>CuO + 2 HCl → CuCl<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
<dd>CuO + H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> → CuSO<sub>4</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>It reacts with concentrated alkali to form the corresponding cuprate salts:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 MOH + CuO + H<sub>2</sub>O   →   M<sub>2</sub>[Cu(OH)<sub>4</sub>]</dd></dl>
<p>It can also be reduced to <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> metal using <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>CuO + H<sub>2</sub>   →   Cu + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
<dd>CuO + CO   →   Cu + CO<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd>2CuO + C   →   2Cu + CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>When cupric oxide is substituted for iron oxide in <a href="/wiki/Thermite" title="Thermite">thermite</a> the resulting mixture is a low explosive, not an incendiary.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_physical_properties">Structure and physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Structure and physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Copper(II) oxide belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Monoclinic_crystal_system" title="Monoclinic crystal system">monoclinic crystal system</a>. The copper atom is coordinated by 4 oxygen atoms in an approximately square planar configuration.<sup id="cite_ref-Hull_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hull-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Work_function" title="Work function">work function</a> of bulk CuO is 5.3<a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">eV</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Copper(II) oxide is a <a href="/wiki/P-type_semiconductor" class="mw-redirect" title="P-type semiconductor">p-type semiconductor</a>, with a narrow <a href="/wiki/Band_gap" title="Band gap">band gap</a> of 1.2 eV. Cupric oxide can be used to produce <a href="/wiki/Dry_cell_battery" class="mw-redirect" title="Dry cell battery">dry cell batteries</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a significant product of copper mining, copper(II) oxide is the starting point for the production of other copper salts. For example, many wood preservatives are produced from copper oxide.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cupric oxide is used as a <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a> in ceramics to produce blue, red, and green, and sometimes gray, pink, or black glazes.  
</p><p>It is also incorrectly used as a dietary supplement in animal feed.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Due to low bioactivity, negligible copper is absorbed.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is also used when welding with <a href="/wiki/Copper_alloys" class="mw-redirect" title="Copper alloys">copper alloys</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_disposal">Use in disposal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Use in disposal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cupric oxide can be used to safely dispose of <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_materials" class="mw-redirect" title="Hazardous materials">hazardous materials</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Cyanide" title="Cyanide">cyanide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbons" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrocarbons">hydrocarbons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Halogenated_hydrocarbons" class="mw-redirect" title="Halogenated hydrocarbons">halogenated hydrocarbons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dioxins_and_dioxin-like_compounds" title="Dioxins and dioxin-like compounds">dioxins</a>, through <a href="/wiki/Oxidation" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The decomposition reactions of phenol and pentachlorophenol follow these pathways:
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>OH + 14CuO → 6CO<sub>2</sub> + 3H<sub>2</sub>O + 14Cu</dd>
<dd>C<sub>6</sub>Cl<sub>5</sub>OH + 2H<sub>2</sub>O + 9CuO → 6CO<sub>2</sub> + 5HCl + 9Cu</dd></dl>
