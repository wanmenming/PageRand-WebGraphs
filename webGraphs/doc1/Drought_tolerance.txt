<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Drought tolerance</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sedum_weinbergii.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Sedum_weinbergii.jpg/250px-Sedum_weinbergii.jpg" width="250" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Sedum_weinbergii.jpg/375px-Sedum_weinbergii.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Sedum_weinbergii.jpg/500px-Sedum_weinbergii.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sedum_weinbergii.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sedum" title="Sedum">Sedum</a> is a drought tolerant plant whose specific adaptations include <a href="/wiki/Succulent_plant" title="Succulent plant">succulence</a> and a waxy surface on its leaves and stems.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Drought tolerance</b> is the degree to which a <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> is adapted to <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> conditions. <i><a href="/wiki/Desiccation_tolerance" title="Desiccation tolerance">Desiccation tolerance</a></i> is an extreme degree of drought tolerance.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Plants naturally adapted to dry conditions are called <i><a href="/wiki/Xerophyte" title="Xerophyte">xerophytes</a></i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations_to_dry_conditions">Adaptations to dry conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drought_tolerance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Adaptations to dry conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Xerophyte" title="Xerophyte">Xerophyte</a></div>
<p>Drought tolerant plants typically make use of either <a href="/wiki/C4_carbon_fixation" title="C4 carbon fixation">C4 carbon fixation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Crassulacean_acid_metabolism" title="Crassulacean acid metabolism">crassulacean acid metabolism</a> (CAM) to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_fixation" title="Carbon fixation">fix carbon</a> during <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>. Both are improvements over the more common but more <a href="/wiki/Basal_(phylogenetics)" title="Basal (phylogenetics)">basal</a> <a href="/wiki/C3_carbon_fixation" title="C3 carbon fixation">C3</a> pathway in that they are more energy efficient. CAM is particularly good for arid conditions because <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> can be taken up at night, allowing the <a href="/wiki/Stoma" title="Stoma">stomata</a> to stay closed during the heat of the day and thus reducing water loss.<sup id="cite_ref-botany_online_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-botany_online-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many adaptations for dry conditions are structural, including the following:
</p>
<ul><li>Adaptations of the <a href="/wiki/Stoma" title="Stoma">stomata</a> to reduce water loss, such as reduced numbers or waxy surfaces.</li>
<li>Water storage in <a href="/wiki/Succulent_plant" title="Succulent plant">succulent</a> above-ground parts or water-filled <a href="/wiki/Tuber" title="Tuber">tubers</a>.</li>
<li>Adaptations in the <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">root</a> system to increase <a href="/wiki/Water_absorption" class="mw-redirect" title="Water absorption">water absorption</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Trichome" title="Trichome">Trichomes</a> (small hairs) on the <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> to absorb atmospheric water.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance_for_agriculture">Importance for agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drought_tolerance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Importance for agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arid conditions can lower the <a href="/wiki/Crop_yield" title="Crop yield">yield</a> of many <a href="/wiki/Crops" class="mw-redirect" title="Crops">crops</a>. <a href="/wiki/Plant_breeding" title="Plant breeding">Plant breeding</a> programs for improved yield during drought conditions have great economic importance, and these programs may be broad in scope. For example, one study on <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybeans</a> currently being conducted by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">United States Department of Agriculture</a> is scheduled to span several years, with research taking place across that country, and has among its goals the identification of specific mechanism by which soybeans resist <a href="/wiki/Wilting" title="Wilting">wilting</a> and of the specific <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a> for drought tolerance.<sup id="cite_ref-usda_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usda-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance_in_horticulture">Importance in horticulture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drought_tolerance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Importance in horticulture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In landscapes in arid or drought-prone regions, drought tolerance is an important consideration in plant selection. <a href="/wiki/Xeriscaping" title="Xeriscaping">Xeriscaping</a> is an approach to landscaping first developed in <a href="/wiki/Denver,_Colorado" class="mw-redirect" title="Denver, Colorado">Denver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, a region with hot, dry summers. The use of drought tolerant plants is essential to a successful xeriscape, which ideally requires no supplemental <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>.
</p>
