<p><b>Hafnium(IV) oxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> <a href="/wiki/Hafnium" title="Hafnium">Hf</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>2</sub>.  Also known as <b>hafnia</b>, this colourless solid is one of the most common and stable compounds of <a href="/wiki/Hafnium" title="Hafnium">hafnium</a>.  It is an electrical insulator with a <a href="/wiki/Band_gap" title="Band gap">band gap</a> of 5.3~5.7 <a href="/wiki/Electron_volt" class="mw-redirect" title="Electron volt">eV</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Hafnium dioxide is an intermediate in some processes that give hafnium metal.
</p><p>Hafnium(IV) oxide is quite inert.  It reacts with strong <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acids</a> such as concentrated <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> and with strong <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">bases</a>.  It dissolves slowly in <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a> to give fluorohafnate anions.  At elevated temperatures, it reacts with <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a> to give <a href="/wiki/Hafnium_tetrachloride" title="Hafnium tetrachloride">hafnium tetrachloride</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hafnium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hafnia adopts the same structure as zirconia (ZrO<sub>2</sub>).  Unlike TiO<sub>2</sub>, which features six-coordinate Ti in all phases, zirconia and hafnia consists of seven-coordinate metal centres. A variety of crystalline phases have been experimentally observed, including cubic (Fm-3m), tetragonal (P4<sub>2</sub>/nmc), monoclinic (P2<sub>1</sub>/c) and orthorhombic (Pbca and Pnma).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is also known that hafnia may adopt two other orthorhombic metastable phases (space group Pca2<sub>1</sub> and Pmn2<sub>1</sub>) over a wide range of pressures and temperatures,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> presumably being the sources of the ferroelectricity recently observed in thin films of hafnia.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Thin films of hafnium oxides, used in modern semiconductor devices, are often deposited with an amorphous structure (commonly by <a href="/wiki/Atomic_layer_deposition" title="Atomic layer deposition">atomic layer deposition</a>). Possible benefits of the amorphous structure have led researchers to alloy hafnium oxide with silicon (forming <a href="/wiki/Hafnium_silicate" class="mw-redirect" title="Hafnium silicate">hafnium silicates</a>) or aluminium, which were found to increase the crystallization temperature of hafnium oxide.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hafnium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hafnia is used in <a href="/wiki/Optical_coating" title="Optical coating">optical coatings</a>, and as a <a href="/wiki/High-%CE%BA_dielectric" title="High-κ dielectric">high-κ dielectric</a> in <a href="/wiki/DRAM" class="mw-redirect" title="DRAM">DRAM</a> capacitors and in advanced <a href="/wiki/Metal-oxide-semiconductor" class="mw-redirect" title="Metal-oxide-semiconductor">metal-oxide-semiconductor</a> devices.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Hafnium-based oxides were introduced by <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">Intel</a> in 2007 as a replacement for <a href="/wiki/Silicon_oxide" title="Silicon oxide">silicon oxide</a> as a gate insulator in <a href="/wiki/Field-effect_transistors" class="mw-redirect" title="Field-effect transistors">field-effect transistors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The advantage for transistors is its high <a href="/wiki/Dielectric_constant" class="mw-redirect" title="Dielectric constant">dielectric constant</a>: the dielectric constant of HfO<sub>2</sub> is 4–6 times higher than that of SiO<sub>2</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The dielectric constant and other properties depend on the deposition method, composition and microstructure of the material.
</p><p>In recent years, hafnium oxide (as well as doped and oxygen-deficient hafnium oxide) attracts additional interest as a possible candidate for resistive-switching memories<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and CMOS-compatible ferroelectric field effect transistors and memory chips.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HAF1_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HAF1-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HFO1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HFO1-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Because of its very high melting point, hafnia is also used as a refractory material in the insulation of such devices as <a href="/wiki/Thermocouple" title="Thermocouple">thermocouples</a>, where it can operate at temperatures up to 2500&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Multilayered films of hafnium dioxide, silica, and other materials have been developed for use in passive cooling of buildings.  The films reflect sunlight and radiate heat at wavelengths that pass through Earth's atmosphere, and can have temperatures several degrees cooler than surrounding materials under the same conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
