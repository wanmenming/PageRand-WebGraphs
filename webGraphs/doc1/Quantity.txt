<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Quantity</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p><p><b>Quantity</b> is a property that can exist as a <a href="/wiki/Counting" title="Counting">multitude</a> or <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_(mathematics)" title="Magnitude (mathematics)">magnitude</a>. Quantities can be compared in terms of "more", "less", or "equal", or by assigning a numerical value in terms of a unit of measurement. Quantity is among the basic <a href="/wiki/Class_(philosophy)" title="Class (philosophy)">classes</a> of things along with <a href="/wiki/Quality_(philosophy)" title="Quality (philosophy)">quality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Substance_theory" title="Substance theory">substance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Change_(mathematics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Change (mathematics)">change</a>, and relation. Some quantities are such by their inner nature (as number), while others are functioning as states (properties, dimensions, attributes) of things such as heavy and light, long and short, broad and narrow, small and great, or much and little. 
</p><p>Two basic divisions of quantity, magnitude and multitude, imply the principal distinction between continuity (<a href="/wiki/Continuum_(theory)" class="mw-redirect" title="Continuum (theory)">continuum</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Discontinuity_(mathematics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Discontinuity (mathematics)">discontinuity</a>.
</p><p>Under the name of multitude comes what is discontinuous and discrete and divisible ultimately into indivisibles, such as: <i>army, fleet, flock, government, company, party, people, mess (military), chorus, crowd</i>, and <i>number</i>; all which are cases of <a href="/wiki/Collective_nouns" class="mw-redirect" title="Collective nouns">collective nouns</a>. Under the name of magnitude comes what is continuous and unified and divisible only into smaller divisibles, such as: <i>matter, mass, energy, liquid, material</i>—all cases of non-collective nouns.
</p><p>Along with analyzing its nature and classification, the issues of quantity involve such closely related topics as the relation of magnitudes and multitudes, dimensionality, equality, proportion, the measurements of quantities, the units of measurements, number and numbering systems, the types of numbers and their relations to each other as numerical ratios.
</p><p>Thus quantity is a property that exists in a range of magnitudes or multitudes. <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">Mass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a>, <a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">distance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>, and angular separation are among the familiar examples of quantitative properties.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span></h2>
<p>In mathematics, the concept of quantity is an ancient one extending back to the time of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> and earlier. Aristotle regarded quantity as a fundamental ontological and scientific category. In Aristotle's <a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">ontology</a>, quantity or quantum was classified into two different types, which he characterized as follows:
</p>
<dl><dd>'Quantum' means that which is divisible into two or more constituent parts, of which each is by nature a 'one' and a 'this'. A quantum is a plurality if it is numerable, a magnitude if it is measurable. 'Plurality' means that which is divisible potentially into non-continuous parts, magnitude that which is divisible into continuous parts; of magnitude, that which is continuous in one dimension is length; in two breadth, in three depth. Of these, limited plurality is number, limited length is a line, breadth a surface, depth a solid. (Aristotle, book v, chapters 11-14, Metaphysics).</dd></dl>
<p>In his <a href="/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements" title="Euclid&#39;s Elements"><i>Elements</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/Euclid" title="Euclid">Euclid</a> developed the theory of ratios of magnitudes without studying the nature of magnitudes, as Archimedes, but giving the following significant definitions:
</p>
<dl><dd>A magnitude is a <i>part</i> of a magnitude, the less of the greater, when it measures the greater; A <i>ratio</i> is a sort of relation in respect of size between two magnitudes of the same kind.</dd></dl>
<p>For Aristotle and Euclid, relations were conceived as <a href="/wiki/Integer" title="Integer">whole numbers</a> (Michell, 1993). <a href="/wiki/John_Wallis" title="John Wallis">John Wallis</a> later conceived of ratios of magnitudes as <a href="/wiki/Real_numbers" class="mw-redirect" title="Real numbers">real numbers</a> as reflected in the following:
</p>
<dl><dd>When a comparison in terms of ratio is made, the resultant ratio often [namely with the exception of the 'numerical genus' itself] leaves the genus of quantities compared, and passes into the numerical genus, whatever the genus of quantities compared may have been. (John Wallis, <i>Mathesis Universalis</i>)</dd></dl>
<p>That is, the ratio of magnitudes of any quantity, whether volume, mass, heat and so on, is a number. Following this, <a href="/wiki/Sir_Isaac_Newton" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Isaac Newton">Newton</a> then defined number, and the relationship between quantity and number, in the following terms: "By <i>number</i> we understand not so much a multitude of unities, as the abstracted ratio of any quantity to another quantity of the same kind, which we take for unity" (Newton, 1728).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantitative_structure">Quantitative structure</span></h2>
<p>Continuous quantities possess a particular structure that was first explicitly characterized by <a href="/wiki/Otto_H%C3%B6lder" title="Otto Hölder">Hölder</a> (1901) as a set of axioms that define such features as <i>identities</i> and <i>relations</i> between magnitudes. In science, quantitative structure is the subject of <a href="/wiki/Empirical_research" title="Empirical research">empirical investigation</a> and cannot be assumed to exist <i><a href="/wiki/A_priori_and_a_posteriori" title="A priori and a posteriori">a priori</a></i> for any given property. The linear <a href="/wiki/Continuum_(theory)" class="mw-redirect" title="Continuum (theory)">continuum</a> represents the prototype of continuous quantitative structure as characterized by Hölder (1901) (translated in Michell &amp; Ernst, 1996). A fundamental feature of any type of quantity is that the relationships of equality or inequality can in principle be stated in comparisons between particular magnitudes, unlike quality, which is marked by likeness, similarity and difference, diversity. Another fundamental feature is additivity. Additivity may involve concatenation, such as adding two lengths A and B to obtain a third A + B. Additivity is not, however, restricted to extensive quantities but may also entail relations between magnitudes that can be established through experiments that permit tests of hypothesized <a href="/wiki/Observable" title="Observable">observable</a> manifestations of the additive relations of magnitudes. Another feature is continuity, on which Michell (1999, p.&#160;51) says of length, as a type of quantitative attribute, "what continuity means is that if any arbitrary length, a, is selected as a unit, then for every positive real number, <i>r</i>, there is a length b such that b = <i>r</i>a". A further generalization is given by the <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_conjoint_measurement" title="Theory of conjoint measurement">theory of conjoint measurement</a>, independently developed by French economist <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_Debreu" title="Gérard Debreu">Gérard Debreu</a> (1960) and by the American mathematical psychologist <a href="/wiki/R._Duncan_Luce" title="R. Duncan Luce">R. Duncan Luce</a> and statistician <a href="/wiki/John_Tukey" title="John Tukey">John Tukey</a> (1964).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantity_in_mathematics">Quantity in mathematics</span></h2>

<p>Magnitude (how much) and multitude (how many), the two principal types of quantities, are further divided as mathematical and physical. In formal terms, quantities—their ratios, proportions, order and formal relationships of equality and inequality—are studied by mathematics. The essential part of mathematical quantities consists of having a collection of <a href="/wiki/Variable_(mathematics)" title="Variable (mathematics)">variables</a>, each assuming a <a href="/wiki/Set_(mathematics)" title="Set (mathematics)">set</a> of values. These can be a set of a single quantity, referred to as a <a href="/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics)" title="Scalar (mathematics)">scalar</a> when represented by real numbers, or have multiple quantities as do <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_vector" title="Euclidean vector">vectors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tensor" title="Tensor">tensors</a>, two kinds of geometric objects.
</p><p>The mathematical usage of a quantity can then be varied and so is situationally dependent. Quantities can be used as being <a href="/wiki/Infinitesimal" title="Infinitesimal">infinitesimal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argument_of_a_function" title="Argument of a function">arguments of a function</a>, variables in an <a href="/wiki/Expression_(mathematics)" title="Expression (mathematics)">expression</a> (independent or dependent), or probabilistic as in random and <a href="/wiki/Stochastic" title="Stochastic">stochastic</a> quantities. In mathematics, magnitudes and multitudes are also not only two distinct kinds of quantity but furthermore relatable to each other.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">Number theory</a> covers the topics of the <a href="/wiki/Continuous_and_discrete_variables" class="mw-redirect" title="Continuous and discrete variables">discrete quantities</a> as numbers: number systems with their kinds and relations. <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">Geometry</a> studies the issues of spatial magnitudes: straight lines, curved lines, surfaces and solids, all with their respective measurements and relationships.
</p><p>A traditional <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_mathematics" title="Philosophy of mathematics">philosophy of mathematics</a>, stemming from <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> and remaining popular until the eighteenth century, held that mathematics is the "science of quantity". Quantity was considered to be divided into the discrete (studied by arithmetic) and the continuous (studied by geometry and later <a href="/wiki/Calculus" title="Calculus">calculus</a>). The theory fits reasonably well elementary or school mathematics but less well the abstract topological and algebraic structures of modern mathematics.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantity_in_physical_science">Quantity in physical science</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Physical_quantity" title="Physical quantity">Physical quantity</a></div>
<p>Establishing quantitative structure and relationships <i>between</i> different quantities is the cornerstone of modern physical sciences. Physics is fundamentally a quantitative science. Its progress is chiefly achieved due to rendering the abstract qualities of material entities into physical quantities, by postulating that all material bodies marked by quantitative properties or physical dimensions are subject to some measurements and observations. Setting the units of measurement, physics covers such fundamental quantities as space (length, breadth, and depth) and time, mass and force, temperature, energy, and <a href="/wiki/Quantum" title="Quantum">quanta</a>.
</p><p>A distinction has also been made between <a href="/wiki/Intensive_quantity" class="mw-redirect" title="Intensive quantity">intensive quantity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Extensive_quantity" class="mw-redirect" title="Extensive quantity">extensive quantity</a> as two types of quantitative property, state or relation. The magnitude of an <i>intensive quantity</i> does not depend on the size, or extent, of the object or system of which the quantity is a property, whereas magnitudes of an <i>extensive quantity</i> are additive for parts of an entity or subsystems. Thus, magnitude does depend on the extent of the entity or system in the case of extensive quantity. Examples of intensive quantities are <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>, while examples of extensive quantities are <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">volume</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quantity_in_natural_language">Quantity in natural language</span></h2>
<p>In human languages, including <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">number</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Syntactic_category" title="Syntactic category">syntactic category</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Person" title="Person">person</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gender" title="Gender">gender</a>. The quantity is expressed by identifiers, definite and indefinite, and quantifiers, definite and indefinite, as well as by three types of <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">nouns</a>: 1. count unit nouns or countables; 2. <a href="/wiki/Mass_nouns" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass nouns">mass nouns</a>, uncountables, referring to the indefinite, unidentified amounts; 3. nouns of multitude (<a href="/wiki/Collective_noun" title="Collective noun">collective nouns</a>). The word ‘number’ belongs to a noun of multitude standing either for a single entity or for the individuals making the whole. An amount in general is expressed by a special class of words called identifiers, indefinite and definite and quantifiers, definite and indefinite.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The amount may be expressed by: singular form and plural from, ordinal numbers before a count noun singular (first, second, third...), the demonstratives; definite and indefinite numbers and measurements (hundred/hundreds, million/millions), or cardinal numbers before count nouns. The set of language quantifiers covers "a few, a great number, many, several (for count names); a bit of, a little, less, a great deal (amount) of, much (for mass names); all, plenty of, a lot of, enough, more, most, some, any, both, each, either, neither, every, no". For the complex case of unidentified amounts, the parts and examples of a mass are indicated with respect to the following: a measure of a mass (two kilos of rice and twenty bottles of milk or ten pieces of paper); a piece or part of a mass (part, element, atom, item, article, drop); or a shape of a container (a basket, box, case, cup, bottle, vessel, jar).
</p>
