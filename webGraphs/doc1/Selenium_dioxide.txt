<p><b>Selenium dioxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> SeO<sub>2</sub>. This colorless solid is one of the most frequently encountered compounds of <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Selenium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Solid SeO<sub>2</sub> is a one-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Polymer" title="Polymer">polymer</a>, the chain consisting of alternating selenium and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> atoms.  Each Se atom is pyramidal and bears a terminal oxide group. The bridging Se-O bond lengths are 179 pm and the terminal Se-O distance is 162 pm.<sup id="cite_ref-Devillanova_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Devillanova-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  The relative <a href="/wiki/Stereochemistry" title="Stereochemistry">stereochemistry</a> at Se alternates along the polymer chain (<a href="/wiki/Syndiotactic" class="mw-redirect" title="Syndiotactic">syndiotactic</a>). 
In the gas phase selenium dioxide is present as dimers and other oligomeric species, at higher temperatures it is monomeric.<sup id="cite_ref-Holl&amp;Wib_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holl&amp;Wib-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The monomeric form adopts a bent structure very similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> with a bond length of 161 pm.<sup id="cite_ref-Holl&amp;Wib_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holl&amp;Wib-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The dimeric form has been isolated in a low temperature argon matrix and vibrational spectra indicate that it has a centrosymmetric chair form.<sup id="cite_ref-Devillanova_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Devillanova-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Dissolution of SeO<sub>2</sub> in <a href="/wiki/Selenium_oxydichloride" title="Selenium oxydichloride">selenium oxydichloride</a> give the trimer [Se(O)O]<sub>3</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-Holl&amp;Wib_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holl&amp;Wib-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> 
Monomeric SeO<sub>2</sub> is a polar molecule, with the dipole moment of 2.62 D <sup id="cite_ref-TakeoHirota1972_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TakeoHirota1972-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  pointed from the midpoint of the two oxygen atoms to the selenium atom.
</p><p>The solid sublimes readily. At very low concentrations the vapour has a revolting odour, resembling decayed horseradishes. At higher concentrations the vapour has an odour resembling horseradish sauce and can burn the nose and throat on inhalation.  Whereas SO<sub>2</sub> tends to be molecular and SeO<sub>2</sub> is a one-dimensional chain, <a href="/wiki/Tellurium_dioxide" title="Tellurium dioxide">TeO<sub>2</sub></a> is a cross-linked polymer.<sup id="cite_ref-Devillanova_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Devillanova-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>SeO<sub>2</sub> is considered an <a href="/wiki/Acidic_oxide" title="Acidic oxide">acidic oxide</a>: it dissolves in water to form <a href="/wiki/Selenous_acid" title="Selenous acid">selenous acid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Holl&amp;Wib_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holl&amp;Wib-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Often the terms <i><a href="/wiki/Selenous_acid" title="Selenous acid">selenous acid</a></i> and <i>selenium dioxide</i> are used interchangeably. It reacts with base to form <a href="/wiki/Selenite_(ion)" title="Selenite (ion)">selenite</a> <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a> containing the <span class="chemf nowrap">SeO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> anion. For example, reaction with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> produces <a href="/wiki/Sodium_selenite" title="Sodium selenite">sodium selenite</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>SeO<sub>2</sub> +  2 NaOH  →  Na<sub>2</sub>SeO<sub>3</sub>  +  H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Selenium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Selenium dioxide is prepared by oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a> by burning in air or by reaction with nitric acid or <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a>, but perhaps the most convenient preparation is by the dehydration of <a href="/wiki/Selenous_acid" title="Selenous acid">selenous acid</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd>3 Se + 4 HNO<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O  → 3 H<sub>2</sub>SeO<sub>3</sub> + 4 NO</dd>
<dd>2 H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> + Se  → SeO<sub>2</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
<dd>H<sub>2</sub>SeO<sub>3</sub> ⇌ SeO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Selenium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The natural form of selenium dioxide, downeyite, is a very rare mineral. It is found in only a very few burning coal dumps.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Selenium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_synthesis">Organic synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Selenium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Organic synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>SeO<sub>2</sub> is an important reagent in <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a>.  Oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Paraldehyde" title="Paraldehyde">paraldehyde</a> (acetaldehyde trimer) with SeO<sub>2</sub> gives <a href="/wiki/Glyoxal" title="Glyoxal">glyoxal</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and the oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Cyclohexanone" title="Cyclohexanone">cyclohexanone</a> gives cyclohexane-1,2-dione.<sup id="cite_ref-Hachorgsyn_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hachorgsyn-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The selenium starting material is <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduced</a> to selenium, and precipitates as a red <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_solid" title="Amorphous solid">amorphous solid</a> which can easily be filtered off.<sup id="cite_ref-Hachorgsyn_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hachorgsyn-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This type of reaction is called a <a href="/wiki/Riley_oxidation" title="Riley oxidation">Riley oxidation</a>. It is also renowned as a reagent for "allylic" oxidation,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> a reaction that entails the following conversion
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a href="/wiki/File:Seleniumdioxide_oxidation.svg" class="image" title="Allylic oxidation"><img alt="Allylic oxidation" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Seleniumdioxide_oxidation.svg/300px-Seleniumdioxide_oxidation.svg.png" width="300" height="183" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Seleniumdioxide_oxidation.svg/450px-Seleniumdioxide_oxidation.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Seleniumdioxide_oxidation.svg/600px-Seleniumdioxide_oxidation.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="2470" data-file-height="1503" /></a></div></div>
<p>This can be described more generally as;
</p>
<dl><dd>R<sub>2</sub>C=CR'-CHR"<sub>2</sub>  +  [O] →  R<sub>2</sub>C=CR'-C(OH)R"<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>where R, R', R" may be <a href="/wiki/Alkyl" title="Alkyl">alkyl</a> or <a href="/wiki/Aryl" title="Aryl">aryl</a> substituents.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_colorant">As a colorant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Selenium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: As a colorant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Selenium dioxide imparts a red colour to <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>. It is used in small quantities to counteract the colour due to <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> impurities and so to create (apparently) colourless glass. In larger quantities, it gives a deep ruby red colour.
</p><p>Selenium dioxide is the active ingredient in some <a href="/wiki/Bluing_(steel)" title="Bluing (steel)">cold-blueing</a> solutions.
</p><p>It was also used as a toner in <a href="/wiki/Photographic_developing" class="mw-redirect" title="Photographic developing">photographic developing</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Selenium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

</p>
