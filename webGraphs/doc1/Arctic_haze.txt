<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Arctic haze</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Contamination_pathways_large.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Contamination_pathways_large.jpg/250px-Contamination_pathways_large.jpg" width="250" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Contamination_pathways_large.jpg/375px-Contamination_pathways_large.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Contamination_pathways_large.jpg/500px-Contamination_pathways_large.jpg 2x" data-file-width="669" data-file-height="399" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Contamination_pathways_large.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Long-range pollution pathways to the Arctic</div></div></div>
<p><b>Arctic haze</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Phenomenon" title="Phenomenon">phenomenon</a> of a visible reddish-brown springtime <a href="/wiki/Haze" title="Haze">haze</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> at high <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitudes</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Anthropogenic_hazard" title="Anthropogenic hazard">anthropogenic</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>. A major distinguishing factor of Arctic haze is the ability of its <a href="/wiki/Chemical" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical">chemical</a> ingredients to persist in the atmosphere for an extended period of time compared to other pollutants. Due to limited amounts of <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>, or turbulent air to displace pollutants from the polar air mass in spring, Arctic haze can linger for more than a month in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">northern</a> atmosphere.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_haze&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arctic haze was first noticed in 1750 when the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> began. Explorers and whalers could not figure out where the foggy layer was coming from. "<i>Poo-jok</i>" was the term the <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> used for it.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Another hint towards clarifying this issue was relayed in notes approximately a century ago by Norwegian explorer <a href="/wiki/Fridtjof_Nansen" title="Fridtjof Nansen">Fridtjof Nansen</a>. After trekking through the Arctic he found dark stains on the ice.<sup id="cite_ref-findarticles.com_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-findarticles.com-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The term "Arctic haze" was coined in 1956 by <a href="/wiki/J._Murray_Mitchell" title="J. Murray Mitchell">J. Murray Mitchell</a>, a <a href="/wiki/US_Air_Force" class="mw-redirect" title="US Air Force">US Air Force</a> officer stationed in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> to describe an unusual reduction in visibility observed by North American weather reconnaissance planes. From his investigations, Mitchell thought the haze had come from industrial areas in Europe and China. He went on to become an eminent <a href="/wiki/Climatologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Climatologist">climatologist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-www.nytimes.com_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www.nytimes.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
The haze is seasonal, reaching a peak in late winter and spring. When an aircraft is within a layer of Arctic haze, pilots report that horizontal visibility can drop to one tenth that of normally clear sky. At this time it was unknown whether the haze was natural or was formed by pollutants.
</p><p>In 1972, Glenn Shaw of the <a href="/wiki/Geophysical_Institute" title="Geophysical Institute">Geophysical Institute</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alaska" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Alaska">University of Alaska</a> attributed this smog to transboundary anthropogenic pollution, whereby the Arctic is the recipient of contaminants whose sources are thousands of miles away. Further research continues with the aim of understanding the impact of this pollution on <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_pollutants">Origin of pollutants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_haze&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin of pollutants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The pollutants are commonly thought to originate from coal-burning in northern mid-latitudes, especially in Asia. The <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosols</a> contain about 90% <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> and the rest is <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, which makes the haze reddish in color. This pollution is helping the Arctic warm up faster than any other region, although increases in <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> are the main driver of this <a href="/wiki/Climatic_change" class="mw-redirect" title="Climatic change">climatic change</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sulfur aerosols in the atmosphere affect cloud formation, leading to localized cooling effects over industrialized regions due to increased <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">reflection of sunlight</a>, which masks the opposite effect of trapped warmth beneath the cloud cover.  During the Arctic winter, however, there is no sunlight to reflect.  In the absence of this cooling effect, the dominant effect of changes to Arctic clouds is an increased trapping of <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> radiation from the surface.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ship_pollution" class="mw-redirect" title="Ship pollution">Ship emissions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> pollutants are many examples of the pollution that is affecting this atmosphere, but the <a href="/wiki/Smoke" title="Smoke">smoke</a> from <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">forest fires</a> is not a significant contributor.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Some of those pollutants figure among <a href="/wiki/Environmental_effects_of_coal#Burning" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental effects of coal">environmental effects of coal burning</a>. Due to low deposition rates, these pollutants are not yet having adverse effects on people or animals. Different pollutants actually represent different colors of haze. Dr. Shaw discovered in 1976 that the yellowish haze is from <a href="/wiki/Dust_storm" title="Dust storm">dust storms</a> in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>. The particles were carried polwards by unusual <a href="/wiki/Air_current" title="Air current">air currents</a>. The trapped particles were dark gray the next year he took a sample. That was caused by a heavy amount of industrial pollutants.<sup id="cite_ref-findarticles.com_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-findarticles.com-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Home fires in India also contribute.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_studies">Recent studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_haze&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Recent studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Tim Garrett, an assistant professor of meteorology at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Utah" title="University of Utah">University of Utah</a> involved in the study of Arctic haze at the university, mid-latitude cities contribute pollution to the Arctic, and it mixes with thin clouds, allowing them to trap heat more easily. Garret's study found that during the dark <a href="/wiki/Polar_night" title="Polar night">Arctic winter</a>, when there is no precipitation to wash out pollution, the effects are strongest, because pollutants can warm the environment up to three degrees Fahrenheit.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_predictions">Scientific predictions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_haze&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Scientific predictions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>European climatologists predicted in 2009 that by the end of the 21st century, the temperature of the Arctic region is expected to rise 3° <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">Celsius</a> on an average day.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In that same article, <i><a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic</a></i> quoted the co-author of the study, Andreas Stohl, of the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Institute_for_Air_Research" title="Norwegian Institute for Air Research">Norwegian Institute for Air Research</a>, "Previous climate models have suggested that the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice" class="mw-redirect" title="Arctic sea ice">Arctic's summer sea ice</a> may completely disappear by 2040 if warming continues unabated."
</p>
