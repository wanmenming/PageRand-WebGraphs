<p><b>Desalination</b> is a process that takes away mineral components from <a href="/wiki/Saline_water" title="Saline water">saline water</a>. More generally, desalination refers to the removal of salts and minerals from a target substance,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> as in <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity_control" title="Soil salinity control">soil desalination</a>, which is an issue for agriculture.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Saline_water" title="Saline water">Saltwater</a> is desalinated to produce water suitable for <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">human consumption</a> or <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>. One <a href="/wiki/By-product" title="By-product">by-product</a> of desalination is <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">salt</a>. Desalination is used on many seagoing <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ships</a> and <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarines</a>. Most of the modern interest in desalination is focused on cost-effective provision of <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a> for human use. Along with recycled <a href="/wiki/Wastewater" title="Wastewater">wastewater</a>, it is one of the few rainfall-independent water sources.<sup id="cite_ref-Fischetti2007_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fischetti2007-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to its energy consumption, desalinating sea water is generally more costly than fresh water from rivers or <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_recycling" class="mw-redirect" title="Water recycling">water recycling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_conservation" title="Water conservation">water conservation</a>. However, these alternatives are not always available and depletion of reserves is a critical problem worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Currently, approximately 1% of the world's population is dependent on desalinated water to meet daily needs, but the UN expects that 14% of the world's population will encounter <a href="/wiki/Water_scarcity" title="Water scarcity">water scarcity</a> by 2025.<sup id="cite_ref-gwi_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gwi-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Desalination is particularly relevant in dry countries such as <a href="/wiki/Seawater_desalination_in_Australia" title="Seawater desalination in Australia">Australia</a>, which traditionally have relied on collecting rainfall behind dams for water.
</p><p>According to the International Desalination Association, in June 2015, 18,426 desalination plants operated worldwide, producing 86.8 million cubic meters per day, providing water for 300 million people.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> This number increased from 78.4 million cubic meters in 2013,<sup id="cite_ref-gwi_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gwi-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> a 10.71% increase in 2 years. The single largest desalination project is <a href="/wiki/Ras_Al-Khair_Power_and_Desalination_Plant" title="Ras Al-Khair Power and Desalination Plant">Ras Al-Khair</a> in <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, which produced 1,025,000 cubic meters per day in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-gwi_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gwi-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a> produces a higher proportion of its water than any other country, totaling 100% of its water use.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods">Methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Reverse_osmosis_desalination_plant.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Reverse_osmosis_desalination_plant.JPG/220px-Reverse_osmosis_desalination_plant.JPG" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Reverse_osmosis_desalination_plant.JPG/330px-Reverse_osmosis_desalination_plant.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Reverse_osmosis_desalination_plant.JPG/440px-Reverse_osmosis_desalination_plant.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2112" data-file-height="2816" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Reverse_osmosis_desalination_plant.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reverse osmosis desalination plant in Barcelona, Spain</div></div></div>
<p>There are several methods. Each has advantages and disadvantages.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vacuum_distillation">Vacuum distillation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vacuum distillation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The traditional process used in these operations is <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_distillation" title="Vacuum distillation">vacuum distillation</a>—essentially boiling it to leave impurities behind. In desalination, atmospheric pressure is reduced, thus lowering the required temperature needed. Liquids boil when the <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">vapor pressure</a> equals the ambient pressure and vapor pressure increases with temperature. Effectively, liquids boil at a lower temperature, when the ambient atmospheric pressure is less than usual atmospheric pressure. Thus, because of the reduced pressure, low-temperature "waste" heat from electrical power generation or industrial processes can be employed.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multi-stage_flash_distillation">Multi-stage flash distillation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Multi-stage flash distillation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Water is evaporated and separated from sea water through <a href="/wiki/Multi-stage_flash_distillation" title="Multi-stage flash distillation">multi-stage flash distillation</a>, which is a series of <a href="/wiki/Flash_evaporation" title="Flash evaporation">flash evaporations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Khawaji_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Khawaji-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Each subsequent flash process utilizes energy released from the condensation of the water vapor from the previous step.<sup id="cite_ref-Khawaji_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Khawaji-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multiple-effect_distillation">Multiple-effect distillation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Multiple-effect distillation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Multiple-effect_distillation" title="Multiple-effect distillation">Multiple-effect distillation</a> (MED) works through a series of steps called "effects".<sup id="cite_ref-Khawaji_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Khawaji-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Incoming water is sprayed onto pipes which are then heated to generate steam. The steam is then used to heat the next batch of incoming sea water.<sup id="cite_ref-Khawaji_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Khawaji-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  To increase efficiency, the steam used to heat the sea water can be taken from nearby power plants.<sup id="cite_ref-Khawaji_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Khawaji-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Although this method is the most thermodynamically efficient among methods powered by heat,<sup id="cite_ref-WarsingerEntropy_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WarsingerEntropy-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> a few limitations exist such as a max temperature and max number of effects.<sup id="cite_ref-Al-Shammiri_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Shammiri-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vapor-compression_distillation">Vapor-compression distillation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vapor-compression distillation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vapor-compression_evaporation" title="Vapor-compression evaporation">Vapor-compression evaporation</a> involves using either a mechanical compressor or a jet stream to compress the vapor present above the liquid.<sup id="cite_ref-WarsingerEntropy_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WarsingerEntropy-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  The compressed vapor is then used to provide the heat needed for the evaporation of the rest of the sea water.<sup id="cite_ref-Khawaji_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Khawaji-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Since this system only requires power, it is more cost effective if kept at a small scale.<sup id="cite_ref-Khawaji_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Khawaji-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reverse_osmosis">Reverse osmosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reverse osmosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The leading process for desalination in terms of installed capacity and yearly growth is <a href="/wiki/Reverse_osmosis" title="Reverse osmosis">reverse osmosis (RO)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The RO membrane processes use semipermeable membranes and applied  pressure (on the membrane feed side) to preferentially induce water permeation through the membrane while rejecting salts. <a href="/wiki/Reverse_osmosis_plant" title="Reverse osmosis plant">Reverse osmosis plant</a> membrane systems typically use less energy than thermal desalination processes.<sup id="cite_ref-WarsingerEntropy_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WarsingerEntropy-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Desalination processes are driven by either thermal (e.g., distillation) or electrical (e.g., RO) as the primary energy types. Energy cost in desalination processes varies considerably depending on water salinity, plant size and process type. At present the cost of seawater desalination, for example, is higher than traditional water sources, but it is expected that costs will continue to decrease with technology improvements that include, but are not limited to, improved efficiency,<sup id="cite_ref-WarsingerBatch_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WarsingerBatch-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> reduction in plants footprint, improvements to plant operation and optimization, more effective feed pretreatment, and lower cost energy sources.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Reverse osmosis uses a thin-film composite membrane, which comprises an ultra-thin, aromatic polyamide thin-film. This polyamide film gives the membrane its transport properties, whereas the remainder of the thin-film composite membrane provides mechanical support. The polyamide film is a dense, void-free polymer with a high surface area, allowing for its high water permeability.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Reverse Osmosis process is not maintenance free. Various factors interfere with efficiency: ionic contamination (calcium, magnesium etc.); <a href="/wiki/Dissolved_organic_carbon" title="Dissolved organic carbon">DOC</a>; bacteria; viruses; <a href="/wiki/Colloid" title="Colloid">colloids</a> &amp; insoluble particulates; <a href="/wiki/Biofouling" title="Biofouling">biofouling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fouling" title="Fouling">scaling</a>. In extreme cases the RO membranes are destroyed. To mitigate damage, various pretreatment stages are introduced.  Anti-scaling inhibitors include acids and other agents like the organic polymers <a href="/wiki/Acrylamide" title="Acrylamide">Polyacrylamide</a> and Polymaleic Acid, <a href="/wiki/Phosphonates" class="mw-redirect" title="Phosphonates">Phosphonates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polyphosphate" title="Polyphosphate">Polyphosphates</a>. Inhibitors for fouling are <a href="/wiki/Biocide" title="Biocide">biocides</a> (as oxidants against bacteria and viruses), like chlorine, ozone, sodium or calcium hypochlorite. At regular intervals, depending on the membrane contamination; fluctuating seawater conditions; or when prompted by monitoring processes, the membranes need to be cleaned, known as emergency or shock-flushing. Flushing is done with inhibitors in a fresh water solution and the system must go offline. This procedure is environmental risky, since contaminated water is diverted into the ocean without treatment. Sensitive <a href="/wiki/Marine_habitats" title="Marine habitats">marine habitats</a> can be irreversibly damaged.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Freeze-thaw">Freeze-thaw</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Freeze-thaw">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Freeze-thaw desalination uses freezing to remove fresh water from salt water.  Salt water is sprayed during freezing conditions into a pad where an ice-pile builds up. When seasonal conditions warm, naturally desalinated melt water is recovered. This technique relies on extended periods of natural sub-freezing conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A different freeze-thaw method, not weather dependent and invented by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Zarchin" title="Alexander Zarchin">Alexander Zarchin</a>, freezes seawater in a vacuum.  Under vacuum conditions the ice, desalinated, is melted and diverted for collection and the salt is collected.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_evaporation">Solar evaporation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Solar evaporation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Solar evaporation mimics the natural water cycle, in which the sun heats the sea water enough for evaporation to occur.<sup id="cite_ref-Khawaji_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Khawaji-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> After evaporation, the water vapor is condensed onto a cool surface.<sup id="cite_ref-Khawaji_8-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Khawaji-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrodialysis_reversal">Electrodialysis reversal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Electrodialysis reversal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Electrodialysis" title="Electrodialysis">Electrodialysis</a> utilizes electric potential to move the salts through pairs of charged membranes, which trap salt in alternating channels.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Membrane_distillation">Membrane distillation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Membrane distillation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Membrane_distillation" title="Membrane distillation">Membrane distillation</a> uses a temperature difference across a membrane to evaporate vapor from a salty brine solution and condense pure condensate on the colder side.<sup id="cite_ref-WarsingerFramework_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WarsingerFramework-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wave-powered_desalination">Wave-powered desalination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Wave-powered desalination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/CETO" title="CETO">CETO</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Wave_power" title="Wave power">wave power</a> technology that desalinates seawater using submerged buoys.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Wave-powered desalination plants began operating on <a href="/wiki/Garden_Island_(Western_Australia)" title="Garden Island (Western Australia)">Garden Island</a> in Western Australia in 2013<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Perth" title="Perth">Perth</a> in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Considerations_and_criticism">Considerations and criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Considerations and criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_consumption">Energy consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Energy consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Energy consumption of seawater desalination has reached as low as 3 kWh/m<sup>3</sup>,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> including pre-filtering and ancillaries, similar to the energy consumption of other fresh water supplies transported over large distances,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> but much higher than local fresh water supplies that use 0.2 kWh/m<sup>3</sup> or less.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A minimum energy consumption for seawater desalination of around 1 kWh/m<sup>3</sup> has been determined,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> excluding prefiltering and intake/outfall pumping. Under 2 kWh/m<sup>3</sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> has been achieved with <a href="/wiki/Reverse_osmosis" title="Reverse osmosis">reverse osmosis</a> membrane technology, leaving limited scope for further energy reductions.
</p><p>Supplying all US domestic water by desalination would increase domestic <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_the_United_States" title="Energy in the United States">energy consumption</a> by around 10%, about the amount of energy used by domestic refrigerators.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Domestic consumption is a relatively small fraction of the total water usage.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cogeneration">Cogeneration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cogeneration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">Cogeneration</a> is generating excess heat and electricity generation from a single process. Cogeneration can provide usable heat for desalination in an integrated, or "dual-purpose", facility where a power plant provides the energy for desalination. Alternatively, the facility's energy production may be dedicated to the production of potable water (a stand-alone facility), or excess energy may be produced and incorporated into the energy grid. Cogeneration takes various forms, and theoretically any form of energy production could be used. However, the majority of current and planned cogeneration desalination plants use either <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> as their source of energy. Most plants are located in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a> or <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, which use their petroleum resources to offset limited water resources. The advantage of dual-purpose facilities is they can be more efficient in energy consumption, thus making desalination more viable.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shevchenko_BN350_desalinati.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Shevchenko_BN350_desalinati.jpg/300px-Shevchenko_BN350_desalinati.jpg" width="300" height="208" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Shevchenko_BN350_desalinati.jpg/450px-Shevchenko_BN350_desalinati.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8b/Shevchenko_BN350_desalinati.jpg 2x" data-file-width="490" data-file-height="339" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shevchenko_BN350_desalinati.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Shevchenko BN350, a nuclear-heated desalination unit</div></div></div>
<p>The current trend in dual-purpose facilities is hybrid configurations, in which the permeate from reverse osmosis desalination is mixed with distillate from thermal desalination. Basically, two or more desalination processes are combined along with power production. Such facilities have been implemented in Saudi Arabia at <a href="/wiki/Jeddah" title="Jeddah">Jeddah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yanbu" title="Yanbu">Yanbu</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A typical <a href="/wiki/Supercarrier" class="mw-redirect" title="Supercarrier">Supercarrier</a> in the US military uses nuclear power to desalinate 1,500,000 L of water per day.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">Economics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Economics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Costs of desalinating sea water (infrastructure, energy, and maintenance) are generally higher than fresh water from rivers or <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_recycling" class="mw-redirect" title="Water recycling">water recycling</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Water_conservation" title="Water conservation">water conservation</a>, but alternatives are not always available. Desalination costs in 2013 ranged from US$0.45 to $1.00/cubic metre. More than half of the cost comes directly from energy cost, and since energy prices are very volatile, actual costs can vary substantially.<sup id="cite_ref-Zhang_2012_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zhang_2012-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The cost of untreated fresh water in the developing world can reach US$5/cubic metre.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Factors that determine the costs for desalination include capacity and type of facility, location, feed water, labor, energy, financing and concentrate disposal. Desalination <a href="/wiki/Still" title="Still">stills</a> control pressure, temperature and brine concentrations to optimize efficiency. <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">Nuclear-powered</a> desalination might be economical on a large scale.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While noting costs are falling, and generally positive about the technology for affluent areas in proximity to oceans, a 2004 study argued, "Desalinated water may be a solution for some water-stress regions, but not for places that are poor, deep in the interior of a continent, or at high elevation. Unfortunately, that includes some of the places with biggest water problems.", and, "Indeed, one needs to lift the water by 2000 m, or transport it over more than 1600 km to get transport costs equal to the desalination costs. Thus, it may be more economical to transport fresh water from somewhere else than to desalinate it. In places far from the sea, like <a href="/wiki/New_Delhi" title="New Delhi">New Delhi</a>, or in high places, like <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>, transport costs  could match desalination costs. Desalinated water is also expensive in places that are both somewhat far from the sea and somewhat high, such as <a href="/wiki/Riyadh" title="Riyadh">Riyadh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harare" title="Harare">Harare</a>. By contrast in other locations transport costs are much less, such as <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangkok" title="Bangkok">Bangkok</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zaragoza" title="Zaragoza">Zaragoza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">Phoenix</a>, and, of course, coastal cities like <a href="/wiki/Tripoli" title="Tripoli">Tripoli</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> After desalination at <a href="/wiki/Jubail" title="Jubail">Jubail</a>, Saudi Arabia, water is pumped 320&#160;km inland to <a href="/wiki/Riyadh" title="Riyadh">Riyadh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> For coastal cities, desalination is increasingly viewed as a competitive choice.
</p><p>In 2014, the Israeli facilities of Hadera, Palmahim, Ashkelon, and Sorek were desalinizing water for less than US$0.40 per cubic meter.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> As of 2006, Singapore was desalinating water for US$0.49 per cubic meter.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The city of <a href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Perth, Western Australia">Perth</a> began operating a reverse osmosis seawater desalination plant in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> A desalination plant now operates in <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Wonthaggi_desalination_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Wonthaggi desalination plant">Wonthaggi desalination plant</a> was under construction in <a href="/wiki/Wonthaggi,_Victoria" class="mw-redirect" title="Wonthaggi, Victoria">Wonthaggi, Victoria</a>.
</p><p>The Perth desalination plant is powered partially by renewable energy from the <a href="/wiki/Emu_Downs_Wind_Farm" title="Emu Downs Wind Farm">Emu Downs Wind Farm</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-npr_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-npr-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> A wind farm at <a href="/wiki/Bungendore" title="Bungendore">Bungendore</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> was purpose-built to generate enough <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> to offset the Sydney plant's energy use,<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> mitigating concerns about harmful <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions.
</p><p>In December 2007, the South Australian government announced it would build a seawater desalination plant for the city of Adelaide, Australia, located at <a href="/wiki/Lonsdale,_South_Australia" title="Lonsdale, South Australia">Port Stanvac</a>. The desalination plant was to be funded by raising water rates to achieve full cost recovery.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A January 17, 2008, article in the <i><a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Journal" class="mw-redirect" title="Wall Street Journal">Wall Street Journal</a></i> stated, "In November, Connecticut-based Poseidon Resources Corp. won a key regulatory approval to build the $300 million water-<a href="/wiki/Carlsbad_desalination_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Carlsbad desalination plant">desalination plant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Carlsbad,_California" title="Carlsbad, California">Carlsbad</a>, north of <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>. The facility would produce 190,000 cubic metres of drinking water per day, enough to supply about 100,000 homes.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> As of June 2012, the cost for the desalinated water had risen to $2,329 per acre-foot.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Each $1,000 per acre-foot works out to $3.06 for 1,000 gallons, or $.81 per cubic meter.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Poseidon Resources made an unsuccessful attempt to construct a desalination plant in Tampa Bay, FL, in 2001. The board of directors of <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay_Water" title="Tampa Bay Water">Tampa Bay Water</a> was forced to buy the plant from Poseidon in 2001 to prevent a third failure of the project. Tampa Bay Water faced five years of engineering problems and operation at 20% capacity to protect marine life. The facility reached capacity only in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-tampabaywater.org_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tampabaywater.org-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008, a <a href="/wiki/Energy_Recovery_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Energy Recovery Inc.">Energy Recovery Inc.</a> was desalinating water for $0.46 per cubic meter.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental">Environmental</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Environmental">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Factors that determine the costs for desalination include capacity and type of facility, location, feed water, labor, energy, financing and concentrate disposal.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Intake">Intake</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Intake">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, cooling water intake structures are regulated by the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA). These structures can have the same impacts to the environment as desalination facility intakes<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2017)">according to whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. According to EPA, water intake structures cause adverse environmental impact by sucking fish and shellfish or their eggs into an industrial system. There, the organisms may be killed or injured by heat, physical stress, or chemicals. Larger organisms may be killed or injured when they become trapped against screens at the front of an intake structure.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Alternative intake types that mitigate these impacts include beach wells, but they require more energy and higher costs.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Kwinana_Desalination_Plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Kwinana Desalination Plant">Kwinana Desalination Plant</a> opened in Perth in 2007. Water there and at Queensland's <a href="/wiki/Gold_Coast_Desalination_Plant" title="Gold Coast Desalination Plant">Gold Coast Desalination Plant</a> and Sydney's <a href="/wiki/Kurnell_Desalination_Plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Kurnell Desalination Plant">Kurnell Desalination Plant</a> is withdrawn at 0.1&#160;m/s (0.33&#160;ft/s), which is slow enough to let fish escape. The plant provides nearly 140,000&#160;m<sup>3</sup> (4,900,000&#160;cu&#160;ft) of clean water per day.<sup id="cite_ref-npr_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-npr-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Outflow">Outflow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Outflow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span>  <small><i>(January 2012)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Desalination processes produce large quantities of <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">brine</a>, possibly at above ambient temperature, and contain residues of pretreatment and cleaning chemicals, their reaction byproducts and heavy metals due to corrosion.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> Chemical pretreatment and cleaning are a necessity in most desalination plants, which typically includes prevention of biofouling, scaling, foaming and corrosion in thermal plants, and of biofouling, suspended solids and scale deposits in membrane plants.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>To limit the environmental impact of returning the brine to the ocean, it can be diluted with another stream of water entering the ocean, such as the outfall of a <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">wastewater treatment</a> or power plant. With medium to large power plant and desalination plants, the power plant's cooling water flow is likely to be several times larger than that of the desalination plant, reducing the salinity of the combination. Another method to dilute the brine is to mix it via a diffuser in a mixing zone. For example, once a pipeline containing the brine reaches the sea floor, it can split into many branches, each releasing brine gradually through small holes along its length. Mixing can be combined with power plant or wastewater plant dilution.
</p><p>Brine is denser than seawater and therefore sinks to the ocean bottom and can damage the ecosystem. Careful reintroduction can minimize this problem. Typical ocean conditions allow for rapid dilution, thereby minimizing harm.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives_without_pollution">Alternatives without pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Alternatives without pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some methods of desalination, particularly in combination with <a href="/wiki/Evaporation_pond" title="Evaporation pond">evaporation ponds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_still" title="Solar still">solar stills</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Condensation_trap" class="mw-redirect" title="Condensation trap">condensation trap</a> (<a href="/wiki/Solar_desalination" title="Solar desalination">solar desalination</a>), do not discharge brine. They do not use chemicals or burn fossil fuels. They do not work with membranes or other critical parts, such as components that include heavy metals, thus do not produce toxic waste (and high maintenance).
</p><p>A new approach that works like a solar still, but on the scale of industrial evaporation ponds is the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Integrated_biotectural_system&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Integrated biotectural system (page does not exist)">integrated biotectural system</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PrototypeCreation_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PrototypeCreation-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> It can be considered "full desalination" because it converts the entire amount of saltwater intake into distilled water. One of the advantages of this system is the feasibility for inland operation. Standard advantages also include no air pollution and no temperature increase of endangered natural water bodies from power plant cooling-water discharge. Another important advantage is the production of sea salt for industrial and other uses. As of 2015, 50% of the world's sea salt production relies on fossil energy sources.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternatives_to_desalination">Alternatives to desalination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Alternatives to desalination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Increased <a href="/wiki/Water_conservation" title="Water conservation">water conservation</a> and efficiency remain the most cost-effective approaches in areas with a large potential to improve the efficiency of water use practices.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Wastewater reclamation provides multiple benefits over desalination.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Urban runoff and storm water capture also provide benefits in treating, restoring and recharging groundwater.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A proposed alternative to desalination in the American Southwest is the commercial importation of bulk water from water-rich areas either by <a href="/wiki/Oil_tanker" title="Oil tanker">oil tankers</a> converted to water carriers, or pipelines. The idea is politically unpopular in Canada, where governments imposed trade barriers to bulk water exports as a result of a <a href="/wiki/North_American_Free_Trade_Agreement" title="North American Free Trade Agreement">North American Free Trade Agreement</a> (NAFTA) claim.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_health_concerns">Public health concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Public health concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Desalination removes iodine from water and could increase the risk of iodine deficiency disorders. Israeli researchers claimed a possible link between seawater desalination and iodine deficiency,<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> finding deficits among <a href="/wiki/Euthyroid" title="Euthyroid">euthyroid</a> adults exposed to iodine-poor water<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> concurrently with an increasing proportion of their area's drinking water from seawater reverse osmosis (SWRO).<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> They later found probable iodine deficiency disorders in a population reliant on desalinated seawater.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
A possible link of heavy desalinated water use and national iodine deficiency was suggested by Israeli researchers.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> They found a high burden of iodine deficiency in the general population of Israel: 62% of school-age children and 85% of pregnant women fall below the WHO’s adequacy range.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> They also pointed out the national reliance on iodine-depleted desalinated water, the absence of a universal salt iodization program and reports of increased use of thyroid medication in Israel as a possible reasons that the population’s iodine intake is low. In the year that the survey was conducted, the amount of water produced from the desalination plants constitutes about 50% of the quantity of fresh water supplied for all needs and about 80% of the water supplied for domestic and industrial needs in Israel.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_issues">Other issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Other issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to the nature of the process, there is a need to place the plants on approximately 25 acres of land on or near the shoreline.<sup id="cite_ref-Einav_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Einav-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> In the case a plant is built inland, pipes have to be laid into the ground to allow for easy intake and outtake.<sup id="cite_ref-Einav_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Einav-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> However, once the pipes are laid into the ground, they have a possibility of leaking into and contaminating nearby aquifers.<sup id="cite_ref-Einav_73-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Einav-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> Aside from environmental risks, the noise generated by certain types of desalination plants can be loud.<sup id="cite_ref-Einav_73-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Einav-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_techniques">Experimental techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Experimental techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Other desalination techniques include:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Waste_heat">Waste heat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Waste heat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Thermally-driven desalination technologies are frequently suggested for use with low-temperature <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">waste heat</a> sources, as the low temperatures are not useful for many industrial processes, but ideal for the lower temperatures found in desalaination.<sup id="cite_ref-WarsingerEntropy_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WarsingerEntropy-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In fact, such pairing with waste heat can even improve electrical process: 
<a href="/wiki/Diesel_generators" class="mw-redirect" title="Diesel generators">Diesel generators</a> commonly provide electricity in remote areas. About 40%–50% of the energy output is low-grade heat that leaves the engine via the exhaust. Connecting a thermal desalination technology such as <a href="/wiki/Membrane_distillation" title="Membrane distillation">membrane distillation</a> system to the diesel engine exhaust repurposes this low-grade heat for desalination. The system actively cools the <a href="/wiki/Diesel_generator" title="Diesel generator">diesel generator</a>, improving its efficiency and increasing its electricity output. This results in an energy-neutral desalination solution. An example plant was commissioned by Dutch company <a href="/wiki/Aquaver" title="Aquaver">Aquaver</a> in March 2014 for <a href="/wiki/Gulhi" class="mw-redirect" title="Gulhi">Gulhi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Low-temperature_thermal">Low-temperature thermal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Low-temperature thermal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Originally stemming from <a href="/wiki/Ocean_thermal_energy_conversion" title="Ocean thermal energy conversion">ocean thermal energy conversion</a> research, <a href="/wiki/Low-temperature_thermal_desalination" title="Low-temperature thermal desalination">low-temperature thermal desalination</a> (LTTD) takes advantage of water boiling at low pressure, even at <a href="/wiki/Ambient_temperature" class="mw-redirect" title="Ambient temperature">ambient temperature</a>. The system uses pumps to create a low-pressure, low-temperature environment in which water boils at a temperature gradient of 8–10&#160;°C (46–50&#160;°F) between two volumes of water. Cool ocean water is supplied from depths of up to 600&#160;m (2,000&#160;ft). This water is pumped through coils to condense the water vapor. The resulting condensate is purified water. LTTD may take advantage of the temperature gradient available at power plants, where large quantities of warm wastewater are discharged from the plant, reducing the energy input needed to create a temperature gradient.<sup id="cite_ref-isope1_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isope1-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Experiments were conducted in the US and Japan to test the approach. In Japan, a spray-flash evaporation system was tested by Saga University.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> In Hawaii, the National Energy Laboratory tested an open-cycle OTEC plant with fresh water and power production using a temperature difference of 20 C° between surface water and water at a depth of around 500&#160;m (1,600&#160;ft). LTTD was studied by India's National Institute of Ocean Technology (NIOT) in 2004. Their first LTTD plant opened in 2005 at Kavaratti in the <a href="/wiki/Lakshadweep" title="Lakshadweep">Lakshadweep</a> islands. The plant's capacity is 100,000&#160;L (22,000&#160;imp&#160;gal; 26,000&#160;US&#160;gal)/day, at a capital cost of INR 50 million (€922,000). The plant uses deep water at a temperature of 10 to 12&#160;°C (50 to 54&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-irc_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irc-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> In 2007, NIOT opened an experimental, floating LTTD plant off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Chennai</a>, with a capacity of 1,000,000&#160;L (220,000&#160;imp&#160;gal; 260,000&#160;US&#160;gal)/day. A smaller plant was established in 2009 at the North Chennai Thermal Power Station to prove the LTTD application where power plant cooling water is available.<sup id="cite_ref-isope1_76-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isope1-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermoionic_process">Thermoionic process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Thermoionic process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In October 2009, Saltworks Technologies announced a process that uses solar or other thermal heat to drive an <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ionic</a> current that removes all <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> ions from the water using ion-exchange membranes.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Evaporation_and_condensation_for_crops">Evaporation and condensation for crops</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Evaporation and condensation for crops">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Seawater_greenhouse" title="Seawater greenhouse">Seawater greenhouse</a> uses natural evaporation and condensation processes inside a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a> powered by solar energy to grow crops in arid coastal land.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_approaches">Other approaches</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Other approaches">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adsorption-based desalination (AD) relies on the moisture absorption properties of certain materials such as Silica Gel.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Forward_osmosis">Forward osmosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Forward osmosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>One process was commercialized by Modern Water PLC using <a href="/wiki/Forward_osmosis" title="Forward osmosis">forward osmosis</a>, with a number of plants reported to be in operation.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrogel_based_desalination">Hydrogel based desalination</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Hydrogel based desalination">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TOC_new.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/TOC_new.png/220px-TOC_new.png" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/TOC_new.png/330px-TOC_new.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/TOC_new.png/440px-TOC_new.png 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="739" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TOC_new.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Scheme of the desalination machine: the desalination box of volume <i>V</i>_<sub>box</sub> contains a gel of volume <i>V</i>_<sub>gel</sub> which is separated by a sieve from the outer solution volume <i>V</i>_<sub>out</sub> =<i>V</i>_<sub>box</sub>- <i>V</i>_<sub>gel</sub>. 	The box is connected to two big tanks with high and low salinity by two taps which can be opened and closed as desired. 	The chain of buckets expresses the fresh water consumption followed by refilling the low-salinity reservoir by salt water.</div></div></div>
<p>The idea of the method is in the fact that when the hydrogel is put into contact with aqueous salt solution, it swells absorbing a solution with the ion composition different from the original one. This solution can be easily squeezed out from the gel by means of sieve or microfiltration membrane. The compression of the gel in closed system lead to change in salt concentration, whereas the compression in open system, while the gel is exchanging ions with bulk, lead to the change in the number of ions. The consequence of the compression and swelling in open and closed system conditions mimics the reverse Carnot Cycle of refrigerator machine. The only difference is that instead of heat this cycle transfers salt ions from the bulk of low salinity to a bulk of high salinity. Similarly to the Carnot cycle this cycle is fully reversible, so can in principle work with an ideal thermodynamic efficiency. Because the method is free from the use of osmotic membranes it can compete with reverse osmosis method. In addition, unlike the reverse osmosis, the approach is not sensitive to the quality of feed water and its seasonal changes, and allows the production of water of any desired concentration.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Small-scale_solar">Small-scale solar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Small-scale solar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The United States, France and the United Arab Emirates are working to develop practical <a href="/wiki/Solar_desalination" title="Solar desalination">solar desalination</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> AquaDania's WaterStillar has been installed at Dahab, Egypt, and in Playa del Carmen, Mexico. In this approach, a solar thermal collector measuring two square metres can distill from 40 to 60 litres per day from any local water source – five times more than conventional stills. It eliminates the need for plastic <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate" title="Polyethylene terephthalate">PET</a> bottles or energy-consuming water transport.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> In Central California, a startup company WaterFX is developing a solar-powered method of desalination that can enable the use of local water, including runoff water that can be treated and used again. Salty groundwater in the region would be treated to become freshwater, and in areas near the ocean, seawater could be treated.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Passarell">Passarell</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Passarell">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Passarell process uses reduced atmospheric pressure rather than heat to drive evaporative desalination. The pure water vapor generated by distillation is then compressed and condensed using an advanced compressor. The compression process improves distillation efficiency by creating the reduced pressure in the evaporation chamber. The compressor <a href="/wiki/Centrifuge" title="Centrifuge">centrifuges</a> the pure water vapor after it is drawn through a demister (removing residual impurities) causing it to compress against tubes in the collection chamber. The compression of the vapor increases its temperature. The heat is transferred to the input water falling in the tubes, vaporizing the water in the tubes. Water vapor condenses on the outside of the tubes as product water. By combining several physical processes, Passarell enables most of the system's energy to be recycled through its evaporation, demisting, vapor compression, condensation, and water movement processes.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Geothermal">Geothermal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Geothermal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Geothermal energy can drive desalination. In most locations, <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_desalination" title="Geothermal desalination">geothermal desalination</a> beats using scarce groundwater or surface water, environmentally and economically.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nanotechnology">Nanotechnology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Nanotechnology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nanotube_membrane" title="Nanotube membrane">Nanotube membranes</a> of higher permeability than current generation of membranes may lead to eventual reduction in the footprint of RO desalination plants. It has also been suggested that the use of such membranes will lead to reduction in the energy needed for desalination.<sup id="cite_ref-LLNL_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LLNL-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hermetic, sulphonated nano-composite membranes have shown to be capable of removing a various contaminants to the parts per billion level. s, have little or no susceptibility to high salt concentration levels.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Biomimesis">Biomimesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Biomimesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biomimetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Biomimetic">Biomimetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Biological_membrane" title="Biological membrane">membranes</a> are another approach.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrochemical">Electrochemical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Electrochemical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 2008, Siemens Water Technologies announced technology that applied electric fields to desalinate one cubic meter of water while using only a purported 1.5 kWh of energy. If accurate, this process would consume one-half the energy of other processes.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup> As of 2012 a demonstration plant was operating in Singapore.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup> Researchers at the University of Texas at Austin and the University of Marburg are developing more efficient methods of electrochemically mediated seawater desalination.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrokinetic_shocks">Electrokinetic shocks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Electrokinetic shocks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A process employing electrokinetic shocks waves can be used to accomplish membraneless desalination at ambient temperature and pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup> In this process, anions and cations in salt water are exchanged for carbonate anions and calcium cations, respectively using electrokinetic shockwaves. Calcium and carbonate ions react to form <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>, which precipitates, leaving fresh water. The theoretical energy efficiency of this method is on par with <a href="/wiki/Electrodialysis" title="Electrodialysis">electrodialysis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reverse_osmosis" title="Reverse osmosis">reverse osmosis</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_nature">In nature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: In nature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Saltcrystals_on_avicennia_marina_var_resinifera_leaves.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Saltcrystals_on_avicennia_marina_var_resinifera_leaves.JPG/100px-Saltcrystals_on_avicennia_marina_var_resinifera_leaves.JPG" width="100" height="161" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Saltcrystals_on_avicennia_marina_var_resinifera_leaves.JPG/150px-Saltcrystals_on_avicennia_marina_var_resinifera_leaves.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Saltcrystals_on_avicennia_marina_var_resinifera_leaves.JPG/200px-Saltcrystals_on_avicennia_marina_var_resinifera_leaves.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1242" data-file-height="2001" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Saltcrystals_on_avicennia_marina_var_resinifera_leaves.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mangrove leaf with salt crystals</div></div></div>
<p>Evaporation of water over the oceans in the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a> is a natural desalination process.
</p><p>The formation of <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">sea ice</a> produces ice with little salt, much lower than in seawater.
</p><p>Seabirds distill seawater using <a href="/wiki/Countercurrent_exchange" title="Countercurrent exchange">countercurrent exchange</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Salt_gland" title="Salt gland">gland</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Rete_mirabile" title="Rete mirabile">rete mirabile</a>. The gland <a href="/wiki/Na%2B/K%2B-ATPase" title="Na+/K+-ATPase">secretes highly concentrated brine</a> stored near the nostrils above the beak. The bird then "sneezes" the brine out. As freshwater is not usually available in their environments, some seabirds, such as <a href="/wiki/Pelican" title="Pelican">pelicans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petrel" title="Petrel">petrels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albatross" title="Albatross">albatrosses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gull" title="Gull">gulls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tern" title="Tern">terns</a>, possess this gland, which allows them to drink the salty water from their environments while they are far from land.<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">Mangrove</a> trees grow in seawater; they secrete salt by trapping it in parts of the root, which are then eaten by animals (usually crabs). Additional salt is removed by storing it in leaves that fall off. Some types of mangroves have glands on their leaves, which work in a similar way to the seabird desalination gland. Salt is extracted to the leaf exterior as small <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystals</a>, which then fall off the leaf.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Willow" title="Willow">Willow</a> trees and <a href="/wiki/Reed_(plant)" title="Reed (plant)">reeds</a> absorb salt and other contaminants, effectively desalinating the water. This is used in artificial <a href="/wiki/Constructed_wetland" title="Constructed wetland">constructed wetlands</a>, for treating <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Desalination&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Desalination has been known to history for millennia as both a concept, and later practice, though in a limited form. The ancient Greek philosopher <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> observed in his work <i><a href="/wiki/Meteorology_(Aristotle)" title="Meteorology (Aristotle)">Meteorology</a></i> that “salt water, when it turns into vapour, becomes sweet and the vapour does not form salt water again when it condenses,” and also noticed that a fine wax vessel would hold potable water after being submerged long enough in seawater, having acted as a membrane to filter the salt.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> There are numerous other examples of experimentation in desalination throughout Antiquity and the Middle Ages,<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup> but desalination was never feasible on a large scale until the modern era.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Before the Industrial Revolution, desalination was primarily of concern to oceangoing ships, which otherwise needed to keep on board supplies of fresh water. When <a href="/wiki/Protector_(1779_frigate)" title="Protector (1779 frigate)">Protector (1779 frigate)</a> was sold to Denmark in the 1780s (as the ship Hussaren) the desalination plant was studied and recorded in great detail.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup> <br />
In the newly formed United States, Thomas Jefferson catalogued heat-based methods going back to the 1500s, and formulated practical advice that was publicized to all U.S. ships on the backs of sailing clearance permits.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Significant research into improved desalination methods occurred in the United States after World War II. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Office_of_Saline_Water&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Office of Saline Water (page does not exist)">Office of Saline Water</a> was created in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Interior" title="United States Department of the Interior">United States Department of the Interior</a> by the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Saline_Water_Conversion_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Saline Water Conversion Act (page does not exist)">Saline Water Conversion Act</a> of 1952.  It was merged into the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Office_of_Water_Resources_Research&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Office of Water Resources Research (page does not exist)">Office of Water Resources Research</a> in 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Research also took place at state universities in California, followed by development at the <a href="/wiki/Dow_Chemical_Company" title="Dow Chemical Company">Dow Chemical Company</a> and <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">DuPont</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup> Many studies focus on ways to optimize desalination systems.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">&#91;112&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
