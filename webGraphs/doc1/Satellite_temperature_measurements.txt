<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Satellite temperature measurements</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>Satellite temperature measurements</b> are <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing#Data_processing" title="Remote sensing">inferences</a>  of the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> at various altitudes as well as sea and land surface temperatures obtained from <a href="/wiki/Radiometric" class="mw-redirect" title="Radiometric">radiometric</a> measurements by <a href="/wiki/Satellites" class="mw-redirect" title="Satellites">satellites</a>. These measurements can be used to locate <a href="/wiki/Weather_fronts" class="mw-redirect" title="Weather fronts">weather fronts</a>, monitor the <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" class="mw-redirect" title="El Niño-Southern Oscillation">El Niño-Southern Oscillation</a>, determine the strength of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclones" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical cyclones">tropical cyclones</a>, study <a href="/wiki/Urban_heat_island" title="Urban heat island">urban heat islands</a> and monitor the global climate. <a href="/wiki/Wildfires" class="mw-redirect" title="Wildfires">Wildfires</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanos</a>, and industrial hot spots can also be found via thermal imaging from weather satellites.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">Weather satellites</a> do not measure temperature directly. They measure <a href="/wiki/Radiance" title="Radiance">radiances</a> in various <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">wavelength</a> bands. Since 1978 <a href="/wiki/Microwave_sounding_unit" title="Microwave sounding unit">microwave sounding units</a> (MSUs) on <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> <a href="/wiki/Polar_orbit" title="Polar orbit">polar orbiting</a> satellites have measured the intensity of upwelling microwave radiation from atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, which is related to the temperature of broad vertical layers of the atmosphere. Measurements of <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> radiation pertaining to sea surface temperature have been collected since 1967.
</p><p>Satellite datasets show that over the past four decades the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> has warmed and the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a> has cooled. Both of these trends are consistent with the influence of increasing atmospheric concentrations of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurements">Measurements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_temperature_measurements&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Measurements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">Satellites</a> do not measure temperature. They measure radiances in various wavelength bands, which must then be mathematically inverted to obtain indirect inferences of temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-NRC2000_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NRC2000-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Uddstrom1988_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Uddstrom1988-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The resulting temperature profiles depend on details of the methods that are used to obtain temperatures from radiances. As a result, different groups that have analyzed the satellite data have produced differing temperature datasets. Among these are the <a href="/wiki/UAH_satellite_temperature_dataset" title="UAH satellite temperature dataset">UAH dataset</a> prepared at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alabama_in_Huntsville" title="University of Alabama in Huntsville">University of Alabama in Huntsville</a> and the RSS dataset prepared by <a href="/wiki/Remote_Sensing_Systems" title="Remote Sensing Systems">Remote Sensing Systems</a>.
</p><p>The satellite time series is not homogeneous. It is constructed from a series of satellites with similar but not identical sensors. The sensors also deteriorate over time, and corrections are necessary for orbital drift and decay.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Particularly large differences between reconstructed temperature series occur at the few times when there is little temporal overlap between successive satellites, making intercalibration difficult.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Satellites may also be used to retrieve surface temperatures in cloud-free conditions, generally via measurement of thermal infrared from <a href="/wiki/AVHRR" class="mw-redirect" title="AVHRR">AVHRR</a>. Weather satellites have been available to infer <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperature</a> (SST) information since 1967, with the first global composites occurring during 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  Since 1982,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> have been increasingly utilized to measure SST and have allowed its <a href="/wiki/Spatial_variability" title="Spatial variability">spatial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">temporal</a> variation to be viewed more fully. For example, changes in SST monitored via satellite have been used to document the progression of the El Niño-Southern Oscillation since the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  Over the land the retrieval of temperature from radiances is harder, because of the inhomogeneities in the surface.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  Studies have been conducted on the urban heat island effect via satellite imagery.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  Use of <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Very_High_Resolution_Radiometer" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced Very High Resolution Radiometer">advanced very high resolution infrared satellite imagery</a> can be used, in the absence of cloudiness, to detect <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> discontinuities (<a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">weather fronts</a>) such as <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold fronts</a> at ground level.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  Using the <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak technique</a>, infrared satellite imagery can be used to determine the temperature difference between the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a> top temperature of the <a href="/wiki/Central_dense_overcast" title="Central dense overcast">central dense overcast</a> of mature tropical cyclones to estimate their <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained winds</a> and their minimum central <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">pressures</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/AATSR" title="AATSR">Along Track Scanning Radiometers</a> aboard weather satellites are able to detect wildfires, which show up at night as pixels with a greater temperature than 308&#160;K (95&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/Moderate-Resolution_Imaging_Spectroradiometer" class="mw-redirect" title="Moderate-Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer">Moderate-Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer</a> aboard the <a href="/wiki/Terra_(satellite)" title="Terra (satellite)">Terra satellite</a> can detect thermal hot spots associated with wildfires, volcanoes, and industrial hot spots.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>From 1979 to 2005 the <a href="/wiki/Microwave_sounding_unit" title="Microwave sounding unit">microwave sounding units</a> (MSUs) and since 1998 the <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Microwave_Sounding_Unit" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced Microwave Sounding Unit">Advanced Microwave Sounding Units</a> on NOAA polar orbiting satellites have measured the intensity of upwelling <a href="/wiki/Microwave_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Microwave radiation">microwave radiation</a> from atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. The intensity is proportional to the temperature of broad vertical layers of the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, as demonstrated by theory and direct comparisons with atmospheric temperatures from radiosonde (balloon) profiles. Upwelling radiance is measured at different frequencies; these different frequency bands sample a different weighted range of the atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>

</p><p>MSU Channel 1 is not used to monitor atmospheric temperature because it's too much sensitive to the emission from the surface, furthermore it is heavily contaminated by water vapor/liquid water in the lowermost troposphere.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Channel 2 or TMT is broadly representative of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, albeit with a significant overlap with the lower stratosphere (the weighting function has its maximum at 350 hPa and half-power at about 40 and 800 hPa). In an attempt to remove the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratospheric</a> influence, <a href="/wiki/Roy_Spencer_(scientist)" title="Roy Spencer (scientist)">Spencer</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Christy" title="John Christy">Christy</a> developed the synthetic "2LT or TLT" product by subtracting signals at different view angles; this has a maximum at about 650 hPa. However, this amplifies noise,<sup id="cite_ref-Christy1998_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christy1998-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> increases inter-satellite calibration biases and enhances surface contamination.<sup id="cite_ref-Fu2005_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fu2005-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>  The 2LT product has gone through numerous versions as various corrections have been applied.
</p><p>Another methodology to reduce the influence of the stratosphere has been developed by Fu and Johanson,<sup id="cite_ref-fu_2004_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fu_2004-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  the TTT(Total Troposphere Temperature) channel is a linear combination of the TMT and TLS channel: TTT=1.156*TMT-0.153*TLS for the global average and TTT=1.12*TMT-0.11*TLS at tropical latitudes.
</p><p>The T4 or TLS channel in representative of the temperature in the lower stratosphere with a peak weighting function at around 17&#160;km above the earth surface.
</p><p>Since 1979 the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stratospheric_sounding_unit&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Stratospheric sounding unit (page does not exist)">Stratospheric sounding units</a> (SSUs) on the NOAA operational satellites provided near global stratospheric temperature data above the lower stratosphere.
The SSU is a far-infrared spectrometer employing a pressure modulation technique to make measurement in three channels in the 15 μm carbon dioxide absorption band. The three channels use the same frequency but different carbon dioxide cell pressure, the corresponding weighting functions peaks at 29&#160;km for channel1, 37&#160;km for channel2 and 45&#160;km for channel3.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trends_from_the_record">Trends from the record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_temperature_measurements&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Trends from the record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/UAH_satellite_temperature_dataset" title="UAH satellite temperature dataset">UAH satellite temperature dataset</a> and <a href="/wiki/MSU_temperature_measurements" title="MSU temperature measurements">MSU temperature measurements</a></div>
<p>Records have been created by merging data from nine different MSUs, each with peculiarities (e.g., time drift of the spacecraft relative to the local solar time) that must be calculated and removed because they can have substantial impacts on the resulting trend.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The process of constructing a temperature record from a radiance record is difficult. The satellite temperature record comes from a succession of different satellites and problems with inter-calibration between the satellites are important, especially NOAA-9, which accounts for most of the difference between various analyses.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> NOAA-11 played a significant role in a 2005 study by Mears <i>et al.</i> identifying an error in the diurnal correction that leads to the 40% jump in Spencer and Christy's trend from version 5.1 to 5.2.<sup id="cite_ref-Mears2005_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mears2005-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  There are ongoing efforts to resolve differences in satellite temperature datasets.
</p><p>Christy <i>et al.</i> (2007) find that the tropical temperature trends from <a href="/wiki/Radiosondes" class="mw-redirect" title="Radiosondes">radiosondes</a> matches closest with his v5.2 UAH dataset.<sup id="cite_ref-Christy2007_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christy2007-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>  Furthermore, they assert there is a growing discrepancy between RSS and sonde trends beginning in 1992, when the NOAA-12 satellite was launched.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This research found that the tropics were warming, from the balloon data, +0.09 (corrected to UAH) or +0.12 (corrected to RSS) or 0.05 K (from UAH MSU; ±0.07 K room for error) a decade.
</p><p>Using the T2 or TMT channel (which include significant contributions from the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>, which has cooled), Mears et al. of Remote Sensing Systems (RSS) find (through January 2017) a trend of +0.140&#160;°C/decade.<sup id="cite_ref-RSS_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSS-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>  Spencer and Christy of the University of Alabama in Huntsville (UAH), find a smaller trend of +0.08&#160;°C/decade.<sup id="cite_ref-UAH_TMT_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UAH_TMT-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A no longer updated analysis of Vinnikov and Grody found +0.20&#160;°C per decade (1978&#8211;2005).<sup id="cite_ref-Vinnikov2006_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vinnikov2006-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>  Another satellite temperature analysis is provided by NOAA/NESDIS STAR Center for Satellite Application and Research and use simultaneous nadir overpasses (SNO)<sup id="cite_ref-Zou2006_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zou2006-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> to remove satellite intercalibration biases yielding more accurate temperature trends. The STAR-NOAA analysis finds a 1979-2016 trend of  +0.129&#160;°C/decade for TMT channel.<sup id="cite_ref-star_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-star-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>
Lower stratospheric cooling is mainly caused by the effects of <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a> with a possible contribution from increased stratospheric water vapor and greenhouse gases increase.<sup id="cite_ref-Shine_2003_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shine_2003-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>  There is a decline in stratospheric temperatures, interspersed by warmings related to volcanic eruptions. <a href="/wiki/Global_Warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Global Warming">Global Warming</a> theory suggests that the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a> should cool while the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> warms <sup id="cite_ref-Clough_1995_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clough_1995-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:STAR_TTS_SSU_Trend.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/STAR_TTS_SSU_Trend.png/220px-STAR_TTS_SSU_Trend.png" width="220" height="123" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/STAR_TTS_SSU_Trend.png/330px-STAR_TTS_SSU_Trend.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/STAR_TTS_SSU_Trend.png/440px-STAR_TTS_SSU_Trend.png 2x" data-file-width="1813" data-file-height="1010" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:STAR_TTS_SSU_Trend.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Top of the stratosphere (TTS) 1979-2006 temperature trend.</div></div></div><p>  The long term cooling in the lower stratosphere occurred in two downward steps in temperature both after the transient warming related to explosive volcanic eruptions of <a href="/wiki/El_Chich%C3%B3n" title="El Chichón">El Chichón</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pinatubo" title="Mount Pinatubo">Mount Pinatubo</a>, this behavior of the global stratospheric temperature has been attributed to global ozone concentration variation in the two years following volcanic eruptions.<sup id="cite_ref-Thompson_2009_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson_2009-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since 1996 the trend is slightly positive<sup id="cite_ref-Liu_2009_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Liu_2009-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> due to ozone recovery juxtaposed to a cooling trend of 0.1K/decade that is consistent with the predicted impact of increased greenhouse gases.<sup id="cite_ref-Thompson_2009_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson_2009-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The process of deriving trends from SSUs measurement has proved particularly difficult because of satellites drift, inter-calibration between different satellite with scant overlap and gas leak in the instrument carbon dioxide pressure cell, furthermore since the radiances measured by SSUs are due to emission by <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> the weighting functions move to higher altitudes as the carbon dioxide concentration in the stratosphere increase.
Mid to upper stratosphere temperature show strong negative trend interspersed by transient volcanic warming after the explosive volcanic eruptions of <a href="/wiki/El_Chich%C3%B3n" title="El Chichón">El Chichón</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pinatubo" title="Mount Pinatubo">Mount Pinatubo</a>, little temperature trend has been observed since 1995.
The greatest cooling occurred in the tropical stratosphere consistent with enhanced <a href="/wiki/Brewer-Dobson_circulation" class="mw-redirect" title="Brewer-Dobson circulation">Brewer-Dobson circulation</a> under greenhouse gas concentrations increase.<sup id="cite_ref-Wang_2011_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wang_2011-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_to_instrumental_record">Comparison to instrumental record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_temperature_measurements&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Comparison to instrumental record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Radiosonde_Satellite_Surface_Temperature.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Radiosonde_Satellite_Surface_Temperature.svg/220px-Radiosonde_Satellite_Surface_Temperature.svg.png" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Radiosonde_Satellite_Surface_Temperature.svg/330px-Radiosonde_Satellite_Surface_Temperature.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Radiosonde_Satellite_Surface_Temperature.svg/440px-Radiosonde_Satellite_Surface_Temperature.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="700" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Radiosonde_Satellite_Surface_Temperature.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1958-2011 radiosonde, satellite and surface temperature record.</div></div></div>
<p>The satellite records have the advantage of global coverage, whereas the radiosonde record is longer. There have been complaints of data problems with both records.
</p><p>To compare to the trend from the <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_temperature_record" title="Instrumental temperature record">surface temperature record</a> (+0.161&#177;0.033&#160;°C/decade from 1979 to 2012 according to NASA GISS<sup id="cite_ref-:2_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>) it is most appropriate to derive trends for the part of the atmosphere nearest the surface, i.e., the lower <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>. Doing this, through January 2017:
</p>
<ul><li>RSS v3.3 finds a trend of +0.135&#160;°K/decade.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> revised up to .184K/Decade in version 4</li>
<li>UAH v6.0 finds a trend of +0.12&#160;°K/decade.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>An alternative adjustment to remove the stratospheric contamination has been introduced by Fu <i>et al.</i> (2004),<sup id="cite_ref-Fu2004_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fu2004-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> after the correction the vertical weighting function is nearly the same of the T2(TMT) channel in the troposphere,<sup id="cite_ref-Johanson_2006_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johanson_2006-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> the University of Washington analysis finds 1979-2012 trends of +0.13&#160;°C/decade when applied to the RSS data set and +0.10&#160;°C/decade when applied to the UAH data set.<sup id="cite_ref-NOAA2012_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA2012-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The IPCC fifth assessment report concluded: " based on multiple independent analyses of measurements from radiosondes and satellite sensors it is virtually certain that globally the troposphere has warmed and the stratosphere has cooled since the mid-20th century. Despite unanimous agreement on the sign of the trends, substantial disagreement exists among available estimates as to the rate of temperature changes, particularly outside the NH extratropical troposphere, which has been well sampled by radiosondes.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconciliation_with_climate_models">Reconciliation with climate models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_temperature_measurements&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reconciliation with climate models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">Climate model</a> and <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a></div>
<p>Surface temperature data showed substantial global average warming, while early versions of satellite and radiosonde data showed little or no warming above the surface. While the satellite  data now show global warming, there is still a significant difference between what <a href="/wiki/General_circulation_model" title="General circulation model">Climate models</a> predict and what the satellite data show for warming of the lower troposphere with the climate models predicting significantly more warming than what the satellites measure.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> The climate models also overpredict the results of the radiosonde measurements.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The most recent climate model simulations give a range of results for changes in global average temperature. Some models show more warming in the troposphere than at the surface, while a slightly smaller number of simulations show the opposite behavior. There is no fundamental inconsistency among these model results and observations at the global scale,<sup id="cite_ref-SAP1_1_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SAP1_1-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> with the trends now being similar.
</p>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Exclamation mark with arrows pointing at each other" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Ambox_contradict.svg/38px-Ambox_contradict.svg.png" title="Accuracy dispute" width="38" height="38" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Ambox_contradict.svg/57px-Ambox_contradict.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Ambox_contradict.svg/76px-Ambox_contradict.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="620" data-file-height="620" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article or section <b>appears to contradict itself</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please see the <a href="/wiki/Talk:Satellite_temperature_measurements" title="Talk:Satellite temperature measurements">talk page</a> for more information.</span>  <small><i>(January 2016)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Globally, the troposphere is predicted by models to warm about 1.2 times more than the surface;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> in the tropics, the troposphere should warm about 1.5 times more than the surface.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Most climate models used by the IPCC in preparation of their third assessment show a slightly greater warming at the TLT level than at the surface (0.03&#160;°C/decade difference) for 1979-1999<sup id="cite_ref-SAP1_1_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SAP1_1-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Santer_et_al_2006_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Santer_et_al_2006-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> while the GISS trend is +0.161&#160;°C/decade for 1979 to 2012,<sup id="cite_ref-:2_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> the lower troposphere trends calculated from satellite data by UAH and RSS are +0.12&#160;°C/decade<sup id="cite_ref-:1_42-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> and +0.184&#160;°C/decade.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_39-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>
This greater global average warming in the troposphere compared to the surface (present in the models but not observed data) is most marked in the tropics. </p><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"In the tropics, surface temperature changes are amplified in the free troposphere. Models and observations show similar amplification behavior for monthly and interannual temperature variations, but not for decadal temperature changes. Tropospheric amplification of surface temperature anomalies is due to the release of latent heat by moist, rising air in regions experiencing convection."<sup id="cite_ref-Santer_et_al_2006_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Santer_et_al_2006-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Although all the datasets show the expected tropospheric amplification at seasonal and annual timescales it is still debated whether or not the long term trends are consistent with the expected moist <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate#Saturated_adiabatic_lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">adiabatic lapse rate</a><sup id="cite_ref-Stone_1979_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stone_1979-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> amplification due to difficulty of producing homogenized datasets,<sup id="cite_ref-Thorne_2010_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thorne_2010-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> some satellite temperature reconstruction are consistent with the expected amplification<sup id="cite_ref-Zou2009_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zou2009-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> while others are not.<sup id="cite_ref-Thorne_2010_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thorne_2010-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The IPCC fifth assessment report concluded "Although there have been substantial methodological debates about the calculation of trends and their uncertainty, a 95% confidence interval of around ±0.1°C per decade has been obtained consistently for both LT and MT (e.g., Section 2.4.4; McKitrick et al., 2010). In summary, despite unanimous agreement on the sign of the observed trends, there exists substantial disagreement between available estimates as to the rate of temperature changes in the tropical troposphere, and there is only low confidence in the rate of change and its vertical structure."<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_differences">Historic differences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Satellite_temperature_measurements&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Historic differences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/UAH_satellite_temperature_dataset#Corrections_made" title="UAH satellite temperature dataset">UAH_satellite_temperature_dataset §&#160;Corrections_made</a></div>
<p>For some time the only available satellite record was the UAH version, which (with early versions of the processing <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">algorithm</a>) showed a global cooling trend for its first decade. Since then, a longer record and a number of corrections to the processing have revised this picture: the UAH dataset has shown an overall warming trend since 1998, though less than the RSS version. In 2001, an extensive comparison and discussion of trends from different data sources and periods was given in the <a href="/wiki/Third_Assessment_Report" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Assessment Report">Third Assessment Report</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC)  (section 2.2.4).<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2017, RSS released v4 which significantly increased the trend making the difference between RSS and UAH substantial again.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
