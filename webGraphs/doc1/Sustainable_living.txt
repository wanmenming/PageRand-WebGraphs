<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Sustainable living</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p><p><b>Sustainable living</b> describes a <a href="/wiki/Lifestyle_(sociology)" title="Lifestyle (sociology)">lifestyle</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> that attempts to reduce an individual's or <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a>'s use of the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Natural_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a>, and one's personal resources.<sup id="cite_ref-Ain09_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ain09-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Its practitioners often attempt to reduce their <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">carbon footprint</a> by altering their methods of <a href="/wiki/Transportation" class="mw-redirect" title="Transportation">transportation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Domestic_energy_consumption" title="Domestic energy consumption">energy consumption</a>, and/or diet.<sup id="cite_ref-Winter_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Winter-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Its proponents aim to conduct their lives in ways that are consistent with <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>, naturally balanced, and respectful of humanity's <a href="/wiki/Symbiotic" class="mw-redirect" title="Symbiotic">symbiotic</a> relationship with the Earth's natural <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link since July 2018">dead link</span></a></i>&#93;</span></sup> The practice and general philosophy of <b>ecological living</b> closely follows the overall principles of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lester_R._Brown" title="Lester R. Brown">Lester R. Brown</a>, a prominent <a href="/wiki/Environmentalist" title="Environmentalist">environmentalist</a> and founder of the <a href="/wiki/Worldwatch_Institute" title="Worldwatch Institute">Worldwatch Institute</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earth_Policy_Institute" title="Earth Policy Institute">Earth Policy Institute</a>, describes sustainable living in the twenty-first century as "shifting to a renewable energy-based, reuse/recycle economy with a diversified transport system."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Derrick_Jensen" title="Derrick Jensen">Derrick Jensen</a> ("the poet-philosopher of the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological movement">ecological movement</a>"), a celebrated American <a href="/wiki/Author" title="Author">author</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radical_environmentalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Radical environmentalist">radical environmentalist</a> and prominent critic of <a href="/wiki/Bright_green_environmentalism" title="Bright green environmentalism">mainstream environmentalism</a> argues that "<a href="/wiki/Industrial_civilization" title="Industrial civilization">industrial civilization</a> is not and can never be sustainable".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> From this statement, the natural conclusion is that sustainable living is at odds with industrialization. Thus, practitioners of the philosophy potentially face the challenge of living in an industrial society and adapting alternative norms, technologies, or practices.
</p><p>Additionally, practical <a href="/wiki/Ecovillage" title="Ecovillage">ecovillage</a> builders like <a href="/wiki/Living_Villages" title="Living Villages">Living Villages</a> maintain that the shift to alternative technologies will only be successful if the resultant built environment is attractive to a local <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a> and can be maintained and adapted as necessary over multiple generations.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Sustainable living is fundamentally the application of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> to lifestyle choice and decisions.  One conception of sustainable living expresses what it means in triple-bottom-line terms as meeting present ecological, societal, and economical needs without compromising these factors for future generations.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Another broader conception describes sustainable living in terms of four interconnected <i>social</i> domains: economics, ecology, politics and culture. In the first conception, sustainable living can be described as living within the innate carrying capacities defined by these factors. In the second or <a href="/wiki/Circles_of_Sustainability" title="Circles of Sustainability">Circles of Sustainability</a> conception, sustainable living can be described as negotiating the relationships of needs within limits across all the interconnected domains of social life, including consequences for future human generations and non-human <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_design" title="Sustainable design">Sustainable design</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> are critical factors to sustainable living. Sustainable design encompasses the development of <a href="/wiki/Appropriate_technology" title="Appropriate technology">appropriate technology</a>, which is a staple of sustainable living practices.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  Sustainable development in turn is the use of these technologies in infrastructure.  <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_architecture" title="Sustainable architecture">Sustainable architecture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">agriculture</a> are the most common examples of this practice.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><b>1954</b> The publication of <i>Living the Good Life</i> by <a href="/wiki/Helen_Nearing" title="Helen Nearing">Helen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scott_Nearing" title="Scott Nearing">Scott Nearing</a> marked the beginning of the modern day sustainable living movement.  The publication paved the way for the "<a href="/wiki/Back-to-the-land_movement" title="Back-to-the-land movement">back-to-the-land movement</a>" in the late 1960s and early 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>1962</b> The publication of <i><a href="/wiki/Silent_Spring" title="Silent Spring">Silent Spring</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Carson" title="Rachel Carson">Rachel Carson</a> marked another major milestone for the sustainability movement.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>1972</b> <a href="/wiki/Donella_Meadows" title="Donella Meadows">Donella Meadows</a> wrote the international bestseller <i><a href="/wiki/The_Limits_to_Growth" title="The Limits to Growth">The Limits to Growth</a></i>, which reported on a study of long-term global trends in <a href="/wiki/Population" title="Population">population</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a>. It sold millions of copies and was translated into 28 languages.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>1973</b> <a href="/wiki/E._F._Schumacher" title="E. F. Schumacher">E. F. Schumacher</a> published a collection of essays on shifting towards sustainable living through the appropriate use of technology in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Small_is_Beautiful" class="mw-redirect" title="Small is Beautiful">Small is Beautiful</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>1992–2002</b> The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> held a series of conferences, which focused on increasing sustainability within societies to conserve the Earth's natural resources. The <a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit" title="Earth Summit">Earth Summit</a> conferences were held in 1992, <a href="/wiki/Conference_on_the_Human_Environment" class="mw-redirect" title="Conference on the Human Environment">1972</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit_2002" title="Earth Summit 2002">2002</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>2007</b> the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> published <i>Sustainable Consumption and Production, Promoting Climate-Friendly Household Consumption Patterns</i>, which promoted sustainable lifestyles in communities and homes.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shelter">Shelter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Shelter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PA120016.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/PA120016.JPG/240px-PA120016.JPG" width="240" height="180" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/PA120016.JPG/360px-PA120016.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/PA120016.JPG/480px-PA120016.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PA120016.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An example of ecological housing</div></div></div>
<p>On a global scale, shelter is associated with about 25% of the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions embodied in household purchases and 26% of households' <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a>.<sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ivanova_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ivanova-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Green_building" title="Green building">Sustainable homes</a> are built using sustainable methods, materials, and facilitate green practices, enabling a more sustainable lifestyle. Their construction and maintenance have neutral impacts on the Earth. Often, if necessary, they are close in proximity to essential services such as grocery stores, schools, daycares, work, or public transit making it possible to commit to sustainable transportation choices.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Sometimes, they are <a href="/wiki/Off-the-grid" title="Off-the-grid">off-the-grid</a> homes that do not require any public energy, water, or sewer service.
</p><p>If not off-the-grid, sustainable homes may be linked to a grid supplied by a power plant that is using sustainable power sources, buying power as is normal convention. Additionally, sustainable homes may be connected to a grid, but generate their own electricity through renewable means and sell any excess to a utility. There are two common methods to approaching this option: net metering and double metering.<sup id="cite_ref-McDilda,_Diane_Gow_2007_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McDilda,_Diane_Gow_2007-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Net metering uses the common meter that is installed in most homes, running forward when power is used from the grid, and running backward when power is put into the grid (which allows them to “net“ out their total energy use, putting excess energy into the grid when not needed, and using energy from the grid during peak hours, when you may not be able to produce enough immediately). Power companies can quickly purchase the power that is put back into the grid, as it is being produced. Double metering involves installing two meters: one measuring electricity consumed, the other measuring electricity created. Additionally, or in place of selling their renewable energy, sustainable home owners may choose to bank their excess energy by using it to charge batteries. This gives them the option to use the power later during less favorable power-generating times (i.e.: night-time, when there has been no wind, etc.), and to be completely independent of the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">electrical grid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sustainably designed (see <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Design" class="mw-redirect" title="Sustainable Design">Sustainable Design</a>) houses <sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> are generally sited so as to create as little of a negative impact on the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> as possible, oriented to the sun so that it creates the best possible <a href="/wiki/Microclimate" title="Microclimate">microclimate</a> (typically, the long axis of the house or building should be oriented east-west), and provide natural shading or wind barriers where and when needed, among many other considerations. The design of a sustainable shelter affords the options it has later (i.e.: using <a href="/wiki/Passive_solar_building_design" title="Passive solar building design">passive solar lighting and heating</a>, creating temperature buffer zones by adding porches, deep overhangs to help create favorable microclimates, etc.)<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hamilton,_Andy_2009_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hamilton,_Andy_2009-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  Sustainably constructed houses involve environmentally friendly management of waste building materials such as recycling and composting, use non-toxic and renewable, recycled, reclaimed, or low-impact production materials that have been created and treated in a sustainable fashion (such as using organic or water-based finishes), use as much locally available materials and tools as possible so as to reduce the need for transportation, and use low-impact production methods (methods that minimize effects on the environment).<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many materials can be considered a “green” material until its background is revealed. Any material that has used toxic or carcinogenic chemicals in its treatment or manufacturing (such as <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a> in glues used in woodworking), has traveled extensively from its source or manufacturer, or has been cultivated or harvested in an unsustainable manner might not be considered green. In order for any material to be considered green, it must be resource efficient, not compromise <a href="/wiki/Indoor_air_quality" title="Indoor air quality">indoor air quality</a> or <a href="/wiki/Water_conservation" title="Water conservation">water conservation</a>, and be energy efficient (both in processing and when in use in the shelter).<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Resource efficiency can be achieved by using as much recycled content, reusable or recyclable content, materials that employ recycled or recyclable packaging, locally available material, salvaged or remanufactured material, material that employs resource efficient manufacturing, and long-lasting material as possible.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Building_insulation" title="Building insulation">Insulation</a> of a sustainable home is important because of the energy it conserves throughout the life of the home. Well insulated walls and lofts using green materials are a must as it reduces or, in combination with a house that is well designed, eliminates the need for heating and cooling altogether. Installation of insulation varies according to the type of insulation being used. Typically, lofts are insulated by strips of insulating material laid between rafters. Walls with cavities are done in much the same manner. For walls that do not have cavities behind them, solid-wall insulation may be necessary which can decrease internal space and can be expensive to install.<sup id="cite_ref-Hamilton,_Andy_2009_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hamilton,_Andy_2009-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Energy-efficient windows are another important factor in insulation. Simply assuring that windows (and doors) are well sealed greatly reduces energy loss in a home.<sup id="cite_ref-McDilda,_Diane_Gow_2007_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McDilda,_Diane_Gow_2007-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  Double or Triple glazed windows are the typical method to insulating windows, trapping gas or creating a vacuum between two or three panes of glass allowing heat to be trapped inside or out.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Low-emissivity" class="mw-redirect" title="Low-emissivity">Low-emissivity</a> or Low-E glass is another option for window insulation. It is a coating on windowpanes of a thin, transparent layer of metal oxide and works by reflecting heat back to its source, keeping the interior warm during the winter and cool during the summer. Simply hanging heavy-backed curtains in front of windows may also help their insulation.<sup id="cite_ref-Hamilton,_Andy_2009_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hamilton,_Andy_2009-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> “Superwindows,” mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Natural_Capitalism:_Creating_the_Next_Industrial_Revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural Capitalism: Creating the Next Industrial Revolution">Natural Capitalism: Creating the Next Industrial Revolution</a>, became available in the 1980s and use a combination of many available technologies, including two to three transparent low-e coatings, multiple panes of glass, and a heavy gas filling. Although more expensive, they are said to be able to insulate four and a half times better than a typical double-glazed windows.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Equipping roofs with highly reflective material (such as aluminum) increases a roof's <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> and will help reduce the amount of heat it absorbs, hence, the amount of energy needed to cool the building it is on. <a href="/wiki/Green_roofs" class="mw-redirect" title="Green roofs">Green roofs</a> or “living roofs” are a popular choice for thermally insulating a building. They are also popular for their ability to catch storm-water runoff and, when in the broader picture of a community, reduce the heat island effect (see <a href="/wiki/Urban_heat_island" title="Urban heat island">urban heat island</a>) thereby reducing energy costs of the entire area. It is arguable that they are able to replace the physical “footprint” that the building creates, helping reduce the adverse environmental impacts of the building‘s presence.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Energy efficiency and water conservation are also major considerations in sustainable housing. If using appliances, computers, <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">HVAC</a> systems, electronics, or lighting the sustainable-minded often look for an <a href="/wiki/Energy_Star" title="Energy Star">Energy Star</a> label, which is government-backed and holds stricter regulations in energy and water efficiency than is required by law.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Ideally, a sustainable shelter should be able to completely run the appliances it uses using renewable energy and should strive to have a neutral impact on the Earth’s water sources<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Greywater" title="Greywater">Greywater</a>, including water from washing machines, sinks, showers, and baths may be reused in landscape irrigation and toilets as a method of water conservation. Likewise, <a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting" title="Rainwater harvesting">rainwater harvesting</a> from storm-water runoff is also a sustainable method to conserve water use in a sustainable shelter.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_urban_drainage_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Sustainable urban drainage systems">Sustainable Urban Drainage Systems</a> replicate the natural systems that clean water in wildlife and implement them in a city’s drainage system so as to minimize contaminated water and unnatural rates of runoff into the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>See related articles in: <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">LEED</a> (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Power">Power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ombri%C3%A8re_SUDI_-_Sustainable_Urban_Design_%26_Innovation.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Ombri%C3%A8re_SUDI_-_Sustainable_Urban_Design_%26_Innovation.jpg/300px-Ombri%C3%A8re_SUDI_-_Sustainable_Urban_Design_%26_Innovation.jpg" width="300" height="235" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Ombri%C3%A8re_SUDI_-_Sustainable_Urban_Design_%26_Innovation.jpg/450px-Ombri%C3%A8re_SUDI_-_Sustainable_Urban_Design_%26_Innovation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Ombri%C3%A8re_SUDI_-_Sustainable_Urban_Design_%26_Innovation.jpg/600px-Ombri%C3%A8re_SUDI_-_Sustainable_Urban_Design_%26_Innovation.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3232" data-file-height="2532" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ombri%C3%A8re_SUDI_-_Sustainable_Urban_Design_%26_Innovation.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_design" title="Sustainable design">Sustainable urban design</a> and <a href="/wiki/Innovation" title="Innovation">innovation</a>: Photovoltaic ombrière SUDI is an autonomous and mobile station that replenishes energy for electric vehicles using <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>As mentioned under Shelter, some sustainable households may choose to produce their own renewable energy, while others may choose to purchase it through the grid from a power company that harnesses sustainable sources (also mentioned previously are the methods of metering the production and consumption of electricity in a household). Purchasing sustainable energy, however, may simply not be possible in some locations due to its limited availability. 6 out of the 50 states in the US do not offer green energy, for example. For those that do, its consumers typically buy a fixed amount or a percentage of their monthly consumption from a company of their choice and the bought green energy is fed into the entire national grid. Technically, in this case, the green energy is not being fed directly to the household that buys it.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> In this case, it is possible that the amount of green electricity that the buying household receives is a small fraction of their total incoming electricity. This may or may not depend on the amount being purchased. The purpose of buying green electricity is to support their utility’s effort in producing sustainable energy.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Producing sustainable energy on an individual household or community basis is much more flexible, but can still be limited in the richness of the sources that the location may afford (some locations may not be rich in renewable energy sources while others may have an abundance of it).
</p><p>When generating renewable energy and feeding it back into the grid (in participating countries such as the US and Germany), producing households are typically paid at least the full standard electricity rate by their utility and are also given separate renewable energy credits that they can then sell to their utility, additionally (utilities are interested in buying these renewable energy credits because it allows them to claim that they produce renewable energy). In some special cases, producing households may be paid up to four times the standard electricity rate, but this is not common.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Solar_panels_in_Ogiinuur.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Solar_panels_in_Ogiinuur.jpg/250px-Solar_panels_in_Ogiinuur.jpg" width="250" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Solar_panels_in_Ogiinuur.jpg/375px-Solar_panels_in_Ogiinuur.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Solar_panels_in_Ogiinuur.jpg/500px-Solar_panels_in_Ogiinuur.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2030" data-file-height="1218" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Solar_panels_in_Ogiinuur.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An installation of <a href="/wiki/Solar_panel" title="Solar panel">solar panels</a> in rural Mongolia</div></div></div>
<p>Solar power harnesses the energy of the sun to make electricity. Two typical methods for converting solar energy into electricity are <a href="/wiki/Solar_cells" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar cells">photo-voltaic cells</a> that are organized into panels and <a href="/wiki/Concentrated_solar_power" title="Concentrated solar power">concentrated solar power</a>, which uses mirrors to concentrate sunlight to either heat a fluid that runs an <a href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical generator">electrical generator</a> via a <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">steam turbine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engine</a>, or to simply cast onto photo-voltaic cells.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2007_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2007-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The energy created by photo-voltaic cells is a <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">direct current</a> and has to be converted to <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">alternating current</a> before it can be used in a household. At this point, users can choose to either store this direct current in batteries for later use, or use an AC/DC inverter for immediate use. To get the best out of a solar panel, the <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_incidence_(optics)" title="Angle of incidence (optics)">angle of incidence</a> of the sun should be between 20 and 50 degrees. Solar power via photo-voltaic cells are usually the most expensive method to harnessing renewable energy, but is falling in price as technology advances and public interest increases. It has the advantages of being portable, easy to use on an individual basis, readily available for government grants and incentives, and being flexible regarding location (though it is most efficient when used in hot, arid areas since they tend to be the most sunny).<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2007_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2007-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> For those that are lucky, affordable rental schemes may be found.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Concentrated solar power plants are typically used on more of a community scale rather than an individual household scale, because of the amount of energy they are able to harness but can be done on an individual scale with a <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_reflector" title="Parabolic reflector">parabolic reflector</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2007_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2007-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Jeffery,_Yvonne_2008_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jeffery,_Yvonne_2008-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Solar_thermal_energy" title="Solar thermal energy">Solar thermal energy</a> is harnessed by collecting direct heat from the sun. One of the most common ways that this method is used by households is through <a href="/wiki/Solar_water_heating" title="Solar water heating">solar water heating</a>. In a broad perspective, these systems involve well insulated tanks for storage and collectors, are either passive or active systems (active systems have pumps that continuously circulate water through the collectors and storage tank) and, in active systems, involve either directly heating the water that will be used or heating a non-freezing heat-transfer fluid that then heats the water that will be used. Passive systems are cheaper than active systems since they do not require a pumping system (instead, they take advantage of the natural movement of hot water rising above cold water to cycle the water being used through the collector and storage tank).<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other methods of harnessing solar power are <a href="/wiki/Solar_combisystem" title="Solar combisystem">solar space heating</a> (for heating internal building spaces), solar drying (for drying wood chips, fruits, grains, etc.), <a href="/wiki/Solar_cookers" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar cookers">solar cookers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_distillation" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar distillation">solar distillers</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Passive_solar" class="mw-redirect" title="Passive solar">passive solar</a> technologies (simply, harnessing sunlight without any mechanical means).
</p><p>Wind power is harnessed through turbines, set on tall towers (typically 20’ or 6m with 10‘ or 3m diameter blades for an individual household‘s needs) that power a generator that creates electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_40-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2007_43-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2007-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> They typically require an average of wind speed of 9&#160;mi/hr (14&#160;km/hr) to be worth their investment (as prescribed by the US Department of Energy), and are capable of paying for themselves within their lifetimes. Wind turbines in urban areas usually need to be mounted at least 30’ (10m) in the air to receive enough wind and to be void of nearby obstructions (such as neighboring buildings). Mounting a wind turbine may also require permission from authorities. Wind turbines have been criticized for the noise they produce, their appearance, and the argument that they can affect the migratory patterns of birds (their blades obstruct passage in the sky). Wind turbines are much more feasible for those living in rural areas<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_40-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> and are one of the most cost-effective forms of renewable energy per kilowatt, approaching the cost of fossil fuels, and have quick paybacks.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2007_43-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2007-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For those that have a body of water flowing at an adequate speed (or falling from an adequate height) on their property, <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectricity</a> may be an option. On a large scale, hydroelectricity, in the form of dams, has adverse environmental and social impacts. When on a small scale, however, in the form of single turbines, hydroelectricity is very sustainable. Single water turbines or even a group of single turbines are not environmentally or socially disruptive. On an individual household basis, single turbines are the probably the only economically feasible route (but can have high paybacks and is one of the most efficient methods of renewable energy production). It is more common for an eco-village to use this method rather than a singular household.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_40-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Geothermal energy production involves harnessing the hot water or steam below the earth’s surface, in reservoirs, to produce energy. Because the hot water or steam that is used is reinjected back into the reservoir, this source is considered sustainable. However, those that plan on getting their electricity from this source should be aware that there is controversy over the lifespan of each geothermal reservoir as some believe that their lifespans are naturally limited (they cool down over time, making geothermal energy production there eventually impossible). This method is often large scale as the system required to harness geothermal energy can be complex and requires deep drilling equipment. There do exist small individual scale geothermal operations, however, which harness reservoirs very close to the Earth’s surface, avoiding the need for extensive drilling and sometimes even taking advantage of lakes or ponds where there is already a depression. In this case, the heat is captured and sent to a <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heat_pump" title="Geothermal heat pump">geothermal heat pump</a> system located inside the shelter or facility that needs it (often, this heat is used directly to warm a greenhouse during the colder months).<sup id="cite_ref-Jeffery,_Yvonne_2008_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jeffery,_Yvonne_2008-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Although geothermal energy is available everywhere on Earth, practicality and cost-effectiveness varies, directly related to the depth required to reach reservoirs. Places such as the Philippines, Hawaii,  Alaska, Iceland, California, and Nevada have geothermal reservoirs closer to the Earth’s surface, making its production cost-effective.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2007_43-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2007-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Biomass power is created when any biological matter is burned as fuel. As with the case of using green materials in a household, it is best to use as much locally available material as possible so as to reduce the carbon footprint created by transportation. Although burning biomass for fuel releases <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, sulfur compounds, and nitrogen compounds into the atmosphere, a major concern in a sustainable lifestyle, the amount that is released is sustainable (it will not contribute to a rise in carbon dioxide levels in the atmosphere). This is because the biological matter that is being burned releases the same amount of carbon dioxide that it consumed during its lifetime.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_40-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2007_43-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2007-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> However, burning <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> and bioethanol (see <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a>) when created from virgin material, is increasingly controversial and may or may not be considered sustainable because it inadvertently increases global poverty, the clearing of more land for new agriculture fields (the source of the biofuel is also the same source of food), and may use unsustainable growing methods (such as the use of environmentally harmful pesticides and fertilizers).<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_40-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2007_43-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2007-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Digestion of organic material to produce methane is becoming an increasingly popular method of biomass energy production. Materials such as waste sludge can be digested to release methane gas that can then be burnt to produce electricity. Methane gas is also a natural by-product of landfills, full of decomposing waste, and can be harnessed here to produce electricity as well. The advantage in burning methane gas is that is prevents the methane from being released into the atmosphere, exacerbating the greenhouse effect. Although this method of biomass energy production is typically large scale (done in landfills), it can be done on a smaller individual or community scale as well.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2007_43-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2007-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Food">Food</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Food">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Globally, food accounts for 48% and 70% of household environmental impacts on land and water resources respectively, with consumption of meat, dairy and processed food rising quickly with income<sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ivanova_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ivanova-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></sup>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impacts_of_industrial_agriculture">Environmental impacts of industrial agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Environmental impacts of industrial agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Industrial_agriculture" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial agriculture">Industrial agricultural production</a> is highly resource and energy intensive.  Industrial agriculture systems typically require heavy <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>, extensive <a href="/wiki/Pesticide_application" title="Pesticide application">pesticide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer application</a>, intensive <a href="/wiki/Tillage" title="Tillage">tillage</a>, concentrated <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monoculture</a> production, and other continual inputs. As a result of these industrial farming conditions, today’s mounting environmental stresses are further exacerbated. These stresses include: declining <a href="/wiki/Water_table" title="Water table">water tables</a>, chemical <a href="/wiki/Leaching_(agriculture)" title="Leaching (agriculture)">leaching</a>, chemical <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Land_degradation" title="Land degradation">land degradation</a>, loss in <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, and other ecological concerns.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conventional_food_distribution_and_long_distance_transport">Conventional food distribution and long distance transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Conventional food distribution and long distance transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Conventional" class="mw-redirect" title="Conventional">Conventional</a> food distribution and long distance transport are additionally <a href="/wiki/Resource" title="Resource">resource</a> and <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> exhaustive.  Substantial climate-disrupting <a href="/wiki/Carbon_emissions" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon emissions">carbon emissions</a>, boosted by the transport of food over long distances, are of growing concern as the world faces such <a href="/wiki/Global_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Global crisis">global crisis</a> as <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resource</a> depletion, <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">peak oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> “The average American meal currently costs about 1500 miles, and takes about 10 <a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">calories</a> of oil and other <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> to produce a single calorie of food.”<sup id="cite_ref-Astyk,_Sharon_2008_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Astyk,_Sharon_2008-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_and_seasonal_foods">Local and seasonal foods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Local and seasonal foods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A more sustainable means of acquiring food is to purchase locally and seasonally. Buying food from local farmers reduces <a href="/wiki/Carbon_output" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon output">carbon output</a>, caused by long-distance food transport, and stimulates the local economy.<sup id="cite_ref-Astyk,_Sharon_2008_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Astyk,_Sharon_2008-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Local, small-scale farming operations also typically utilize more sustainable methods of agriculture than conventional industrial farming systems such as decreased tillage, <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_cycling" class="mw-redirect" title="Nutrient cycling">nutrient cycling</a>, fostered biodiversity and reduced chemical pesticide and fertilizer applications.<sup id="cite_ref-Shiva,_Vandana_2000_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shiva,_Vandana_2000-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Adapting a more regional, seasonally based diet is more sustainable as it entails purchasing less energy and resource demanding produce that naturally grow within a local area and require no long-distance transport. These vegetables and fruits are also grown and harvested within their suitable <a href="/wiki/Growing_season" title="Growing season">growing season</a>. Thus, <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_food" title="Seasonal food">seasonal food</a> farming does not require energy intensive <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a> production, extensive irrigation, plastic packaging and long-distance transport from importing non-regional foods, and other environmental stressors.<sup id="cite_ref-Seymour,_John_2003_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seymour,_John_2003-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Local, seasonal produce is typically fresher, unprocessed and argued to be more nutritious. Local produce also contains less to no chemical residues from applications required for long-distance shipping and handling.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Farmers%27_market" title="Farmers&#39; market">Farmers' markets</a>, public events where local small-scale farmers gather and sell their produce, are a good source for obtaining local food and knowledge about local farming productions.  As well as promoting localization of food, farmers markets are a central gathering place for community interaction.<sup id="cite_ref-Todd,_J_1994_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Todd,_J_1994-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>  Another way to become involved in regional food distribution is by joining a local <a href="/wiki/Community-supported_agriculture" title="Community-supported agriculture">community-supported agriculture</a> (CSA). A CSA consists of a community of growers and consumers who pledge to support a farming operation while equally sharing the risks and benefits of food production. CSA's usually involve a system of weekly pick-ups of locally farmed vegetables and fruits, sometimes including dairy products, meat and special food items such as baked goods.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Considering the previously noted rising environmental crisis, the United States and much of the world is facing immense vulnerability to famine. Local food production ensures food security if potential transportation disruptions and climatic, economical, and sociopolitical disasters were to occur.<sup id="cite_ref-Astyk,_Sharon_2008_49-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Astyk,_Sharon_2008-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reducing_meat_consumption">Reducing meat consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reducing meat consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Industrial meat production also involves high environmental costs such as <a href="/wiki/Land_degradation" title="Land degradation">land degradation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a> and depletion of <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a>, especially pertaining to water and food.<sup id="cite_ref-Seymour,_John_2003_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seymour,_John_2003-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Mass meat production increase the amount of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_methane" title="Atmospheric methane">methane in the atmosphere</a>.  For more information on the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_meat_production" title="Environmental impact of meat production">environmental impact of meat production</a> and consumption, see the <a href="/wiki/Ethics_of_eating_meat" title="Ethics of eating meat">ethics of eating meat</a>. Reducing meat consumption, perhaps to a few meals a week, or adopting a vegetarian or vegan diet, alleviates the demand for environmentally damaging industrial meat production. Buying and consuming organically raised, <a href="/wiki/Free_range" title="Free range">free range</a> or <a href="/wiki/Grass_fed" class="mw-redirect" title="Grass fed">grass fed meat</a> is another alternative towards more sustainable meat consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-Shiva,_Vandana_2000_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shiva,_Vandana_2000-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_farming">Organic farming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Organic farming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Purchasing and supporting organic products is another fundamental contribution to sustainable living. <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">Organic farming</a> is a rapidly emerging trend in the food industry and in the web of sustainability. According to the <a href="/wiki/USDA" class="mw-redirect" title="USDA">USDA</a> <a href="/wiki/National_Organic_Standards_Board" title="National Organic Standards Board">National Organic Standards Board</a> (NOSB), <a href="/wiki/Organic_agriculture" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic agriculture">organic agriculture</a> is defined as "an ecological production management system that promotes and enhances biodiversity, biological cycles, and soil biological activity. It is based on minimal use of off-farm inputs and on management practices that restore, maintain, or enhance ecological harmony. The primary goal of organic agriculture is to optimize the health and productivity of interdependent communities of soil life, plants, animals and people." Upon sustaining these goals, organic agriculture uses techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">crop rotation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permaculture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">compost</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_manure" title="Green manure">green manure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biological_pest_control" title="Biological pest control">biological pest control</a>. In addition, organic farming prohibits or strictly limits the use of manufactured fertilizers and pesticides, <a href="/wiki/Plant_growth_regulators" class="mw-redirect" title="Plant growth regulators">plant growth regulators</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Hormones" class="mw-redirect" title="Hormones">hormones</a>, livestock <a href="/wiki/Antibiotics" class="mw-redirect" title="Antibiotics">antibiotics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Food_additives" class="mw-redirect" title="Food additives">food additives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_organisms" class="mw-redirect" title="Genetically modified organisms">genetically modified organisms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Organically farmed products include vegetables, fruit, grains, herbs, meat, dairy, eggs, fibers, and flowers. See <a href="/wiki/Organic_certification" title="Organic certification">organic certification</a> for more information.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_gardening">Urban gardening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Urban gardening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ganjiaxiang_-_edible_landscaping_-_P1070640.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Ganjiaxiang_-_edible_landscaping_-_P1070640.JPG/220px-Ganjiaxiang_-_edible_landscaping_-_P1070640.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Ganjiaxiang_-_edible_landscaping_-_P1070640.JPG/330px-Ganjiaxiang_-_edible_landscaping_-_P1070640.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Ganjiaxiang_-_edible_landscaping_-_P1070640.JPG/440px-Ganjiaxiang_-_edible_landscaping_-_P1070640.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1920" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ganjiaxiang_-_edible_landscaping_-_P1070640.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"Edible landscaping": a vegetable garden incorporated by the local residents into a roadside park. <a href="/wiki/Qixia_District" title="Qixia District">Qixia District</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nanjing" title="Nanjing">Nanjing</a>, China</div></div></div>
<p>In addition to local, small-scale farms, there has been a recent emergence in <a href="/wiki/Urban_agriculture" title="Urban agriculture">urban agriculture</a> expanding from <a href="/wiki/Community_gardens" class="mw-redirect" title="Community gardens">community gardens</a> to private <a href="/wiki/Home_gardens" class="mw-redirect" title="Home gardens">home gardens</a>. With this trend, both farmers and ordinary people are becoming involved in <a href="/wiki/Food_production" class="mw-redirect" title="Food production">food production</a>. A network of urban farming systems helps to further ensure regional food security and encourages self-sufficiency and cooperative interdependence within communities.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> With every bite of food raised from urban gardens, negative environmental impacts are reduced in numerous ways. For instance, vegetables and fruits raised within small-scale gardens and farms are not grown with tremendous applications of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_fertilizer" class="mw-redirect" title="Nitrogen fertilizer">nitrogen fertilizer</a> required for industrial agricultural operations. The nitrogen fertilizers cause toxic chemical leaching and runoff that enters our water tables. Nitrogen fertilizer also produces <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a>, a more damaging <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> than <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. Local, community-grown food also requires no imported, long-distance transport which further depletes our <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> reserves.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> In developing more efficiency per land acre, urban gardens can be started in a wide variety of areas: in vacant lots, public parks, private yards, church and school yards, on roof tops (<a href="/wiki/Roof-top_garden" class="mw-redirect" title="Roof-top garden">roof-top gardens</a>), and many other places.  Communities can work together in changing zoning limitations in order for public and private gardens to be permissible.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> Aesthetically pleasing <a href="/wiki/Edible_landscaping" class="mw-redirect" title="Edible landscaping">edible landscaping</a> plants can also be incorporated into city landscaping such as blueberry bushes, grapevines trained on an arbor, pecan trees, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-Todd,_J_1994_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Todd,_J_1994-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> With as small a scale as home or community farming, sustainable and organic farming methods can easily be utilized. Such sustainable, organic farming techniques include: <a href="/wiki/Composting" class="mw-redirect" title="Composting">composting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biological_pest_control" title="Biological pest control">biological pest control</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">crop rotation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mulching" class="mw-redirect" title="Mulching">mulching</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drip_irrigation" title="Drip irrigation">drip irrigation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nutrient_cycling" class="mw-redirect" title="Nutrient cycling">nutrient cycling</a> and <a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permaculture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> For more information on sustainable farming systems, see <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable agriculture</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_preservation_and_storage">Food preservation and storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Food preservation and storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Preserving and storing foods reduces reliance on long-distance transported food and the market industry. Home-grown foods can be preserved and stored outside of their growing season and continually consumed throughout the year, enhancing self-sufficiency and independence from the supermarket. Food can be preserved and saved by <a href="/wiki/Dehydration_(food)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dehydration (food)">dehydration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Freezing_(food)" class="mw-redirect" title="Freezing (food)">freezing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_packing" title="Vacuum packing">vacuum packing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canning" title="Canning">canning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bottling" class="mw-redirect" title="Bottling">bottling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pickling" title="Pickling">pickling</a> and jellying.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> For more information, see <a href="/wiki/Food_preservation" title="Food preservation">food preservation</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_transport" title="Sustainable transport">Sustainable transport</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:20120623_ChevyVolt-Chicago-Compost.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/20120623_ChevyVolt-Chicago-Compost.jpg/220px-20120623_ChevyVolt-Chicago-Compost.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/20120623_ChevyVolt-Chicago-Compost.jpg/330px-20120623_ChevyVolt-Chicago-Compost.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/20120623_ChevyVolt-Chicago-Compost.jpg/440px-20120623_ChevyVolt-Chicago-Compost.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3872" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:20120623_ChevyVolt-Chicago-Compost.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Carsharing" title="Carsharing">carsharing</a> <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_hybrid" title="Plug-in hybrid">plug-in hybrid</a> vehicle being used to drop off <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">compost</a> at an urban facility in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>With rising <a href="/wiki/Peak_oil" title="Peak oil">peak oil</a> concerns, <a href="/wiki/Climate_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Climate warming">climate warming</a> exacerbated by <a href="/wiki/Carbon_emissions" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon emissions">carbon emissions</a> and high energy prices, the conventional <a href="/wiki/Automobile_industry" class="mw-redirect" title="Automobile industry">automobile industry</a> is becoming less and less feasible to the conversation of sustainability. Revisions of <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">urban transport</a> systems that foster mobility, low-cost transportation and healthier urban environments are needed. Such urban transport systems should consist of a combination of <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">rail transport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bus_transport" class="mw-redirect" title="Bus transport">bus transport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bicycle" title="Bicycle">bicycle</a> pathways and <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian" title="Pedestrian">pedestrian</a> <a href="/wiki/Walkways" class="mw-redirect" title="Walkways">walkways</a>. Public transport systems such as underground rail systems and bus transit systems shift huge numbers of people away from reliance on car <a href="/wiki/Mobilization" title="Mobilization">mobilization</a> and dramatically reduce the rate of carbon emissions caused by automobile transport.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown,_Lester_R._2009_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown,_Lester_R._2009-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Carpooling" class="mw-redirect" title="Carpooling">Carpooling</a> is another alternative for reducing oil consumption and carbon emissions by transit.
</p><p>In comparison to automobiles, bicycles are a paragon of energy efficient personal transportation with the bicycle roughly 50 times more energy efficient than driving.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Bicycles increase mobility while alleviating <a href="/wiki/Traffic_congestion" title="Traffic congestion">congestion</a>, lowering <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air</a> and <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise pollution</a>, and increasing <a href="/wiki/Physical_exercise" class="mw-redirect" title="Physical exercise">physical exercise</a>. Most importantly, they do not emit climate-disturbing <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown,_Lester_R._2009_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown,_Lester_R._2009-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bike-sharing" class="mw-redirect" title="Bike-sharing">Bike-sharing</a> programs are beginning to boom throughout the world and are modeled in leading cities such as <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a> and <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Bike-sharing programs offer <a href="/wiki/Kiosks" class="mw-redirect" title="Kiosks">kiosks</a> and docking stations that supply hundreds to thousands of bikes for rental throughout a city through small deposits or affordable memberships.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A recent boom has occurred in <a href="/wiki/Electric_bicycles" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric bicycles">electric bikes</a> especially in China and other Asian countries. Electric bikes are similar to <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_hybrid" title="Plug-in hybrid">plug-in hybrid</a> vehicles in that they are battery powered and can be plugged into the provincial <a href="/wiki/Electric_grid" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric grid">electric grid</a> for recharging as needed. In contrast to plug-in hybrid cars, electric bikes do not directly use any <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>.  Adequate sustainable urban transportation is dependent upon proper city <a href="/wiki/Infrastructure" title="Infrastructure">infrastructure</a> and planning that incorporates efficient public transit along with bicycle and pedestrian-friendly pathways.<sup id="cite_ref-Brown,_Lester_R._2009_61-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brown,_Lester_R._2009-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>  Patrick Maria Johnson was the founder of this.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water">Water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Accuracy dispute" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Ambox_contradict.svg/40px-Ambox_contradict.svg.png" title="Accuracy dispute" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Ambox_contradict.svg/60px-Ambox_contradict.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Ambox_contradict.svg/80px-Ambox_contradict.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="620" data-file-height="620" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article <b>appears to contradict the  article <a href="/wiki/Water_efficiency" title="Water efficiency">Water efficiency</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please see discussion on the linked <a href="/wiki/Talk:Sustainable_living" title="Talk:Sustainable living">talk page</a>.</span>  <small><i>(December 2013)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Water_efficiency" title="Water efficiency">Water efficiency</a></div>
<p>A major factor of sustainable living involves that which no human can live without, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>. Unsustainable water use has far reaching implications for humankind.  Currently, humans use one-fourth of the Earth’s total fresh water in natural circulation, and over half the accessible <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hawken,_Paul_1999_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hawken,_Paul_1999-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">population growth</a> and water demand is ever increasing. Thus, it is necessary to use available water more efficiently. In sustainable living, one can use water more sustainably through a series of simple, everyday measures.  These measures involve considering indoor <a href="/wiki/Home_appliance" title="Home appliance">home appliance</a> efficiency, outdoor water use, and daily water use awareness.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indoor_home_appliances">Indoor home appliances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Indoor home appliances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Housing and <a href="/wiki/Commercial_buildings" class="mw-redirect" title="Commercial buildings">commercial buildings</a> account for 12 percent of America’s freshwater withdrawals.<sup id="cite_ref-Hawken,_Paul_1999_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hawken,_Paul_1999-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>  A typical American single family home uses about 70 US gallons (260&#160;L) per person per day indoors.<sup id="cite_ref-Hawken,_Paul_1999_65-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hawken,_Paul_1999-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> This use can be reduced by simple alterations in behavior and upgrades to <a href="/wiki/Home_appliance" title="Home appliance">appliance</a> quality.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Toilets">Toilets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Toilets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Toilets" class="mw-redirect" title="Toilets">Toilets</a> accounted for almost 30% of residential indoor water use in the United States in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> One flush of a standard U.S. toilet requires more water than most individuals, and many families, in the world use for all their needs in an entire day.<sup id="cite_ref-HawkenPaul_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HawkenPaul-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
A home’s toilet water sustainability can be improved in one of two ways: improving the current toilet or installing a more efficient toilet.  To improve the current toilet, one possible method is to put weighted plastic bottles in the toilet tank.  Also, there are inexpensive tank banks or float booster available for purchase.  A tank bank is a plastic bag to be filled with water and hung in the toilet tank. A <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.floatbooster.com/Product.html">float booster</a> attaches underneath the float ball of pre-1986 three and a half gallon capacity toilets.  It allows these toilets to operate at the same valve and float setting but significantly reduces their water level, saving between one and one and a third gallons of water per flush.  A major waste of water in existing toilets is leaks.  A slow toilet leak is undetectable to the eye, but can waste hundreds of gallons each month.  One way to check this is to put food dye in the tank, and to see if the water in the toilet bowl turns the same color.  In the event of a leaky flapper, one can replace it with an adjustable toilet flapper, which allows self-adjustment of the amount of water per flush.
</p><p>If installing a new toilet there are a number of options to obtain the most water efficient model. A <a href="/wiki/Low_flush_toilet" class="mw-redirect" title="Low flush toilet">low flush toilet</a> uses one to two gallons per flush.  Traditionally, toilets use three to five gallons per flush. If an eighteen-liter per flush toilet is removed and a six-liter per flush toilet is put in its place, 70% of the water flushed will be saved while the overall indoor water use by will be reduced by 30%.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> It is possible to have a toilet that uses no water.  A <a href="/wiki/Composting_toilet" title="Composting toilet">composting toilet</a> treats human waste through <a href="/wiki/Composting" class="mw-redirect" title="Composting">composting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dehydration" title="Dehydration">dehydration</a>, producing a valuable soil additive.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>  These toilets feature a two-compartment bowl to separate urine from feces.  The urine can be collected or sold as fertilizer.  The feces can be dried and bagged or composted.  These toilets cost scarcely more than regularly installed toilets and do not require a sewer hookup.  In addition to providing valuable fertilizer, these toilets are highly sustainable because they save sewage collection and treatment, as well as lessen agricultural costs and improve <a href="/wiki/Topsoil" title="Topsoil">topsoil</a>.
</p><p>Additionally, one can reduce toilet water sustainability by limiting total toilet flushing. For instance, instead of flushing small wastes, such as tissues, one can dispose of these items in the trash or compost.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Showers">Showers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Showers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On average, showers were 18% of U.S. indoor water use in 1999, at 6–8 US gallons (23–30&#160;L) per minute traditionally in America.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> A simple method to reduce this use is to switch to low-flow, high-performance showerheads.  These showerheads use only 1.0-1.5 gpm or less. An alternative to replacing the showerhead is to install a converter. This device arrests a running shower upon reaching the desired temperature.  <a href="/wiki/Solar_water_heater" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar water heater">Solar water heaters</a> can be used to obtain optimal water temperature, and are more sustainable because they reduce dependence on fossil fuels. To lessen excess water use, water pipes can be insulated with pre-slit foam pipe insulation. This insulation decreases hot water generation time. A simple, straightforward method to conserve water when showering is to take shorter showers.   One method to accomplish this is to turn off the water when it is not necessary (such as while lathering) and resuming the shower when water is necessary.  This can be facilitated when the plumbing or showerhead allow turning off the water without disrupting the desired temperature setting (common in the UK but not the United States).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dishwashers_and_sinks">Dishwashers and sinks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Dishwashers and sinks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On average, sinks were 15% of U.S. indoor water use in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_66-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> There are, however, easy methods to rectify excessive water loss. Available for purchase is a screw-on aerator. This device works by combining water with air thus generating a frothy substance with greater perceived volume, reducing water use by half. Additionally, there is a flip-valve available that allows flow to be turned off and back on at the previously reached temperature. Finally, a <a href="/wiki/Laminar_flow" title="Laminar flow">laminar flow</a> device creates a 1.5-2.4 gpm stream of water that reduces water use by half, but can be turned to normal water level when optimal.
</p><p>In addition to buying the above devices, one can live more sustainably by checking sinks for leaks, and fixing these links if they exist. According to the EPA, "A small drip from a worn faucet washer can waste 20 gallons of water per day, while larger leaks can waste hundreds of gallons".<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_66-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> When washing dishes by hand, it is not necessary to leave the water running for rinsing, and it is more efficient to rinse dishes simultaneously.
</p><p>On average, dishwashing consumes 1% of indoor water use.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_66-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> When using a <a href="/wiki/Dishwasher" title="Dishwasher">dishwasher</a>, water can be conserved by only running the machine when it is full.  Some have a "low flow" setting to use less water per wash cycle. Enzymatic <a href="/wiki/Detergents" class="mw-redirect" title="Detergents">detergents</a> clean dishes more efficiently and more successfully with a smaller amount of water at a lower temperature.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Washing_machines">Washing machines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Washing machines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On average, 23% of U.S. indoor water use in 1999 was due to clothes washing.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_66-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> In contrast to other machines, American <a href="/wiki/Washing_machines" class="mw-redirect" title="Washing machines">washing machines</a> have changed little to become more sustainable. A typical washing machine has a vertical-axis design, in which clothes are agitated in a tubful of water. Horizontal-axis machines, in contrast, put less water into the bottom of the rub and rotate clothes through it. These machines are more efficient in terms of soap use and clothing stability.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Outdoor_water_use">Outdoor water use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Outdoor water use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are a number of ways one can incorporate a personal <a href="/wiki/Yard" title="Yard">yard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roof" title="Roof">roof</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Garden" title="Garden">garden</a> in more sustainable living. While conserving water is a major element of sustainability, so is sequestering water.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Conserving_water">Conserving water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Conserving water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In planning a yard and garden space, it is most sustainable to consider the plants, soil, and available water.  Drought resistant shrubs, plants, and grasses require a smaller amount of water in comparison to more traditional species.  Additionally, native plants (as opposed to herbaceous perennials) will use a smaller supply of water and have a heightened resistance to plant diseases of the area.  <a href="/wiki/Xeriscaping" title="Xeriscaping">Xeriscaping</a> is a technique that selects drought-tolerant plants and accounts for endemic features such as <a href="/wiki/Slope" title="Slope">slope</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil_type" title="Soil type">soil type</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Native_plant" title="Native plant">native plant</a> range.  It can reduce landscape water use by 50 – 70%, while providing <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a> space for wildlife.  Plants on slopes help reduce <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a> by slowing and absorbing accumulated rainfall.  Grouping plants by watering needs further reduces water waste.
</p><p>After planting, placing a circumference of <a href="/wiki/Mulch" title="Mulch">mulch</a> surrounding plants functions to lessen <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>.  To do this, firmly press two to four inches of organic matter along the plant's dripline. This prevents <a href="/wiki/Water_runoff" class="mw-redirect" title="Water runoff">water runoff</a>.   When watering, consider the range of sprinklers; watering paved areas is unnecessary.  Additionally, to conserve the maximum amount of water, watering should be carried out during early mornings on non-windy days to reduce water loss to evaporation.  Drip-irrigation systems and soaker hoses are a more sustainable alternative to the traditional sprinkler system.  Drip-irrigation systems employ small gaps at standard distances in a hose, leading to the slow trickle of water droplets which percolate the soil over a protracted period.  These systems use 30 – 50% less water than conventional methods.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> Soaker hoses help to reduce water use by up to 90%.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>  They connect to a garden hose and lay along the row of plants under a layer of mulch.  A layer of <a href="/wiki/Organic_material" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic material">organic material</a> added to the soil helps to increase its absorption and water retention; previously planted areas can be covered with <a href="/wiki/Compost" title="Compost">compost</a>.
</p><p>In caring for a lawn, there are a number of measures that can increase the sustainability of lawn maintenance techniques.  A primary aspect of lawn care is watering.  To conserve water, it is important to only water when necessary, and to deep soak when watering.  Additionally, a lawn may be left to go dormant, renewing after a dry spell to its original vitality.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequestering_water">Sequestering water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Sequestering water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A common method of water sequestrations is <a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting" title="Rainwater harvesting">rainwater harvesting</a>, which incorporates the collection and storage of rain.  Primarily, the rain is obtained from a roof, and stored on the ground in catchment tanks.  Water sequestration varies based on extent, cost, and complexity.   A simple method involves a single barrel at the bottom of a downspout, while a more complex method involves multiple tanks.  It is highly sustainable to use stored water in place of purified water for activities such as irrigation and flushing toilets.  Additionally, using stored rainwater reduces the amount of runoff <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>, picked up from roofs and pavements that would normally enter streams through storm drains.  The following equation can be used to estimate annual water supply:
</p><p>Collection area (square feet) × Rainfall (inch/year) / 12 (inch/foot) = Cubic Feet of Water/Year
</p><p>Cubic Feet/Year × 7.43 (Gallons/Cubic Foot) = Gallons/year
</p><p>Note, however, this calculation does not account for losses such as evaporation or leakage.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Greywater" title="Greywater">Greywater</a> systems function in sequestering used indoor water, such as laundry, bath and sink water, and filtering it for reuse.  Greywater can be reused in <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> and toilet flushing.  There are two types of greywater systems: gravity fed manual systems and package systems.<sup id="cite_ref-greenbuildingsupply1_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenbuildingsupply1-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>  The manual systems do not require electricity but may require a larger yard space.<sup id="cite_ref-greenbuildingsupply1_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenbuildingsupply1-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>  The package systems require electricity but are self-contained and can be installed indoors.<sup id="cite_ref-greenbuildingsupply1_73-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenbuildingsupply1-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Waste">Waste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_living&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Waste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As populations and resource demands climb, waste production contributes to <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas#Greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas">emissions</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, leaching of hazardous materials into the soil and waterways, and <a href="/wiki/Methane_emissions" title="Methane emissions">methane emissions</a>.  In America alone, over the course of a decade, 500 trillion pounds of American resources will have been transformed into nonproductive wastes and gases.<sup id="cite_ref-HawkenPaul_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HawkenPaul-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> Thus, a crucial component of sustainable living is being waste conscious.  One can do this by reducing waste, reusing commodities, and recycling.
</p><p>There are a number of ways to reduce waste in sustainable living.  Two methods to reduce paper waste are canceling junk mail like <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.optoutprescreen.com/?rf=t">credit card and insurance offers</a> and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.dmachoice.org/">direct mail marketing</a> and changing monthly paper statements to paperless emails.  Junk mail alone accounted for 1.72 million tons of landfill waste in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> Another method to reduce waste is to buy in bulk, reducing packaging materials. Preventing food waste can limit the amount of organic waste sent to landfills producing the powerful greenhouse gas methane.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> Another example of waste reduction involves being cognizant of purchasing excessive amounts when buying materials with limited use like cans of paint. Non-hazardous or less hazardous alternatives can also limit the toxicity of waste.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By reusing materials, one lives more sustainably by not contributing to the addition of waste to landfills. Reusing saves <a href="/wiki/Natural_resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural resources">natural resources</a> by decreasing the necessity of <a href="/wiki/Raw_material" title="Raw material">raw material</a> extraction. For example, reusable bags can reduce the amount of waste created by grocery shopping eliminating the need to create and ship plastic bags and the need to manage their disposal and recycling or polluting effects.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">Recycling</a>, a process that breaks down used items into raw materials to make new materials, is a particularly useful means of contributing to the renewal of goods.  Recycling incorporates three primary processes; collection and processing, manufacturing, and purchasing recycled products.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>  A natural example of recycling involves using food waste as compost to enrich the quality of soil, which can be carried out at home or locally with community <a href="/wiki/Composting" class="mw-redirect" title="Composting">composting</a>. An offshoot of recycling, <a href="/wiki/Upcycling" title="Upcycling">upcycling</a>, strives to convert material into something of similar or greater value in its second life.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> By integrating measures of reusing, reducing, and recycling one can effectively reduce personal waste and use materials in a more sustainable manner.
</p>
