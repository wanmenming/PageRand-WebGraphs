<p><b>Spider mites</b> are members of the <a href="/wiki/Acari" title="Acari">Acari</a> (mite) family <b>Tetranychidae</b>, which includes about 1,200 species.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> They generally live on the undersides of <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> of <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, where they may spin protective silk <a href="/wiki/Spider_web" title="Spider web">webs</a>, and they can cause damage by puncturing the plant <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> to feed.<sup id="cite_ref-Saito_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saito-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Spider mites are known to feed on several hundred <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of plants.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spider_mite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Spider mites are less than 1&#160;mm (0.04&#160;in) in size and vary in color. They lay small, spherical, initially transparent <a href="/wiki/Egg_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Egg (biology)">eggs</a> and many species spin silk webbing to help protect the <a href="/wiki/Colony_(biology)" title="Colony (biology)">colony</a> from <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predators</a>; they get the "spider" part of their common name from this webbing.<sup id="cite_ref-Saito_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saito-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle">Life cycle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spider_mite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Life cycle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tetranychidae.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Tetranychidae.jpg/220px-Tetranychidae.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Tetranychidae.jpg/330px-Tetranychidae.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Tetranychidae.jpg/440px-Tetranychidae.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2848" data-file-height="2136" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tetranychidae.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Spider mites on a <a href="/wiki/Lemon" title="Lemon">lemon</a> plant</div></div></div>
<p>Hot, dry conditions are often associated with population build-up of spider mites. Under optimal conditions (approximately 27 °C), the two-spotted spider mite can hatch in as little as 3 days, and become sexually mature in as little as 5 days. One female can lay up to 20 eggs per day and can live for 2 to 4 weeks, laying hundreds of eggs. This accelerated reproductive rate allows spider mite populations to adapt quickly to <a href="/wiki/Pesticide_resistance" title="Pesticide resistance">resist pesticides</a>, so chemical control methods can become somewhat ineffectual when the same pesticide is used over a prolonged period.<sup id="cite_ref-IFAS_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IFAS-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Spider mites, like <a href="/wiki/Hymenoptera" title="Hymenoptera">hymenopterans</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Scale_insect" title="Scale insect">scale insects</a>, are <a href="/wiki/Arrhenotoky" title="Arrhenotoky">arrhenotochous</a>: females are <a href="/wiki/Diploid" class="mw-redirect" title="Diploid">diploid</a> and males are <a href="/wiki/Haploid" class="mw-redirect" title="Haploid">haploid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> When mated, females avoid the <a href="/wiki/Fecundation" class="mw-redirect" title="Fecundation">fecundation</a> of some eggs to produce males. Fertilized eggs produce diploid females. Unmated, unfertilized females still lay eggs that originate exclusively <a href="/wiki/Haploid" class="mw-redirect" title="Haploid">haploid</a> males.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genera">Genera</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spider_mite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Genera">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The best known member of the group is <i><a href="/wiki/Tetranychus_urticae" title="Tetranychus urticae">Tetranychus urticae</a></i>, which has a <a href="/wiki/Cosmopolitan_distribution" title="Cosmopolitan distribution">cosmopolitan distribution</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Raworth_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raworth-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and attacks a wide range of plants, including <a href="/wiki/Capsicum" title="Capsicum">peppers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tomato" title="Tomato">tomatoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">potatoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bean" title="Bean">beans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cannabis" title="Cannabis">cannabis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Strawberry" title="Strawberry">strawberries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IFAS_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IFAS-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Other species which can be important pests of commercial plants include <i><a href="/wiki/Panonychus_ulmi" title="Panonychus ulmi">Panonychus ulmi</a></i> (fruit tree red spider mite) and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Panonychus_citri&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Panonychus citri (page does not exist)">Panonychus citri</a></i> (citrus red mite).
</p><p>The family is divided into these <a href="/wiki/Subfamily" title="Subfamily">subfamilies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tribe_(biology)" title="Tribe (biology)">tribes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genera" class="mw-redirect" title="Genera">genera</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Countermeasures">Countermeasures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spider_mite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Countermeasures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neem_oil">Neem oil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spider_mite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Neem oil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Neem_oil" title="Neem oil">Neem oil</a> may provide control, when combined with a suitable surfactant and diluted with water. As with chemical control, repeated applications are required.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Predatory_mites">Predatory mites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spider_mite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Predatory mites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Predatory mites of the <a href="/wiki/Phytoseiidae" title="Phytoseiidae">Phytoseiidae</a> family, including <i><a href="/wiki/Phytoseiulus_persimilis" class="mw-redirect" title="Phytoseiulus persimilis">Phytoseiulus persimilis</a></i>, eat adult mites, their eggs, and all developmental stages between.<sup id="cite_ref-IFAS_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IFAS-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Predatory mites can consume as many as 5 adult spider mites per day, or 20 eggs per day.<sup id="cite_ref-IFAS_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IFAS-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Insecticidal_soap">Insecticidal soap</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spider_mite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Insecticidal soap">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Insecticidal_soap" title="Insecticidal soap">Insecticidal soap</a> spray is effective against spider mites. It is commercially available or can be made of certain types of household soap. However, since it will also kill predatory mites, its use is not recommended if the latter are present.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harpin_Alpha_Beta">Harpin Alpha Beta</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spider_mite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Harpin Alpha Beta">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In some cases, the application of Harpin Alpha Beta protein may help in the treatment and prevention of infestation by stimulating the plant's natural defenses, restoring sap sugar levels and encouraging replacement of damaged tissues.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This affects the spider mites' ability to down-regulate the immune response of a plant.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acaricides">Acaricides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spider_mite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Acaricides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Acaricides" class="mw-redirect" title="Acaricides">Acaricides</a> are applied to crops to control spider mites. They can be either <a href="/wiki/Insecticide#Type_of_activity_systemic" title="Insecticide">systemic</a> or non-systemic in nature and can be persistent by providing residual activity for over a month. Drawbacks include high potential for development of <a href="/wiki/Pesticide_resistance" title="Pesticide resistance">resistance</a> in mite populations, as has been observed in previous generations of miticides, and toxicity of some miticides towards fish. Thus proper selection, precautions and application are required to minimize risks.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_conditions">Environmental conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spider_mite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Environmental conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Temporarily modifying environmental conditions has proven an effective method for insect pest control including spider mites. Generally dramatically decreased oxygen and increased carbon dioxide concentrations at elevated temperatures can lead to mortality at all developmental stages. However mild CO<sub>2</sub> enrichment has been shown to in fact increase mite reproduction.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> One study determined a concentration of 0.4% O<sub>2</sub> and 20% CO<sub>2</sub> gave a <a href="/wiki/LT50" title="LT50">LT</a><sub>99</sub> (time to 99% mortality) of 113h at 20&#160;°C and 15.5h at 40&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Another study reported 100% mortality of various stages of the two spotted spidermite using 60% CO<sub>2</sub> and 20% O<sub>2</sub> at 30&#160;°C for 16h.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
Advantages would include decreased ability for resistance development compared to miticides and potential ease of application while drawbacks might include sensitivity of the plant to the conditions, feasibility of application, and human safety.
</p>
