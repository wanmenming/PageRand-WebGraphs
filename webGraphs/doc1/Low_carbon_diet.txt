<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Low-carbon diet</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Low_carbon_diet&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Low carbon diet">Low carbon diet</a>)</span></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Low-carb_diet" class="mw-redirect" title="Low-carb diet">Low-carb diet</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>low-carbon diet</b> refers to making lifestyle choices to reduce the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions (GHGe) resulting from consumption decisions.<sup id="cite_ref-one_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-one-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  It is estimated that the <a href="/wiki/U.S._food_system" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. food system">U.S. food system</a> is responsible for at least 20 percent of <a href="/wiki/U.S._greenhouse_gases" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. greenhouse gases">U.S. greenhouse gases</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-three_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-three-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This estimate may be low, as it counts only direct sources of GHGe. Indirect sources, such as demand for products from other countries, are often not counted. A low-carbon diet minimizes the emissions released from the production, <a href="/wiki/Packaging" class="mw-redirect" title="Packaging">packaging</a>, <a href="/wiki/Food_processing" title="Food processing">processing</a>, transport, preparation and waste of food. Major tenets of a low-carbon diet include eating less industrial <a href="/wiki/Meat" title="Meat">meat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dairy" title="Dairy">dairy</a>, eating less industrially produced food in general, eating food grown locally and seasonally, eating less processed and packaged foods and reducing waste from food by proper portion size, <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a> or <a href="/wiki/Composting" class="mw-redirect" title="Composting">composting</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-two_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-two-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overall_trends">Overall trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-carbon_diet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overall trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A 2014 study into the real-life diets of British people estimated their <a href="/wiki/GHG_footprint" class="mw-redirect" title="GHG footprint">greenhouse gas footprints</a> in terms of kilograms of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_equivalent" title="Carbon dioxide equivalent">carbon dioxide equivalent</a> per day:<sup id="cite_ref-scarborough-etal-2014_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scarborough-etal-2014-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>7.19 for high meat-eaters (≥100 g/day)</li>
<li>5.63 for medium meat-eaters (50–99 g/day)</li>
<li>4.67 for low meat-eaters (&lt;50 g/day)</li>
<li>3.91 for fish-eaters</li>
<li>3.81 for vegetarians</li>
<li>2.89 for vegans</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_on_diet_and_greenhouse_gas_emissions">Background on diet and greenhouse gas emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-carbon_diet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background on diet and greenhouse gas emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the U.S., the food system emits four of the greenhouse gases associated with climate change: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbons" class="mw-redirect" title="Chlorofluorocarbons">chlorofluorocarbons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-four_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-four-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The burning of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a> (such as oil and gasoline) to power vehicles that transport food for long distances by air, ship, truck and rail releases carbon dioxide (CO2), the primary gas responsible for global warming. Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) are emitted from mechanical <a href="/wiki/Refrigerating" class="mw-redirect" title="Refrigerating">refrigerating</a> and freezing mechanisms&#160;– both staples in food shipment and storage.<sup id="cite_ref-six_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-six-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  Anthropogenic methane emission sources include agriculture (ruminants, manure management, wetland rice production),  various other industries and landfills.  Anthropogenic nitrous oxide sources include fertilizer, manure, crop residues and nitrogen-fixing crops production.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_2011_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_2011-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  Methane and nitrous oxide are also emitted in large amounts from natural sources.  The 100-year global warming potentials of methane and nitrous oxide are recently estimated at 25 and 298 carbon dioxide equivalents, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Steinfeld et al. estimate that livestock production accounts for 18 percent of anthropogenic GHG emissions expressed as carbon dioxide equivalents.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>   Of this amount, 34 percent is carbon dioxide emission from deforestation, principally in Central and South America, that they assigned to livestock production.  However, deforestation associated with livestock production is not an issue in many regions.  In the US, the land area occupied by forest increased between 1990 and 2009<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and a net increase in forest land area was also reported in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Of emissions they attribute to livestock production, Steinfeld et al. estimate that globally, methane accounts  for 30.2 percent.   Like other greenhouse gases, methane contributes to global warming when its atmospheric concentration rises.  Although methane emission from agriculture and other anthropogenic sources has contributed substantially to past warming, it is of much less significance for current and recent warming. This is because there has been relatively little increase in atmospheric methane concentration in recent years <sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  The anomalous increase in methane concentration in 2007, discussed by Rigby et al., has since been attributed principally to anomalous methane flux from natural wetlands, mostly in the tropics, rather than to anthropogenic sources.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Livestock sources (including enteric fermentation and manure) account for about 3.1 percent of US anthropogenic GHG emissions expressed as carbon dioxide equivalents.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_2011_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_2011-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>   This EPA estimate is based on methodologies agreed to by the Conference of Parties of the UNFCCC, with 100-year global warming potentials from the <a href="/wiki/IPCC" class="mw-redirect" title="IPCC">IPCC</a> <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Second_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Second Assessment Report">Second Assessment Report</a> used in estimating GHG emissions as carbon dioxide equivalents.
</p><p>A 2016 study published in <a href="/wiki/Nature_Climate_Change" title="Nature Climate Change">Nature Climate Change</a> concludes that climate taxes on meat and milk would simultaneously produce substantial cuts in greenhouse gas emissions and lead to healthier diets.  Such taxes would need to be designed with care: exempting and subsidising some food groups, selectively compensating for income loss, and using part of the revenue for health promotion.  The study analyzed surcharges of 40% on beef and 20% on milk and their effects on consumption, climate emissions, and <a href="/wiki/Distribution_(economics)" title="Distribution (economics)">distribution</a>.  An optimum plan would reduce emissions by 1<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>billion tonnes per year – similar in amount to those from aviation globally.<sup id="cite_ref-carrington-2016_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carrington-2016-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-springmann-etal-2016_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-springmann-etal-2016-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="High-carbon_and_low-carbon_food_choices">High-carbon and low-carbon food choices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-carbon_diet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: High-carbon and low-carbon food choices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clune2016_food_lca.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Clune2016_food_lca.svg/220px-Clune2016_food_lca.svg.png" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Clune2016_food_lca.svg/330px-Clune2016_food_lca.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Clune2016_food_lca.svg/440px-Clune2016_food_lca.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clune2016_food_lca.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Greenhouse impact across food categories: results from a 2017 review of 389 <a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_assessment" title="Life-cycle assessment">Life-cycle assessments</a><sup id="cite_ref-Cune2017_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cune2017-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Certain foods require more fossil fuel inputs than others, making it possible to go on a low-carbon diet and reduce one’s <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">carbon footprint</a> by choosing foods that need less fossil fuel and therefore emit less carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases.
</p><p>In June 2010, a report from <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme" title="United Nations Environment Programme">United Nations Environment Programme</a> declared that a global shift towards a vegan diet was needed to save the world from hunger, fuel shortages and climate change.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cundiff and Harris<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> write: "The American Dietetic Association (ADA) and Dietitians of Canada position paper officially recognizes that well-planned <a href="/wiki/Vegan" class="mw-redirect" title="Vegan">vegan</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Vegetarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Vegetarian">vegetarian</a> diets are appropriate for infancy and childhood.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  The American Academy of Pediatrics concurs.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>"  However, care is essential to ensure adequate nutrition with a vegetarian diet.  For example, there is evidence of higher frequencies of non-anemic iron deficiency among vegetarians versus omnivores<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and lower zinc status of vegetarian children,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> in addition to the need for vitamin B12 supplementation with vegan diets.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>  Statistically, a higher risk of dietary deficiency has been reported with vegan diets.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>  Risks associated with such diets for children are discussed by Jacobs and Dwyer.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>  Also, some studies indicate higher frequency of hypospadias among children of vegetarian mothers.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>China introduced new dietary guidelines in 2016 which aim to cut meat consumption by 50% and thereby reduce greenhouse gas emissions by 1<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>billion tonnes by 2030.<sup id="cite_ref-milman-2016_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-milman-2016-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_versus_pastured_livestock">Industrial versus pastured livestock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-carbon_diet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Industrial versus pastured livestock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Beef" title="Beef">Beef</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dairy_product" title="Dairy product">dairy</a> cattle can be particularly high in their levels of greenhouse gas emissions.  Feed is a significant contributor to emissions from animals raised in Confined Animal Feeding Operations (<a href="/wiki/CAFOs" class="mw-redirect" title="CAFOs">CAFOs</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Intensive_animal_farming" title="Intensive animal farming">factory farms</a>, as <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a> or <a href="/wiki/Soy_beans" class="mw-redirect" title="Soy beans">soy beans</a> must be fertilised, irrigated, processed into animal feed, packaged and then transported to the CAFO.  In 2005, CAFOs accounted for 74% of the world's poultry production, 50% of pork, 43% of beef, and 68% of eggs, according to the <a href="/wiki/Worldwatch_Institute" title="Worldwatch Institute">Worldwatch Institute</a>.  Proportions are significantly higher in developed countries, but are growing rapidly in developing countries, where demand is also growing fast.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>  However, in the US, only about 11 percent of soybean acres and 14 percent of corn acres are irrigated; in contrast, about 66 percent of vegetable acres and 79 percent of orchard acres are irrigated.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>   In 1995, commercial fertilizer inputs averaged 11 pounds per acre for US soybean production, versus 157 pounds per acre for US potato production.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>  Soybean meal for livestock feed is commonly produced after extraction of soybean oil (used for cooking, food products, biodiesel, etc.,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> so that only a fraction of processing is assignable to feed.  Such examples illustrate that issues relating to irrigation, fertilization and processing for meat production should also be of concern with regard to production of other foods.
</p><p>In one study, grass-fed cattle were estimated to account for 40% less greenhouse emissions than CAFO cattle<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>  However, comparative effects on emissions can vary.  in a US study, lower GHG emissions were associated with feedlot-finished beef production than with beef production on pasture and hay.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>  Similarly, a study in New Zealand concluded that environmental emissions per kilogram of beef produced can be reduced by incorporating feedlot finishing in a beef production system.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>  Another factor to be considered is the role of  a healthy pastoral ecosystem in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">carbon sequestration</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Rotational_grazing" class="mw-redirect" title="Rotational grazing">Rotational grazing</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">ruminants</a> (cattle, sheep, goats, etc.) and birds (chickens, turkeys, etc.) on untilled pasture land promotes rapid topsoil accumulation, representing a major carbon sink.
</p><p>Because CAFO production is highly centralised, the transport of animals to slaughter and then to distant retail outlets is a further source of greenhouse gas emissions.  However, this can be more or less compensated by reduced transport of feed, where CAFOs are located in feed-producing areas.
</p><p>In livestock production, emissions are reduced by feeding human-inedible materials that might otherwise by wasted.  Elferink et al.  state that "Currently, 70&#160;% of the feedstock used in the Dutch feed industry originates from the food processing industry."<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>  Among several US examples is the feeding of distillers grains remaining from biofuel production. For the marketing year 2009/2010, the amount of dried distillers grains used as livestock feed (and residual) in the US amounted to 25.0 million tonnes.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distance_traveled_and_method_of_transit">Distance traveled and method of transit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-carbon_diet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distance traveled and method of transit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Food_miles" title="Food miles">Food miles</a></div>
<p>Carbon emissions from transport account for 11% of the total carbon emissions of food, of which the transportation from producer to consumer accounts for 4%.<sup id="cite_ref-nine_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nine-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> However, "food miles" are a very misleading measure; in many cases food imported from the other side of the world may have a lower carbon footprint than a locally produced equivalent, due to differences in farming methods; "local food" campaigns may be motivated by <a href="/wiki/Protectionism" title="Protectionism">protectionism</a> rather than genuine environmentalism.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When looking at total greenhouse gases (not just carbon dioxide), 83% of emissions come from the actual production of the food because of the methane released by livestock and the nitrous oxide due to fertilizer.<sup id="cite_ref-nine_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nine-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The word <a href="/wiki/Locavore" class="mw-redirect" title="Locavore">locavore</a> describes a person attempting to eat a diet consisting of foods harvested from within a 100-mile radius.
</p><p>Some studies have criticized the emphasis on local food, claiming that it romanticizes local production, but does not produce very much environmental benefit. Transportation accounts for a relatively small portion of overall energy consumption in food production, and locally produced food may be much more energy intensive than food produced in a better area. Additionally the emphasis on "inefficient" local producers over more efficient ones further away may be damaging.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Processing.2C_packaging_and_waste"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Processing,_packaging_and_waste">Processing, packaging and waste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Low-carbon_diet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Processing, packaging and waste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Highly processed foods such as <a href="/wiki/Granola_bars" class="mw-redirect" title="Granola bars">granola bars</a> come in individual packaging, demanding high energy inputs and resulting in packaging <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">waste</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> These products contribute up to a third of total energy inputs for food consumption, as their ingredients are shipped from all over, processed, packaged, trucked to storage, then transported to retail outlets.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Bottled_water" title="Bottled water">Bottled water</a> is another example of a highly packaged, wasteful food product. It is estimated that Americans throw away 40 million plastic water bottles every day, and bottled water is often shipped trans-continentally. <a href="/wiki/Carbonated_water" title="Carbonated water">Carbonated water</a> must be chilled and kept under pressure during storage and transport so as to keep the carbon dioxide dissolved. This factor contributes greater energy usage for products shipped longer distances. Drinking purified <a href="/wiki/Tap_water" title="Tap water">tap water</a> treated with an <a href="/wiki/Active_carbon" class="mw-redirect" title="Active carbon">active carbon</a> filter for taste (most imp. chlorine), is a lower-carbon choice.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
