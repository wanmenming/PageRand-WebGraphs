<p><b>Silver(I,III) oxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the formula Ag<sub>4</sub>O<sub>4</sub>. It is a component of <a href="/wiki/Silver-oxide_battery" title="Silver-oxide battery">silver oxide-zinc alkaline batteries</a>. It can be prepared by the slow addition of a silver(I) <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Persulfate" title="Persulfate">persulfate</a> <a href="/wiki/Solution" title="Solution">solution</a> e.g. <a href="/wiki/Silver_nitrate" title="Silver nitrate">AgNO<sub>3</sub></a> to a <a href="/wiki/Sodium_persulfate" title="Sodium persulfate">Na<sub>2</sub>S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>8</sub></a> solution.<sup id="cite_ref-Wells_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wells-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  It adopts an unusual structure, being a <a href="/wiki/Mixed-valence_compound" class="mw-redirect" title="Mixed-valence compound">mixed-valence compound</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  It is a dark brown solid that decomposes with evolution of O<sub>2</sub> in water.  It dissolves in concentrated <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> to give brown solutions containing the Ag<sup>2+</sup> ion.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silver(I,III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although its empirical formula, AgO, suggests that silver is in the +2 <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a> in this compound, AgO is in fact <a href="/wiki/Diamagnetism" title="Diamagnetism">diamagnetic</a>. <a href="/wiki/X-ray_diffraction" class="mw-redirect" title="X-ray diffraction">X-ray diffraction</a> studies show that the silver atoms adopt two different coordination environments, one having two collinear oxide neighbours and the other four coplanar oxide neighbours.<sup id="cite_ref-Wells_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wells-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> AgO is therefore formulated as Ag<sup>I</sup>Ag<sup>III</sup>O<sub>2</sub><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> or Ag<sub>2</sub>O·Ag<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. It is being a 1:1 molar mixture of <a href="/wiki/Silver(I)_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Silver(I) oxide">silver(I) oxide</a>, Ag<sub>2</sub>O, and silver(III) oxide, Ag<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. It has previously been called silver peroxide, which is incorrect since does not contain the <a href="/wiki/Peroxide" title="Peroxide">peroxide</a> ion, O<sub>2</sub><sup>2−</sup>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Silver(I,III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>US patent 4003757 (Lux and Chobanov) describes one method for preparing this oxide (then called Ag(II)-oxide) in a form suitable for batteries and gives the following example:
</p><p>In 1.5 liters of aqueous solution containing 150 grams of sodium hydroxide, 65 grams of silver powder are suspended with continuous stirring. The silver powder has a density of approximately 1.6 grams per cubic centimeter. Its grain size distribution is: 52% under 10 microns; 33% 10 microns to 30 microns, 15% above 30 microns. 
</p><p>The liquid is then heated to about 85° C. Upon reaching this temperature, a total of 200 grams of potassium peroxydisulfate (K<sub>2</sub>S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>8</sub>) in portions of about 40 grams each is added at intervals of, for example, 1 hour. After addition of the final portion of oxidant, stirring is continued for 3 hours. The product is then filtered, washed to free it of alkali substances, dried at a temperature of approximately 80° C and reduced to particle form. 
</p><p>The foregoing yields approximately 73 grams of silver-(I,III)-oxide with more than 95% content of pure silver-(I,III)-oxide. The silver oxide produced is characterized by high thermodynamic stability, low internal discharge and consequent long shelf life. The rate of gas evolution of their products in 18% NaOH is below 1 microliter per gram-hour at room temperature. This stability is attributable to the fact that the process embodying the invention produces single crystals of exceptionally regular shape and monoclinic form.
</p>
