<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Storage of wine</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Storage_(wine)&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Storage (wine)">Storage (wine)</a>)</span></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wine_rack_7_storage.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Wine_rack_7_storage.jpg/220px-Wine_rack_7_storage.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Wine_rack_7_storage.jpg/330px-Wine_rack_7_storage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Wine_rack_7_storage.jpg/440px-Wine_rack_7_storage.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wine_rack_7_storage.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wines stored in a rack</div></div></div>
<p><b>Storage of wine</b> is an important consideration for <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a> that is being kept for <a href="/wiki/Aging_(wine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Aging (wine)">long-term aging</a>. While most wine is consumed within 24 hours of purchase, fine wines are often set aside for long-term storage. Wine is one of the few <a href="/wiki/Commodities" class="mw-redirect" title="Commodities">commodities</a> that can improve in flavour and value with age, but it can also rapidly deteriorate if kept in inadequate conditions. 
</p><p>The three factors that have the most direct impact on a wine's condition are <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>. Historically, the storage of wine was handled by wine merchants. Since the mid-20th century, however, consumers have been increasingly storing their own wine in home-based <a href="/wiki/Wine_cellar" title="Wine cellar">wine cellars</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_664_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_664-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conditions_affecting_wine">Conditions affecting wine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storage_of_wine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Conditions affecting wine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The three factors that have the most pronounced effect on wine in storage are light, humidity, and temperature.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Light">Light</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storage_of_wine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Light">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Direct <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> or <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light_bulb" title="Incandescent light bulb">incandescent</a> light can adversely react with <a href="/wiki/Phenolic_compounds_(wine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Phenolic compounds (wine)">phenolic compounds</a> in wine and create "<a href="/wiki/Wine_faults" class="mw-redirect" title="Wine faults">wine faults</a>". <a href="/wiki/Body_(wine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Body (wine)">Light-bodied</a> white wines run the greatest risk from light exposure, and for that reason, they are often packaged in tinted <a href="/wiki/Wine_bottle" title="Wine bottle">wine bottles</a> that offer some protection from  light. Wines packaged in clear, light green and blue colored bottles are the most vulnerable to light and may need extra precautions for storage. In the cellar, wines are stored in corrugated <a href="/wiki/Box" title="Box">boxes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wooden</a> crates to protect them from direct light.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Humidity">Humidity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storage_of_wine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Humidity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some degree of humidity is required in order to keep wines with <a href="/wiki/Cork_(material)" title="Cork (material)">cork</a> enclosures from drying out. Even when wine bottles are stored on their sides, one side of the cork is still exposed to air. Should the cork begin to dry out, it can allow oxygen to enter the bottle, filling the <a href="/wiki/Ullage_(wine)" title="Ullage (wine)">ullage</a> space and possibly causing the wine to spoil or <a href="/wiki/Oxidize" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxidize">oxidize</a>. Excessive humidity can also pose the risk of damaging <a href="/wiki/Wine_label" title="Wine label">wine labels</a>, which may hinder identification or hurt potential <a href="/wiki/Resale" class="mw-redirect" title="Resale">resale</a> value. Wine experts such as <a href="/wiki/Jancis_Robinson" title="Jancis Robinson">Jancis Robinson</a> note that 75% humidity is often cited as ideal but also that there is very little significant research to definitively establish an optimal range.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_664_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_664-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Concern about humidity is one of the primary reasons why wine experts such as <a href="/wiki/Tom_Stevenson" title="Tom Stevenson">Tom Stevenson</a> recommend that wine should not be kept in a <a href="/wiki/Refrigerator" title="Refrigerator">refrigerator</a> since the refrigeration process often includes <a href="/wiki/Dehumidifying" class="mw-redirect" title="Dehumidifying">dehumidifying</a>, which can quickly dry out corks.
</p><p>Some wine experts debate the importance of humidity for proper wine storage. In the <i><a href="/wiki/Wine_Spectator" title="Wine Spectator">Wine Spectator</a>,</i> writer <a href="/wiki/Matt_Kramer_(wine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Matt Kramer (wine)">Matt Kramer</a> cites a <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> study which claimed that the <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a> within a bottle is maintained at 100% regardless of the <a href="/wiki/Wine_closure" class="mw-redirect" title="Wine closure">closure</a> used or the orientation of the bottle.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Alexis_Lichine" title="Alexis Lichine">Alexis Lichine</a> contends that low humidity can still be detrimental to premium wine quality due to the risk of the cork drying out. As a way of maintaining optimal humidity, Lichine recommends spreading half an inch of gravel on the floor of a wine cellar and periodically sprinkling it with some water.<sup id="cite_ref-ALE_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ALE-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature">Temperature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storage_of_wine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Temperature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Justino_Henriques_Madeira_wine,_colheita_1996.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Justino_Henriques_Madeira_wine%2C_colheita_1996.JPG/220px-Justino_Henriques_Madeira_wine%2C_colheita_1996.JPG" width="220" height="432" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Justino_Henriques_Madeira_wine%2C_colheita_1996.JPG/330px-Justino_Henriques_Madeira_wine%2C_colheita_1996.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Justino_Henriques_Madeira_wine%2C_colheita_1996.JPG/440px-Justino_Henriques_Madeira_wine%2C_colheita_1996.JPG 2x" data-file-width="550" data-file-height="1079" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Justino_Henriques_Madeira_wine,_colheita_1996.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Madeira is exposed to high temperatures during its winemaking process and is thereby able to sustain exposure to higher temperatures more easily than other wines.</div></div></div>
<p>Wine is very susceptible to changes in temperature, with temperature control being an important consideration in wine storage. If the wine is exposed to too high a temperature (in excess of 77&#160;°F (25&#160;°C)) for long periods of time, it may be spoiled or become "cooked" and develop off-flavors that taste <a href="/wiki/Raisin" title="Raisin">raisiny</a> or <a href="/wiki/Stewed" class="mw-redirect" title="Stewed">stewed</a>. The exact length of time that a wine is at risk of exposure to high temperatures will vary depending on the wine, with some wines (such as <a href="/wiki/Madeira_(wine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Madeira (wine)">Madeira</a> which is exposed to high temperatures during its <a href="/wiki/Winemaking" title="Winemaking">winemaking</a>) being able to sustain exposure to high temperatures more easily than other, more delicate wines (such as <a href="/wiki/Riesling" title="Riesling">Riesling</a>). If the wine is exposed to temperatures that are too cold, the wine can freeze and expand, causing the cork to be pushed out or (more usually) the bottle to crack; this will allow more oxygen to be exposed to the wine. Temperature swings (such as repeated transferring a wine from a warm room to a cool refrigerator) can also cause adverse chemical reactions in the wine that may lead to a variety of wine faults. 
</p><p>In general, a wine has a greater potential to develop complexity and a more aromatic bouquet if it is allowed to age slowly in a relatively cool environment. The lower the temperature, the more slowly a wine develops.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_5-7_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_5-7-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> On average, the rate of chemical reactions in wine doubles with each 18 <a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">°F</a> (10 <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a>) increase in temperature. Most experts, such as Jancis Robinson, recommend that wine be kept at constant temperatures between 50 and 59&#160;°F (10 and 15&#160;°C).<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_664_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_664-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Tom Stevenson speculates that 52&#160;°F (11&#160;°C) may be the most ideal temperature for storage and aging, while <a href="/wiki/Karen_MacNeil" title="Karen MacNeil">Karen MacNeil</a> recommends keeping wine intended for ageing in a cool area with a constant temperature around 55&#160;°F (13&#160;°C). Wine can be stored at temperatures as high as 69&#160;°F (21&#160;°C) without long-term negative effect. Professor Cornelius Ough of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Davis" title="University of California, Davis">University of California, Davis</a> believes that wine can be exposed to temperatures as high as 120&#160;°F (49&#160;°C) for a few hours and not be damaged.<sup id="cite_ref-MacNeil_pg_79-82_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacNeil_pg_79-82-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vibration">Vibration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storage_of_wine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Vibration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although anecdotal information regarding the contributions of vibration in wine storage states that it contributes to the accelerated ageing of wine with adverse effects, this remains a research area with relatively little data. In a particular study, vibrations of different frequencies have been shown to have their own distinct effect on the chemistry of the wine. The study concludes, "Vibration could be used to accelerate the ageing of wine, but in most cases, this may lead to negative effects on wine quality. Therefore, to store red wines with limited changes in physicochemical properties, vibrations should be minimized."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orientation_of_the_bottle">Orientation of the bottle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storage_of_wine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Orientation of the bottle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most <a href="/wiki/Wine_rack" title="Wine rack">wine racks</a> are designed to allow a wine to be stored on its side. The thinking behind this orientation is that the cork is more likely to stay moist and not dry out if it is kept in constant contact with the wine. Some wineries package their wines upside down in the box for much the same reason. 
</p><p>Research in the late 1990s suggested that the ideal orientation for wine bottles is at a slight angle, rather than completely horizontal. This allows the cork to maintain partial contact with the wine in order to stay damp but also keeps the air bubble formed by a wine's <a href="/wiki/Ullage" title="Ullage">ullage</a> at the top rather than in the middle of the bottle if the wine is lying on its side. Keeping the ullage near the top, it has been argued, allows for a slower and more gradual oxidation and maturation process. This is because the pressure of the air bubble that is the ullage space rises and falls depending on temperature fluctuation. When exposed to higher temperatures the bubble's pressure increases (becomes positive relative to the air outside of the bottle), and if the wine is tilted at an angle, this compressed gas will diffuse through the cork and not harm the wine.  When the temperature falls the process reverses.  
</p><p>If the wine is completely on its side then this action will eject some wine through the cork. Through this "breathing" which can result from variations in temperature, oxygen may be repeatedly introduced into the bottle and as a result, can react with the wine.  An appropriate and constant temperature is therefore preferred. Additionally, oxidation will occur more rapidly at higher temperatures and gases dissolve into liquids faster the lower the temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_pg_42-44_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson_pg_42-44-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Moet_and_glass.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Moet_and_glass.jpg/220px-Moet_and_glass.jpg" width="220" height="284" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Moet_and_glass.jpg/330px-Moet_and_glass.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Moet_and_glass.jpg/440px-Moet_and_glass.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1557" data-file-height="2013" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Moet_and_glass.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Champagne is often recommended to be stored upright rather than lying on its side.</div></div></div>
<p>While most wines can benefit from lying on their side, <a href="/wiki/Champagne_(wine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Champagne (wine)">Champagne</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Sparkling_wine" title="Sparkling wine">sparkling wines</a> tend to age better if they are kept upright.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_664_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_664-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> This is because the internal pressure caused by the trapped <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbonic gas">carbonic gas</a> provides enough humidity and protection from oxygen. Caterer Magazine claims that the preference for upright storage of Champagne is shared by the <i><a href="/wiki/Comit%C3%A9_Interprofessionnel_du_Vin_de_Champagne" class="mw-redirect" title="Comité Interprofessionnel du Vin de Champagne">Comité Interprofessionnel du Vin de Champagne</a></i> (CIVC) who conducted an extensive study of Champagnes that were stored in various conditions and orientations. This study found that Champagne stored on its side aged more quickly because oxygen was allowed to seep in after the Champagne corks lost their <a href="/wiki/Elasticity_(physics)" title="Elasticity (physics)">elasticity</a> due to contact with the wine.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  However, the <a href="/wiki/Comit%C3%A9_Interprofessionnel_du_Vin_de_Champagne" class="mw-redirect" title="Comité Interprofessionnel du Vin de Champagne">Comité Interprofessionnel du Vin de Champagne</a><i> (CIVC) still recommends storing Champagne on its side, which can be read on their website at:</i>
</p><p><a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.champagne.fr/en/tasting-and-appreciation/art-of-serving-champagne/storing-champagne-bottles">http://www.champagne.fr/en/tasting-and-appreciation/art-of-serving-champagne/storing-champagne-bottles</a>
</p><p>where it is stated:
</p><p>"Champagne is ready for drinking upon release, matured to perfection in the producer’s cellars. 
</p><p>"It will, however, keep well for several years if stored on its side in a cool, dark, draft-free place, following the three golden rules of Champagne storage:
</p>
<ul><li>Constant, low ambient temperature (around 10°C/50°F)</li>
<li>Generous humidity</li>
<li>No direct exposure to sunlight, noise or excessive vibration."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_wine_closures">Alternative wine closures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storage_of_wine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Alternative wine closures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Storing wine that is bottled with <a href="/wiki/Alternative_wine_closure" title="Alternative wine closure">alternative wine closures</a> other than cork, such as a <a href="/wiki/Bung" title="Bung">bung</a>, have many of the same considerations in regards to temperature and light sensitivity. While humidity and concerns about oxidation are not as pronounced, the relatively recent popularity and increased usage of these closures have not given many opportunities for research into the storage and ageing potential of wines that use these closures.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_pg_664_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_pg_664-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Places_to_store_wine">Places to store wine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storage_of_wine&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Places to store wine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the end of the 20th century, there has been growth in industries relating to wine storage. Some wine connoisseurs may elect to store their wine at home in a dedicated room, closet or <a href="/wiki/Cellarette" title="Cellarette">cellarette</a>. Other options involve purchases and rentals at off-site wine storage facilities that are specifically designed for the task.
</p><p>Wine will prematurely develop if stored in an environment that has large temperature variations, particularly if these occur frequently. Temperature control systems ensure the wine cellar temperature is very stable. The variations cause corks to expand and contract which leads to oxidation of the wine.
</p><p>If wine is stored in conditions that are too dry, the cork will shrink and cause leakage. Too moist, and mould and contamination may occur. Climate Controlled Wine Storage maintains moderate humidity levels (55%-75%) to avoid these problems and assist in the optimum wine development conditions.
</p><p>Some industries focus on the construction of home <a href="/wiki/Wine_cellars" class="mw-redirect" title="Wine cellars">wine cellars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wine_cave" title="Wine cave">wine caves</a>, small rooms or spaces in which to store wine. Others produce smaller <a href="/wiki/Wine_accessories" class="mw-redirect" title="Wine accessories">wine accessories</a>, such as racks and wine refrigerators.<sup id="cite_ref-Robinson_pg_42-44_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson_pg_42-44-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> These appliances can feature adjustable temperature interfaces, two chambers for red and white wines and materials which protect the wine from the sun and ambient environment.
</p><p>Wine preservation and dispensing systems have also been developed that store wine at their optimal temperatures and use nitrogen gas to dispense the wine. Nitrogen gas helps prevent wine from oxidation and premature spoilage.
</p>
