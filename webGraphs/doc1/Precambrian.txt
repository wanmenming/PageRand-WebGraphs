<p>The <b>Precambrian</b> (or <b>Pre-Cambrian</b>, sometimes abbreviated <b>pЄ</b>, or <b>Cryptozoic</b>) is the earliest part of <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Earth" class="mw-redirect" title="History of the Earth">Earth's history</a>, set before the current <a href="/wiki/Phanerozoic" title="Phanerozoic">Phanerozoic</a> Eon. The Precambrian is so named because it preceded the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a>, the first <a href="/wiki/Geologic_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Geologic period">period</a> of the Phanerozoic eon, which is named after <a href="/wiki/Cambria" title="Cambria">Cambria</a>, the Latinised name for <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, where rocks from this age were first studied. The Precambrian accounts for 88% of the Earth's geologic time.
</p><p>The Precambrian (colored green in the timeline figure) is an informal unit of geologic time,<sup id="cite_ref-Gradstein_et-al_2012_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gradstein_et-al_2012-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> subdivided into three <a href="/wiki/Eon_(geology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Eon (geology)">eons</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hadean" title="Hadean">Hadean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archean" title="Archean">Archean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proterozoic" title="Proterozoic">Proterozoic</a>) of the <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time_scale" title="Geologic time scale">geologic time scale</a>. It spans from the formation of Earth about 4.6 billion years ago (<a href="/wiki/Ga_(unit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ga (unit)">Ga</a>) to the beginning of the Cambrian Period, about 541 million years ago (<a href="/wiki/Ma_(unit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ma (unit)">Ma</a>), when hard-shelled creatures first appeared in abundance.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precambrian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Relatively little is known about the Precambrian, despite it making up roughly seven-eighths of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Earth" class="mw-redirect" title="History of the Earth">Earth's history</a>, and what is known has largely been discovered from the 1960s onwards. The Precambrian fossil record is poorer than that of the succeeding <a href="/wiki/Phanerozoic" title="Phanerozoic">Phanerozoic</a>, and fossils from the Precambrian (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Stromatolites" class="mw-redirect" title="Stromatolites">stromatolites</a>) are of limited <a href="/wiki/Biostratigraphy" title="Biostratigraphy">biostratigraphic</a> use.<sup id="cite_ref-Monroe_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monroe-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This is because many Precambrian rocks have been heavily <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphosed</a>, obscuring their origins, while others have been destroyed by erosion, or remain deeply buried beneath Phanerozoic strata.<sup id="cite_ref-Monroe_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monroe-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is thought that the Earth coalesced from material in orbit around the Sun at roughly 4,543 Ma, and may have been struck by a very large (<a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>-sized) <a href="/wiki/Planetesimal" title="Planetesimal">planetesimal</a> shortly after it formed, splitting off material that formed the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis" class="mw-redirect" title="Giant impact hypothesis">Giant impact hypothesis</a>). A stable crust was apparently in place by 4,433 Ma, since <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">zircon</a> crystals from <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a> have been <a href="/wiki/Radiometric_dating" title="Radiometric dating">dated</a> at 4,404 ± 8 Ma.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "Precambrian" is recognized by the <a href="/wiki/International_Commission_on_Stratigraphy" title="International Commission on Stratigraphy">International Commission on Stratigraphy</a> as the only "supereon" in geologic time;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> it is so called because it includes the Hadean (~4.6—4 billion), Archean (4—2.5 billion), and Proterozoic (2.5 billion—541 million) eons. (There is only one other eon: the Phanerozoic, 541 million-present.)<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> "Precambrian" is still used by <a href="/wiki/Geologist" title="Geologist">geologists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paleontologists" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleontologists">paleontologists</a> for general discussions not requiring the more specific eon names. As of  2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Precambrian&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">United States Geological Survey</a> considers the term informal, lacking a stratigraphic rank.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_forms">Life forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precambrian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Life forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_life" class="mw-redirect" title="Origin of life">Origin of life</a> and <a href="/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms" title="Earliest known life forms">Earliest known life forms</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Positions_of_ancient_continents,_550_million_years_ago.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Positions_of_ancient_continents%2C_550_million_years_ago.jpg/220px-Positions_of_ancient_continents%2C_550_million_years_ago.jpg" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Positions_of_ancient_continents%2C_550_million_years_ago.jpg/330px-Positions_of_ancient_continents%2C_550_million_years_ago.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Positions_of_ancient_continents%2C_550_million_years_ago.jpg/440px-Positions_of_ancient_continents%2C_550_million_years_ago.jpg 2x" data-file-width="820" data-file-height="820" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Positions_of_ancient_continents,_550_million_years_ago.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Landmass positions near the end of the Precambrian</div></div></div>
<p>A specific date for the origin of life has not been determined. <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">Carbon</a> found in 3.8 billion-year-old rocks (Archean eon) from islands off western <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> may be of organic origin. Well-preserved microscopic fossils of <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> older than 3.46 billion years have been found in <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Probable fossils 100 million years older have been found in the same area. However, there is evidence that life could have evolved over 4.280 billion years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-NAT-20170301_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NAT-20170301-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT-20170301_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20170301-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BBC-20170301_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-20170301-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4.3b_oldest_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4.3b_oldest-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> There is a fairly solid record of bacterial life throughout the remainder (Proterozoic eon) of the Precambrian.
</p><p>Excluding a few contested reports of much older forms from North America and India, the first complex multicellular life forms seem to have appeared at roughly 1500 Ma, in the Mesoproterozoic era of the Proterozoic eon.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Fossil evidence from the later Ediacaran period of such complex life comes from the <a href="/wiki/Lantian_formation" class="mw-redirect" title="Lantian formation">Lantian formation</a>, at least 580 million years ago. A very diverse collection of soft-bodied forms is found in a variety of locations worldwide and date to between 635 and 542 Ma. These are referred to as <a href="/wiki/Ediacaran_biota" title="Ediacaran biota">Ediacaran or Vendian biota</a>. Hard-shelled creatures appeared toward the end of that time span, marking the beginning of the Phanerozoic eon. By the middle of the following Cambrian period, a very diverse fauna is recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Burgess_Shale" title="Burgess Shale">Burgess Shale</a>, including some which may represent stem groups of modern taxa. The increase in diversity of lifeforms during the early Cambrian is called the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian_explosion" title="Cambrian explosion">Cambrian explosion</a> of life.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While land seems to have been devoid of plants and animals, cyanobacteria and other microbes formed prokaryotic mats that covered terrestrial areas.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Tracks from an animal with leg like appendages have been found in what was mud 551 million years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Planetary_environment_and_the_oxygen_catastrophe">Planetary environment and the oxygen catastrophe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precambrian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Planetary environment and the oxygen catastrophe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Temagami_greenstone_belt_pillow_lava.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Temagami_greenstone_belt_pillow_lava.jpg/220px-Temagami_greenstone_belt_pillow_lava.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Temagami_greenstone_belt_pillow_lava.jpg/330px-Temagami_greenstone_belt_pillow_lava.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Temagami_greenstone_belt_pillow_lava.jpg/440px-Temagami_greenstone_belt_pillow_lava.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="1728" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Temagami_greenstone_belt_pillow_lava.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Weathered Precambrian <a href="/wiki/Pillow_lava" title="Pillow lava">pillow lava</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Temagami_Greenstone_Belt" title="Temagami Greenstone Belt">Temagami Greenstone Belt</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Shield" title="Canadian Shield">Canadian Shield</a></div></div></div>
<p>Evidence of the details of <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate motions</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Tectonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Tectonic">tectonic</a> activity in the Precambrian has been poorly preserved. It is generally believed that small proto-continents existed prior to 4280 Ma, and that most of the Earth's landmasses collected into a single <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinent</a> around 1130 Ma. The supercontinent, known as <a href="/wiki/Rodinia" title="Rodinia">Rodinia</a>, broke up around 750 Ma. A number of <a href="/wiki/Glaciation" class="mw-redirect" title="Glaciation">glacial periods</a> have been identified going as far back as the <a href="/wiki/Huronian" class="mw-redirect" title="Huronian">Huronian</a> epoch, roughly 2400–2100 Ma. One of the best studied is the <a href="/wiki/Sturtian-Varangian" class="mw-redirect" title="Sturtian-Varangian">Sturtian-Varangian</a> glaciation, around 850–635 Ma, which may have brought glacial conditions all the way to the equator, resulting in a "<a href="/wiki/Snowball_Earth" title="Snowball Earth">Snowball Earth</a>".
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth&#39;s atmosphere">atmosphere</a> of the early Earth is not well understood. Most geologists believe it was composed primarily of nitrogen, carbon dioxide, and other relatively inert gases, and was lacking in free <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. There is, however, evidence that an oxygen-rich atmosphere existed since the early Archean.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At present, it is still believed that <a href="/wiki/Molecular" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular">molecular</a> <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> was not a significant fraction of Earth's atmosphere until after <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthetic</a> life forms evolved and began to produce it in large quantities as a byproduct of their metabolism. This radical shift from a chemically inert to an oxidizing atmosphere caused an ecological crisis, sometimes called the <a href="/wiki/Great_Oxygenation_Event" title="Great Oxygenation Event">oxygen catastrophe</a>. At first, oxygen would have quickly combined with other elements in Earth's crust, primarily iron, removing it from the atmosphere. After the supply of oxidizable surfaces ran out, oxygen would have begun to accumulate in the atmosphere, and the modern high-oxygen atmosphere would have developed. Evidence for this lies in older rocks that contain massive <a href="/wiki/Banded_iron_formation" title="Banded iron formation">banded iron formations</a> that were laid down as iron oxides.
</p>

<p>A terminology has evolved covering the early years of the Earth's existence, as <a href="/wiki/Radiometric_dating" title="Radiometric dating">radiometric dating</a> has allowed real dates to be assigned to specific formations and features.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The Precambrian is divided into three eons: the <a href="/wiki/Hadean" title="Hadean">Hadean</a> (4600–4000 Ma), <a href="/wiki/Archean" title="Archean">Archean</a> (4000-2500 Ma) and <a href="/wiki/Proterozoic" title="Proterozoic">Proterozoic</a> (2500-541 Ma). See <a href="/wiki/Timetable_of_the_Precambrian" class="mw-redirect" title="Timetable of the Precambrian">Timetable of the Precambrian</a>.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Proterozoic" title="Proterozoic">Proterozoic</a>: this eon refers to the time from the lower <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a> boundary, 541 Ma, back through 2500 Ma. As originally used, it was a synonym for "Precambrian" and hence included everything prior to the Cambrian boundary. The Proterozoic eon is divided into three eras: the <a href="/wiki/Neoproterozoic" title="Neoproterozoic">Neoproterozoic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesoproterozoic" title="Mesoproterozoic">Mesoproterozoic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paleoproterozoic" title="Paleoproterozoic">Paleoproterozoic</a>.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Neoproterozoic" title="Neoproterozoic">Neoproterozoic</a>: The youngest <a href="/wiki/Geologic_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Geologic era">geologic era</a> of the Proterozoic Eon, from the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian" title="Cambrian">Cambrian</a> Period lower boundary (541 Ma) back to 1000 Ma. The Neoproterozoic corresponds to Precambrian Z rocks of older North American geology.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ediacaran" title="Ediacaran">Ediacaran</a>: The youngest <a href="/wiki/Geologic_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Geologic period">geologic period</a> within the Neoproterozoic Era. The "2012 Geologic Time Scale" dates it from 541 to 635 Ma. In this period the <a href="/wiki/Ediacaran_fauna" class="mw-redirect" title="Ediacaran fauna">Ediacaran fauna</a> appeared.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cryogenian" title="Cryogenian">Cryogenian</a>: The middle period in the <a href="/wiki/Neoproterozoic" title="Neoproterozoic">Neoproterozoic</a> Era: 635-720 Ma.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tonian" title="Tonian">Tonian</a>: the earliest period of the Neoproterozoic Era: 720-1000 Ma.</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mesoproterozoic" title="Mesoproterozoic">Mesoproterozoic</a>: the middle era of the <a href="/wiki/Proterozoic" title="Proterozoic">Proterozoic</a> Eon, 1000-1600 Ma. Corresponds to "Precambrian Y" rocks of older North American geology.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Paleoproterozoic" title="Paleoproterozoic">Paleoproterozoic</a>: oldest era of the Proterozoic Eon, 1600-2500 Ma. Corresponds to "Precambrian X" rocks of older North American geology.</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Archean" title="Archean">Archean</a> Eon: 2500-4000 Ma.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hadean" title="Hadean">Hadean</a> Eon: 4000–4600 Ma. This term was intended originally to cover the time before any preserved rocks were deposited, although some <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">zircon</a> crystals from about 4400 Ma demonstrate the existence of crust in the Hadean Eon. Other records from Hadean time come from the <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_the_Moon" title="Geology of the Moon">moon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteorites</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>It has been proposed that the Precambrian should be divided into eons and eras that reflect stages of planetary evolution, rather than the current scheme based upon numerical ages. Such a system could rely on events in the stratigraphic record and be demarcated by <a href="/wiki/Global_Boundary_Stratotype_Section_and_Point" title="Global Boundary Stratotype Section and Point">GSSPs</a>. The Precambrian could be divided into five "natural" eons, characterized as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Accretion and differentiation: a period of planetary formation until <a href="/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis" class="mw-redirect" title="Giant impact hypothesis">giant Moon-forming impact event</a>.</li>
<li>Hadean: dominated by heavy bombardment from about 4.51 Ga (possibly including a <a href="/wiki/Cool_Early_Earth" class="mw-redirect" title="Cool Early Earth">Cool Early Earth</a> period) to the end of the <a href="/wiki/Late_Heavy_Bombardment" title="Late Heavy Bombardment">Late Heavy Bombardment</a> period.</li>
<li>Archean: a period defined by the first crustal formations (the <a href="/wiki/Isua_greenstone_belt" class="mw-redirect" title="Isua greenstone belt">Isua greenstone belt</a>) until the deposition of <a href="/wiki/Banded_iron_formation" title="Banded iron formation">banded iron formations</a> due to increasing atmospheric oxygen content.</li>
<li>Transition: a period of continued iron banded formation until the first continental <a href="/wiki/Red_beds" title="Red beds">red beds</a>.</li>
<li>Proterozoic: a period of modern <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a> until the first <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>.</li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precambrian_supercontinents">Precambrian supercontinents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Precambrian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Precambrian supercontinents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The movement of Earth's <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plates</a> has caused the formation and break-up of continents over time, including occasional formation of a <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinent</a> containing most or all of the landmass. The earliest known supercontinent was <a href="/wiki/Vaalbara" title="Vaalbara">Vaalbara</a>. It formed from proto-continents and was a supercontinent 3.636 billion years ago. <a href="/wiki/Vaalbara" title="Vaalbara">Vaalbara</a> broke up c. 2.845–2.803 <a href="/wiki/Giga-annum" class="mw-redirect" title="Giga-annum">Ga</a> ago. The supercontinent <a href="/wiki/Kenorland" title="Kenorland">Kenorland</a> was formed c. 2.72 <a href="/wiki/Giga-annum" class="mw-redirect" title="Giga-annum">Ga</a> ago and then broke sometime after 2.45–2.1 <a href="/wiki/Giga-annum" class="mw-redirect" title="Giga-annum">Ga</a> into the proto-continent <a href="/wiki/Craton" title="Craton">cratons</a> called <a href="/wiki/Laurentia" title="Laurentia">Laurentia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baltica" title="Baltica">Baltica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yilgarn_craton" class="mw-redirect" title="Yilgarn craton">Yilgarn craton</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kalahari_Craton" title="Kalahari Craton">Kalahari</a>. The supercontinent <a href="/wiki/Columbia_(supercontinent)" title="Columbia (supercontinent)">Columbia</a> or Nuna formed 2.06–1.82 billion years ago and broke up about 1.5–1.35 billion years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-Zhao1_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zhao1-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Zhao2_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zhao2-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (January 2017)">not in citation given</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The supercontinent <a href="/wiki/Rodinia" title="Rodinia">Rodinia</a> is thought to have formed about 1.13–1.071 billion years ago, to have embodied most or all of Earth's continents and to have broken up into eight continents around 750–600 million years ago.
</p>
