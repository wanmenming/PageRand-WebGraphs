<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Projected_effects">Projected effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_South_Asia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Projected effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">effects of global warming</a>, including steady <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a>, increased cyclonic activity, and changes in ambient temperature and precipitation patterns, have affected or are projected to affect the subcontinent.
</p><p>As per the <a href="/wiki/IPCC" class="mw-redirect" title="IPCC">IPCC</a>, depending upon the scenario visualised, the projected global average surface warming will result in temperature increases worldwide at the end of the 21st Century relative to the end of the 20th Century  ranges from 0.6 to 4&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-IPCC_AR4_–_WG1_–_SPM_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_AR4_–_WG1_–_SPM-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Regarding local temperature rises, the IPCC figure projected for the mean annual increase in temperature by the end of the century in South Asia is 3.3&#160;°C with the min-max range as 2.7 – 4.7&#160;°C. The mean value for Tibet would be higher with mean increase of 3.8&#160;°C and min-max figures of 2.6 and 6.1&#160;°C respectively which implies harsher warming conditions for the Himalayan watersheds.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_in_sea_level">Rise in sea level</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_South_Asia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rise in sea level">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <span class="cleanup-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">corresponding sea level rise at the end of the 21st Century relative to the end of the 20th Century ranges from 0.18 to 0.59 m (excluding any rapid dynamical changes in ice flows in the future)</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="needs context (October 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-IPCC_AR4_–_WG1_–_SPM_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_AR4_–_WG1_–_SPM-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>. More recent analysis of a number of semi empirical models predict a sea level rise of about 1 metre by the year 2100.
<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> 
Ongoing sea level rises have already submerged several low-lying islands in the <a href="/wiki/Sundarbans" title="Sundarbans">Sundarbans</a>, displacing thousands of people.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_Harrabin_2007_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_Harrabin_2007-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Temperature rises on the Tibetan Plateau, which are causing <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">Himalayan glaciers to retreat</a>. It has been predicted that the historical city of <a href="/wiki/Thatta" title="Thatta">Thatta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Badin" title="Badin">Badin</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Sindh" title="Sindh">Sindh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> would have been swallowed by the sea by 2025, as the sea is already encroaching 80 acres of land here, every day. 
<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observed_changes_in_the_natural_and_human_environment">Observed changes in the natural and human environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_South_Asia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Observed changes in the natural and human environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming_on_human_health" title="Effects of global warming on human health">Effects of global warming on human health</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental">Environmental</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_South_Asia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Environmental">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Increased landslides and flooding are projected to have an impact upon states such as Assam.<sup id="cite_ref-TOI_Dasgupta_2007-02-03_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TOI_Dasgupta_2007-02-03-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Ecological disasters, such as a 1998 <a href="/wiki/Coral_bleaching" title="Coral bleaching">coral bleaching</a> event that killed off more than 70% of <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a> in the reef ecosystems off <a href="/wiki/Lakshadweep" title="Lakshadweep">Lakshadweep</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_and_Nicobar" class="mw-redirect" title="Andaman and Nicobar">Andamans</a>, and was brought on by elevated ocean temperatures tied to global warming, are also projected to become increasingly common.<sup id="cite_ref-Aggarwal_Lal_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aggarwal_Lal-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Normile_2000_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Normile_2000-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-UCS_2005_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UCS_2005-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first among the countries to be affected by severe climate change is Bangladesh. Its sea level, temperature and evaporation are increasing, and the changes in precipitation and cross boundary river flows are already beginning to cause drainage congestion. There is a reduction in fresh water availability, disturbance of morphologic processes and a higher intensity of flooding and other such disasters. Bangladesh only contributes 0.1% of the world’s emissions yet it has 2.4% of the world’s population. In contrast, the United States makes up about 5 percent of the world's population, yet they produce approximately 25 percent of the pollution that causes global warming.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic">Economic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_South_Asia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Economic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>India has the world's highest <a href="/wiki/Social_cost_of_carbon" class="mw-redirect" title="Social cost of carbon">social cost of carbon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Indira_Gandhi_Institute_of_Development_Research" title="Indira Gandhi Institute of Development Research">Indira Gandhi Institute of Development Research</a> has reported that, if the predictions relating to global warming made by the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> come to fruition, climate-related factors could cause India's GDP to decline by up to 9%; contributing to this would be shifting growing seasons for major crops such as rice, production of which could fall by 40%. Around seven million people are projected to be displaced due to, among other factors, submersion of parts of Mumbai and Chennai, if global temperatures were to rise by a mere 2&#160;°C (3.6&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-TOI_Sethi_2007-02-03_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TOI_Sethi_2007-02-03-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Villagers in India's North Eastern state of Meghalaya are also concerned that rising sea levels will submerge neighbouring low-lying Bangladesh, resulting in an influx of refugees into Meghalaya<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>—which has few resources to handle such a situation.
</p><p>If severe climate changes occur, Bangladesh will lose land along the coast line.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  This will be highly damaging to Bangladeshis especially because nearly two-thirds of Bangladeshis are employed in the agriculture sector, with rice as the single most important product. The economy has grown 5–6% over the past few years despite inefficient state-owned enterprises, delays in exploiting natural gas resources insufficient power supplies, and slow implementation of economic reforms. However, Bangladesh remains a poor, overpopulated, and inefficiently governed nation.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  If no further steps are taken to improve the current conditions global warming will affect the economy severely worsening the present issues further.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>.  The climate change would increase expenditure towards health care, cool drinks, alcoholic beverages, air conditioners, ice cream, cosmetics, agro chemicals etc.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social">Social</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_South_Asia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Social">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Climate Change in India and Pakistan will have a disproportionate impact on the more than 400 million that make up India's poor. This is because so many depend on natural resources for their food, shelter and income. More than 56% of people in India work in agriculture, while in Pakistan 43℅ of its population work in agriculture while many others earn their living in coastal areas.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollution">Pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_South_Asia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution_in_India" title="Air pollution in India">Air pollution in India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pollution_in_Pakistan" class="mw-redirect" title="Pollution in Pakistan">Pollution in Pakistan</a></div>
<p>Thick haze and smoke, originating from burning biomass in northeastern India<sup id="cite_ref-Badarinath_2006_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Badarinath_2006-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and air pollution from large industrial cities in northern India,<sup id="cite_ref-Lau_2005_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lau_2005-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> often concentrate inside the <a href="/wiki/Ganges_Basin" title="Ganges Basin">Ganges Basin</a>. Prevailing westerlies carry aerosols along the southern margins of the steep-faced Tibetan Plateau to eastern India and the Bay of Bengal. Dust and <a href="/wiki/Black_carbon" title="Black carbon">black carbon</a>, which are blown towards higher altitudes by winds at the southern faces of the Himalayas, can absorb shortwave radiation and heat the air over the Tibetan Plateau. The net atmospheric heating due to aerosol absorption causes the air to warm and <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convect</a> upwards, increasing the concentration of moisture in the mid-<a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> and providing positive feedback that stimulates further heating of aerosols.<sup id="cite_ref-Lau_2005_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lau_2005-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
Pollution of mercury in India is shocking. The environment is being packed with approximately 70 tonnes of mercury per year by existing mercury-cell plants. One gram of mercury is sufficient to pollute a lake of surface area of around 20 acres that would harm the fish which as a result would be dangerous to consume.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awareness">Awareness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Effects_of_global_warming_on_South_Asia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Awareness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Indian  and Pakistani media can contribute to increased awareness of climate change and related issues. A qualitative analysis of some mainstream Indian newspapers (particularly opinion and editorial pieces) during the release of the IPCC 4th Assessment Report and during the Nobel Peace Prize win by Al Gore and the IPCC found that Indian media strongly pursue the frame of scientific certainty in their coverage of climate change. This is in contrast to the scepticism displayed by American newspapers at the time. Alongside, Indian media highlight frames of energy challenge, social progress, public accountability and looming disaster. This sort of coverage finds parallels in European media narratives as well and helps build a transnational, globalised discourse on climate change.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Another study has found that the media in India are divided along the lines of a north-south, risk-responsibility discourse.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> However, much more research is required to analyse Indian media's role in shaping public perceptions on climate change.
</p><p>Tribal people in India's remote northeast plan to <sup id="cite_ref-reuters_khasi_gore_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters_khasi_gore-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> honour former U.S. Vice President <a href="/wiki/Al_Gore" title="Al Gore">Al Gore</a> with an award for promoting awareness on climate change that they say will have a devastating impact on their homeland.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Meghalaya" title="Meghalaya">Meghalaya</a>- meaning 'Abode of the Clouds' in <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a>—is home to the towns of <a href="/wiki/Cherrapunji" title="Cherrapunji">Cherrapunji</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mawsynram" title="Mawsynram">Mawsynram</a>, which are credited with being the wettest places in the world due to their high rainfall. But scientists state that global climate change is causing these areas to experience an increasingly sparse and erratic rainfall pattern and a lengthened dry season,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> affecting the livelihoods of thousands of villagers who cultivate paddy and maize. Some areas are also facing water shortages.
</p><p>People are becoming aware of ills of global warming. Taking initiative on their own people from Sangamner, Maharashtra (near Shirdi) have started a campaign of planting trees known as Dandakaranya- The Green Movement. It was started by visionary &amp; ace freedom fighter the late Shri Bhausaheb Thorat in the year 2005. To date, they have sowed more than 12 million seeds &amp; planted half a million plants.
</p><p>According to data from 2009 India is the world's third biggest emitter of CO2 after <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_China" title="Climate change in China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_the_United_States" title="Climate change in the United States">the United States</a> – pushing <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_Russia" title="Climate change in Russia">Russia</a> into fourth place.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
