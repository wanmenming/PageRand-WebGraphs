<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Raman spectroscopy</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Raman_spectrum&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Raman spectrum">Raman spectrum</a>)</span></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Raman_energy_levels.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Raman_energy_levels.svg/450px-Raman_energy_levels.svg.png" width="450" height="344" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Raman_energy_levels.svg/675px-Raman_energy_levels.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Raman_energy_levels.svg/900px-Raman_energy_levels.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="850" data-file-height="650" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Raman_energy_levels.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Energy-level diagram showing the states involved in Raman spectra.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Raman spectroscopy</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/ɑː/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;father&#39;">ɑː</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ən/: &#39;on&#39; in &#39;button&#39;">ən</span></span>/</a></span></span>; named after Indian physicist Sir <a href="/wiki/C._V._Raman" title="C. V. Raman">C. V. Raman</a>) is a <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">spectroscopic</a> technique used to observe vibrational, rotational, and other low-frequency modes in a system.<sup id="cite_ref-Gardiner_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardiner-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  Raman spectroscopy is commonly used in chemistry to provide a structural fingerprint by which molecules can be identified.
</p><p>It relies on <a href="/wiki/Inelastic_scattering" title="Inelastic scattering">inelastic scattering</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Raman_scattering" title="Raman scattering">Raman scattering</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Monochromatic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monochromatic">monochromatic</a> light, usually from a <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">near infrared</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">near ultraviolet</a> range. The laser light interacts with molecular vibrations, <a href="/wiki/Phonon" title="Phonon">phonons</a> or other excitations in the system, resulting in the energy of the laser photons being shifted up or down. The shift in energy gives information about the vibrational modes in the system. <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">Infrared spectroscopy</a> yields similar, but complementary, information.
</p><p>Typically, a sample is illuminated with a laser beam. Electromagnetic radiation from the illuminated spot is collected with a <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lens</a> and sent through a <a href="/wiki/Monochromator" title="Monochromator">monochromator</a>. Elastic scattered radiation at the wavelength corresponding to the laser line (<a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">Rayleigh scattering</a>) is filtered out by either a notch filter, edge pass filter, or a band pass filter, while the rest of the collected light is dispersed onto a detector.
</p><p>Spontaneous <a href="/wiki/Raman_scattering" title="Raman scattering">Raman scattering</a> is typically very weak, and as a result the main difficulty of Raman spectroscopy is separating the weak inelastically scattered light from the intense Rayleigh scattered laser light. Historically, Raman <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">spectrometers</a> used <a href="/wiki/Holographic_grating" title="Holographic grating">holographic gratings</a> and multiple dispersion stages to achieve a high degree of laser rejection. In the past, <a href="/wiki/Photomultiplier" title="Photomultiplier">photomultipliers</a> were the detectors of choice for dispersive Raman setups, which resulted in long acquisition times. However, modern instrumentation almost universally employs <a href="/wiki/Band-stop_filter" title="Band-stop filter">notch or edge filters</a> for laser rejection and spectrographs either axial transmissive (AT), <a href="/wiki/Monochromator#Czerny–Turner_monochromator" title="Monochromator">Czerny–Turner (CT) monochromator</a>, or FT (<a href="/wiki/Fourier_transform_spectroscopy" class="mw-redirect" title="Fourier transform spectroscopy">Fourier transform spectroscopy</a> based), and <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">CCD</a> detectors.
</p><p>The advanced types of Raman spectroscopy  include <a href="/wiki/Surface_enhanced_Raman_spectroscopy" class="mw-redirect" title="Surface enhanced Raman spectroscopy">surface-enhanced Raman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Resonance_Raman_spectroscopy" title="Resonance Raman spectroscopy">resonance Raman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tip-enhanced_Raman_spectroscopy" title="Tip-enhanced Raman spectroscopy">tip-enhanced Raman</a>, polarized Raman, <a href="/wiki/Raman_scattering#Stimulated_Raman_scattering_and_Raman_amplification" title="Raman scattering">stimulated Raman</a> (analogous to <a href="/wiki/Stimulated_emission" title="Stimulated emission">stimulated emission</a>), transmission Raman, spatially offset Raman, and <a href="/wiki/Resonance_Raman_spectroscopy#Resonance_hyper_Raman_spectroscopy" title="Resonance Raman spectroscopy">hyper Raman</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theory">Theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raman_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Raman_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p>The magnitude of the Raman effect correlates with polarizability of the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> in a molecule. It is a form of inelastic <a href="/wiki/Light_scattering" class="mw-redirect" title="Light scattering">light scattering</a>, where a <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">photon</a> excites the sample. This excitation puts the molecule into a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_state_(physics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Virtual state (physics)">virtual energy state</a> for a short time before the photon is emitted. Inelastic scattering means that the energy of the emitted photon is of either lower or higher energy than the incident photon. After the scattering event, the sample is in a different <a href="/wiki/Rigid_rotor#Quantum_mechanical_rigid_rotor" title="Rigid rotor">rotational</a> or <a href="/wiki/Molecular_vibration" title="Molecular vibration">vibrational state</a>. 
</p><p>For the total energy of the system to remain constant after the molecule moves to a new rovibronic state, the scattered photon shifts to a different energy, and therefore a different frequency. This energy difference is equal to that between the initial and final rovibronic states of the molecule. If the final state is higher in energy than the initial state, the scattered photon will be shifted to a lower frequency (lower energy) so that the total energy remains the same. This shift in frequency is called a <a href="/wiki/Stokes_shift" title="Stokes shift">Stokes shift</a>, or downshift. If the final state is lower in energy, the scattered photon will be shifted to a higher frequency, which is called an anti-Stokes shift, or upshift.
</p><p>For a molecule to exhibit a Raman effect, there must be a change in its electric dipole-electric dipole polarizability with respect to the vibrational coordinate corresponding to the rovibronic state. The intensity of the Raman scattering is proportional to this polarizability change. Therefore, the Raman spectrum, scattering intensity as a function of the frequency shifts, depends on the rovibronic states of the molecule.
</p><p>The Raman effect is based on the interaction between the electron cloud of a sample and the external electrical field of the monochromatic light, which can create an induced dipole moment within the molecule based on its polarizability. Because the laser light does not excite the molecule there can be no real transition between energy levels.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The Raman effect should not be confused with emission (<a href="/wiki/Fluorescence" title="Fluorescence">fluorescence</a> or <a href="/wiki/Phosphorescence" title="Phosphorescence">phosphorescence</a>), where a molecule in an excited electronic state emits a photon and returns to the ground electronic state, in many cases to a vibrationally excited state on the ground electronic state potential energy surface. Raman scattering also contrasts with infrared (IR) absorption, where the energy of the absorbed photon matches the difference in energy between the initial and final rovibronic states. The dependence of Raman on the electric dipole-electric dipole polarizability derivative also differs from IR spectroscopy, which depends on the electric dipole moment derivative, the atomic polar tensor (APT). This contrasting feature allows rovibronic transitions that might not be active in IR to be analyzed using Raman spectroscopy, as exemplified by the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_mutual_exclusion" title="Rule of mutual exclusion">rule of mutual exclusion</a> in <a href="/wiki/Centrosymmetry" title="Centrosymmetry">centrosymmetric molecules</a>. Transitions which have large Raman intensities often have weak IR intensities and vice versa. A third vibrational spectroscopy technique, inelastic incoherent neutron scattering (IINS), can be used to determine the frequencies of vibrations in highly symmetric molecules that may be both IR and Raman inactive. The IINS selection rules, or allowed transitions, differ from those of IR and Raman, so the three techniques are complementary. They all give the same frequency for a given vibrational transition, but the relative intensities provide different information due to the different types of interaction between the molecule and the incoming particles, photons for IR and Raman, and neutrons for IINS.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raman_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the inelastic scattering of light was predicted by <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Smekal" title="Adolf Smekal">Adolf Smekal</a> in 1923,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> it was not observed in practice until 1928. The Raman effect was named after one of its discoverers, the Indian scientist <a href="/wiki/Sir_C._V._Raman" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir C. V. Raman">Sir C. V. Raman</a>, who observed the effect in organic liquids by means of sunlight in 1928 together with <a href="/wiki/K._S._Krishnan" title="K. S. Krishnan">K. S. Krishnan</a>, and independently by <a href="/wiki/Grigory_Landsberg" title="Grigory Landsberg">Grigory Landsberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Isaakovich_Mandelstam" class="mw-redirect" title="Leonid Isaakovich Mandelstam">Leonid Mandelstam</a>) in inorganic crystals.<sup id="cite_ref-Gardiner_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardiner-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Raman won the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics" title="Nobel Prize in Physics">Nobel Prize in Physics</a> in 1930 for this discovery accomplished using sunlight, a narrow-band photographic filter to create monochromatic light, and a "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Crossed_filter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Crossed filter (page does not exist)">crossed filter</a>" to block this monochromatic light.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He found that a small amount of light had changed frequency and passed through the "crossed" filter. The first observation of Raman spectra in gases was in 1929 by <a href="/wiki/Franco_Rasetti#Raman_spectroscopy_and_the_model_of_the_atomic_nucleus" title="Franco Rasetti">Franco Rasetti</a> using ultraviolet light from a <a href="/wiki/Mercury_vapor_lamp" class="mw-redirect" title="Mercury vapor lamp">mercury vapor lamp</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Systematic pioneering theory of the Raman effect was developed by Czechoslovak physicist <a href="/wiki/George_Placzek" title="George Placzek">George Placzek</a> between 1930 and 1934.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The mercury <a href="/wiki/Arc_lamp" title="Arc lamp">arc</a> became the principal light source, first with photographic detection and then with spectrophotometric detection.
</p><p>In the years following its discovery, Raman spectroscopy was used to provide the first catalog of molecular vibrational frequencies. Originally, heroic measures were required to obtain Raman spectra due to the low sensitivity of the technique. Typically, the sample was held in a long tube and illuminated along its length with a beam of filtered <a href="/wiki/Monochromatic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monochromatic">monochromatic</a> light generated by a <a href="/wiki/Gas_discharge_lamp" class="mw-redirect" title="Gas discharge lamp">gas discharge lamp</a>. The photons that were scattered by the sample were collected through an <a href="/wiki/Optical_flat" title="Optical flat">optical flat</a> at the end of the tube. To maximize the sensitivity, the sample was highly concentrated (1 M or more) and relatively large volumes (5 mL or more) were used. Consequently, the use of Raman spectroscopy dwindled when commercial <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">IR</a> <a href="/wiki/Spectrophotometry" title="Spectrophotometry">spectrophotometers</a> became available in the 1940s. However, the advent of the <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> in the 1960s resulted in simplified Raman spectroscopy instruments and also boosted the sensitivity of the technique. This has revived the use of Raman spectroscopy as a common analytical technique.
</p><p>It is convenient in polarised Raman spectroscopy to describe the propagation and polarisation directions using Porto's notation,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> described by and named after Brazilian physicist <a href="/wiki/Sergio_Pereira_da_Silva_Porto" class="mw-redirect" title="Sergio Pereira da Silva Porto">Sergio Pereira da Silva Porto</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raman_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Raman spectroscopy is used in chemistry to identify molecules and study chemical bonding and intramolecular bonds. Because vibrational frequencies are specific to a molecule’s <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bonds</a> and symmetry (the fingerprint region of organic molecules is in the <a href="/wiki/Wavenumber" title="Wavenumber">wavenumber</a> range 500–1500&#160;cm<sup>−1</sup>),<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Raman provides a fingerprint to identify molecules. For instance, Raman and IR spectra were used to determine the vibrational frequencies of SiO, Si<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, and Si<sub>3</sub>O<sub>3</sub> on the basis of normal coordinate analyses.<sup id="cite_ref-R._K._Khanna_and_D._D._Stranz_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R._K._Khanna_and_D._D._Stranz-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  Raman is also used to study the addition of a substrate to an enzyme.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_physics" title="Solid-state physics">solid-state physics</a>, Raman spectroscopy is used to characterize materials, measure <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, and find the crystallographic orientation of a sample. As with single molecules, a solid material can be identified by characteristic <a href="/wiki/Phonon" title="Phonon">phonon</a> modes. Information on the population of a phonon mode is given by the ratio of the Stokes and anti-Stokes intensity of the spontaneous Raman signal. Raman spectroscopy can also be used to observe other low frequency excitations of a solid, such as <a href="/wiki/Plasmon" title="Plasmon">plasmons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnon" title="Magnon">magnons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/BCS_theory" title="BCS theory">superconducting gap</a> excitations. <a href="/wiki/Distributed_temperature_sensing" title="Distributed temperature sensing">Distributed temperature sensing</a> (DTS) uses the Raman-shifted backscatter from laser pulses to determine the temperature along optical fibers. The orientation of an anisotropic <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystal</a> can be found from the <a href="/wiki/Polarization_(waves)" title="Polarization (waves)">polarization</a> of Raman-scattered light with respect to the crystal and the polarization of the laser light, if the <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">crystal structure</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Crystallographic_point_group" title="Crystallographic point group">point group</a> is known.
</p><p>In nanotechnology, a Raman microscope can be used to analyze nanowires to better understand their structures, and the radial breathing mode of carbon nanotubes is commonly used to evaluate their diameter.
</p><p>Raman active fibers, such as aramid and carbon, have vibrational modes that show a shift in Raman frequency with applied stress. <a href="/wiki/Polypropylene" title="Polypropylene">Polypropylene</a> fibers exhibit similar shifts.
</p><p>In solid state chemistry and the bio-pharmaceutical industry, Raman spectroscopy can be used to not only identify active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs), but to identify their polymorphic forms, if more than one exist. For example, the drug <a href="/wiki/Cayston" class="mw-redirect" title="Cayston">Cayston</a> (<a href="/wiki/Aztreonam" title="Aztreonam">aztreonam</a>), marketed by <a href="/wiki/Gilead_Sciences" title="Gilead Sciences">Gilead Sciences</a> for <a href="/wiki/Cystic_fibrosis" title="Cystic fibrosis">cystic fibrosis</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> can be identified and characterized by IR and Raman spectroscopy. Using the correct polymorphic form in bio-pharmaceutical formulations is critical, since different forms have different physical properties, like solubility and melting point.
</p><p>Raman spectroscopy has a wide variety of applications in biology and medicine. It has helped confirm the existence of low-frequency phonons<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> in proteins and DNA,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> promoting studies of <a href="/wiki/Low-frequency_collective_motion_in_proteins_and_DNA" title="Low-frequency collective motion in proteins and DNA">low-frequency collective motion in proteins and DNA</a> and their biological functions.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Raman reporter molecules with <a href="/wiki/Olefin" class="mw-redirect" title="Olefin">olefin</a> or <a href="/wiki/Alkyne" title="Alkyne">alkyne</a> moieties are being developed for tissue imaging with SERS-labeled <a href="/wiki/Antibodies" class="mw-redirect" title="Antibodies">antibodies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Raman spectroscopy has also been used as a noninvasive technique for real-time, in situ biochemical characterization of wounds. Multivariate analysis of Raman spectra has enabled development of a quantitative measure for wound healing progress.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Spatially_offset_Raman_spectroscopy" title="Spatially offset Raman spectroscopy">Spatially offset Raman spectroscopy</a> (SORS), which is less sensitive to surface layers than conventional Raman, can be used to discover <a href="/wiki/Counterfeit_drug" class="mw-redirect" title="Counterfeit drug">counterfeit drugs</a> without opening their packaging, and to non-invasively study biological tissue.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> A huge reason why Raman spectroscopy is so useful in biological applications is because its results often do not face interference from water molecules, due to the fact that they have permanent dipole moments, and as a result, the Raman scattering cannot be picked up on. This is a large advantage, specifically in biological applications.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Raman spectroscopy also has a wide usage for studying biominerals.<sup id="cite_ref-TaylorVinnKudryavtsevSchopf2010_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TaylorVinnKudryavtsevSchopf2010-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Lastly, Raman gas analyzers have many practical applications, including real-time monitoring of anesthetic and respiratory gas mixtures during surgery.
</p><p>Raman spectroscopy is an efficient and non-destructive way to investigate works of art.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Identifying individual pigments in paintings and their degradation products provides insight into the working method of the artist. It also gives information about the original state of the painting in cases where the pigments degraded with age.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In addition to paintings, Raman spectroscopy can be used to investigate the chemical composition of historical documents (such as the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Kells" title="Book of Kells">Book of Kells</a>), which can provide insight about the social and economic conditions when they were created.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> It also offers a noninvasive way to determine the best method of <a href="/wiki/Preservation_(library_and_archival_science)" title="Preservation (library and archival science)">preservation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Art_conservation" class="mw-redirect" title="Art conservation">conservation</a> of such materials.
</p><p>Raman spectroscopy has been used in several research projects as a means to detect <a href="/wiki/Explosive" title="Explosive">explosives</a> from a safe distance using laser beams.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Raman Spectroscopy is being further developed so it could be used in the clinical setting. Raman4Clinic is a European organization that is working on incorporating Raman Spectroscopy techniques in the medical field. They are currently working on different projects, one of them being monitoring cancer using bodily fluids such as urine and blood samples which are easily accessible. This technique would be less stressful on the patients than constantly having to take biopsies which are not always risk free.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Microspectroscopy">Microspectroscopy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raman_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Microspectroscopy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Raman spectroscopy offers several advantages for <a href="/wiki/Microscopy" title="Microscopy">microscopic</a> analysis. Since it is a scattering technique, specimens do not need to be fixed or sectioned. Raman spectra can be collected from a very small volume (&lt; 1&#160;µm in diameter); these spectra allow the identification of species present in that volume. Water does not generally interfere with Raman spectral analysis. Thus, Raman spectroscopy is suitable for the microscopic examination of <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>, materials such as polymers and ceramics, <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proteins" class="mw-redirect" title="Proteins">proteins</a> and forensic trace evidence. A <a href="/wiki/Raman_microscope" title="Raman microscope">Raman microscope</a> begins with a standard optical microscope, and adds an excitation laser, a <a href="/wiki/Monochromator" title="Monochromator">monochromator</a>, and a sensitive detector (such as a <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">charge-coupled device</a> (CCD), or <a href="/wiki/Photomultiplier" title="Photomultiplier">photomultiplier</a> tube (PMT)). <a href="/wiki/Fourier_transform_spectroscopy" class="mw-redirect" title="Fourier transform spectroscopy">FT-Raman</a> has also been used with microscopes.  <a href="/wiki/Microscopy#Ultraviolet_microscopy" title="Microscopy">Ultraviolet microscopes</a> and UV enhanced optics must be used when a UV laser source is used for Raman microspectroscopy.
</p><p>In <i>direct imaging</i>, the whole field of view is examined for scattering over a small range of wavenumbers (Raman shifts). For instance, a wavenumber characteristic for cholesterol could be used to record the distribution of cholesterol within a cell culture.
</p><p>The other approach is <i><a href="/wiki/Hyperspectral_imaging" title="Hyperspectral imaging">hyperspectral imaging</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Chemical_imaging" title="Chemical imaging">chemical imaging</a></i>, in which thousands of Raman spectra are acquired from all over the field of view. The data can then be used to generate images showing the location and amount of different components. Taking the cell culture example, a hyperspectral image could show the distribution of cholesterol, as well as proteins, nucleic acids, and fatty acids. Sophisticated signal- and image-processing techniques can be used to ignore the presence of water, culture media, buffers, and other interference.
</p><p>Raman microscopy, and in particular <a href="/wiki/Confocal_microscopy" title="Confocal microscopy">confocal microscopy</a>, has very high spatial resolution. For example, the lateral and depth resolutions were 250&#160;nm and 1.7&#160;µm, respectively, using a confocal Raman microspectrometer with the 632.8&#160;nm line from a <a href="/wiki/Helium%E2%80%93neon_laser" title="Helium–neon laser">helium–neon laser</a> with a pinhole of 100&#160;µm diameter. Since the objective lenses of microscopes focus the laser beam to several micrometres in diameter, the resulting photon flux is much higher than achieved in conventional Raman setups. This has the added benefit of enhanced <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence_quenching" class="mw-redirect" title="Fluorescence quenching">fluorescence quenching</a>. However, the high photon flux can also cause sample degradation, and for this reason some setups require a thermally conducting substrate (which acts as a heat sink) in order to mitigate this process.
</p><p>Another approach called <b>global Raman imaging</b><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> uses complete monochromatic images instead of reconstruction of images from acquired spectra. This technique is being used for the characterization of large scale devices, mapping of different compounds and dynamics study. It has already been use for the characterization of <a href="/wiki/Graphene" title="Graphene">graphene</a> layers,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> J-aggregated dyes inside <a href="/wiki/Carbon_nanotubes" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon nanotubes">carbon nanotubes</a><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and multiple other 2D materials such as <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_disulfide" title="Molybdenum disulfide">MoS<sub>2</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Tungsten_diselenide" title="Tungsten diselenide">WSe<sub>2</sub></a>. Since the excitation beam is dispersed over the whole field of view, those measurements can be done without damaging the sample.
</p><p>By using Raman microspectroscopy, <i>in vivo</i> time- and space-resolved Raman spectra of microscopic regions of samples can be measured. Sampling is non-destructive and water, media, and buffers typically do not interfere with the analysis. Consequently, <i>in vivo</i> time- and space-resolved Raman spectroscopy is suitable to examine <a href="/wiki/Proteins" class="mw-redirect" title="Proteins">proteins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> and <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organs</a>. In the field of microbiology, confocal Raman microspectroscopy has been used to map intracellular distributions of macromolecules, such as proteins, polysaccharides, and nucleic acids and polymeric inclusions, such as poly-B-hydroxybutyric acid and polyphosphates in bacteria and sterols in microalgae. Combining stable isotopic probing (SIP) experiments with confocal Raman microspectroscopy has permitted determination of assimilation rates of 13C and 15N-substrates as well as D2O by individual bacterial cells <sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>. Using confocal Raman microspectroscopy essentially as a single-cell mass spectrometer is enabled by the fact that the vibrational frequency of any molecular bonds is a function of the masses of the bound atoms. Thus, incorporation of heavy isotopes will cause quantitative "red shifts" in diagnostic Raman peaks.
</p><p>Raman microscopy for biological and medical specimens generally uses <a href="/wiki/Near-infrared" class="mw-redirect" title="Near-infrared">near-infrared</a> (NIR) lasers (785&#160;nm diodes and 1064&#160;nm <a href="/wiki/Nd:YAG" class="mw-redirect" title="Nd:YAG">Nd:YAG</a> are especially common). The use of these lower energy wavelengths reduces the risk of damaging the specimen. However, the intensity of NIR Raman is low (owing to the ω<sup>4</sup> dependence of Raman scattering intensity), and most detectors require very long collection times. Recently advances were made which had no destructive effect on <a href="/wiki/Mitochondria" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a> in the observation of changes in cytochrome c structure that occur in the process of <a href="/wiki/Electron_transport" class="mw-redirect" title="Electron transport">electron transport</a> and ATP synthesis.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sensitive detectors have become available, making the technique better suited to general use. Raman microscopy of inorganic specimens, such as rocks and ceramics and polymers, can use a broader range of excitation wavelengths.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polarization_dependence_of_Raman_scattering">Polarization dependence of Raman scattering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raman_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Polarization dependence of Raman scattering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Raman scattering is polarization sensitive and can provide detailed information on symmetry of Raman active modes. While conventional Raman spectroscopy identifies chemical composition, polarization effects on Raman spectra can reveal information on the orientation of molecules in single crystals and anisotropic materials, e.g. strained plastic sheets, as well as the symmetry of vibrational modes.
</p><p>Polarization–dependent Raman spectroscopy uses (plane) polarized laser excitation from a <a href="/wiki/Polarizer" title="Polarizer">polarizer</a>. The Raman scattered light collected is passed through a second polarizer (called the analyzer) before entering the detector. The analyzer is oriented either parallel or perpendicular to the polarization of the laser. Spectra acquired with the analyzer set at both perpendicular and parallel to the excitation plane can be used to calculate the <a href="/wiki/Depolarization_ratio" title="Depolarization ratio">depolarization ratio</a>. Typically a <a href="/wiki/Polarization_scrambling" title="Polarization scrambling">polarization scrambler</a> is placed between the analyzer and detector also. It is convenient in polarized Raman spectroscopy to describe the propagation and polarization directions using Porto's notation,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> described by and named after Brazilian physicist <a href="/wiki/Sergio_Pereira_da_Silva_Porto" class="mw-redirect" title="Sergio Pereira da Silva Porto">Sergio Pereira da Silva Porto</a>.
</p><p>For isotropic solutions, the Raman scattering from each mode either retains the polarization of the laser or becomes partly or fully depolarized.  If the vibrational mode involved in the Raman scattering process is totally symmetric then the polarization of the Raman scattering will be the same as that of the incoming laser beam. In the case that the vibrational mode is not totally symmetric then the polarization will be lost (scrambled) partially or totally, which is referred to as depolarization. Hence polarized Raman spectroscopy can provide detailed information as to the symmetry labels of vibrational modes. 
</p><p>In the solid state, polarized Raman spectroscopy can be useful in the study of oriented samples such as single crystals. The polarizability of a vibrational mode is not equal along and across the bond. Therefore the intensity of the Raman scattering will be different when the lasers polarization is along and orthogonal to a particular bonds axis. This effect can provide information on the orientation of molecules with a single crystal or material. The spectral information arising from this analysis is often used to understand macromolecular orientation in crystal lattices, <a href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal" title="Liquid crystal">liquid crystals</a> or polymer samples.<sup id="cite_ref-Joseph_M._Grzybowski,_R._K._Khanna,_E._R._Lippincott_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Joseph_M._Grzybowski,_R._K._Khanna,_E._R._Lippincott-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Characterization_of_the_symmetry_of_a_vibrational_mode">Characterization of the symmetry of a vibrational mode</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raman_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Characterization of the symmetry of a vibrational mode">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Raman_spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several variations of Raman spectroscopy have been developed. The usual purpose is to enhance the sensitivity (e.g., surface-enhanced Raman), to improve the spatial resolution (Raman microscopy), or to acquire very specific information (resonance Raman).
</p>
<ul><li><i>Spontaneous Raman spectroscopy</i> – Term used to describe Raman spectroscopy without enhancement of sensitivity.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Surface_Enhanced_Raman_Spectroscopy" class="mw-redirect" title="Surface Enhanced Raman Spectroscopy">Surface-enhanced Raman spectroscopy</a> (SERS)</i> – Normally done in a silver or gold colloid or a substrate containing silver or gold. Surface <a href="/wiki/Plasmons" class="mw-redirect" title="Plasmons">plasmons</a> of silver and gold are excited by the laser, resulting in an increase in the electric fields surrounding the metal. Given that Raman intensities are proportional to the electric field, there is large increase in the measured signal (by up to 10<sup>11</sup>). This effect was originally observed by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Fleischmann" title="Martin Fleischmann">Martin Fleischmann</a> but the prevailing explanation was proposed by Van Duyne in 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> A  comprehensive theory of the effect was given by Lombardi and Birke.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Resonance_Raman_spectroscopy" title="Resonance Raman spectroscopy">Resonance Raman spectroscopy</a></i> – The excitation wavelength is matched to an electronic transition of the molecule or crystal, so that vibrational modes associated with the excited electronic state are greatly enhanced. This is useful for studying large molecules such as <a href="/wiki/Polypeptide" class="mw-redirect" title="Polypeptide">polypeptides</a>, which might show hundreds of bands in "conventional" Raman spectra. It is also useful for associating normal modes with their observed frequency shifts.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Surface-enhanced resonance Raman spectroscopy (SERRS)</i> – A combination of SERS and resonance Raman spectroscopy that uses proximity to a surface to increase Raman intensity, and excitation wavelength matched to the maximum absorbance of the molecule being analysed.</li>
<li><i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Angle_Resolved_Raman_Spectroscopy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Angle Resolved Raman Spectroscopy (page does not exist)">Angle-resolved Raman spectroscopy</a></i> – Not only are standard Raman results recorded but also the angle with respect to the incident laser. If the orientation of the sample is known then detailed information about the phonon dispersion relation can also be gleaned from a single test.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Hyper Raman</i> – A <a href="/wiki/Non-linear_optics" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-linear optics">non-linear</a> effect in which the vibrational modes interact with the <a href="/wiki/Second_harmonic_generation" class="mw-redirect" title="Second harmonic generation">second harmonic</a> of the excitation beam. This requires very high power, but allows the observation of vibrational modes that are normally "silent". It frequently relies on SERS-type enhancement to boost the sensitivity.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Optical tweezers Raman spectroscopy (OTRS)</i> – Used to study individual particles, and even biochemical processes in single cells trapped by <a href="/wiki/Optical_tweezers" title="Optical tweezers">optical tweezers</a>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Stimulated_Raman_spectroscopy" title="Stimulated Raman spectroscopy">Stimulated Raman spectroscopy</a></i> <i>(SRS)</i> – A <a href="/wiki/Pump-probe_spectroscopy" class="mw-redirect" title="Pump-probe spectroscopy">pump-probe</a> technique, where a spatially coincident, two color pulse (with polarization either parallel or perpendicular) transfers the population from ground to a <a href="/wiki/Rovibrational_coupling" class="mw-redirect" title="Rovibrational coupling">rovibrationally</a> excited state. If the difference in energy corresponds to an allowed Raman transition, scattered light will correspond to loss or gain in the pump beam.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Spatially_offset_Raman_spectroscopy" title="Spatially offset Raman spectroscopy">Spatially offset Raman spectroscopy</a> (SORS)</i> – The Raman scattering beneath an obscuring surface is retrieved from a scaled subtraction of two spectra taken at two spatially offset points</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Coherent_anti-Stokes_Raman_spectroscopy" title="Coherent anti-Stokes Raman spectroscopy">Coherent anti-Stokes Raman spectroscopy</a> (CARS)</i> – Two laser beams are used to generate a coherent anti-Stokes frequency beam, which can be enhanced by resonance.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Raman_optical_activity" title="Raman optical activity">Raman optical activity</a> (ROA)</i> – Measures vibrational optical activity by means of a small difference in the intensity of Raman scattering from chiral molecules in right- and left-circularly polarized incident light or, equivalently, a small circularly polarized component in the scattered light.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Transmission_raman" class="mw-redirect" title="Transmission raman">Transmission Raman</a></i> – Allows probing of a significant bulk of a <a href="/wiki/Turbid" class="mw-redirect" title="Turbid">turbid</a> material, such as powders, capsules, living tissue, etc. It was largely ignored following investigations in the late 1960s (<a href="/wiki/Bernhard_Schrader" title="Bernhard Schrader">Schrader</a> and Bergmann, 1967)<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> but was rediscovered in 2006 as a means of rapid assay of <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical" class="mw-redirect" title="Pharmaceutical">pharmaceutical</a> <a href="/wiki/Dosage_forms" class="mw-redirect" title="Dosage forms">dosage forms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>  There are medical diagnostic applications particularly in the detection of cancer.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Inverse_Raman_effect" class="mw-redirect" title="Inverse Raman effect">Inverse Raman spectroscopy</a></i>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Tip-enhanced_Raman_spectroscopy" title="Tip-enhanced Raman spectroscopy">Tip-enhanced Raman spectroscopy</a> (TERS)</i> – Uses a metallic (usually silver-/gold-coated AFM or STM) tip to enhance the Raman signals of molecules situated in its vicinity. The spatial resolution is approximately the size of the tip apex (20–30&#160;nm). TERS has been shown to have sensitivity down to the single molecule level and holds some promise for <a href="/wiki/Bioanalysis" title="Bioanalysis">bioanalysis</a> applications.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Surface_plasmon_polariton" title="Surface plasmon polariton">Surface plasmon polariton</a> enhanced Raman scattering (SPPERS)</i> – This approach exploits apertureless metallic conical tips for near field excitation of molecules. This technique differs from the TERS approach due to its inherent capability of suppressing the background field. In fact, when an appropriate laser source impinges on the base of the cone, a TM0 mode<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> (polaritonic mode) can be locally created, namely far away from the excitation spot (apex of the tip). The mode can propagate along the tip without producing any radiation field up to the tip apex where it interacts with the molecule. In this way, the focal plane is separated from the excitation plane by a distance given by the tip length, and no background plays any role in the Raman excitation of the molecule.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Micro-cavity substrates</i> – A method that improves the detection limit of conventional Raman spectra using micro-Raman in a micro-cavity coated with reflective Au or Ag. The micro-cavity has a radius of several micrometers and enhances the entire Raman signal by providing multiple excitations of the sample and couples the forward-scattered Raman photons toward the collection optics in the back-scattered Raman geometry.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Stand-off remote Raman</i>. In standoff Raman, the sample is measured at a distance from the Raman spectrometer, usually by using a telescope for light collection. Remote Raman spectroscopy was proposed in the 1960s<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> and initially developed for the measurement of atmospheric gases.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> The technique was extended In 1992 by Angel et al. for standoff Raman detection of hazardous inorganic and organic compounds.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Standoff Raman detection offers a fast-Raman mode of analyzing large areas such as a football field in minutes. A pulsed laser source and gated detector allow Raman spectra measurements in the daylight<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> and reduces the long-lived fluorescent background generated by transition ions and rare earth ions. Another way to avoid fluorescence, first demonstrated by Sandy Asher in 1984, is to use a UV laser probe beam. At wavelengths of 260&#160;nm, there is effectively no fluorescence interference and the UV signal is inherently strong.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> A 10X beam expander mounted in front of the laser allows focusing of the beam and a telescope is directly coupled through the camera lens for signal collection. With the system's time-gating capability it is possible to measure remote Raman of your distant target and the atmosphere between the laser and target.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_28-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
