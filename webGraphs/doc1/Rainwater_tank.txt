<p>A <b>rainwater tank</b> (sometimes called a <b>rain barrel</b> in North America in reference to smaller tanks, or a <b>water butt</b> in the UK) is a <a href="/wiki/Water_tank" title="Water tank">water tank</a> used to collect and store rain water <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">runoff</a>, typically from <a href="/wiki/Rooftop" class="mw-redirect" title="Rooftop">rooftops</a> via <a href="/wiki/Pipe_(fluid_conveyance)" title="Pipe (fluid conveyance)">pipes</a>. A rainwater catchment or collection (also known as "<a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting" title="Rainwater harvesting">rainwater harvesting</a>") system can yield 2,358 litres (623&#160;US&#160;gal) of water from 2.54&#160;cm (1.00&#160;in) of rain on a 92.9&#160;m<sup>2</sup> (1,000&#160;sq&#160;ft) roof. Rainwater tanks are devices for collecting and maintaining <a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting" title="Rainwater harvesting">harvested rain</a>.
</p><p>Rainwater tanks are installed to make use of rain water for later use, reduce <a href="/wiki/Public_utility" title="Public utility">mains</a> water use for economic or environmental reasons, and aid <a href="/wiki/Self-sufficiency" class="mw-redirect" title="Self-sufficiency">self-sufficiency</a>. Stored water may be used for watering <a href="/wiki/Garden" title="Garden">gardens</a>, agriculture, <a href="/wiki/Flush_toilet" title="Flush toilet">flushing toilets</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Washing_machine" title="Washing machine">washing machines</a>, washing cars, and also for <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking</a>, especially when other water supplies are unavailable, expensive, or of poor quality, and when adequate care is taken that the water is not <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution#Contaminants" title="Water pollution">contaminated</a> and is adequately filtered.
</p><p>Underground rainwater tanks can also be used for retention of <a href="/wiki/Stormwater" title="Stormwater">stormwater</a> for release at a later time and offer a variety of benefits described in more detail below. In <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> climates, rain barrels are often used to store water during the rainy season for use during dryer periods.
</p><p>Rainwater tanks may have a high (perceived) initial cost. However, many homes use small scale rain barrels to harvest minute quantities of water for landscaping/gardening applications rather than as a potable water surrogate. These small rain barrels, often recycled from food storage and transport barrels or, in some cases, whiskey and wine <a href="/wiki/Aging_barrels" class="mw-redirect" title="Aging barrels">aging barrels</a>, are often inexpensive.  There are also many low cost designs that use locally available materials and village level technologies for applications in developing countries where there are limited alternatives for potable drinking water.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  While most are properly engineered to screen out <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">mosquitoes</a>, the lack of proper filtering or closed loop systems may create breeding grounds for larvae. With tanks used for drinking water, the user runs a health risk if maintenance is not carried out.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contamination_and_maintenance">Contamination and maintenance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_tank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Contamination and maintenance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>If rainwater is used for drinking, it is often filtered first. Filtration (such as <a href="/wiki/Reverse_osmosis" title="Reverse osmosis">reverse osmosis</a>, ultraviolet sterilization, or <a href="/wiki/Ultrafiltration" title="Ultrafiltration">ultrafiltration</a>) may remove pathogens. While rain water is pure it may become contaminated during collection or by collection of particulate matter in the air as it falls.<sup id="cite_ref-twdb_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-twdb-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> While rain water does not contain <a href="/wiki/Water_chlorination" title="Water chlorination">chlorine</a>, contamination from <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">airborne pollutants</a>, which settles onto rooftops, may be a risk in urban or industrial areas. Many water suppliers and health authorities, such as the <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales_Department_of_Health" class="mw-redirect" title="New South Wales Department of Health">New South Wales Department of Health</a>, do not advise using rainwater for drinking when there is an alternative mains water supply available. However, reports of illness associated with rainwater tanks are relatively infrequent, and public health studies in <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a> (the Australian state with the highest rainwater usage rate) have not identified a correlation. Rainwater is generally considered fit to drink if it smells, tastes and looks fine;<sup id="cite_ref-choice_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-choice-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However some pathogens, chemical contamination and sub-micrometre suspended metal may produce neither smell nor taste and may not be visible.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Australian standards may differ greatly from other places in the world where rainwater is commonly used for drinking water.  In the United States, rainwater is being increasingly used throughout the country for various purposes.  In the semi-arid western state of New Mexico, for instance, many residents in the Taos and Santa Fe areas in particular use rainwater either for landscaping purposes or even all household uses (including potable indoor water).  The "smells, tastes, and looks fine" standard used in the above paragraph is not an absolute indicator of rainwater safety.  Most people who are rainwater users for potable purposes in the USA make certain that their water is safe through filtration, ultraviolet sterilization, and testing.
</p><p>Certain paints and roofing materials may cause contamination. In particular, a <a href="/wiki/Melbourne_Water" title="Melbourne Water">Melbourne Water</a> publication advises that <a href="/wiki/Lead-based_paint" class="mw-redirect" title="Lead-based paint">lead-based paints</a> never be used. <a href="/wiki/Tar" title="Tar">Tar</a>-based coatings are also not recommended, as they affect the taste of the water. Zinc can also be a source of contamination in some paints, as well as <a href="/wiki/Galvanized_iron" class="mw-redirect" title="Galvanized iron">galvanized iron</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> or <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zincalume&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Zincalume (page does not exist)">zincalume</a> roofs, particularly when new, should not collect water for potable use. Roofs painted with <a href="/wiki/Acrylic_paint" title="Acrylic paint">acrylic paints</a> may have detergents and other chemicals dissolve in the runoff. Runoff from <a href="/wiki/Fibrous_Cement" class="mw-redirect" title="Fibrous Cement">fibrous cement</a> roofs should be discarded for an entire winter, due to leaching of lime. Chemically treated timbers and <a href="/wiki/Lead_flashing" class="mw-redirect" title="Lead flashing">lead flashing</a> should not be used in roof catchments. Likewise, rainwater should not be collected from parts of the roof incorporating flues from wood burners without a high degree of filtration. Overflows or discharge pipes from roof-mounted appliances such as air-conditioners or hot-water systems should not have their discharge feed into a rainwater tank.
</p><p>"Copper Poisoning," a 2010 news article, linked copper poisoning to plastic tanks. The article indicated that rainwater was collected and stored in plastic tanks and that the tank did nothing to mitigate the low pH.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The water was then brought into homes by copper piping. The copper was released by the high acid rainwater and caused poisoning in humans. It is important to note that, while the plastic tank is an inert container, the collected acid rain could and should be analysed and pH adjusted before being brought into a domestic water supply system. The solution is to monitor stored rainwater with <i>swimming pool strips,</i> cheap and available at swimming pool supply outlets.
</p><p>If the water is too acidic, the state, county or local health officials may be contacted to obtain advice, precise solutions and pH limits, and guidelines as to what should be used to treat rainwater to be used as domestic drinking water.
</p><p>Maintenance includes checking roofs and <a href="/wiki/Rain_gutter" title="Rain gutter">rain gutters</a> for <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Debris" title="Debris">debris</a>, maintaining <a href="/wiki/Sieve" title="Sieve">screens</a> around the tank, and occasionally <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/desludging" class="extiw" title="wikt:desludging">desludging</a> (removing <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> by draining and cleaning the tank of algae and other contaminants).
</p><p>Rainwater tanks which are not properly sealed (secured at the top) may act as breeding grounds for <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">mosquitoes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several options for dealing with the mosquito issue are:
1. Flushing ALL water once a week
2. Using a small amount of cooking oil to suffocate the larvae (the water is still ok for landscape use after this)
3. Adding the bacillus Bt to the water. This bacteria will not harm animals.
4. Adding mosquito eating fish
</p><p>Another way to store rainwater without worry of contamination by mosquitoes is to use underground storage tanks.  Underground tanks keep the water too cool (12.8&#160;°C (55.0&#160;°F)) for mosquito larvae and also are dark, preventing both mosquito, bacterial, and algae growth. An article by Richard Hill goes into depth about the benefits of underground rainwater storage.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tanks">Tanks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_tank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tanks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WaterBarrelPlastic.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/WaterBarrelPlastic.JPG/180px-WaterBarrelPlastic.JPG" width="180" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/WaterBarrelPlastic.JPG/270px-WaterBarrelPlastic.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/WaterBarrelPlastic.JPG/360px-WaterBarrelPlastic.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1944" data-file-height="1776" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WaterBarrelPlastic.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A plastic water container (<a href="/wiki/Intermediate_bulk_container" title="Intermediate bulk container">IBC</a>)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rainwater_tanks_01_pengo.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Rainwater_tanks_01_pengo.jpg/180px-Rainwater_tanks_01_pengo.jpg" width="180" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Rainwater_tanks_01_pengo.jpg/270px-Rainwater_tanks_01_pengo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Rainwater_tanks_01_pengo.jpg/360px-Rainwater_tanks_01_pengo.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="931" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rainwater_tanks_01_pengo.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Large polyethylene rainwater tanks being installed.</div></div></div>
<p>Rainwater tanks may be constructed from materials such as plastic (<a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a>), <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galvanized_steel" class="mw-redirect" title="Galvanized steel">galvanized steel</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Fiberglass" title="Fiberglass">fiberglass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stainless_steel" title="Stainless steel">stainless steel</a> which are rust and chemical-resistant. Those most safely used are typically <a href="/wiki/Upcycling" title="Upcycling">upcycled</a> from previous food-grade purposes. Tanks are usually installed above ground, and are usually <a href="/wiki/Opacity_(optics)" title="Opacity (optics)">opaque</a> to prevent the exposure of stored water to sunlight, to decrease <a href="/wiki/Algal_bloom" title="Algal bloom">algal bloom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-twdb_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-twdb-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Tanks may be <a href="/wiki/Seal_(mechanical)" title="Seal (mechanical)">covered</a> and have screen inlets to exclude <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a href="/wiki/Debris" title="Debris">debris</a>, animals and <a href="/wiki/Bird_droppings" class="mw-redirect" title="Bird droppings">bird droppings</a>. Almost all steel tanks currently produced for household rainwater collection come with a plastic inner lining to increase the life of the tank, prevent leaks and protect the <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a>.
</p><p>Apart from <a href="/wiki/Rooftop" class="mw-redirect" title="Rooftop">rooftops</a>, tanks may also be set up to collect rainwater from concrete <a href="/wiki/Patio" title="Patio">patios</a>, <a href="/wiki/Driveway" title="Driveway">driveways</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Impervious_surface" title="Impervious surface">impervious surfaces</a>.
</p><p>Initial sizes typically ranged in capacity from around 400 to 100,000 litres (110 to 26,420&#160;US&#160;gal), today modern technology has allowed modular and scalable applications to go into sizes of millions of litres or hundreds of thousands of US <a href="/wiki/Gallon" title="Gallon">gallons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Smaller tanks, such as the plastic (<a href="/wiki/44_gallon_drum" class="mw-redirect" title="44 gallon drum">208 litres (55&#160;US&#160;gal)-barrel</a>) are also used in some cases. Modern modular systems which are scalable, like the 193 litres (51&#160;US&#160;gal) <a href="/wiki/Rainwater_Hog" title="Rainwater Hog">Rainwater HOG</a> module<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and the 500 litre (133 gallon) Stratco Aquabarrel<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> can be used to decentralize the rainwater catchment by storing smaller volumes at each downspout.  Larger tanks are commonly used where there is no access to a <a href="/wiki/Centralised_water_supply" class="mw-redirect" title="Centralised water supply">centralised water supply</a>. Companies such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_Survival_Architecture&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Solar Survival Architecture (page does not exist)">Solar Survival Architecture</a> recommend  a 1,135 litres (300&#160;US&#160;gal) tank for a house supporting two people (if <a href="/wiki/Compost_toilet" class="mw-redirect" title="Compost toilet">compost toilets</a> are placed) and if the region receives at least 762&#160;mm (30.0&#160;in) of precipitation a year. If it receives less (between 254–762&#160;mm (10.0–30.0&#160;in), 2 or 3 of these 300-gallon tanks can be placed so that more rain can be gathered at times when it does rain. Also affecting tank size is predicted rainfall and rainfall variability; the higher prices for larger tanks; intended use of rainwater and typical consumption for these uses; the area of roof draining into the tank; security of supply desired.
</p><p>Most rainwater catchment tanks used throughout the world are composed of virgin polyethylene, a substance which in the USA is both FDA and NSF approved for potable water storage.  Other types of tanks used for rainwater storage include fiberglass, galvanized metal, stainless steel, and concrete.  Each type of tank has positive and negative aspects.  Polyethylene tanks, when placed above ground, can be subject to algae growth as well as the possibility of a short life (@20 years) due to normal UV exposure in sunlight. The very strong fiberglass tanks must undergo a specific coating process to be brought up to potable grade.  Galvanized tanks must either be lined or coated both for potability as well as to prevent the inevitable rusting at any welded seams.  Uncoated galvanized tanks will leach zinc into the stored water and are not recommended in most instances - certainly not for water stored for human consumption. Concrete tanks leach a more benign substance - lime - into stored water and many are used around the world for rainwater storage.
</p><p>One method of harvesting rain water has been is modular, scalable systems which are installable underground. These came as an evolution of a <a href="/wiki/Geosynthetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Geosynthetic">geosynthetic</a> applications called <a href="/wiki/Geosynthetic#Drainage_/_Infiltration_Cells" class="mw-redirect" title="Geosynthetic">Infiltration Tanks</a>, which when stacked provide a void space volume which allows for the storing of water. Improved and more cost effective industrial design now allow for theoretically limitless storage of water underground. Examples of these modular structures are Atlantis Matrix Tanks<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> used in the Manly Stormwater Treatment and Re-use project of <a href="/wiki/Manly_Council" title="Manly Council">Manly Council</a> in Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_supply_augmentation">Water supply augmentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_tank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Water supply augmentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In some cities, installation of rainwater tanks may be mandatory, or may help a new building be approved. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Victoria, Australia">Victoria, Australia</a> new houses which have rainwater tank connected to all flush toilets are given an additional 1-star of the required 5-star <a href="/wiki/House_Energy_Rating" title="House Energy Rating">House Energy Rating</a>. Some governments subsidise purchases of rainwater tanks or provide rebates in areas where they are considered an important means of water supply augmentation.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  In the United States, Santa Fe County, New Mexico requires a rainwater collection system on all new construction with greater than 2,500 square feet, mostly for landscaping purposes and to prevent over-reliance on wells but in some instances because ground water is prohibitively expensive to obtain, if even available.
</p><p>Rainwater to supplement drinking water supplies may be seen as an alternative to other water supply options such as <a href="/wiki/Reclaimed_water" title="Reclaimed water">recycling</a> or seawater <a href="/wiki/Desalination" title="Desalination">desalination</a>. Tanks are often perceived to have environmental costs that are comparatively lower than other water supply augmentation options.
</p><p>Rainwater collection can be made compatible with centralised water supply by tapping it using an <a href="/wiki/Electropump" class="mw-redirect" title="Electropump">electropump</a>.
</p><p>Widespread use of rain barrels also changes the amount of rainwater reaching the ground in a particular area and draining into streams.  Depending on the climate, this either helps prevent erosion, sedimentation, and/or pollution, and can reduce the strain on stormwater drainage systems; or it could cause rivers to dry up and ponds to stagnate if the water is diverted to a different watershed.  If collected water is used in the same watershed in which it is collected, rainwater collection actually can stabilize flow in rivers and provide more regular and filtered groundwater transfer into ponds.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colorado_law">Colorado law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_tank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Colorado law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the State of <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, United States, the installation of rainwater collection barrels is subject to the Constitution of the State of Colorado, state statutes and case law.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This is a consequence of the system of water rights in the state; the movement and holding of rainwater is inextricably linked with ownership of water rights and is enshrined in the <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitution</a> of the State of Colorado.  The use of water in Colorado and other <a href="/wiki/Western_United_States" title="Western United States">western states</a> is governed by what is known as the <a href="/wiki/Prior_appropriation_doctrine" class="mw-redirect" title="Prior appropriation doctrine">prior appropriation doctrine</a>. This system of water allocation controls who uses how much water, the types of uses allowed, and when those waters can be used. This is often referred to as the priority system or "first in time, first in right." Since all water arriving in Colorado has been allocated to "senior water right holders" since the 1850s, rainwater prevented from running downstream may not be available to its rightful owner. In 2009, legislation in Colorado was enacted that permits capture of rain water for residential use subject to strong limitations and conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> To be permitted, a residence may not be connected to a domestic water supply system serving more than 3 single-family dwellings. The permit must be purchased from the State Engineer's office and is subject to water usage restrictions.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_rainwater_tank">Internal rainwater tank</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainwater_tank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Internal rainwater tank">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rainwater tanks or drums may be used inside a house to provide thermal mass for a <a href="/wiki/Trombe_wall" title="Trombe wall">trombe wall</a> (or <b>water wall</b>).  <a href="/wiki/Rainwater_Hog" title="Rainwater Hog">Rainwater Hog</a> modular tanks invented by <a href="/wiki/Sally_Dominguez" title="Sally Dominguez">Sally Dominguez</a> to fit within building structure were used in the Modabode House of the Future floor and on the foyer wall of the Department of Sustainability building in Anglesea, Victoria, harnessing the higher value of the stored rainwater to add effective thermal mass to the enclosed spaces.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Specially designed rainwater tanks can also be embedded in or under the <a href="/wiki/Concrete_slab" title="Concrete slab">concrete slab</a> of a building (<i>stab tank</i>).
</p><p>A house in Cape Schanck <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Victoria, Australia">Victoria</a>, Australia uses an internal rainwater tank to provide cooling to the living room in summer. During winter the tank is drained and wrapped in an insulating jacket. The tank also provides structural support to the roof, and excess water is used for domestic use including drinking.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
