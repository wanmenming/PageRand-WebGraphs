<p><b>Boron trioxide</b> (or <b>diboron trioxide</b>) is one of the <a href="/wiki/Boron_oxide" title="Boron oxide">oxides of boron</a>. It is a white, glassy solid with the formula B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. It is almost always found as the vitreous (amorphous) form; however, it can be crystallized after extensive <a href="/wiki/Annealing_(metallurgy)" title="Annealing (metallurgy)">annealing</a> (that is, under prolonged heat).
</p><p>Glassy boron oxide (g-B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) is thought to be composed of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Boroxol_rings&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Boroxol rings (page does not exist)">boroxol rings</a> which are six-membered rings composed of alternating 3-coordinate boron and 2-coordinate oxygen. Because of the difficulty of building disordered models at the correct density with a large number of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Boroxol_rings&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Boroxol rings (page does not exist)">boroxol rings</a>, this view was initially controversial, but such models have recently been constructed and exhibit properties in excellent agreement with experiment.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It is now recognized, from experimental and theoretical studies,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> that the fraction of boron atoms belonging to boroxol rings in glassy B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is somewhere between 0.73 and 0.83, with 0.75 (&#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>3</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span>) corresponding to a 1:1 ratio between ring and non-ring units. 
</p><p>The crystalline form (α-B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) (see structure in the infobox<sup id="cite_ref-a1_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-a1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) is exclusively composed of BO<sub>3</sub> triangles. This trigonal, <a href="/wiki/Trapezohedron" title="Trapezohedron">quartz-like</a> network undergoes a <a href="/wiki/Coesite" title="Coesite">coesite</a>-like transformation to <a href="/wiki/Monoclinic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> β-B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> at several gigapascals (9.5 GPa).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boron_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Boron trioxide is produced by treating <a href="/wiki/Borax" title="Borax">borax</a> with <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> in a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fusion_furnace&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fusion furnace (page does not exist)">fusion furnace</a>. At temperatures above 750&#160;°C, the molten boron oxide layer separates out from <a href="/wiki/Sodium_sulfate" title="Sodium sulfate">sodium sulfate</a>. It is then decanted, cooled and obtained in 96–97% purity.<sup id="cite_ref-patnaik_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another method is heating <a href="/wiki/Boric_acid" title="Boric acid">boric acid</a> above ~300&#160;°C. Boric acid will initially decompose into steam, (H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>(g)</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Metaboric_acid" title="Metaboric acid">metaboric acid</a> (HBO<sub>2</sub>) at around 170&#160;°C, and further heating above 300&#160;°C will produce more steam and boron trioxide. The reactions are:
</p>
<dl><dd>H<sub>3</sub>BO<sub>3</sub> → HBO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>2 HBO<sub>2</sub> → B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Boric acid goes to anhydrous microcrystalline B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> in a heated fluidized bed.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  Carefully controlled heating rate avoids gumming as water evolves.  Molten boron oxide attacks silicates.  Internally graphitized tubes via acetylene thermal decomposition are passivated.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Crystallization of molten α-B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> at ambient pressure is strongly kinetically disfavored (compare liquid and crystal densities).  Threshold conditions for crystallization of the amorphous solid are 10 kbar and ~200&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  Its proposed crystal structure in enantiomorphic space groups P3<sub>1</sub>(#144); P3<sub>2</sub>(#145)<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> (e.g., γ-glycine) has been revised to enantiomorphic space groups P3<sub>1</sub>21(#152); P3<sub>2</sub>21(#154)<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>(e.g., α-quartz).
</p><p>Boron oxide will also form when <a href="/wiki/Diborane" title="Diborane">diborane</a> (B<sub>2</sub>H<sub>6</sub>) reacts with oxygen in the air or trace amounts of moisture:
</p>
<dl><dd>2B<sub>2</sub>H<sub>6</sub>(g) + 3O<sub>2</sub>(g) → 2B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>(s) + 6H<sub>2</sub>(g)</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>B<sub>2</sub>H<sub>6</sub>(g) + 3H<sub>2</sub>O(g) → B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>(s) + 6H<sub>2</sub>(g)<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boron_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ceramic_flux" title="Ceramic flux">Fluxing agent</a> for <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">enamels</a></li>
<li>Starting material for synthesizing other <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a> compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Boron_carbide" title="Boron carbide">boron carbide</a></li>
<li>An additive used in glass fibres (<a href="/wiki/Optical_fibre" class="mw-redirect" title="Optical fibre">optical fibres</a>)</li>
<li>It is used in the production of <a href="/wiki/Borosilicate_glass" title="Borosilicate glass">borosilicate glass</a></li>
<li>The inert capping layer in the Liquid Encapsulation Czochralski process for the production of <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">gallium arsenide</a> single crystal</li>
<li>As an acid <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> in <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a></li></ul>
