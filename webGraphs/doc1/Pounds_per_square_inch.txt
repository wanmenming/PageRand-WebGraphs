<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Pounds per square inch</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>The <b>pound per square inch</b> or, more accurately, <b>pound-force per square inch</b> (symbol: <b>lbf/in<sup>2</sup></b>;<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> abbreviation: <b>psi</b>) is a unit of <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> or of <a href="/wiki/Stress_(mechanics)" title="Stress (mechanics)">stress</a> based on <a href="/wiki/Avoirdupois" class="mw-redirect" title="Avoirdupois">avoirdupois</a> units. It is the pressure resulting from a force of one <a href="/wiki/Pound-force" class="mw-redirect" title="Pound-force">pound-force</a> applied to an area of one <a href="/wiki/Square_inch" title="Square inch">square inch</a>. In <a href="/wiki/SI_units" class="mw-redirect" title="SI units">SI units</a>, 1 psi is approximately equal to 6895 <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">N</a>/<a href="/wiki/Square_meter" class="mw-redirect" title="Square meter">m<sup>2</sup></a>.
</p><p><b>Pounds per square inch absolute</b> (<b>psia</b>) is used to make it clear that the pressure is relative to a vacuum rather than the ambient atmospheric pressure. Since atmospheric pressure at sea level is around 14.7&#160;psi, this will be added to any pressure reading made in air at sea level. The converse is <b>pounds per square inch gauge</b> (<b>psig</b>), indicating that the pressure is relative to atmospheric pressure. For example, a bicycle tire pumped up to 65&#160;psig in a local atmospheric pressure at sea level (14.7&#160;psia) will have a pressure of 79.7&#160;psia (14.7 psi + 65 psi).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> When gauge pressure is referenced to something other than ambient atmospheric pressure, then the units would be <b>pounds per square inch differential (psid)</b>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Multiples">Multiples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pounds_per_square_inch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Multiples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b>kilopound per square inch</b> (ksi) is a scaled unit derived from psi, equivalent to a thousand psi (1000 lbf/in<sup>2</sup>).
</p><p>ksi are not widely used for gas pressures. They are mostly used in <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" class="mw-redirect" title="Tensile strength">tensile strength</a> of a material is measured as a large number of psi.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The conversion in SI Units is 1 ksi = 6.895 MPa, or 1 MPa = 0.145 ksi.
</p><p>The <b>megapound per square inch</b> (Mpsi) is another multiple equal to a million psi. It is used in <a href="/wiki/Mechanics" title="Mechanics">mechanics</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Elastic_modulus" title="Elastic modulus">elastic modulus</a> of materials, especially for metals.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The conversion in SI Units is 1 Mpsi = 6.895 GPa, or 1 GPa = 0.145 Mpsi.
</p>
