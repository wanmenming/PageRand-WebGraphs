<p><b>Arsenic trioxide</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">As<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>. This commercially important <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> of <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a> is the main precursor to other arsenic compounds, including <a href="/wiki/Organoarsenic_compound" class="mw-redirect" title="Organoarsenic compound">organoarsenic compounds</a>. Approximately 50,000 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> are produced annually.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Many applications are controversial given the high toxicity of arsenic compounds.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Large scale applications include its use as a precursor to forestry products, in colorless glass production, and in electronics.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Being the main compound of arsenic, the trioxide is the precursor to elemental arsenic, arsenic alloys, and <a href="/wiki/Arsenide" title="Arsenide">arsenide</a> <a href="/wiki/Semiconductors" class="mw-redirect" title="Semiconductors">semiconductors</a>. <a href="/wiki/Organoarsenic_compound" class="mw-redirect" title="Organoarsenic compound">Organoarsenic compounds</a>, e.g. feed additives (<a href="/wiki/Roxarsone" title="Roxarsone">Roxarsone</a>) and pharmaceuticals (<a href="/wiki/Neosalvarsan" title="Neosalvarsan">Neosalvarsan</a>), are derived from arsenic trioxide. Bulk arsenic-based compounds <a href="/wiki/Sodium_arsenite" title="Sodium arsenite">sodium arsenite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_cacodylate" class="mw-redirect" title="Sodium cacodylate">sodium cacodylate</a> are derived from the trioxide.
</p><p>A variety of applications exploit arsenic's toxicity, including the use of the oxide as a <a href="/wiki/Wood_preservative" class="mw-redirect" title="Wood preservative">wood preservative</a>. <a href="/wiki/Copper_arsenate" class="mw-redirect" title="Copper arsenate">Copper arsenates</a>, which are derived from arsenic trioxide, are used on a large scale as a wood preservative in the US and Malaysia, but such materials are banned in many parts of the world. This practice remains controversial.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In combination with <a href="/wiki/Copper(II)_acetate" title="Copper(II) acetate">copper(II) acetate</a> arsenic trioxide gives the vibrant pigment known as <a href="/wiki/Paris_green" title="Paris green">Paris green</a> used in paints and as a rodenticide. This application has been discontinued.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_applications">Medical applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Medical applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite the well known toxicity of arsenic, arsenic trioxide has long been of biomedical interest, dating to <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_medicine" title="Traditional Chinese medicine">traditional Chinese medicine</a>, where it is known as <i>pi-shuang</i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh">砒霜</span>, Pinyin: pīshuāng) and is still used to treat cancer and other conditions,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and to <a href="/wiki/Homeopathy" title="Homeopathy">homeopathy</a>, where it is called <a href="/wiki/Arsenicum_album" title="Arsenicum album">arsenicum album</a>. Some discredited <a href="/wiki/Patent_medicine" title="Patent medicine">patent medicines</a>, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Fowler%27s_solution" title="Fowler&#39;s solution">Fowler's solution</a>, contained derivatives of arsenic oxide.<sup id="cite_ref-ITOM_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITOM-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1970s Chinese researcher <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Tingdong" title="Zhang Tingdong">Zhang Tingdong</a> and colleagues investigated the potential use of the traditionally used <a href="/wiki/Chinese_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese medicine">Chinese medicine</a> arsenic trioxide to treat <a href="/wiki/Acute_promyelocytic_leukemia" title="Acute promyelocytic leukemia">acute promyelocytic leukemia</a> (APL).<sup id="cite_ref-dfp_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dfp-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Building on his work, research both in China and the West eventually led to the development of the drug Trisenox by PolaRx Biopharmaceuticals, Inc through NDA, which was approved for leukemia treatment by the US FDA in 2000 and subsequently marketed and sold by Cell Therapeutics, Inc., before being acquired by Cephalon.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Arsenic trioxide, under the tradename Trisenox (manufacturer: Cephalon acquired by Teva), is a chemotherapeutic agent approved by the US FDA for the treatment of acute promyelocytic <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemia</a> that is unresponsive to "first line" agents, namely all-trans retinoic acid (ATRA). It has been shown that arsenic trioxide induces cancer cells to undergo <a href="/wiki/Apoptosis" title="Apoptosis">apoptosis</a>. Due to the toxic nature of arsenic, this drug carries significant risks. Use as a <a href="/wiki/Cytostatic" class="mw-redirect" title="Cytostatic">cytostatic</a> in the treatment of refractory promyelocytic (M3) subtype of acute myeloid <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The combination therapy of arsenic trioxide and <a href="/wiki/Tretinoin" title="Tretinoin">all-trans retinoic acid</a> (ATRA) has been approved by the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Food and Drug Administration (United States)">U.S. Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA) for treatment of certain leukemias.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hong_Kong" title="University of Hong Kong">University of Hong Kong</a> developed a liquid form of arsenic trioxide that can be administered orally.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicology">Toxicology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Toxicology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Arsenicosis" class="mw-redirect" title="Arsenicosis">Arsenicosis</a></div>
<p>Arsenic trioxide is readily absorbed by the digestive system: toxic effects are also well known upon inhalation or upon skin contact. Elimination is rapid at first (half-life of 1–2 days), by methylation to monomethylarsonic acid and dimethylarsonic acid, and excretion in the urine, but a certain amount (30–40% in the case of repeated exposure) is incorporated into the bones, muscles, skin, hair and nails (all tissues rich in <a href="/wiki/Keratin" title="Keratin">keratin</a>) and eliminated over a period of weeks or months.
</p><p>The first symptoms of acute <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_poisoning" title="Arsenic poisoning">arsenic poisoning</a> by ingestion are digestive problems: vomiting, abdominal pains, diarrhea often accompanied by bleeding. Sub-lethal doses can lead to <a href="/wiki/Convulsions" class="mw-redirect" title="Convulsions">convulsions</a>, cardiovascular problems, inflammation of the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kidneys" class="mw-redirect" title="Kidneys">kidneys</a> and abnormalities in the coagulation of the blood. These are followed by the appearance of characteristic white lines (<a href="/wiki/Mees%27_lines" title="Mees&#39; lines">Mees' lines</a>) on the nails and by hair loss. Lower doses lead to liver and kidney problems and to changes in the pigmentation of the skin. Even dilute solutions of arsenic trioxide are dangerous on contact with the eyes.
</p><p>The poisonous properties are legendary and the subject of an extensive literature.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Murder_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Murder-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chronic arsenic poisoning is known as arsenicosis. This disorder affects workers in <a href="/wiki/Smelters" class="mw-redirect" title="Smelters">smelters</a>, in populations whose <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking water</a> contains high levels of arsenic (0.3–0.4&#160;ppm), and in patients treated for long periods with arsenic-based pharmaceuticals. Similarly, studies on workers exposed in <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> foundries in the U.S., Japan and Sweden indicate a risk of lung cancer 6–10 times higher for the most exposed workers compared with the general population. Long-term ingestion of arsenic trioxide either in drinking water or as a medical treatment can lead to skin cancer. Reproductive problems (high incidences of miscarriage, low birth weight, congenital deformations) have also been indicated in one study of women exposed to arsenic trioxide dust as employees or neighbours of a copper foundry.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, there lived the so-called "arsenic eaters of <a href="/wiki/Styria" title="Styria">Styria</a>", who ingested doses far beyond the lethal dose of arsenic trioxide without any apparent harm. Arsenic is thought to enable strenuous work at high altitudes, e.g. in the Alps.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the U.S., the <a href="/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration" title="Occupational Safety and Health Administration">OSHA</a> 1910.1018 occupational <a href="/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit" title="Permissible exposure limit">permissible exposure limit</a> for inorganic arsenic compounds in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Breathing_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Breathing zone (page does not exist)">breathing zone</a> air is 0.010&#160;mg/m<sup>3</sup>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_and_occurrence">Production and occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production and occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St.BlasenArsenikstollen5.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/St.BlasenArsenikstollen5.jpg/170px-St.BlasenArsenikstollen5.jpg" width="170" height="302" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/St.BlasenArsenikstollen5.jpg/255px-St.BlasenArsenikstollen5.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/St.BlasenArsenikstollen5.jpg/340px-St.BlasenArsenikstollen5.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2736" data-file-height="4864" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St.BlasenArsenikstollen5.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Historical arsenic mine <a href="/wiki/Sankt_Blasen" title="Sankt Blasen">Sankt Blasen</a>, Austria</div></div></div>
<p>Arsenic trioxide can be generated via routine processing of arsenic compounds including the oxidation (combustion) of arsenic and arsenic-containing minerals in <a href="/wiki/Air" class="mw-redirect" title="Air">air</a>. Illustrative is the roasting of <a href="/wiki/Orpiment" title="Orpiment">orpiment</a>, a typical arsenic sulfide ore.
</p>
<dl><dd>2 <span class="chemf nowrap">As<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>S<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> + 9 <span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> → 2 <span class="chemf nowrap">As<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> + 6 <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>Most arsenic oxide is, however, obtained as a volatile by-product of the processing of other ores. For example, <a href="/wiki/Arsenopyrite" title="Arsenopyrite">arsenopyrite</a>, a common impurity in gold- and copper-containing ores, liberates arsenic trioxide upon heating in air. The processing of such minerals has led to numerous cases of poisonings.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Only in China are arsenic ores intentionally mined.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the laboratory, it is prepared by hydrolysis of <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_trichloride" title="Arsenic trichloride">arsenic trichloride</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-Brauer_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brauer-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 AsCl<sub>3</sub>   +  3 H<sub>2</sub>O   →   As<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 6 HCl</dd></dl>
<p><span class="chemf nowrap">As<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> occurs naturally as two minerals, <a href="/wiki/Arsenolite" title="Arsenolite">arsenolite</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system" title="Cubic crystal system">cubic</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Claudetite" title="Claudetite">claudetite</a> (<a href="/wiki/Monoclinic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a>). Both are relatively rare secondary minerals found in <a href="/wiki/Oxidation" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a> zones of As-rich ore deposits. Sheets of As<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> stand for part of structures of the recently discovered minerals lucabindiite, (K,NH<sub>4</sub>)As<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub>(Cl,Br),<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and its sodium-analogue torrecillasite.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_and_reactions">Properties and reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Properties and reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arsenic trioxide is an <a href="/wiki/Amphoteric" class="mw-redirect" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a> oxide, and its aqueous solutions are weakly <a href="/wiki/Acidic_oxide" title="Acidic oxide">acidic</a>. Thus, it dissolves readily in alkaline solutions to give <a href="/wiki/Arsenite" title="Arsenite">arsenites</a>. It is less soluble in acids, although it will dissolve in <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-G&amp;W_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;W-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With anhydrous HF and HCl, it gives AsF<sub>3</sub> and the trichloride:<sup id="cite_ref-Brauer_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brauer-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>As<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 6 HX  → 2 AsX<sub>3</sub> +  3 H<sub>2</sub>O   (X = F, Cl)</dd></dl>
<p>Only with strong <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agents</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> does it yield <a href="/wiki/Arsenic_pentoxide" title="Arsenic pentoxide">arsenic pentoxide</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">As<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span></span> or its corresponding acid:<sup id="cite_ref-Brauer_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brauer-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 HNO<sub>3</sub> + As<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>  +  2 H<sub>2</sub>O →  2 H<sub>3</sub>AsO<sub>4</sub>  +  N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>In terms of its resistance to oxidation, arsenic trioxide differs from <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_trioxide" title="Phosphorus trioxide">phosphorus trioxide</a>, which readily combusts to <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_pentoxide" title="Phosphorus pentoxide">phosphorus pentoxide</a>.
</p><p>Reduction gives elemental arsenic or <a href="/wiki/Arsine" title="Arsine">arsine</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">AsH<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>) depending on conditions:<sup id="cite_ref-Brauer_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brauer-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>As<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 6 Zn + 12 HNO<sub>3</sub> → 2 AsH<sub>3</sub> + 6 Zn(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>This reaction is used in the <a href="/wiki/Marsh_test" title="Marsh test">Marsh test</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the liquid and gas phase below 800&#160;°C, arsenic trioxide has the formula <span class="chemf nowrap">As<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> and is isostructural with <span class="chemf nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_trioxide" title="Phosphorus trioxide">P<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></a></span>. Above 800&#160;°C <span class="chemf nowrap">As<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup dissociates into molecular <span class="chemf nowrap">As<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup same structure as <span class="chemf nowrap"><a href="/wiki/Dinitrogen_trioxide" title="Dinitrogen trioxide">N<span Three forms (<a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a>) are known in the solid state:  a high temperature ( &gt; 110&#160;°C) cubic <span <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_problems">Environmental problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arsenic_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Environmental problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">Smelting</a> and related ore processing often generate arsenic trioxide, which poses a severe risks to the <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a>. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Giant_Mine" title="Giant Mine">Giant Mine</a> in Canada processed substantial amounts of <a href="/wiki/Arsenopyrite" title="Arsenopyrite">arsenopyrite</a>-contaminated gold ores.
</p>
