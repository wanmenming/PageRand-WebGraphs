<p><b>Antimony pentoxide</b> (<a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">Sb<sub>2</sub></a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O<sub>5</sub></a>) is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> of <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.  It always occurs in hydrated form, Sb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>·<i>n</i>H<sub>2</sub>O.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It contains antimony in the +5 oxidation state.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimony_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Antimony pentoxide has the same structure as the <i>B</i> form of <a href="/wiki/Niobium_pentoxide" title="Niobium pentoxide">niobium pentoxide</a> and can be derived from the <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a> structure, with antimony coordinated by six oxygens in a distorted octahedral arrangement. The SbO<sub>6</sub> octahedra are corner- and edge-sharing.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimony_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The hydrated oxide is prepared by <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Antimony_pentachloride" title="Antimony pentachloride">antimony pentachloride</a>; or by acidification of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Potassium_hexahydroxoantimonate(V)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Potassium hexahydroxoantimonate(V) (page does not exist)">potassium hexahydroxoantimonate(V)</a>.  It may also be prepared by oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Antimony_trioxide" title="Antimony trioxide">antimony trioxide</a> with <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimony_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It finds use as a <a href="/wiki/Flame_retardant" title="Flame retardant">flame retardant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Acrylonitrile_butadiene_styrene" title="Acrylonitrile butadiene styrene">ABS</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastics</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Flocculant" class="mw-redirect" title="Flocculant">flocculant</a> in the production of <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">titanium dioxide</a> and is sometimes used in the production of <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paint" title="Paint">paint</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adhesive" title="Adhesive">adhesives</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is also used as an ion-exchange <a href="/wiki/Resin" title="Resin">resin</a> for a number of cations in acidic solution including Na<sup>+</sup> (especially for their selective retentions); and as a <a href="/wiki/Polymerization" title="Polymerization">polymerization</a> and oxidation catalyst.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_and_reactions">Properties and reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antimony_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Properties and reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The hydrated oxide is insoluble in nitric acid, but dissolves in concentrated <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">potassium hydroxide</a> solution to give potassium hexahydroxoantimonate(V), KSb(OH)<sub>6</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-Handbook_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Handbook-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When heated at 700&#160;°C the yellow hydrated pentoxide converts to an anhydrous white solid with a formula Sb<sub>6</sub>O<sub>13</sub> containing both Sb(III) and Sb(V).  Heating at 900&#160;°C produces a white insoluble powder of Sb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> of both α and β forms. The β form consists of Sb(V) in octahedral interstices and pyramidal Sb(III) O<sub>4</sub> units.  In these compounds, Sb(V) atom is octahedrally coordinated to six –OH groups.
</p><p>The pentoxide can be reduced to <a href="/wiki/Antimony" title="Antimony">antimony</a> metal by heating with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> or <a href="/wiki/Potassium_cyanide" title="Potassium cyanide">potassium cyanide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
