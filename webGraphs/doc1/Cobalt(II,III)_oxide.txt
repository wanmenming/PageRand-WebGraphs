<p><b>Cobalt(II,III) oxide</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>. It is one of two well characterized <a href="/wiki/Cobalt_oxide" title="Cobalt oxide">cobalt oxides</a>. It is a black <a href="/wiki/Antiferromagnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Antiferromagnetic">antiferromagnetic</a> solid.  As a <a href="/wiki/Inner_sphere_electron_transfer#Mixed_valency" title="Inner sphere electron transfer">mixed valence compound</a>, its formula is sometimes written as Co<sup>II</sup>Co<sup>III</sup><sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and sometimes as CoO&#8226;Co<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-G&amp;E_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;E-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cobalt(II,III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cobalt(II)_oxide" title="Cobalt(II) oxide">Cobalt(II) oxide</a>, CoO, converts to Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> upon heating at around 600-700 °C in air.<sup id="cite_ref-GW_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Above 900 °C, CoO is stable.<sup id="cite_ref-GW_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GW-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  These reaction are described by the following equilibrium:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>  ⇌  6 CoO  +  O<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applicatons">Applicatons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cobalt(II,III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applicatons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cobalt(II,III) oxide is used as a blue coloring agent for <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a> <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">enamel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>, as an alternative to cobalt(II) oxide.<sup id="cite_ref-hamer_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hamer-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cobalt(II,III) oxide is used as an electrode in some <a href="/wiki/Lithium-ion_battery" title="Lithium-ion battery">lithium-ion batteries</a>, possibly in the form of <a href="/wiki/Cobalt_oxide_nanoparticles" class="mw-redirect" title="Cobalt oxide nanoparticles">cobalt oxide nanoparticles</a>.
</p>

<p>Cobalt compounds are potentially poisonous in large amounts.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
