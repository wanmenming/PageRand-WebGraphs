<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Cloud forcing</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Cloud forcing</b> (sometimes described as <b>cloud radiative forcing</b> or <b>cloud radiative effect</b>) is, in <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, the difference between the <a href="/wiki/Radiation_budget" class="mw-redirect" title="Radiation budget">radiation budget</a> components for average <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a> conditions and cloud-free conditions.  Much of the interest in cloud forcing relates to its role as a <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">feedback</a> process in the present period of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shortwave_Radiation.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Shortwave_Radiation.jpg/220px-Shortwave_Radiation.jpg" width="220" height="184" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e6/Shortwave_Radiation.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="325" data-file-height="272" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shortwave_Radiation.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This image depicts the effects of clouds scattering incoming shortwave radiation from the sun. This tends to result in overall cooling of the Earth during the daytime as well as in general (because the energy loss caused by the cloud cover is more significant than the gain described in the image below).</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Longwave_Radiation.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Longwave_Radiation.jpg/220px-Longwave_Radiation.jpg" width="220" height="186" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d8/Longwave_Radiation.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="325" data-file-height="275" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Longwave_Radiation.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This image depicts the effects of clouds absorbing longwave rays emitted from the Earth, which are then reemitted back to the surface. This tends to result in overall warming of the Earth during the nighttime.</div></div></div>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_Effects_of_Cloud_Forcing">Current Effects of Cloud Forcing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_forcing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Current Effects of Cloud Forcing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All <a href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Global climate model">global climate models</a> used for climate change projections include the effects of <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> and cloud forcing. The models include the effects of clouds on both incoming (solar) and emitted (terrestrial) radiation.
</p><p>Clouds increase the global <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">reflection of solar radiation</a> from 15% to 30%, reducing the amount of <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> absorbed by the Earth by about 44 W/m². This cooling is offset somewhat by the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a> of clouds which reduces the <a href="/wiki/Outgoing_longwave_radiation" title="Outgoing longwave radiation">outgoing longwave radiation</a> by about 31 W/m².  Thus the net cloud forcing of the radiation budget is a loss of about 13 W/m².<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> If the clouds were removed with all else remaining the same, the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> would gain this last amount in net radiation and begin to warm up. 
</p><p>These numbers should not be confused with the usual <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a> concept, which is for the <i>change</i> in forcing related to <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.
</p><p>Without the inclusion of clouds, water vapor alone contributes 36% to 70% of the greenhouse effect on Earth.  When water vapor and clouds are considered together, the contribution is 66% to 85%. The ranges come about because there are two ways to compute the influence of water vapor and clouds: the lower bounds are the reduction in the greenhouse effect if water vapor and clouds are <i>removed</i> from the atmosphere leaving all other greenhouse gases unchanged, while the upper bounds are the greenhouse effect introduced if water vapor and clouds are <i>added</i> to an atmosphere with no other greenhouse gases.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The two values differ because of overlap in the absorption and emission by the various greenhouse gases.  Trapping of the long-wave radiation due to the presence of clouds reduces the radiative forcing of the greenhouse gases compared to the clear-sky forcing. However, the magnitude of the effect due to clouds varies for different greenhouse gases. Relative to clear <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">skies</a>, clouds reduce the global mean radiative forcing due to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">CO<sub>2</sub></a> by about 15%,<sup id="cite_ref-Pinnock_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pinnock-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> that due to <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">CH<sub>4</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">N<sub>2</sub>O</a> by about 20%,<sup id="cite_ref-Pinnock_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pinnock-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and that due to the <a href="/wiki/Halocarbon" title="Halocarbon">halocarbons</a> by up to 30%.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> 
Clouds remain one of the largest uncertainties in future projections of climate change by global climate models, owing to the physical complexity of cloud processes and the small scale of individual clouds relative to the size of the model computational grid.
</p>
