<p><b>Chromium dioxide</b> or <b>chromium(IV) oxide</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the formula CrO<sub>2</sub>.  It is a black <a href="/wiki/Chemical_synthesis" title="Chemical synthesis">synthetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Magnetism" title="Magnetism">magnetic</a> solid.<sup id="cite_ref-G&amp;E_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;E-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  It once was widely used in <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_tape" title="Magnetic tape">magnetic tape</a> <a href="/wiki/Emulsion" title="Emulsion">emulsion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ullmanns_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ullmanns-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> With the increasing popularity of <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">CDs</a> and <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVDs</a>, the use of chromium(IV) oxide has declined. However, it is still used in data tape applications for enterprise-class storage systems. It is still considered by many oxide and tape manufacturers to have been one of the best magnetic recording particulates ever invented.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation_and_basic_properties">Preparation and basic properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chromium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation and basic properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CrO<sub>2</sub> was first prepared by <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler" title="Friedrich Wöhler">Friedrich Wöhler</a> by decomposition of <a href="/wiki/Chromyl_chloride" title="Chromyl chloride">chromyl chloride</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Crystal_habit" title="Crystal habit">Acicular</a> chromium dioxide was first synthesized in 1956 by Norman L. Cox, a chemist at <a href="/wiki/DuPont" title="DuPont">E.I. DuPont</a>, by decomposing <a href="/wiki/Chromium_trioxide" title="Chromium trioxide">chromium trioxide</a> in the presence of water at a temperature of 800 K and a pressure of 200 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">MPa</a>. The balanced equation for the <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_synthesis" title="Hydrothermal synthesis">hydrothermal synthesis</a> is:
</p>
<dl><dd>3 CrO<sub>3</sub>  +  Cr<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>   →   5 CrO<sub>2</sub>  +  O<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The magnetic crystal that forms is a long, slender glass-like rod — perfect as a magnetic pigment for recording tape. When commercialized in the late 1960s as a recording medium, DuPont assigned it the tradename of <i>Magtrieve</i>.
</p><p>CrO<sub>2</sub> adopts the <a href="/wiki/Rutile_structure" class="mw-redirect" title="Rutile structure">rutile structure</a> (as do many metal dioxides).  As such each Cr(IV) center has <a href="/wiki/Octahedral_coordination_geometry" class="mw-redirect" title="Octahedral coordination geometry">octahedral coordination geometry</a> and each oxide is trigonal planar.<sup id="cite_ref-G&amp;E_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;E-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chromium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The crystal’s magnetic properties, derived from its ideal shape such as  <a href="/wiki/Anisotropy" title="Anisotropy">anisotropy</a> which imparted high <a href="/wiki/Coercivity" title="Coercivity">coercivity</a> and remanent magnetization intensities, resulted in exceptional stability and efficiency for short <a href="/wiki/Wavelengths" class="mw-redirect" title="Wavelengths">wavelengths</a>, and it almost immediately appeared in high performance audio tape used in <a href="/wiki/Compact_audio_cassette" class="mw-redirect" title="Compact audio cassette">audio cassette</a> for which treble response and hiss were always problems. Unlike the spongy looking <a href="/wiki/Ferric_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferric oxide">ferric oxides</a> used in common tape, the chromium dioxide crystals were perfectly formed and could be evenly and densely dispersed in a magnetic coating; and that led to unparalleled low noise in audio tapes. Chrome tapes did, however, require a new generation of audiocassette recorders equipped with a higher <a href="/wiki/Tape_bias" title="Tape bias">bias</a> current capability (roughly 50% greater) than that used by iron oxide to properly magnetize the tape particles. Also introduced was a new equalization (70 <a href="/wiki/%CE%9Cs" class="mw-redirect" title="Μs">µs</a>) that traded some of the extended high-frequency response for lower noise resulting in a 5–6 <a href="/wiki/Decibel" title="Decibel">dB</a> improvement in signal-to-noise ratio over ferric-oxide audio tapes. These bias and EQ settings were later carried over to “chrome-equivalent” cobalt-modified tapes introduced in the mid 1970s by <a href="/wiki/TDK" title="TDK">TDK</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maxell" title="Maxell">Maxell</a>, and others. Later research significantly increased the coercivity of the particle by doping or adsorbing rare elements such as <a href="/wiki/Iridium" title="Iridium">iridium</a> onto the crystal matrix or by improving the axial length-to-deprecated<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> ratios. The resulting product was potentially a competitor to metallic iron pigments but apparently achieved little market penetration.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Problems">Problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chromium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Until manufacturers developed new ways to mill the oxide, the crystals could easily be broken in the manufacturing process, and this led to excessive <a href="/wiki/Print-through" title="Print-through">print-through</a> (echo). Output from a tape could drop about 1&#160;<a href="/wiki/Decibel" title="Decibel">dB</a> or so in a year's time. Although the decrease was uniform across the frequency range and noise also dropped the same amount, preserving the dynamic range, the decrease misaligned <a href="/wiki/Dolby" class="mw-redirect" title="Dolby">Dolby</a> noise reduction decoders that were sensitive to level settings. The chrome coating was harder than competitive coatings, and that led to accusations of excessive head wear. Although the tape wore hard <a href="/wiki/Ferrite_(magnet)" title="Ferrite (magnet)">ferrite</a> heads faster than oxide based tapes, it actually wore softer <a href="/wiki/Permalloy" title="Permalloy">permalloy</a> heads at a slower rate; and head wear was more a problem for permalloy heads than for ferrite heads. The head wear scare and licensing issues with DuPont kept blank consumer chrome tapes at a great disadvantage versus the eventually more popular Type II tapes that used cobalt-modified iron oxide, but chrome was the tape of choice for the music industry's cassette releases. Because of its low <a href="/wiki/Curie_temperature" title="Curie temperature">Curie temperature</a> (approximately 386&#160;K (235&#160;°F)), chrome tape lent itself to high-speed <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20150403010618/http://ieeexplore.ieee.org/xpl/freeabs_all.jsp?arnumber=1063031">thermomagnetic duplication</a> of audio and video cassettes for pre-recorded product sales to the consumer and industrial markets.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Producers">Producers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chromium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Producers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>DuPont licensed the product to <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> in Japan and <a href="/wiki/BASF" title="BASF">BASF</a> in Germany in the early 1970s for regional production and distribution. Japanese competitors  developed cobalt-adsorbed (TDK: <i>Avilyn</i>) and cobalt ferrite (Maxell: <i><a href="/wiki/Epitaxial" class="mw-redirect" title="Epitaxial">Epitaxial</a></i>) "chrome equivalent" Type II audio cassettes and various videotape formats as substitutes. Added to that was the problem that the production of CrO<sub>2</sub> yielded toxic by-products of which Japanese manufacturers had great difficulty properly disposing. BASF eventually became the largest producer of both the chromium dioxide pigment and chrome tapes, basing its <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">VHS</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/S-VHS" title="S-VHS">S-VHS</a> video tape, audio cassettes, and 3480 data cartridges on this formulation. Dupont and BASF had also introduced chrome-cobalt "blended" oxide pigments which combined about 70% cobalt-modified iron oxide with 30% chrome oxide into a single coating, presumably to offer improved performance at lower costs than pure chrome. Many high grade VHS tapes also used much smaller amounts of chrome in their formulations because its magnetic properties combined with its cleaning effects on heads made it a better choice than <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">aluminium oxide</a> or other non-magnetic materials added to VHS tape to keep heads clean. Dupont discontinued its production of chromium dioxide particles in the 1990s. In addition to BASF, which no longer owns a tape manufacturing division, Bayer AG of Germany, Toda Kogyo and Sakai Chemical of Japan also do or can produce the magnetic particles for commercial applications.
</p>
