<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Millimeter of mercury</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=MmHg&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="MmHg">MmHg</a>)</span></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>millimetre of mercury</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Pressure_measurement#Liquid_column_(manometer)" title="Pressure measurement">manometric unit</a> of <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>, formerly defined as the extra pressure generated by a column of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a> one <a href="/wiki/Millimetre" title="Millimetre">millimetre</a> high and now defined as precisely <span class="nowrap"><span style="display:none" class="sortkey">7002133322387415000♠</span>133.322<span style="margin-left:.25em;">387</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">415</span></span> <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">pascals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is denoted by the symbol <b>mmHg</b><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or <b>mm Hg</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-AMA_MOS_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMA_MOS-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although not an <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI</a> unit, the millimetre of mercury is still routinely used in medicine, meteorology, aviation, and many other scientific fields.
</p><p>One millimetre of mercury is approximately 1 <a href="/wiki/Torr" title="Torr">Torr</a>, which is 1/760 of standard <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit)" title="Atmosphere (unit)">atmospheric pressure</a> (<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;"><span class="nowrap"><span style="display:none" class="sortkey">7005101325000000000♠</span>101<span style="margin-left:.25em;">325</span></span></span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">760</span></span> = <span class="nowrap"><span style="display:none" class="sortkey">7002133322368421053♠</span>133.322<span style="margin-left:.25em;">368</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">421</span><span style="margin-left:.25em;">053</span></span> pascals). The two units are not exactly equal; however, the <a href="/wiki/Relative_difference" class="mw-redirect" title="Relative difference">relative difference</a> (less than <span style="white-space:nowrap">0.000<span style="margin-left:0.25em">015%</span></span>) is negligible for most practical uses.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_definition">History and definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Millimeter_of_mercury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Pressure_head" title="Pressure head">pressure head</a></div>
<p>Mercury manometers were the first accurate pressure gauges; they are less used today due to <a href="/wiki/Mercury_poisoning" title="Mercury poisoning">mercury's toxicity</a>, the mercury column's sensitivity to temperature and local gravity, and the greater convenience of other instrumentation.  They displayed the pressure difference between two fluids as a vertical difference between the mercury levels in two connected reservoirs. 
</p><p>An actual mercury column reading may be converted to more fundamental units of pressure by multiplying the difference in height between two mercury levels by the density of mercury and the local gravitational acceleration. Because the density of mercury depends on temperature and <a href="/wiki/Gravity_of_Earth" title="Gravity of Earth">surface gravity</a>, both of which vary with local conditions, specific standard values for these two parameters were adopted. This resulted in defining a "millimetre of mercury" as the pressure exerted at the base of a column of mercury 1 millimetre high with a precise density of 13595.1 kg/m<sup>3</sup> when the acceleration due to gravity is exactly 9.80665&#160;m/s<sup>2</sup>.
</p><p>The density 13595.1&#160;kg/m<sup>3</sup> chosen for this definition is the approximate density of mercury at 0&#160;°C (32&#160;°F), and 9.80665&#160;m/s<sup>2</sup> is <a href="/wiki/Standard_gravity" title="Standard gravity">standard gravity</a>. The use of an actual column of mercury to measure pressure normally requires correction for the density of mercury at the actual temperature and the sometimes marked variation of gravity with location, and may be further corrected to take account of the density of the measured air, water or other fluid.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_the_torr">Relation to the torr</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Millimeter_of_mercury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Relation to the torr">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The precision of modern transducers is often insufficient to show the difference between the torr and the millimetre of mercury. The torr is about one part in seven million or <span style="white-space:nowrap">0.000<span style="margin-left:0.25em">015%</span></span> less.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_medicine_and_physiology">Use in medicine and physiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Millimeter_of_mercury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use in medicine and physiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In medicine, pressure is still generally measured in millimetres of mercury. These measurements are in general given relative to the current atmospheric pressure: for example, a blood pressure of 120&#160;mmHg, when the current atmospheric pressure is 760&#160;mmHg, means 880&#160;mmHg relative to perfect vacuum.
</p><p>Routine pressure measurements in medicine include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">Blood pressure</a>, measured with a <a href="/wiki/Sphygmomanometer" title="Sphygmomanometer">sphygmomanometer</a></li>
<li>Intraocular pressure, with a <a href="/wiki/Tonometer" class="mw-redirect" title="Tonometer">tonometer</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cerebrospinal_fluid" title="Cerebrospinal fluid">Cerebrospinal fluid</a> pressure</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Intracranial_pressure" title="Intracranial pressure">Intracranial pressure</a></li>
<li>Intramuscular pressure (<a href="/wiki/Compartment_syndrome" title="Compartment syndrome">compartment syndrome</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Central_venous_pressure" title="Central venous pressure">Central venous pressure</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_artery_catheter" title="Pulmonary artery catheter">Pulmonary artery catheterization</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mechanical_ventilation" title="Mechanical ventilation">Mechanical ventilation</a></li></ul>
<p>In physiology <a href="/wiki/Manometric" class="mw-redirect" title="Manometric">manometric</a> units are used to measure <a href="/wiki/Starling_equation" title="Starling equation">Starling forces</a>.
</p>
