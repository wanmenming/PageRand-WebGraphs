<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbohydrate</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lactose.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Lactose.svg/310px-Lactose.svg.png" width="310" height="129" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Lactose.svg/465px-Lactose.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Lactose.svg/620px-Lactose.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="440" data-file-height="183" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lactose.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lactose" title="Lactose">Lactose</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Disaccharide" title="Disaccharide">disaccharide</a> found in animal milk. It consists of a molecule of <a href="/wiki/Galactose" title="Galactose">D-galactose</a> and a molecule of <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">D-glucose</a> bonded by beta-1-4 <a href="/wiki/Glycosidic_linkage" class="mw-redirect" title="Glycosidic linkage">glycosidic linkage</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>carbohydrate</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/ɑːr/: &#39;ar&#39; in &#39;far&#39;">ɑːr</span><span title="&#39;b&#39; in &#39;buy&#39;">b</span><span title="/oʊ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;code&#39;">oʊ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;h&#39; in &#39;hi&#39;">h</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/eɪ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;face&#39;">eɪ</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Biomolecule" title="Biomolecule">biomolecule</a> consisting of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> (C), <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> (H) and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (O) atoms, usually with a hydrogen–oxygen <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a> ratio of 2:1 (as in water) and thus with the <a href="/wiki/Empirical_formula" title="Empirical formula">empirical formula</a> <span class="nowrap">C<sub><i>m</i></sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub><i>n</i></sub></span> (where <i>m</i> may be different from <i>n</i>). This formula holds true for <a href="/wiki/Monosaccharide" title="Monosaccharide">monosaccharides</a>. Some exceptions exist; for example, <a href="/wiki/Deoxyribose" title="Deoxyribose">deoxyribose</a>, a sugar component of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> has the empirical formula C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>10</sub>O<sub>4</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The carbohydrates are technically <a href="/wiki/Hydrate" title="Hydrate">hydrates</a> of carbon; structurally it is more accurate to view them as <a href="/wiki/Aldose" title="Aldose">aldoses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ketose" title="Ketose">ketoses</a>.
</p><p>The term is most common in <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a>, where it is a synonym of 'saccharide', a group that includes <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a>. The saccharides are divided into four chemical groups: monosaccharides, <a href="/wiki/Disaccharide" title="Disaccharide">disaccharides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oligosaccharide" title="Oligosaccharide">oligosaccharides</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharides</a>. Monosaccharides and disaccharides, the smallest (lower <a href="/wiki/Molecular_weight" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular weight">molecular weight</a>) carbohydrates, are  commonly referred to as sugars.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The word <i>saccharide</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> word <i>σάκχαρον</i> (<i>sákkharon</i>), meaning "sugar".<sup id="cite_ref-avenas_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avenas-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> While the scientific nomenclature of carbohydrates is complex, the names of the monosaccharides and disaccharides very often end in the suffix <a href="/wiki/-ose" title="-ose">-ose</a>, as in the monosaccharides <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a> (fruit sugar) and <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> (starch sugar) and the disaccharides <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sugar_cane" class="mw-redirect" title="Sugar cane">cane</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sugar_beet" title="Sugar beet">beet</a> sugar) and <a href="/wiki/Lactose" title="Lactose">lactose</a> (milk sugar).
</p><p>Carbohydrates perform numerous roles in living organisms. Polysaccharides serve for the storage of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glycogen" title="Glycogen">glycogen</a>) and as structural components (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> in plants and <a href="/wiki/Chitin" title="Chitin">chitin</a> in arthropods). The 5-carbon monosaccharide <a href="/wiki/Ribose" title="Ribose">ribose</a> is an important component of <a href="/wiki/Coenzyme" class="mw-redirect" title="Coenzyme">coenzymes</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flavin_adenine_dinucleotide" title="Flavin adenine dinucleotide">FAD</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide" title="Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide">NAD</a>) and the backbone of the genetic molecule known as <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>. The related <a href="/wiki/Deoxyribose" title="Deoxyribose">deoxyribose</a> is a component of DNA. Saccharides and their derivatives include many other important <a href="/wiki/Biomolecules" class="mw-redirect" title="Biomolecules">biomolecules</a> that play key roles in the <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fertilization" class="mw-redirect" title="Fertilization">fertilization</a>, preventing <a href="/wiki/Pathogenesis" title="Pathogenesis">pathogenesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blood_clotting" class="mw-redirect" title="Blood clotting">blood clotting</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Developmental_biology" title="Developmental biology">development</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Starch and sugars are the most important carbohydrates in human diet. They are found in a wide variety of natural and processed foods. Starch is a polysaccharide. It is abundant in cereals (wheat, maize, rice), potatoes, and processed food based on cereal <a href="/wiki/Flour" title="Flour">flour</a>, such as bread, pizza or pasta. Sugars appear in human diet mainly as table sugar (sucrose, extracted from <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sugar_beet" title="Sugar beet">sugar beets</a>)), lactose (abundant in milk), glucose and fructose, both of which occur naturally in <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a>, many fruits, and some vegetables. Table sugar, milk, or honey are often added to drinks and many prepared foods such as jam, biscuits and cakes.
</p><p>Cellulose, a polysaccharide found in the cell walls of all plants, is one of the main components of insoluble <a href="/wiki/Dietary_fiber" title="Dietary fiber">dietary fiber</a>. Although it is not digestible, insoluble dietary fiber helps to maintain a healthy digestive system<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> by easing <a href="/wiki/Defecation" title="Defecation">defecation</a>. Other polysaccharides contained in dietary fiber include <a href="/wiki/Resistant_starch" title="Resistant starch">resistant starch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Inulin" title="Inulin">inulin</a>, which feed some bacteria in the <a href="/wiki/Microbiota" title="Microbiota">microbiota</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Large_intestine" title="Large intestine">large intestine</a>, and are <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolized</a> by these bacteria to yield <a href="/wiki/Short-chain_fatty_acids" class="mw-redirect" title="Short-chain fatty acids">short-chain fatty acids</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_Handbook_of_Dietary_Fiber_in_Human_Nutrition_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC_Handbook_of_Dietary_Fiber_in_Human_Nutrition-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbohydrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In scientific literature, the term "carbohydrate" has many synonyms, like "sugar" (in the broad sense), "saccharide", "ose",<sup id="cite_ref-avenas_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avenas-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> "glucide",<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> "hydrate of carbon" or "<a href="/wiki/Hydroxy_group" title="Hydroxy group">polyhydroxy</a> compounds with <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehyde</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketone</a>". Some of these terms, specially "carbohydrate" and "sugar", are also used with other meanings.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Food_science" title="Food science">food science</a> and in many informal contexts, the term "carbohydrate" often means any food that is particularly rich in the complex carbohydrate <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> (such as cereals, bread and pasta) or simple carbohydrates, such as sugar (found in candy, <a href="/wiki/Jam" class="mw-redirect" title="Jam">jams</a>, and desserts).
</p><p>Often in lists of <a href="/wiki/Nutritional_information" class="mw-redirect" title="Nutritional information">nutritional information</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/USDA_National_Nutrient_Database" title="USDA National Nutrient Database">USDA National Nutrient Database</a>, the term "carbohydrate" (or "carbohydrate by difference") is used for everything other than water, protein, fat, ash, and ethanol.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This includes chemical compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a>, which are not normally considered carbohydrates. It also includes <a href="/wiki/Dietary_fiber" title="Dietary fiber">dietary fiber</a> which is a carbohydrate but which does not contribute much in the way of <a href="/wiki/Food_energy" title="Food energy">food energy</a> (<a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">kilocalories</a>), even though it is often included in the calculation of total food energy just as though it were a sugar.
</p><p>In the strict sense, "<a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>" is applied for sweet, soluble carbohydrates, many of which are used in food.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbohydrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Formerly the name "carbohydrate" was used in <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> for any compound with the formula C<sub><i>m</i></sub> (H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub><i>n</i></sub>. Following this definition, some chemists considered <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a> (CH<sub>2</sub>O) to be the simplest carbohydrate,<sup id="cite_ref-coulter_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-coulter-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> while others claimed that title for <a href="/wiki/Glycolaldehyde" title="Glycolaldehyde">glycolaldehyde</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tietz_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tietz-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Today, the term is generally understood in the biochemistry sense, which excludes compounds with only one or two carbons and includes many biological carbohydrates which deviate from this formula. For example, while the above representative formulas would seem to capture the commonly known carbohydrates, ubiquitous and abundant carbohydrates often deviate from this. For example, carbohydrates often display chemical groups such as: <i>N</i>-acetyl (e.g. chitin), sulphate (e.g. glycosaminoglycans), carboxylic acid (e.g. sialic acid) and deoxy modifications (e.g. fucose and sialic acid).
</p><p>Natural saccharides are generally built of simple carbohydrates called <a href="/wiki/Monosaccharide" title="Monosaccharide">monosaccharides</a> with general formula (CH<sub>2</sub>O)<sub><i>n</i></sub> where <i>n</i> is three or more. A typical monosaccharide has the structure H–(CHOH)<sub><i>x</i></sub>(C=O)–(CHOH)<sub><i>y</i></sub>–H, that is, an <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehyde</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketone</a> with many <a href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroxyl">hydroxyl</a> groups added, usually one on each <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atom</a> that is not part of the aldehyde or ketone <a href="/wiki/Functional_group" title="Functional group">functional group</a>. Examples of monosaccharides are <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Glyceraldehyde" title="Glyceraldehyde">glyceraldehydes</a>. However, some biological substances commonly called "monosaccharides" do not conform to this formula (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Uronic_acid" title="Uronic acid">uronic acids</a> and deoxy-sugars such as <a href="/wiki/Fucose" title="Fucose">fucose</a>) and there are many chemicals that do conform to this formula but are not considered to be monosaccharides (e.g. formaldehyde CH<sub>2</sub>O and <a href="/wiki/Inositol" title="Inositol">inositol</a> (CH<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>6</sub>).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Open-chain" class="mw-redirect" title="Open-chain">open-chain</a> form of a monosaccharide often coexists with a <a href="/wiki/Heterocyclic_compound" title="Heterocyclic compound">closed ring form</a> where the <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehyde</a>/<a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketone</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbonyl" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> group carbon (C=O) and hydroxyl group (–OH) react forming a <a href="/wiki/Hemiacetal" title="Hemiacetal">hemiacetal</a> with a new C–O–C bridge.
</p><p>Monosaccharides can be linked together into what are called <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharides</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Oligosaccharide" title="Oligosaccharide">oligosaccharides</a>) in a large variety of ways. Many carbohydrates contain one or more modified monosaccharide units that have had one or more groups replaced or removed. For example, <a href="/wiki/Deoxyribose" title="Deoxyribose">deoxyribose</a>, a component of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>, is a modified version of <a href="/wiki/Ribose" title="Ribose">ribose</a>; <a href="/wiki/Chitin" title="Chitin">chitin</a> is composed of repeating units of <a href="/wiki/N-acetyl_glucosamine" class="mw-redirect" title="N-acetyl glucosamine">N-acetyl glucosamine</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>-containing form of glucose.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Monosaccharides">Monosaccharides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbohydrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Monosaccharides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Monosaccharide" title="Monosaccharide">Monosaccharide</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;"><a href="/wiki/File:D-glucose_color_coded.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/D-glucose_color_coded.png/130px-D-glucose_color_coded.png" width="130" height="224" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/D-glucose_color_coded.png/195px-D-glucose_color_coded.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/D-glucose_color_coded.png/260px-D-glucose_color_coded.png 2x" data-file-width="1185" data-file-height="2040" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:D-glucose_color_coded.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">D-glucose</a> is an aldohexose with the formula (C·H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>6</sub>. The red atoms highlight the <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehyde</a> group and the blue atoms highlight the <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">asymmetric center</a> furthest from the aldehyde; because this -OH is on the right of the <a href="/wiki/Fischer_projection" title="Fischer projection">Fischer projection</a>, this is a D sugar.</div></div></div>
<p>Monosaccharides are the simplest carbohydrates in that they cannot be <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolyzed</a> to smaller carbohydrates. They are aldehydes or ketones with two or more hydroxyl groups. The general <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> of an unmodified monosaccharide is (C•H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>n</sub>, literally a "carbon hydrate". Monosaccharides are important fuel molecules as well as building blocks for nucleic acids. The smallest monosaccharides, for which n=3, are dihydroxyacetone and D- and L-glyceraldehydes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_of_monosaccharides">Classification of monosaccharides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbohydrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Classification of monosaccharides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:187px;">
<p><a href="/wiki/File:Alpha-D-glucopyranose-2D-skeletal.png" class="image"><img alt="Alpha-D-glucopyranose-2D-skeletal.png" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Alpha-D-glucopyranose-2D-skeletal.png/185px-Alpha-D-glucopyranose-2D-skeletal.png" width="185" height="126" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Alpha-D-glucopyranose-2D-skeletal.png/278px-Alpha-D-glucopyranose-2D-skeletal.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Alpha-D-glucopyranose-2D-skeletal.png/370px-Alpha-D-glucopyranose-2D-skeletal.png 2x" data-file-width="1100" data-file-height="747" /></a>
<a href="/wiki/File:Beta-D-glucopyranose-2D-skeletal.png" class="image"><img alt="Beta-D-glucopyranose-2D-skeletal.png" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Beta-D-glucopyranose-2D-skeletal.png/185px-Beta-D-glucopyranose-2D-skeletal.png" width="185" height="101" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Beta-D-glucopyranose-2D-skeletal.png/278px-Beta-D-glucopyranose-2D-skeletal.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Beta-D-glucopyranose-2D-skeletal.png/370px-Beta-D-glucopyranose-2D-skeletal.png 2x" data-file-width="1100" data-file-height="603" /></a>
</p>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<p>The <a href="/wiki/%CE%91" class="mw-redirect" title="Α">α</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beta_(letter)" class="mw-redirect" title="Beta (letter)">β</a> <a href="/wiki/Anomer" title="Anomer">anomers</a> of glucose. Note the position of the hydroxyl group (red or green) on the anomeric carbon relative to the CH<sub>2</sub>OH group bound to carbon 5: they either have identical absolute configurations (R,R or S,S) (α), or opposite absolute configurations (R,S or S,R) (β).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Monosaccharides are classified according to three different characteristics: the placement of its <a href="/wiki/Carbonyl" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> group, the number of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> atoms it contains, and its <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chiral</a> handedness. If the carbonyl group is an <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehyde</a>, the monosaccharide is an <a href="/wiki/Aldose" title="Aldose">aldose</a>; if the carbonyl group is a <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketone</a>, the monosaccharide is a <a href="/wiki/Ketose" title="Ketose">ketose</a>. Monosaccharides with three carbon atoms are called <a href="/wiki/Triose" title="Triose">trioses</a>, those with four are called <a href="/wiki/Tetrose" title="Tetrose">tetroses</a>, five are called <a href="/wiki/Pentose" title="Pentose">pentoses</a>, six are <a href="/wiki/Hexose" title="Hexose">hexoses</a>, and so on.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> These two systems of classification are often combined. For example, <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Aldohexose" title="Aldohexose">aldohexose</a> (a six-carbon aldehyde), <a href="/wiki/Ribose" title="Ribose">ribose</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Aldopentose" class="mw-redirect" title="Aldopentose">aldopentose</a> (a five-carbon aldehyde), and <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Ketohexose" title="Ketohexose">ketohexose</a> (a six-carbon ketone).
</p><p>Each carbon atom bearing a <a href="/wiki/Hydroxyl_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroxyl group">hydroxyl group</a> (-OH), with the exception of the first and last carbons, are <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">asymmetric</a>, making them <a href="/wiki/Stereogenic" class="mw-redirect" title="Stereogenic">stereo centers</a> with two possible configurations each (R or S). Because of this asymmetry, a number of <a href="/wiki/Isomer" title="Isomer">isomers</a> may exist for any given monosaccharide formula. Using <a href="/wiki/Le_Bel-van%27t_Hoff_rule" class="mw-redirect" title="Le Bel-van&#39;t Hoff rule">Le Bel-van't Hoff rule</a>, the aldohexose D-glucose, for example, has the formula (C·H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>6</sub>, of which four of its six carbons atoms are stereogenic, making D-glucose one of 2<sup>4</sup>=16 possible <a href="/wiki/Stereoisomer" class="mw-redirect" title="Stereoisomer">stereoisomers</a>. In the case of <a href="/wiki/Glyceraldehyde" title="Glyceraldehyde">glyceraldehydes</a>, an aldotriose, there is one pair of possible stereoisomers, which are <a href="/wiki/Enantiomers" class="mw-redirect" title="Enantiomers">enantiomers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Epimer" title="Epimer">epimers</a>. <a href="/wiki/Dihydroxyacetone" title="Dihydroxyacetone">1, 3-dihydroxyacetone</a>, the ketose corresponding to the aldose glyceraldehydes, is a symmetric molecule with no stereo centers. The assignment of D or L is made according to the orientation of the asymmetric carbon furthest from the carbonyl group: in a standard Fischer projection if the hydroxyl group is on the right the molecule is a D sugar, otherwise it is an L sugar. The "D-" and "L-" prefixes should not be confused with "d-" or "l-", which indicate the direction that the sugar  <a href="/wiki/Levorotation_and_dextrorotation" class="mw-redirect" title="Levorotation and dextrorotation">rotates</a> plane <a href="/wiki/Polarization_(waves)" title="Polarization (waves)">polarized light</a>. This usage of "d-" and "l-" is no longer followed in carbohydrate chemistry.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ring-straight_chain_isomerism">Ring-straight chain isomerism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbohydrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ring-straight chain isomerism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Glucose_Fisher_to_Haworth.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Glucose_Fisher_to_Haworth.gif/200px-Glucose_Fisher_to_Haworth.gif" width="200" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/af/Glucose_Fisher_to_Haworth.gif 1.5x" data-file-width="231" data-file-height="181" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Glucose_Fisher_to_Haworth.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">Glucose</a> can exist in both a straight-chain and ring form.</div></div></div>
<p>The aldehyde or ketone group of a straight-chain monosaccharide will react reversibly with a hydroxyl group on a different carbon atom to form a <a href="/wiki/Hemiacetal" title="Hemiacetal">hemiacetal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hemiketal" class="mw-redirect" title="Hemiketal">hemiketal</a>, forming a <a href="/wiki/Heterocyclic" class="mw-redirect" title="Heterocyclic">heterocyclic</a> ring with an oxygen bridge between two carbon atoms. Rings with five and six atoms are called <a href="/wiki/Furanose" title="Furanose">furanose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pyranose" title="Pyranose">pyranose</a> forms, respectively, and exist in equilibrium with the straight-chain form.<sup id="cite_ref-pigman_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pigman-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the conversion from straight-chain form to the cyclic form, the carbon atom containing the carbonyl oxygen, called the <a href="/wiki/Anomeric_carbon" class="mw-redirect" title="Anomeric carbon">anomeric carbon</a>, becomes a stereogenic center with two possible configurations: The oxygen atom may take a position either above or below the plane of the ring. The resulting possible pair of stereoisomers is called <a href="/wiki/Anomer" title="Anomer">anomers</a>. In the <i>α anomer</i>, the -OH substituent on the anomeric carbon rests on the opposite side (<a href="/wiki/Cis-trans_isomerism" class="mw-redirect" title="Cis-trans isomerism">trans</a>) of the ring from the CH<sub>2</sub>OH side branch. The alternative form, in which the CH<sub>2</sub>OH substituent and the anomeric hydroxyl are on the same side (cis) of the plane of the ring, is called the <i>β anomer</i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_living_organisms">Use in living organisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbohydrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Use in living organisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Monosaccharides are the major source of fuel for <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a>, being used both as an energy source (<a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> being the most important in nature) and in <a href="/wiki/Biosynthesis" title="Biosynthesis">biosynthesis</a>. When monosaccharides are not immediately needed by many cells they are often converted to more space-efficient forms, often <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharides</a>. In many animals, including humans, this storage form is <a href="/wiki/Glycogen" title="Glycogen">glycogen</a>, especially in liver and muscle cells. In plants, <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> is used for the same purpose. The most abundant carbohydrate, <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a>, is a structural component of the <a href="/wiki/Cell_wall#plant_cell_walls" title="Cell wall">cell wall</a> of plants and many forms of algae. <a href="/wiki/Ribose" title="Ribose">Ribose</a> is a component of <a href="/wiki/RNA" title="RNA">RNA</a>. <a href="/wiki/Deoxyribose" title="Deoxyribose">Deoxyribose</a> is a component of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>. <a href="/wiki/Lyxose" title="Lyxose">Lyxose</a> is a component of lyxoflavin found in the human heart.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ribulose" title="Ribulose">Ribulose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xylulose" title="Xylulose">xylulose</a> occur in the <a href="/wiki/Pentose_phosphate_pathway" title="Pentose phosphate pathway">pentose phosphate pathway</a>. <a href="/wiki/Galactose" title="Galactose">Galactose</a>, a component of milk sugar <a href="/wiki/Lactose" title="Lactose">lactose</a>, is found in <a href="/wiki/Galactolipid" title="Galactolipid">galactolipids</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane#lipids" title="Cell membrane">plant cell membranes</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Glycoprotein" title="Glycoprotein">glycoproteins</a> in many <a href="/wiki/Biological_tissue" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological tissue">tissues</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mannose" title="Mannose">Mannose</a> occurs in human metabolism, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Glycosylation" title="Glycosylation">glycosylation</a> of certain proteins. <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">Fructose</a>, or fruit sugar, is found in many plants and in humans, it is metabolized in the liver, absorbed directly into the intestines during <a href="/wiki/Digestion" title="Digestion">digestion</a>, and found in <a href="/wiki/Semen" title="Semen">semen</a>. <a href="/wiki/Trehalose" title="Trehalose">Trehalose</a>, a major sugar of insects, is rapidly hydrolyzed into two glucose molecules to support continuous flight.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disaccharides">Disaccharides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbohydrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Disaccharides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sucrose_3Dprojection.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Sucrose_3Dprojection.png/250px-Sucrose_3Dprojection.png" width="250" height="114" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Sucrose_3Dprojection.png/375px-Sucrose_3Dprojection.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Sucrose_3Dprojection.png/500px-Sucrose_3Dprojection.png 2x" data-file-width="823" data-file-height="375" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sucrose_3Dprojection.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">Sucrose</a>, also known as table sugar, is a common disaccharide. It is composed of two monosaccharides: <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">D-glucose</a> (left) and <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">D-fructose</a> (right).</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Disaccharide" title="Disaccharide">Disaccharide</a></div>
<p>Two joined monosaccharides are called a <a href="/wiki/Disaccharide" title="Disaccharide">disaccharide</a> and these are the simplest polysaccharides. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lactose" title="Lactose">lactose</a>. They are composed of two monosaccharide units bound together by a <a href="/wiki/Covalent" class="mw-redirect" title="Covalent">covalent</a> bond known as a <a href="/wiki/Glycosidic_linkage" class="mw-redirect" title="Glycosidic linkage">glycosidic linkage</a> formed via a <a href="/wiki/Dehydration_reaction" title="Dehydration reaction">dehydration reaction</a>, resulting in the loss of a <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> atom from one monosaccharide and a <a href="/wiki/Hydroxyl_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroxyl group">hydroxyl group</a> from the other. The <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> of unmodified disaccharides is C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>22</sub>O<sub>11</sub>. Although there are numerous kinds of disaccharides, a handful of disaccharides are particularly notable.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">Sucrose</a>, pictured to the right, is the most abundant disaccharide, and the main form in which carbohydrates are transported in plants. It is composed of one <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">D-glucose</a> molecule and one <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">D-fructose</a> molecule. The <a href="/wiki/Systematic_name" title="Systematic name">systematic name</a> for sucrose, <i>O</i>-α-D-glucopyranosyl-(1→2)-D-fructofuranoside, indicates four things:
</p>
<ul><li>Its monosaccharides: glucose and fructose</li>
<li>Their ring types: glucose is a <a href="/wiki/Pyranose" title="Pyranose">pyranose</a> and fructose is a <a href="/wiki/Furanose" title="Furanose">furanose</a></li>
<li>How they are linked together: the oxygen on carbon number 1 (C1) of α-D-glucose is linked to the C2 of D-fructose.</li>
<li>The <i>-oside</i> suffix indicates that the <a href="/wiki/Anomeric_carbon" class="mw-redirect" title="Anomeric carbon">anomeric carbon</a> of both monosaccharides participates in the glycosidic bond.</li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lactose" title="Lactose">Lactose</a>, a disaccharide composed of one <a href="/wiki/Galactose" title="Galactose">D-galactose</a> molecule and one <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">D-glucose</a> molecule, occurs naturally in mammalian milk. The <a href="/wiki/Systematic_name" title="Systematic name">systematic name</a> for lactose is <i>O</i>-β-D-galactopyranosyl-(1→4)-D-glucopyranose. Other notable disaccharides include <a href="/wiki/Maltose" title="Maltose">maltose</a> (two D-glucoses linked α-1,4) and cellulobiose (two D-glucoses linked β-1,4). Disaccharides can be classified into two types: reducing and non-reducing disaccharides. If the functional group is present in bonding with another sugar unit, it is called a reducing disaccharide or biose.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrition">Nutrition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbohydrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Nutrition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wheat_products.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Wheat_products.jpg/220px-Wheat_products.jpg" width="220" height="306" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Wheat_products.jpg/330px-Wheat_products.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Wheat_products.jpg/440px-Wheat_products.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="891" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wheat_products.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Cereal" title="Cereal">Grain</a> products: rich sources of carbohydrates</div></div></div>
<p>Carbohydrate consumed in food yields 3.87 kilocalories of energy per <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">gram</a> for simple sugars,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and 3.57 to 4.12 kilocalories per gram for complex carbohydrate in most other foods.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Relatively high levels of carbohydrate are associated with processed foods or refined foods made from plants, including sweets, cookies and candy, table sugar, honey, soft drinks, breads and crackers, jams and fruit products, pastas and breakfast cereals. Lower amounts of carbohydrate are usually associated with unrefined foods, including beans, tubers, rice, and unrefined fruit.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  Animal-based foods generally have the lowest carbohydrate levels, although milk does contain a high proportion of <a href="/wiki/Lactose" title="Lactose">lactose</a>.
</p><p>Organisms typically cannot metabolize all types of carbohydrate to yield energy. Glucose is a nearly universal and accessible source of energy. Many organisms also have the ability to metabolize other <a href="/wiki/Monosaccharide" title="Monosaccharide">monosaccharides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Disaccharide" title="Disaccharide">disaccharides</a> but glucose is often metabolized first. In <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i>, for example, the <a href="/wiki/Lac_operon" title="Lac operon">lac operon</a> will express enzymes for the digestion of lactose when it is present, but if both lactose and glucose are present the <i>lac</i> operon is repressed, resulting in the glucose being used first (see: <a href="/wiki/Diauxie" title="Diauxie">Diauxie</a>). <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">Polysaccharides</a> are also common sources of energy. Many organisms can easily break down starches into glucose; most organisms, however, cannot metabolize <a href="/wiki/Cellulose" title="Cellulose">cellulose</a> or other polysaccharides like <a href="/wiki/Chitin" title="Chitin">chitin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabinoxylans" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabinoxylans">arabinoxylans</a>. These carbohydrate types can be metabolized by some bacteria and protists. <a href="/wiki/Ruminant" title="Ruminant">Ruminants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termites</a>, for example, use microorganisms to process cellulose. Even though these complex carbohydrates are not very digestible, they represent an important dietary element for humans, called <a href="/wiki/Dietary_fiber" title="Dietary fiber">dietary fiber</a>. Fiber enhances digestion, among other benefits.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Based on the effects on risk of heart disease and obesity in otherwise healthy middle-aged adults,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Institute of Medicine">Institute of Medicine</a> recommends that American and Canadian adults get between 45–65% of <a href="/wiki/Food_energy" title="Food energy">dietary energy</a> from whole-grain carbohydrates.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> jointly recommend that national dietary guidelines set a goal of 55–75% of total energy from carbohydrates, but only 10% directly from sugars (their term for simple carbohydrates).<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  A 2017 <a href="/wiki/The_Cochrane_Database_of_Systematic_Reviews" class="mw-redirect" title="The Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews">Cochrane Systematic Review</a> concluded that there was insufficient evidence to support the claim that whole grain diets can affect cardiovascular disease.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid28836672_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid28836672-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbohydrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">Nutritionists</a> often refer to carbohydrates as either simple or complex. However, the exact distinction between these groups can be ambiguous. The term <i>complex carbohydrate</i> was first used in the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Senate_Select_Committee_on_Nutrition_and_Human_Needs" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Senate Select Committee on Nutrition and Human Needs">U.S. Senate Select Committee on Nutrition and Human Needs</a> publication <i>Dietary Goals for the United States</i> (1977) where it was intended to distinguish sugars from other carbohydrates (which were perceived to be nutritionally superior).<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> However, the report put "fruit, vegetables and whole-grains" in the complex carbohydrate column, despite the fact that these may contain sugars as well as polysaccharides. This confusion persists as today some nutritionists use the term complex carbohydrate to refer to any sort of digestible saccharide present in a whole food, where fiber, vitamins and minerals are also found (as opposed to processed carbohydrates, which provide energy but few other nutrients). The standard usage, however, is to classify carbohydrates chemically: simple if they are sugars (<a href="/wiki/Monosaccharide" title="Monosaccharide">monosaccharides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Disaccharide" title="Disaccharide">disaccharides</a>) and complex if they are <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharides</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Oligosaccharide" title="Oligosaccharide">oligosaccharides</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-NutSource_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NutSource-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In any case, the simple vs. complex chemical distinction has little value for determining the nutritional quality of carbohydrates.<sup id="cite_ref-NutSource_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NutSource-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Some simple carbohydrates (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a>) raise blood glucose slowly, while some complex carbohydrates (starches), especially if processed, raise blood sugar rapidly. The speed of digestion is determined by a variety of factors including which other nutrients are consumed with the carbohydrate, how the food is prepared, individual differences in metabolism, and the chemistry of the carbohydrate.<sup id="cite_ref-Jenkins_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jenkins-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">USDA's</a> <i>Dietary Guidelines for Americans 2010</i> call for moderate- to high-carbohydrate consumption from a balanced diet that includes six one-ounce servings of grain foods each day, at least half from <a href="/wiki/Whole_grain" title="Whole grain">whole grain</a> sources and the rest from <a href="/wiki/Enriched_grain" class="mw-redirect" title="Enriched grain">enriched</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Glycemic_index" title="Glycemic index">glycemic index (GI)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glycemic_load" title="Glycemic load">glycemic load</a> concepts have been developed to characterize food behavior during human digestion. They rank carbohydrate-rich foods based on the rapidity and magnitude of their effect on <a href="/wiki/Blood_glucose" class="mw-redirect" title="Blood glucose">blood glucose</a> levels. Glycemic index is a measure of how quickly food <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> is absorbed, while glycemic load is a measure of the total absorbable glucose in foods. The <a href="/wiki/Insulin_index" title="Insulin index">insulin index</a> is a similar, more recent classification method that ranks foods based on their effects on <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">blood insulin</a> levels, which are caused by glucose (or starch) and some amino acids in food.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_dietary_carbohydrate_restriction">Effects of dietary carbohydrate restriction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbohydrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Effects of dietary carbohydrate restriction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Low-carbohydrate_diet" title="Low-carbohydrate diet">Low-carbohydrate diet</a></div>
<p>Carbohydrates are a common source of energy in living organisms; however, no single carbohydrate is an <a href="/wiki/Essential_nutrient" class="mw-redirect" title="Essential nutrient">essential nutrient</a> in humans.<sup id="cite_ref-r1_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-r1-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Humans are able to obtain all of their energy requirement from protein and fats, though the potential for some negative health effects of extreme carbohydrate restriction remains, as the issue has not been studied extensively yet.<sup id="cite_ref-r1_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-r1-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> However, in the case of <a href="/wiki/Dietary_fiber" title="Dietary fiber">dietary fiber</a>&#160;– indigestible carbohydrates which are not a source of energy&#160;– inadequate intake can lead to significant increases in mortality.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pmid16685046_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16685046-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following a diet consisting of very low amounts of daily carbohydrate for several days will usually result in higher levels of blood <a href="/wiki/Ketone_bodies" title="Ketone bodies">ketone bodies</a> than an isocaloric diet with similar protein content. This relatively high level of ketone bodies is commonly known as <a href="/wiki/Ketosis" title="Ketosis">ketosis</a> and is very often confused with the potentially fatal condition often seen in <a href="/wiki/Type_1_diabeties" class="mw-redirect" title="Type 1 diabeties">type 1 diabetics</a> known as diabetic <a href="/wiki/Ketoacidosis" title="Ketoacidosis">ketoacidosis</a>. Somebody suffering ketoacidosis will have much higher levels of blood ketone bodies along with high blood sugar, dehydration and electrolyte imbalance.
</p><p>Long-chain fatty acids cannot cross the <a href="/wiki/Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier" title="Blood–brain barrier">blood–brain barrier</a>, but the liver can break these down to produce ketones. However, the medium-chain fatty acids octanoic and heptanoic acids can cross the barrier and be used by the brain, which normally relies upon glucose for its energy.<sup id="cite_ref-Ebert_2003_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert_2003-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Gluconeogenesis" title="Gluconeogenesis">Gluconeogenesis</a> allows humans to synthesize some glucose from specific amino acids: from the <a href="/wiki/Glycerol" title="Glycerol">glycerol</a> backbone in <a href="/wiki/Triglyceride" title="Triglyceride">triglycerides</a> and in some cases from fatty acids.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metabolism">Metabolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbohydrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Metabolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate_metabolism" title="Carbohydrate metabolism">Carbohydrate metabolism</a></div>
<p>Carbohydrate metabolism denotes the various <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemical</a> processes responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Anabolism" title="Anabolism">formation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catabolism" title="Catabolism">breakdown</a> and interconversion of carbohydrates in <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">living</a> <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a>.
</p><p>The most important carbohydrate is <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>, a simple sugar (<a href="/wiki/Monosaccharide" title="Monosaccharide">monosaccharide</a>) that is metabolized by nearly all known organisms. Glucose and other carbohydrates are part of a wide variety of metabolic pathways across species: <a href="/wiki/Plants" class="mw-redirect" title="Plants">plants</a> synthesize carbohydrates from carbon dioxide and water by <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> storing the absorbed energy internally, often in the form of <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lipid" title="Lipid">lipids</a>. Plant components are consumed by animals and <a href="/wiki/Fungi" class="mw-redirect" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, and used as fuel for <a href="/wiki/Cellular_respiration" title="Cellular respiration">cellular respiration</a>. Oxidation of one gram of carbohydrate yields approximately 9&#160;kJ (4&#160;kcal) of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_energy" title="Chemical energy">energy</a>, while the oxidation of one gram of lipids yields about 38&#160;kJ (9&#160;kcal). The human body stores between 300 to 500&#160;g of carbohydrates depending on body weight, with the skeletal muscle contributing to a large portion of the storage.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Energy obtained from metabolism (e.g., oxidation of glucose) is usually stored temporarily within cells in the form of <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-energetics_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-energetics-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Organisms capable of anaerobic and <a href="/wiki/Aerobic_respiration" class="mw-redirect" title="Aerobic respiration">aerobic respiration</a> that metabolizes glucose and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> (aerobic) to release energy with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> as byproducts.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catabolism">Catabolism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbohydrate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Catabolism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Catabolism is the metabolic reaction which cells undergo to break down larger molecules, extracting energy. There are two major <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_pathway" title="Metabolic pathway">metabolic pathways</a> of monosaccharide <a href="/wiki/Catabolism" title="Catabolism">catabolism</a>: <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis" title="Glycolysis">glycolysis</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle" title="Citric acid cycle">citric acid cycle</a>.
</p><p>In glycolysis, oligo- and polysaccharides are cleaved first to smaller monosaccharides by enzymes called <a href="/wiki/Glycoside_hydrolase" title="Glycoside hydrolase">glycoside hydrolases</a>. The monosaccharide units can then enter into monosaccharide catabolism. A 2&#160;ATP investment is required in the early steps of glycolysis to phosphorylate Glucose to <a href="/wiki/Glucose_6-phosphate" title="Glucose 6-phosphate">Glucose 6-Phosphate</a> (<a href="/wiki/Glucose_6-phosphate" title="Glucose 6-phosphate">G6P</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Fructose_6-phosphate" title="Fructose 6-phosphate">Fructose 6-Phosphate</a> (<a href="/wiki/Fructose_6-phosphate" title="Fructose 6-phosphate">F6P</a>) to <a href="/wiki/Fructose_1,6-bisphosphate" title="Fructose 1,6-bisphosphate">Fructose 1,6-biphosphate</a> (<a href="/wiki/Fructose_1,6-bisphosphate" title="Fructose 1,6-bisphosphate">FBP</a>), thereby pushing the reaction forward irreversibly.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In some cases, as with humans, not all carbohydrate types are usable as the digestive and metabolic enzymes necessary are not present.
</p>
