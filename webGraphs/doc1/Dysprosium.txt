<p><b>Dysprosium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Dy</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 66. It is a <a href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" class="mw-redirect" title="Rare earth element">rare earth element</a> with a metallic silver luster. Dysprosium is never found in nature as a free element, though it is found in various minerals, such as <a href="/wiki/Xenotime" title="Xenotime">xenotime</a>. Naturally occurring dysprosium is composed of seven <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a>, the most <a href="/wiki/Isotopic_abundance" class="mw-redirect" title="Isotopic abundance">abundant</a> of which is <sup>164</sup>Dy.
</p><p>Dysprosium was first identified in 1886 by <a href="/wiki/Paul_%C3%89mile_Lecoq_de_Boisbaudran" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran">Paul Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran</a>, but it was not isolated in pure form until the development of <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a> techniques in the 1950s. Dysprosium has relatively few applications where it cannot be replaced by other chemical elements. It is used for its high thermal neutron absorption cross-section in making control rods in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a>, for its high <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_susceptibility" title="Magnetic susceptibility">magnetic susceptibility</a> in data storage applications, and as a component of <a href="/wiki/Terfenol-D" title="Terfenol-D">Terfenol-D</a> (a magnetostrictive material). Soluble dysprosium salts are mildly toxic, while the insoluble salts are considered non-toxic.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysprosium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysprosium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:92px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dysprosium.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Dysprosium.jpg/90px-Dysprosium.jpg" width="90" height="99" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Dysprosium.jpg/135px-Dysprosium.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Dysprosium.jpg/180px-Dysprosium.jpg 2x" data-file-width="270" data-file-height="297" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dysprosium.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dysprosium sample</div></div></div>
<p>Dysprosium is a <a href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" class="mw-redirect" title="Rare earth element">rare earth element</a> that has a metallic, bright silver luster. It is quite soft, and can be machined without sparking if overheating is avoided. Dysprosium's physical characteristics can be greatly affected by even small amounts of impurities.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Dysprosium and <a href="/wiki/Holmium" title="Holmium">holmium</a> have the highest magnetic strengths of the elements,<sup id="cite_ref-nbb_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> especially at low temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-krebs_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-krebs-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Dysprosium has a simple <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferromagnetic">ferromagnetic</a> ordering at temperatures below 85&#160;K (−188.2&#160;°C). Above 85&#160;K (−188.2&#160;°C), it turns into a helical <a href="/wiki/Antiferromagnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Antiferromagnetic">antiferromagnetic</a> state in which all of the atomic moments in a particular <a href="/wiki/Basal_plane" class="mw-redirect" title="Basal plane">basal plane</a> layer are parallel, and oriented at a fixed angle to the moments of adjacent layers. This unusual antiferromagnetism transforms into a disordered (<a href="/wiki/Paramagnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Paramagnetic">paramagnetic</a>) state at 179&#160;K (−94&#160;°C).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysprosium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dysprosium metal tarnishes slowly in air and burns readily to form <a href="/wiki/Dysprosium(III)_oxide" title="Dysprosium(III) oxide">dysprosium(III) oxide</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>4 Dy + 3 O<sub>2</sub> → 2 Dy<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Dysprosium is quite electropositive and reacts slowly with cold water (and quite quickly with hot water) to form dysprosium hydroxide:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Dy (s) + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O (l) → 2 Dy(OH)<sub>3</sub> (aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<p>Dysprosium metal vigorously reacts with all the halogens at above 200&#160;°C:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Dy (s) + 3 F<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 DyF<sub>3</sub> (s) [green]</dd>
<dd>2 Dy (s) + 3 Cl<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 DyCl<sub>3</sub> (s) [white]</dd>
<dd>2 Dy (s) + 3 Br<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 DyBr<sub>3</sub> (s) [white]</dd>
<dd>2 Dy (s) + 3 I<sub>2</sub> (g) → 2 DyI<sub>3</sub> (s) [green]</dd></dl>
<p>Dysprosium dissolves readily in dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to form solutions containing the yellow Dy(III) ions, which exist as a [Dy(OH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>9</sub>]<sup>3+</sup> complex:<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Dy (s) + 3 H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> (aq) → 2 Dy<sup>3+</sup> (aq) + 3 <span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> (aq) + 3 H<sub>2</sub> (g)</dd></dl>
<p>The resulting compound, dysprosium(III) sulfate, is noticeably paramagnetic.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compounds">Compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysprosium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dysprosium-sulfate.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Dysprosium-sulfate.jpg/170px-Dysprosium-sulfate.jpg" width="170" height="170" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Dysprosium-sulfate.jpg/255px-Dysprosium-sulfate.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Dysprosium-sulfate.jpg/340px-Dysprosium-sulfate.jpg 2x" data-file-width="948" data-file-height="948" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dysprosium-sulfate.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dysprosium sulfate, Dy<sub>2</sub>(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub></div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Dysprosium_compounds" title="Category:Dysprosium compounds">Dysprosium compounds</a></div>
<p>Dysprosium halides, such as DyF<sub>3</sub> and DyBr<sub>3</sub>, tend to take on a yellow color. <a href="/wiki/Dysprosium(III)_oxide" title="Dysprosium(III) oxide">Dysprosium oxide</a>, also known as dysprosia, is a white powder that is highly <a href="/wiki/Magnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnetic">magnetic</a>, more so than iron oxide.<sup id="cite_ref-krebs_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-krebs-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Dysprosium combines with various non-metals at high temperatures to form binary compounds with varying composition and oxidation states +3 and sometimes +2, such as DyN, DyP, DyH<sub>2</sub> and DyH<sub>3</sub>; DyS, DyS<sub>2</sub>, Dy<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> and Dy<sub>5</sub>S<sub>7</sub>; DyB<sub>2</sub>, DyB<sub>4</sub>, DyB<sub>6</sub> and DyB<sub>12</sub>, as well as Dy<sub>3</sub>C and Dy<sub>2</sub>C<sub>3</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-patnaik_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Dysprosium carbonate, Dy<sub>2</sub>(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>, and dysprosium sulfate, Dy<sub>2</sub>(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>, result from similar reactions.<sup id="cite_ref-heiserman_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heiserman-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Most dysprosium compounds are soluble in water, though dysprosium carbonate tetrahydrate (Dy<sub>2</sub>(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>·4H<sub>2</sub>O) and dysprosium oxalate decahydrate (Dy<sub>2</sub>(C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>·10H<sub>2</sub>O) are both insoluble in water.<sup id="cite_ref-perry_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-perry-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Two of the most abundant dysprosium carbonates, tengerite-(Dy) (Dy<sub>2</sub>(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>·2–3H<sub>2</sub>O) and kozoite-(Dy) (DyCO<sub>3</sub>(OH)) are known to form via a poorly ordered (amorphous) precursor phase with a formula of Dy<sub>2</sub>(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>·4H<sub>2</sub>O. This amorphous precursor consists of highly hydrated spherical nanoparticles of 10–20&#160;nm diameter that are exceptionally stable under dry treatment at ambient and high temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysprosium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_dysprosium" title="Isotopes of dysprosium">Isotopes of dysprosium</a></div>
<p>Naturally occurring dysprosium is composed of seven <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a>: <sup>156</sup>Dy, <sup>158</sup>Dy, <sup>160</sup>Dy, <sup>161</sup>Dy, <sup>162</sup>Dy, <sup>163</sup>Dy, and <sup>164</sup>Dy. These are all considered stable, although <sup>156</sup>Dy decays by <a href="/wiki/Alpha_decay" title="Alpha decay">alpha decay</a> with a half-life of over 1×10<sup>18</sup>&#160;years. Of the naturally occurring isotopes, <sup>164</sup>Dy is the most <a href="/wiki/Natural_abundance" title="Natural abundance">abundant</a> at 28%, followed by <sup>162</sup>Dy at 26%. The least abundant is <sup>156</sup>Dy at 0.06%.<sup id="cite_ref-nubase_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nubase-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Twenty-nine <a href="/wiki/Radioisotopes" class="mw-redirect" title="Radioisotopes">radioisotopes</a> have also been synthesized, ranging in atomic mass from 138 to 173. The most stable of these is <sup>154</sup>Dy, with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of approximately 3<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup><span style="white-space:nowrap">6</span></sup>&#160;years, followed by <sup>159</sup>Dy with a half-life of 144.4&#160;days. The least stable is <sup>138</sup>Dy, with a half-life of 200&#160;ms. As a general rule, isotopes that are lighter than the stable isotopes tend to decay primarily by β<sup>+</sup> decay, while those that are heavier tend to decay by <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay#β−_decay" title="Beta decay">β<sup>−</sup> decay</a>. However, <sup>154</sup>Dy decays primarily by alpha decay, and <sup>152</sup>Dy and <sup>159</sup>Dy decay primarily by <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nubase_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nubase-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Dysprosium also has at least 11 <a href="/wiki/Metastable_isomer" class="mw-redirect" title="Metastable isomer">metastable isomers</a>, ranging in atomic mass from 140 to 165. The most stable of these is <sup>165m</sup>Dy, which has a half-life of 1.257&#160;minutes. <sup>149</sup>Dy has two metastable isomers, the second of which, <sup>149m2</sup>Dy, has a half-life of 28&#160;ns.<sup id="cite_ref-nubase_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nubase-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysprosium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1878, <a href="/wiki/Erbium" title="Erbium">erbium</a> ores were found to contain the oxides of <a href="/wiki/Holmium" title="Holmium">holmium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thulium" title="Thulium">thulium</a>. French chemist <a href="/wiki/Paul_%C3%89mile_Lecoq_de_Boisbaudran" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran">Paul Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran</a>, while working with <a href="/wiki/Holmium_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Holmium oxide">holmium oxide</a>, separated dysprosium oxide from it in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> in 1886.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> His procedure for isolating the dysprosium involved dissolving dysprosium oxide in acid, then adding ammonia to precipitate the hydroxide. He was only able to isolate dysprosium from its oxide after more than 30 attempts at his procedure. On succeeding, he named the element <i>dysprosium</i> from the Greek <i>dysprositos</i> (δυσπρόσιτος), meaning "hard to get". The element was not isolated in relatively pure form until after the development of ion exchange techniques by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Spedding" title="Frank Spedding">Frank Spedding</a> at <a href="/wiki/Iowa_State_University" title="Iowa State University">Iowa State University</a> in the early 1950s.<sup id="cite_ref-nbb_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysprosium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Xenot%C3%ADmio1.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Xenot%C3%ADmio1.jpeg/220px-Xenot%C3%ADmio1.jpeg" width="220" height="224" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Xenot%C3%ADmio1.jpeg/330px-Xenot%C3%ADmio1.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Xenot%C3%ADmio1.jpeg/440px-Xenot%C3%ADmio1.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="655" data-file-height="666" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Xenot%C3%ADmio1.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Xenotime</div></div></div>
<p>While dysprosium is never encountered as a free element, it is found in many <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Xenotime" title="Xenotime">xenotime</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fergusonite" title="Fergusonite">fergusonite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gadolinite" title="Gadolinite">gadolinite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Euxenite" title="Euxenite">euxenite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polycrase" title="Polycrase">polycrase</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blomstrandine" class="mw-redirect" title="Blomstrandine">blomstrandine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4site" title="Bastnäsite">bastnäsite</a>, often with <a href="/wiki/Erbium" title="Erbium">erbium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holmium" title="Holmium">holmium</a> or other rare earth elements. No dysprosium-dominant mineral (that is, with dysprosium prevailing over other rare earths in the composition) has yet been found.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Currently, most dysprosium is being obtained from the ion-adsorption clay ores of southern China,<sup id="cite_ref-China_rare_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-China_rare-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and future sources will include the Halls Creek region in Western Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-Australia_rare_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Australia_rare-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In the high-<a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a> version of these, dysprosium happens to be the most abundant of the heavy <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanides</a>, comprising up to 7–8% of the concentrate (as compared to about 65% for yttrium).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The concentration of Dy in the Earth's crust is about 5.2&#160;mg/kg and in sea water 0.9&#160;ng/L.<sup id="cite_ref-patnaik_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysprosium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dysprosium is obtained primarily from <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> sand, a mixture of various <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphates</a>. The metal is obtained as a by-product in the commercial extraction of yttrium. In isolating dysprosium, most of the unwanted metals can be removed magnetically or by a <a href="/wiki/Flotation_process" class="mw-redirect" title="Flotation process">flotation process</a>. Dysprosium can then be separated from other rare earth metals by an <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a> displacement process. The resulting dysprosium ions can then react with either <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> to form dysprosium fluoride, DyF<sub>3</sub>, or dysprosium chloride, DyCl<sub>3</sub>. These compounds can be reduced using either calcium or lithium metals in the following reactions:<sup id="cite_ref-heiserman_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heiserman-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>3 Ca + 2 DyF<sub>3</sub> → 2 Dy + 3 CaF<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd>3 Li + DyCl<sub>3</sub> → Dy + 3 LiCl</dd></dl>
<p>The components are placed in a <a href="/wiki/Tantalum" title="Tantalum">tantalum</a> crucible and fired in a <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a> atmosphere. As the reaction progresses, the resulting halide compounds and molten dysprosium separate due to differences in density. When the mixture cools, the dysprosium can be cut away from the impurities.<sup id="cite_ref-heiserman_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-heiserman-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>About 100 tonnes of dysprosium are produced worldwide each year,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> with 99% of that total produced in China.<sup id="cite_ref-Bradsher,_Keith_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bradsher,_Keith-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Dysprosium prices have climbed nearly twentyfold, from $7 per pound in 2003, to $130 a pound in late 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-Bradsher,_Keith_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bradsher,_Keith-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The price increased to $1,400/kg in 2011 but fell to $240 in 2015, largely due to illegal production in China which circumvented government restrictions.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">United States Department of Energy</a>, the wide range of its current and projected uses, together with the lack of any immediately suitable replacement, makes dysprosium the single most critical element for emerging clean energy technologies - even their most conservative projections predict a shortfall of dysprosium before 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  As of late 2015, there is a nascent rare earth (including dysprosium) extraction industry in Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysprosium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dysprosium is used, in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">vanadium</a> and other elements, in making <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> materials and commercial lighting. Because of dysprosium's high <a href="/wiki/Thermal_neutron" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermal neutron">thermal-neutron</a> absorption cross-section, dysprosium-oxide–nickel <a href="/wiki/Cermet" title="Cermet">cermets</a> are used in neutron-absorbing control rods in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nbb_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Dysprosium–<a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a> <a href="/wiki/Chalcogen" title="Chalcogen">chalcogenides</a> are sources of <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> radiation, which is useful for studying chemical reactions.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Because dysprosium and its compounds are highly susceptible to magnetization, they are employed in various data-storage applications, such as in <a href="/wiki/Hard_disk_drive" title="Hard disk drive">hard disks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-lagowski_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lagowski-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Dysprosium is increasingly in demand for the permanent magnets used in electric car motors and wind turbine generators.<sup id="cite_ref-MIT-TechRev_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MIT-TechRev-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Neodymium" title="Neodymium">Neodymium</a>–iron–boron <a href="/wiki/Neodymium_magnet" title="Neodymium magnet">magnets</a> can have up to 6% of the neodymium substituted by dysprosium<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> to raise the <a href="/wiki/Coercivity" title="Coercivity">coercivity</a> for demanding applications, such as drive motors for electric vehicles and generators for wind turbines. This substitution would require up to 100&#160;grams of dysprosium per electric car produced. Based on <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">Toyota</a>'s projected 2&#160;million units per year, the use of dysprosium in applications such as this would quickly exhaust its available supply.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The dysprosium substitution may also be useful in other applications because it improves the corrosion resistance of the magnets.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Dysprosium is one of the components of <a href="/wiki/Terfenol-D" title="Terfenol-D">Terfenol-D</a>, along with iron and terbium. Terfenol-D has the highest room-temperature <a href="/wiki/Magnetostriction" title="Magnetostriction">magnetostriction</a> of any known material;<sup id="cite_ref-etrema_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etrema-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> which is employed in <a href="/wiki/Transducer" title="Transducer">transducers</a>, wide-band <a href="/wiki/Resonator#Mechanical" title="Resonator">mechanical resonators</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> and high-precision liquid-fuel injectors.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Dysprosium is used in <a href="/wiki/Dosimeter" title="Dosimeter">dosimeters</a> for measuring <a href="/wiki/Ionizing_radiation" title="Ionizing radiation">ionizing radiation</a>. Crystals of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_sulfate" title="Calcium sulfate">calcium sulfate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Calcium_fluoride" title="Calcium fluoride">calcium fluoride</a> are doped with dysprosium. When these crystals are exposed to radiation, the dysprosium atoms become <a href="/wiki/Excited_state" title="Excited state">excited</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luminescent" class="mw-redirect" title="Luminescent">luminescent</a>. The luminescence can be measured to determine the degree of exposure to which the dosimeter has been subjected.<sup id="cite_ref-nbb_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Nanofibers of dysprosium compounds have high strength and a large surface area. Therefore, they can be used to reinforce other materials and act as a catalyst. Fibers of dysprosium oxide fluoride can be produced by heating an aqueous solution of DyBr<sub>3</sub> and NaF to 450&#160;°C at 450 bars for 17 hours. This material is remarkably robust, surviving over 100 hours in various aqueous solutions at temperatures exceeding 400&#160;°C without redissolving or aggregating.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Dysprosium iodide and dysprosium bromide are used in high-intensity <a href="/wiki/Metal-halide_lamp" title="Metal-halide lamp">metal-halide lamps</a>. These compounds dissociate near the hot center of the lamp, releasing isolated dysprosium atoms. The latter re-emit light in the green and red part of the spectrum, thereby effectively producing bright light.<sup id="cite_ref-nbb_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gray_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gray-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several paramagnetic crystal salts of dysprosium (Dysprosium Gallium Garnet, DGG; Dysprosium Aluminum Garnet, DAG; Dysprosium Iron Garnet, DyIG) are used in <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_refrigeration" title="Magnetic refrigeration">adiabatic demagnetization refrigerators</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The trivalent dysprosium ion (Dy<sup>3+</sup>) has been studied due its downshifting luminescence properties. Dy-doped yttrium aluminium garnet (YAG:Dy) excited in the ultraviolet region of the electromagnetic spectrum results in the emission of photons of longer wavelength in the visible region. This idea is the basis for a new generation of UV-pumped white light emitting diodes.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precautions">Precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dysprosium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like many powders, dysprosium powder may present an explosion hazard when mixed with air and when an ignition source is present. Thin foils of the substance can also be ignited by sparks or by <a href="/wiki/Static_electricity" title="Static electricity">static electricity</a>. Dysprosium fires cannot be put out by water. It can react with water to produce flammable <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> gas.<sup id="cite_ref-ESPI_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ESPI-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Dysprosium chloride fires, however, can be extinguished with water,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> while dysprosium fluoride and dysprosium oxide are non-flammable.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Dysprosium nitrate, Dy(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>, is a strong <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agent</a> and will readily ignite on contact with organic substances.<sup id="cite_ref-krebs_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-krebs-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Soluble dysprosium salts, such as dysprosium chloride and dysprosium nitrate, are mildly toxic when ingested. Based on the toxicity of dysprosium chloride to <a href="/wiki/Mice" class="mw-redirect" title="Mice">mice</a>, it is estimated that the ingestion of 500&#160;grams or more could be fatal to a human. The insoluble salts, however, are non-toxic.<sup id="cite_ref-nbb_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nbb-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
