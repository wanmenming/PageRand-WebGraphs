<p><b>Ruthenium tetroxide</b> (<i>Ruthenium(VIII) oxide</i>) is the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the formula RuO<sub>4</sub>. It is a colourless,  <a href="/wiki/Diamagnetism" title="Diamagnetism">diamagnetic</a> liquid, but samples are typically black due to impurities.   It is volatile. The analogous <a href="/wiki/Osmium(VIII)_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Osmium(VIII) oxide">OsO<sub>4</sub></a> is more widely used and better known. One of the few solvents in which it forms stable solutions is <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">CCl<sub>4</sub></a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>RuO<sub>4</sub> is prepared by oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium(III)_chloride" title="Ruthenium(III) chloride">ruthenium(III) chloride</a> with <a href="/wiki/Sodium_periodate" title="Sodium periodate">NaIO<sub>4</sub></a>.
</p>
<dl><dd>8 Ru<sup>3+</sup>(aq) + 5 IO<sub>4</sub><sup>&#8722;</sup>(aq) + 12 H<sub>2</sub>O(l) &#8594; 8 RuO<sub>4</sub>(s) + 5 I<sup>&#8722;</sup>(aq) + 24 H<sup>+</sup>(aq)</dd></dl>
<p>Due to the expense, toxicity, and high reactivity of RuO<sub>4</sub>, it is often generated <i>in situ</i> and used in catalytic quantities in organic reactions, by using a substoichiometric amount of ruthenium(III) or -(IV) precursor and a stoichiometric amount of sodium metaperiodate as the terminal oxidant to continuously regenerate small amounts of RuO<sub>4</sub>.  In typical reactions featuring RuO<sub>4</sub> as the oxidant, many forms of ruthenium usefully serve as precursors to RuO<sub>4</sub>, most commonly used are RuCl<sub>3</sub>·<i>x</i>H<sub>2</sub>O or RuO<sub>2</sub>·<i>x</i>H<sub>2</sub>O.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The molecule adopts a <a href="/wiki/Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry" title="Tetrahedral molecular geometry">tetrahedral</a> geometry, with the Ru–O distances ranging from 169 to 170 pm.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isolation_of_ruthenium_from_ores">Isolation of ruthenium from ores</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Isolation of ruthenium from ores">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main value of RuO<sub>4</sub> is as an intermediate in the production of ruthenium compounds and metal from ores.  Like other <a href="/wiki/Platinum_group_metals" class="mw-redirect" title="Platinum group metals">platinum group metals</a> (PGMs), <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a> occurs at low concentrations and often mixed with other PGMs.  Together with OsO<sub>4</sub>, it is separated from other PGMs by distillation of a chlorine-oxidized extract.  Ruthenium is separated from OsO<sub>4</sub> by reducing RuO<sub>4</sub> with <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a>, a process that exploits the highly positive reduction potential for the [RuO<sub>4</sub>]<sup>0/-</sup> couple.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organic_chemistry">Organic chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Organic chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>RuO<sub>4</sub> is only of specialized value in organic chemistry because it oxidizes virtually any hydrocarbon. For example, it will oxidize <a href="/wiki/Adamantane" title="Adamantane">adamantane</a> to 1-adamantanol. Because it is such an aggressive oxidant, reaction conditions must be mild, generally room temperature. Although a strong oxidant, RuO<sub>4</sub> oxidations do not perturb <a href="/wiki/Stereocenter" title="Stereocenter">stereocenters</a> that are not oxidized. Illustrative is the oxidation of the following diol to a <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acid</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/File:RuO4oxidation.png" class="image"><img alt="RuO4oxidation.png" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/RuO4oxidation.png/450px-RuO4oxidation.png" width="450" height="83" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/RuO4oxidation.png/675px-RuO4oxidation.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/RuO4oxidation.png/900px-RuO4oxidation.png 2x" data-file-width="1763" data-file-height="324" /></a></dd></dl>
<p>Oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Epoxide" title="Epoxide">epoxy</a> alcohols also occurs without degradation of the epoxide ring:
</p>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/File:RuO4epoxy.png" class="image"><img alt="RuO4epoxy.png" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/RuO4epoxy.png/450px-RuO4epoxy.png" width="450" height="92" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/RuO4epoxy.png/675px-RuO4epoxy.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/RuO4epoxy.png/900px-RuO4epoxy.png 2x" data-file-width="1178" data-file-height="240" /></a></dd></dl>
<p>Under milder condition, oxidative reaction yields <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehydes</a> instead. RuO<sub>4</sub> readily converts secondary alcohols into <a href="/wiki/Ketones" class="mw-redirect" title="Ketones">ketones</a>. Although similar results can be achieved with other cheaper <a href="/wiki/Oxidant" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxidant">oxidants</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Pyridinium_chlorochromate" title="Pyridinium chlorochromate">PCC</a>- or <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfoxide" title="Dimethyl sulfoxide">DMSO</a>-based oxidants, RuO<sub>4</sub> is ideal when a very vigorous oxidant is needed but mild conditions must be maintained. It is used in <a href="/wiki/Organic_synthesis" title="Organic synthesis">organic synthesis</a> to oxidize internal <a href="/wiki/Alkynes" class="mw-redirect" title="Alkynes">alkynes</a> to 1,2-<a href="/wiki/Diketone" title="Diketone">diketones</a>, and terminal alkynes along with primary <a href="/wiki/Alcohols" class="mw-redirect" title="Alcohols">alcohols</a> to <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acids" class="mw-redirect" title="Carboxylic acids">carboxylic acids</a>. When used in this fashion, the ruthenium(VIII) oxide is used in catalytic amounts and regenerated by the addition of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_periodate" title="Sodium periodate">sodium periodate</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium(III)_chloride" title="Ruthenium(III) chloride">ruthenium(III) chloride</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvent</a> mixture of <a href="/wiki/Acetonitrile" title="Acetonitrile">acetonitrile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a>. RuO<sub>4</sub> readily cleaves double bonds to yield <a href="/wiki/Carbonyl" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbonyl">carbonyl</a> products, in a manner similar to <a href="/wiki/Ozonolysis" title="Ozonolysis">ozonolysis</a>. <a href="/wiki/Osmium(VIII)_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Osmium(VIII) oxide">Osmium(VIII) oxide</a>, a more familiar oxidant that is structurally similar to RuO<sub>4</sub>, does not cleave double bonds, instead producing <a href="/wiki/Vicinal_(chemistry)" title="Vicinal (chemistry)">vicinal</a> diol products.  However, with short reaction times and carefully controlled conditions, RuO<sub>4</sub> can also be used for dihydroxylation.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Because RuO<sub>4</sub> degrades the "double bonds" of arenes (especially electron-rich ones) by dihydroxylation and cleavage of the C-C bond in a way few other reagents can, it is useful as a "deprotection" reagent for carboxylic acids that are masked as aryl groups (typically phenyl or <i>p</i>-methoxyphenyl).  Because the fragments formed are themselves readily oxidizable by RuO<sub>4</sub>, a substantial fraction of the arene carbon atoms undergo exhaustive oxidation to form carbon dioxide.  Consequently, multiple equivalents of the terminal oxidant (often in excess of 10 equivalents per aryl ring) are required to achieve complete conversion to the carboxylic acid, limiting the practicality of the transformation.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  
</p><p><a href="/wiki/File:RuO4-degradation-rev.png" class="image"><img alt="RuO4-degradation-rev.png" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/RuO4-degradation-rev.png/500px-RuO4-degradation-rev.png" width="500" height="135" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/RuO4-degradation-rev.png/750px-RuO4-degradation-rev.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/RuO4-degradation-rev.png/1000px-RuO4-degradation-rev.png 2x" data-file-width="3005" data-file-height="810" /></a>
</p><p>Although used as a direct <a href="/wiki/Oxidant" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxidant">oxidant</a>, due to the relatively high cost of RuO<sub>4</sub> it is also used <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalyst">catalytically</a> with a cooxidant. For an oxidation of cyclic <a href="/wiki/Alcohols" class="mw-redirect" title="Alcohols">alcohols</a> with RuO<sub>4</sub> as a catalyst and <a href="/wiki/Bromate" title="Bromate">bromate</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a>, RuO<sub>4</sub> is first activated by hydroxide:
</p>
<dl><dd>RuO<sub>4</sub> + OH<sup>&#8722;</sup> → HRuO<sub>5</sub><sup>&#8722;</sup></dd></dl>
<p>The reaction proceeds via a glycolate complex.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ruthenium tetroxide is a potential staining agent.  It is used to expose latent fingerprints by turning to the brown/black ruthenium dioxide when in contact with fatty oils or fats contained in sebaceous contaminants of the print.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_ruthenium_compounds">Related ruthenium compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium_tetroxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Related ruthenium compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because RuO<sub>4</sub> will readily decompose explosively at slightly elevated temperatures, most laboratories do not synthesize it directly, nor is it commercially available. A related reagent is the anionic Ru(VII) derivative in the form of the salt of "TPAP" (<a href="/wiki/Tetrapropylammonium_perruthenate" title="Tetrapropylammonium perruthenate">tetrapropylammonium perruthenate</a>), [N(C<sub>3</sub>H<sub>7</sub>)<sub>4</sub>]RuO<sub>4</sub>. TPAP is synthesized by oxidizing RuCl<sub>3</sub> to RuO<sub>4</sub><sup>&#8722;</sup> by <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bromate" title="Sodium bromate">NaBrO<sub>3</sub></a> and isolated as the tetrapropylamine cation, which allows the salt to be used in organic solvents.
</p>
