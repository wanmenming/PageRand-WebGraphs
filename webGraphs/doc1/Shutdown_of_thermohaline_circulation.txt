<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Shutdown of thermohaline circulation</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">An effect of global warming on a major ocean circulation.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png/320px-Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png" width="320" height="201" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png/480px-Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png/640px-Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png 2x" data-file-width="1100" data-file-height="690" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Thermohaline_Circulation_2.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A summary of the path of the thermohaline circulation. Blue paths represent deep-water currents, while red paths represent surface currents</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>shutdown</b> or <b>slowdown of the thermohaline circulation</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">effect of global warming</a> on a major ocean circulation.
</p><p>Data from <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> in 2010 suggested that the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Meridional_Overturning_Circulation" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation">Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation</a> (AMOC) had not slowed down, but may have actually sped up slightly since 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> A 2015 study suggested that the AMOC has weakened by 15-20% in 200 years.<sup id="cite_ref-RahmstorfBox2015_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RahmstorfBox2015-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><p>
Don Chambers from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_South_Florida" title="University of South Florida">University of South Florida</a> College of Marine Science mentioned, "The major effect of a slowing AMOC is expected to be cooler winters and summers around the North Atlantic, and small regional increases in sea level on the North American coast."<sup id="cite_ref-PhysorgJan2016_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PhysorgJan2016-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/James_Hansen" title="James Hansen">James Hansen</a> and Makiko Sato stated, </p><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>AMOC slowdown that causes cooling ~1°C and perhaps affects weather patterns is very different from an AMOC shutdown that cools the North Atlantic several degrees Celsius; the latter would have dramatic effects on storms and be irreversible on the century time scale.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote><p> Downturn of the Atlantic meridional overturning circulation, has been tied to extreme regional <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2017 review concluded that there is strong evidence for past changes in the strength and structure of the AMOC during abrupt climate events such as the <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a> and many of the <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_event" title="Heinrich event">Heinrich events</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slowdown">Slowdown</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Slowdown">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cold_blob_(North_Atlantic)" title="Cold blob (North Atlantic)">Cold blob (North Atlantic)</a></div>
<p>Lohmann and Dima 2010 found a weakening of the AMOC since the late 1930s.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Climate scientists <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Mann" title="Michael E. Mann">Michael Mann</a> of Penn State and <a href="/wiki/Stefan_Rahmstorf" title="Stefan Rahmstorf">Stefan Rahmstorf</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Institute_for_Climate_Impact_Research" title="Potsdam Institute for Climate Impact Research">Potsdam Institute for Climate Impact Research</a> suggested that the observed cold pattern during years of temperature records is a sign that the Atlantic Ocean’s <a href="/wiki/Meridional_overturning_circulation" class="mw-redirect" title="Meridional overturning circulation">Meridional overturning circulation</a> (AMOC) may be weakening. They published their findings in 2015, and concluded that the AMOC circulation showed exceptional slowdown in the last century, and that <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> melt is a possible contributor, with the slowdown of AMOC since the 1970s being unprecedented over the last millennium.<sup id="cite_ref-Rahmstorf2015_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rahmstorf2015-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A study published in 2016 found further evidence for a considerable impact from <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a> for the <a href="/wiki/U.S._East_Coast" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. East Coast">U.S. East Coast</a>. The study confirms earlier research findings which identified the region as a hotspot for rising seas, with a potential to divert 3–4 times in the rate of rise, compared to the global average. The researchers attribute the possible increase to an ocean circulation mechanism called deep water formation, which is reduced due to AMOC slow down, leading to more warmer water pockets below the surface. Additionally, the study noted, "Our results suggest that higher carbon emission rates also contribute to increased [sea level rise] in this region compared to the global average."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shutdown">Shutdown</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Shutdown">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Abrupt_climate_change" title="Abrupt climate change">Abrupt climate change</a></div>
<p>Global warming could, via a shutdown of the <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a>, trigger cooling in the North <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a>, Europe, and North America.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> This would particularly affect areas such as the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic countries</a>, which are warmed by the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_drift" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic drift">North Atlantic drift</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Major consequences, apart from regional cooling, could also include an increase in major floods and storms, a collapse of <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a> stocks, warming or rainfall changes in the tropics or <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>, more frequent and intense <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Niño">El Niño</a> events due to associated shutdowns of the <a href="/wiki/Kuroshio_Current" title="Kuroshio Current">Kuroshio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leeuwin_Current" title="Leeuwin Current">Leeuwin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/East_Australian_Current" title="East Australian Current">East Australian Currents</a> that are connected to the same thermohaline circulation as the Gulf Stream, or an <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_anoxic_event" class="mw-redirect" title="Oceanic anoxic event">oceanic anoxic event</a> — <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen (<span class="chemf nowrap">O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>)</a> below surface levels of the stagnant oceans becomes completely depleted — a probable cause of past <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event" title="Extinction event">mass extinction events</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Schiermeier2007_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schiermeier2007-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_weather">Effects on weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Effects on weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hansen et al. 2015 found, that the shutdown or substantial slowdown of the AMOC, besides possibly contributing to extreme end-<a href="/wiki/Eemian" title="Eemian">Eemian</a> events, will cause a more general increase of severe weather. Additional surface cooling from ice melt increases surface and lower tropospheric temperature gradients, and causes in model simulations a large increase of mid-latitude <a href="/wiki/Eddy_current" title="Eddy current">eddy energy</a> throughout the midlatitude troposphere. This in turn leads to an increase of <a href="/wiki/Baroclinicity" class="mw-redirect" title="Baroclinicity">baroclinicity</a> produced by stronger temperature gradients, which provides energy for more severe weather events.
</p><p>Many of the most memorable and devastating storms in eastern North America and western Europe, popularly known as superstorms, have been winter cyclonic storms, though sometimes occurring in late fall or early spring, that generate near-hurricane-force winds and often large amounts of snowfall. Continued warming of low latitude oceans in coming decades will provide more water vapor to strengthen such storms. If this tropical warming is combined with a cooler North Atlantic Ocean from AMOC slowdown and an increase in midlatitude eddy energy, we can anticipate more severe baroclinic storms.
</p><p>Hansen et al. results at least imply that strong cooling in the North Atlantic from AMOC shutdown does create higher wind speed. The increment in seasonal mean wind speed of the northeasterlies relative to preindustrial conditions is as much as 10–20&#160;%. Such a percentage increase of wind speed in a storm translates into an increase of storm power dissipation by a factor ∼1.4–2, because wind power dissipation is proportional to the cube of wind speed. However, the simulated changes refer to seasonal mean winds averaged over large grid-boxes, not individual storms.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observations">Observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_and_earlier">2010 and earlier</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2010 and earlier">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 2004, the <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">hypothesis</a> that the Gulf Stream is switching off received a boost when a retrospective analysis of U.S. satellite data seemed to show a slowing of the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Gyre" title="North Atlantic Gyre">North Atlantic Gyre</a>, the northern swirl of the Gulf Stream.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2005, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Wadhams" title="Peter Wadhams">Peter Wadhams</a> reported in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> (London) about the results of investigations in a submarine under the Arctic ice sheet measuring the giant chimneys of cold dense water, in which the cold dense water normally sinks down to the <a href="/wiki/Sea_bed" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea bed">sea bed</a> and is replaced by warm water, forming one of the engines of the North Atlantic Drift. He and his team found the chimneys to have virtually disappeared. Normally there are seven to twelve giant columns, but Wadhams found only two giant columns, both extremely weak.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005 a 30% reduction in the warm currents that carry water north from the Gulf Stream was observed, from the last such measurement in 1992.  The authors noted uncertainties in the measurements.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Following media discussions, Detlef Quadfasel pointed out that the uncertainty of the estimates of Bryden <i>et al.</i> is high, but says other factors and observations do support their results, and implications based on <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">palaeoclimate</a> records show drops of air temperature up to 10&#160;°C within decades, linked to abrupt switches of ocean circulation when a certain threshold is reached. He concluded that further observations and modelling are crucial for providing early warning of a possible devastating breakdown of the circulation.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In response Quirin Schiermeier concluded that natural variation was the culprit for the observations but highlighted possible implications.<sup id="cite_ref-Schiermeier2007_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schiermeier2007-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008, Vage et al. reported "the return of deep convection to the subpolar gyre in both the Labrador and Irminger seas in the winter of 2007–2008," employing "profiling float data from the <a href="/wiki/Argo_(oceanography)" title="Argo (oceanography)">Argo program</a> to document deep mixing," and "a variety of in situ, satellite and reanalysis data" to set the context for the phenomenon. This might have a lot to do with the observations of variations in cold water chimney behaviour.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2010, the Gulf Stream briefly connected with the <a href="/wiki/West_Greenland_Current" title="West Greenland Current">West Greenland Current</a> after fluctuating for a few weeks due to an extreme negative phase of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_oscillation" title="Arctic oscillation">Arctic oscillation</a>, temporarily diverting it west of Greenland.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermohaline_circulation_and_fresh_water">Thermohaline circulation and fresh water</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Thermohaline circulation and fresh water">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:95307main_fig4m.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/95307main_fig4m.jpg/220px-95307main_fig4m.jpg" width="220" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/95307main_fig4m.jpg/330px-95307main_fig4m.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/95307main_fig4m.jpg/440px-95307main_fig4m.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="517" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:95307main_fig4m.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The red end of the spectrum indicates slowing in this presentation of the trend of velocities derived from NASA Pathfinder altimeter data from May 1992 to June 2002. Source: <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Heat is transported from the equator <a href="/wiki/Geographical_pole" title="Geographical pole">polewards</a> mostly by the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> but also by <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean currents</a>, with warm water near the surface and cold water at deeper levels. The best known segment of this circulation is the Gulf Stream, a wind-driven <a href="/wiki/Ocean_gyre" title="Ocean gyre">gyre</a>, which transports warm water from the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> northwards. A northwards branch of the Gulf Stream, the North Atlantic Drift, is part of the <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a> (THC), transporting warmth further north to the North Atlantic, where its effect in warming the atmosphere contributes to warming Europe. Other factors are also important, such as <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_wave" title="Atmospheric wave">atmospheric waves</a> that bring subtropical air further north, which have been suggested to influence the <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_the_British_Isles" title="Climate of the British Isles">climate of the British Isles</a> more than the Gulf Stream.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a> of ocean water in the North Atlantic increases the <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> of the water as well as cooling it, both actions increasing the density of water at the surface. Formation of <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">sea ice</a> further increases the salinity and density, because salt is ejected into the ocean when sea ice forms.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> This dense water then sinks and the circulation stream continues in a southerly direction. However, the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Meridional_Overturning_Circulation" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation">Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation</a> (AMOC) is driven by ocean temperature and salinity differences. But freshwater decreases ocean water salinity, and through this process prevents colder waters sinking. This mechanism possibly caused the cold ocean surface temperature anomaly currently observed near Greenland (<a href="/wiki/Cold_blob_(North_Atlantic)" title="Cold blob (North Atlantic)">Cold blob (North Atlantic)</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-WP2015Sep_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WP2015Sep-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Global warming could lead to an increase in <a href="/wiki/Freshwater" class="mw-redirect" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> in the northern oceans, by melting <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, and by increasing <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>, especially through Siberian rivers.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An AMOC shutdown may be able to trigger the type of abrupt massive temperature shifts which occurred during the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glacial period</a>: a series of <a href="/wiki/Dansgaard-Oeschger_event" class="mw-redirect" title="Dansgaard-Oeschger event">Dansgaard-Oeschger events</a> – rapid climate fluctuations – may be attributed to freshwater forcing at high latitude interrupting the THC. 2002 model runs in which the THC is forced to shut down do show cooling – locally up to 8&#160;°C (14&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Studies of the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Current" title="Florida Current">Florida Current</a> suggest that the Gulf Stream weakens with cooling, being weakest (by ~10%) during the <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subpolar_gyre">Subpolar gyre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Subpolar gyre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Recent studies (2017) suggest potential convection collapse (heat transport) of the <a href="/wiki/Subpolar_gyre" class="mw-redirect" title="Subpolar gyre">subpolar gyre</a> in the North Atlantic, resulting in rapid cooling, with implications for economic sectors, agriculture industry, water resources and energy management in Western Europe and the East Coast of the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Frajka-Williams et al. 2017 pointed out that recent changes in cooling of the subpolar gyre, warm temperatures in the subtropics and cool anomalies over the tropics, increased the spatial distribution of meridional gradient in sea surface temperatures, which is not captured by the <a href="/wiki/AMO_Index" class="mw-redirect" title="AMO Index">AMO Index</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Frajka-Williams_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frajka-Williams-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="IPCC_models">IPCC models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: IPCC models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Based on coupled <a href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Global climate model">Atmosphere-Ocean General Circulation Models</a> from 2001, the THC tends to weaken somewhat rather than stop, and the warming effects outweigh the cooling, even over Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-TAR_WG1_Ch9_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TAR_WG1_Ch9-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> In the IPCC Fifth Assessment Report, it was reported that it is <i>very unlikely</i> that the AMOC will undergo a rapid transition (<i>high confidence</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Link to original source broken (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
