<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Global warming in popular culture</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The issue of <b><a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a></b>, its possible effects, and related <a href="/wiki/Environmental_sociology" title="Environmental sociology">human-environment interaction</a> have entered <b><a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">popular culture</a></b> since the late 20th century.
</p><p>Science historian <a href="/wiki/Naomi_Oreskes" title="Naomi Oreskes">Naomi Oreskes</a> has noted, "There's a huge disconnect between what professional scientists have studied and learned in the last 30 years, and what is out there in the popular culture."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  An academic study contrasts the relatively rapid acceptance of <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a> as reflected in popular culture with the much slower acceptance of the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">scientific consensus on global warming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fiction">Fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Climate_change_novels" title="Category:Climate change novels">Category:Climate change novels</a></div>
<ul><li>British author <a href="/wiki/J._G._Ballard" title="J. G. Ballard">J. G. Ballard</a> used the setting of apocalyptic climate changes in his first science fiction novels. In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wind_from_Nowhere" title="The Wind from Nowhere">The Wind from Nowhere</a></i> (1961) civilisation is reduced by persistent hurricane-force winds. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Drowned_World" title="The Drowned World">The Drowned World</a></i> (1962) describes a future of melted ice-caps and rising sea-levels, caused by solar radiation, creating a landscape mirroring the <a href="/wiki/Collective_unconscious" title="Collective unconscious">collective unconscious</a> desires of the main characters. In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Burning_World_(novel)" title="The Burning World (novel)">The Burning World</a></i> (1964) a surrealistic psychological landscape is formed by drought due to industrial pollution disrupting the <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a> cycle.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Mother_of_Storms" title="Mother of Storms">Mother of Storms</a></i> (1994) by <a href="/wiki/John_Barnes_(author)" title="John Barnes (author)">John Barnes</a> describes a catastrophic, rapid climate and weather change brought on by a nuclear explosion releasing <a href="/wiki/Clathrate_compound" title="Clathrate compound">clathrate compounds</a> from the ocean floor, based on the <a href="/wiki/Clathrate_gun_hypothesis" title="Clathrate gun hypothesis">clathrate gun hypothesis</a>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Carbon_Diaries:_2015" title="The Carbon Diaries: 2015">The Carbon Diaries: 2015</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Saci_Lloyd" title="Saci Lloyd">Saci Lloyd</a> is set in a future where power is scarce and the <a href="/wiki/UK" class="mw-redirect" title="UK">UK</a> has just begun carbon rationing.  The story is told in diary form by Laura Brown, a teenager living in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> in the aftermath of The Great Storm.</li>
<li><i>Far North</i> (2009) by <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Theroux" title="Marcel Theroux">Marcel Theroux</a>, in which the world is largely uninhabitable due to climate change. However, the novel implies that scientists got it wrong and that it were our actions combating global warming that irrevocably altered the climate.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Arctic_Drift" title="Arctic Drift">Arctic Drift</a></i> (2008) by <a href="/wiki/Clive_Cussler" title="Clive Cussler">Clive Cussler</a> and Dirk Cussler.  A <a href="/wiki/Thriller_(genre)" title="Thriller (genre)">thriller</a> involving attempts to reverse global warming, a possible war between the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, and “a mysterious silvery mineral traced to a long-ago expedition in search of the fabled Northwest Passage.”<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Fallen_Angels_(science_fiction_novel)" title="Fallen Angels (science fiction novel)">Fallen Angels</a></i> (1991) by <a href="/wiki/Larry_Niven" title="Larry Niven">Larry Niven</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Pournelle" title="Jerry Pournelle">Jerry Pournelle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Flynn_(author)" class="mw-redirect" title="Michael Flynn (author)">Michael Flynn</a>.  Set in North America in the "near future", a radical <a href="/wiki/Technophobic" class="mw-redirect" title="Technophobic">technophobic</a> <a href="/wiki/Green" title="Green">green</a> movement dramatically cuts greenhouse gas emissions, only to find that manmade global warming was staving off a new <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Forty_Signs_of_Rain" title="Forty Signs of Rain">Forty Signs of Rain</a></i> (2004), <a href="/wiki/Fifty_Degrees_Below" title="Fifty Degrees Below">Fifty Degrees Below</a> (2005), and <a href="/wiki/Sixty_Days_and_Counting" title="Sixty Days and Counting">Sixty Days and Counting</a> (2007) comprise the <a href="/wiki/Science_In_The_Capitol" class="mw-redirect" title="Science In The Capitol">Science in the Capital</a> series, a <a href="/wiki/Hard_science_fiction" title="Hard science fiction">hard science fiction</a> <a href="/wiki/Trilogy" title="Trilogy">trilogy</a> by <a href="/wiki/Kim_Stanley_Robinson" title="Kim Stanley Robinson">Kim Stanley Robinson</a>.  Set primarily in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abrupt_climate_change" title="Abrupt climate change">abrupt climate change</a> causes weather disasters in the US capitol and flooding of the fictional island nation of Khembalung.  Main characters are American scientists, politicians, and <a href="/wiki/Buddhist" class="mw-redirect" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> monks.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Solar_(novel)" title="Solar (novel)">Solar</a></i> (2010) by <a href="/wiki/Ian_McEwan" title="Ian McEwan">Ian McEwan</a> follows the story of a physicist who discovers a way to fight climate change after managing to derive power from artificial photosynthesis.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/State_of_Fear" title="State of Fear">State of Fear</a></i> (2004) a <a href="/wiki/Techno-thriller" title="Techno-thriller">techno-thriller</a> by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Crichton" title="Michael Crichton">Michael Crichton</a> concerning a group of <a href="/wiki/Eco-terrorism" title="Eco-terrorism">eco-terrorists</a> attempting to create "natural" disasters to convince the public of the dangers of global warming. The book is critical of the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_opinion_on_climate_change" title="Scientific opinion on climate change">Scientific opinion on climate change</a> and accusing its proponents of using fear tactics.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Stone_Gods_(novel)" title="The Stone Gods (novel)">The Stone Gods</a></i> (2007) by <a href="/wiki/Jeanette_Winterson" title="Jeanette Winterson">Jeanette Winterson</a>.  This novel opens on the planet Orbus, a world very like Earth, running out of resources and suffering from the severe effects of climate change.  Inhabitants of Orbus hope to take advantage of possibilities offered by a newly discovered planet, Planet Blue, which appears perfect for human life.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Tipping Point</i> (2010) by Simon Rosser. A climate fiction action-thriller.</li>
<li><i>The Current</i> (2014) by Yannick Thoraval is a novel about how greed and vanity contribute to global climate change.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theater">Theater</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Theater">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Contingency_Plan" title="The Contingency Plan">The Contingency Plan</a></i> (2009) by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Waters" title="Steve Waters">Steve Waters</a> is a diptych of plays first performed at the Bush Theatre in London. They are set in the near future, at a time during which severe tidal surges begin to submerge parts of coastal Britain.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Years_of_Living_Dangerously" title="Years of Living Dangerously">Years of Living Dangerously</a></i>, nine-part 2014 Showtime documentary television series</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_World_Set_Free_(Cosmos:_A_Spacetime_Odyssey)" title="The World Set Free (Cosmos: A Spacetime Odyssey)">The World Set Free (Cosmos: A Spacetime Odyssey)</a>, 2014 TV series episode</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/South_Park" title="South Park">South Park</a></i> spoofed global warming in five episodes: "<a href="/wiki/Two_Days_Before_the_Day_After_Tomorrow" title="Two Days Before the Day After Tomorrow">Two Days Before the Day After Tomorrow</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Spontaneous_Combustion_(South_Park)" title="Spontaneous Combustion (South Park)">Spontaneous Combustion</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Goobacks" title="Goobacks">Goobacks</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Smug_Alert!" title="Smug Alert!">Smug Alert!</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/ManBearPig" title="ManBearPig">ManBearPig</a>".</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Next_Generation" title="Star Trek: The Next Generation">Star Trek: The Next Generation</a></i> had two such global-warming themed episodes:
<ul><li>Episode "<a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9j%C3%A0_Q" title="Déjà Q">Déjà Q</a>" (1990) - The crew suggests an artificial amplification of global warming using <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> to counter the cooling effects of dust from the impact of a moon on a planet.</li>
<li>Episode "<a href="/wiki/A_Matter_of_Time_(Star_Trek:_The_Next_Generation)" title="A Matter of Time (Star Trek: The Next Generation)">A Matter of Time</a>" (Season 5 EP 9) - A passing cloud of dust from an asteroid causes global cooling on a planet, the crew of the enterprise use a phaser to release frozen deposits of CO2 on the planet.</li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/The_Inner_Light_(Star_Trek:_The_Next_Generation)" title="The Inner Light (Star Trek: The Next Generation)">The Inner Light</a>" (1992) - <a href="/wiki/Jean-Luc_Picard" title="Jean-Luc Picard">Jean-Luc Picard</a> lives a lifetime on a planet experiencing Global Warming and <a href="/wiki/Aridification" title="Aridification">aridification</a>. Ultimately, the climate change becomes serious enough to threaten all life on the planet. This <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award" title="Hugo Award">Hugo Award</a> winner is among the 5 most popular out of all 178 episodes in the TNG series.</li></ul></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Teenage_Mutant_Ninja_Turtles_(1987_TV_series)" title="Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles (1987 TV series)">1987 <i>Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles</i> cartoon</a> has four episodes dealing with global warming. In <i><a href="/wiki/Shredder%27s_Mom" class="mw-redirect" title="Shredder&#39;s Mom">Shredder's Mom</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Shredder_(Teenage_Mutant_Ninja_Turtles)" title="Shredder (Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles)">Shredder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Krang" title="Krang">Krang</a> use a mirror fixed to a satellite to warm up the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> if the political leaders do not surrender to them. The <a href="/wiki/Teenage_Mutant_Ninja_Turtles" title="Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles">Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles</a> get help from General Yogure to stop them. In <i><a href="/wiki/Northern_Lights_Out" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Lights Out">Northern Lights Out</a></i>, a man named Eric Red in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> plans to melt the <a href="/wiki/Polar_ice_cap" title="Polar ice cap">polar ice cap</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flood</a> all the coastal <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">cities</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> by blowing up underground volcanoes, which will make it "easy" for Eric Red and his gang to take over the Earth. In <i><a href="/wiki/A_Real_Snow_Job" class="mw-redirect" title="A Real Snow Job">A Real Snow Job</a></i>, set in the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> in <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, Krang and Shredder use a <i>Zoetropic wave</i> device to melt the worlds' ice, <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a> the coastal <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">cities</a> and making the Earth easy for Krang and Shredder to take over. In <i>Too Hot to Handle</i>, Vernon Fenwick's nephew Foster has an invention that brings the Earth closer to the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, a "Solar Magnet".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Transformers_(TV_series)" title="The Transformers (TV series)">The 1980s <i>Transformers</i> animated series</a> had at least one global-warming themed episode: "The Revenge of Bruticus". There, the Combaticons (a faction of the series' main villains, the Decepticons, created by rebel Decepticon <a href="/wiki/Starscream_(Transformers)" class="mw-redirect" title="Starscream (Transformers)">Starscream</a>) use the Space Bridge device to hurl Earth toward the Sun, hoping to destroy the Earth and all enemies. The Autobots are forced to help the humans endure the heat while putting aside their differences with the Decepticons in a race against time to restore Earth to its natural orbit.</li>
<li>The TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Utopia_(UK_TV_series)" title="Utopia (UK TV series)">Utopia</a></i> is a violent thriller about a fictional <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_(criminal)" title="Conspiracy (criminal)">conspiracy</a> that has a number of secret agents embedded in key places in government and industry. The conspiracy, known as "The Network", seeks to frighten the populace into taking a <a href="/wiki/Vaccine" title="Vaccine">vaccine</a> which will, as a side-effect, cause mass <a href="/wiki/Infertility" title="Infertility">infertility</a>. Their aim in doing so is to reduce the number of humans on the planet, in order to tackle climate change, resource shortages and other environmental issues.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comic_books">Comic books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Comic books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Teenage_Mutant_Ninja_Turtles_Adventures" title="Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles Adventures">Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles Adventures</a></i> from <a href="/wiki/Archie_Comics" title="Archie Comics">Archie Comics</a>.  Set in their present (1980s/1990s), but also including time travels to a future, in which <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> is flooded because of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_popular_culture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Civilization_II" title="Civilization II">Civilization II</a></i> is a strategy game released in 1996, in which the pollution created by industrial production and transportation, if left unchecked, leads to desertification.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Fuel_(2009_video_game)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fuel (2009 video game)">Fuel</a></i> (2009) is a racing video game set in a post-apocalyptic world ravaged by <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> fueled by global warming.</li>
<li>In 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Tamagotchi" title="Tamagotchi">TamaTown</a> website featured a game that taught children how to prevent global warming.</li></ul>
