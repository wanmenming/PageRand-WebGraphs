<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Jan Baptist van Helmont</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>Jan Baptist van Helmont</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;h&#39; in &#39;hi&#39;">h</span><span title="/ɛ/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">ɛ</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ɒ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;body&#39;">ɒ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <small>Dutch:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Dutch" title="Help:IPA/Dutch">[ˈɦɛlmɔnt]</a></span>; 12 January 1580&#160;– 30 December 1644) was a <a href="/wiki/Flemish_people" title="Flemish people">Flemish</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physiologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Physiologist">physiologist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physician</a>. He worked during the years just after <a href="/wiki/Paracelsus" title="Paracelsus">Paracelsus</a> and the rise of <a href="/wiki/Iatrochemistry" title="Iatrochemistry">iatrochemistry</a>, and is sometimes considered to be "the founder of <a href="/wiki/Pneumatic_chemistry" title="Pneumatic chemistry">pneumatic chemistry</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Holmyard_1931_121_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holmyard_1931_121-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Van Helmont is remembered today largely for his ideas on spontaneous generation, his 5-year tree experiment, and his  introduction of the word "<a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a>" (from the Greek word <i>chaos</i>) into the vocabulary of science.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jan_Baptist_van_Helmont&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Van Helmont was the youngest of five children of Maria (van) Stassaert and Christiaen van Helmont, a public prosecutor and Brussels council member, who had married in the <a href="/wiki/St._Michael_and_Gudula_Cathedral" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Michael and Gudula Cathedral">Sint-Goedele</a> church in 1567.<sup id="cite_ref-leuven_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leuven-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He was educated at <a href="/wiki/Old_University_of_Leuven" title="Old University of Leuven">Leuven</a>, and after ranging restlessly from one science to another and finding satisfaction in none, turned to medicine. He interrupted his studies, and for a few years he traveled through <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, and <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.
</p><p>Returning to his own country, van Helmont obtained a medical degree in 1599.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He practiced at Antwerp at the time of the great plague in 1605. In 1609 he finally obtained his doctoral degree in medicine. The same year he married Margaret van Ranst, who was of a wealthy noble family. Van Helmont and Margaret lived in <a href="/wiki/Vilvoorde" title="Vilvoorde">Vilvoorde</a>, near Brussels, and had six or seven children.<sup id="cite_ref-leuven_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leuven-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The inheritance of his wife enabled him to retire early from his medical practice and occupy himself with chemical experiments until his death on 30 December 1644.
</p>

<p>Van Helmont is regarded as the founder of <a href="/wiki/Pneumatic_chemistry" title="Pneumatic chemistry">pneumatic chemistry</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Holmyard_1931_121_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holmyard_1931_121-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> as he was the first to understand that there are gases distinct in kind from atmospheric air and furthermore invented the word "<a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He perceived that his "gas sylvestre" (<a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>) given off by burning charcoal, was the same as that produced by <a href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermenting</a> <a href="/wiki/Must" title="Must">must</a>, which sometimes renders the air of caves unbreathable. For Van Helmont, <a href="/wiki/Air" class="mw-redirect" title="Air">air</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> were the two primitive elements. Fire he explicitly denied to be an <a href="/wiki/Classical_element" title="Classical element">element</a>, and earth is not one because it can be reduced to water.
</p><p>On the one hand, Van Helmont was a disciple of <a href="/wiki/Paracelsus" title="Paracelsus">Paracelsus</a> (though he scornfully repudiated his errors as well as those of most other contemporary authorities), a mystic and <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemist</a>. On the other hand, he engaged in the <a href="/wiki/New_learning" class="mw-redirect" title="New learning">new learning</a> based on <a href="/wiki/Experiment" title="Experiment">experimentation</a> that was producing men like <a href="/wiki/William_Harvey" title="William Harvey">William Harvey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon" title="Francis Bacon">Francis Bacon</a>. Van Helmont was a careful observer of <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">nature</a>; his analysis of data gathered in his experiments suggests that he had a concept of the conservation of mass. He performed an experiment to determine where plants get their mass. He grew a willow tree and measured the amount of soil, the weight of the tree and the water he added. After five years the plant had gained about 164&#160;lbs (74&#160;kg).  Since the amount of soil was basically the same as it had been when he started his experiment (it lost only 57 grams), he deduced that the tree's weight gain had come from water. Since it had received nothing but water and the soil weighed practically the same as at the beginning, he argued that the increased weight of wood, bark and roots had been formed from water.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_and_philosophical_opinions">Religious and philosophical opinions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jan_Baptist_van_Helmont&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Religious and philosophical opinions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although a faithful Catholic, he incurred the suspicion of the Church by his tract <i>De magnetica vulnerum curatione</i> (1621), against <a href="/wiki/Jean_Roberti" title="Jean Roberti">Jean Roberti</a>, which was thought to derogate from some of the miracles. His works were collected and edited by his son <a href="/wiki/Franciscus_Mercurius_van_Helmont" title="Franciscus Mercurius van Helmont">Franciscus Mercurius van Helmont</a> and published by <a href="/wiki/Lodewijk_Elzevir" title="Lodewijk Elzevir">Lodewijk Elzevir</a> in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a> as <i>Ortus medicinae, vel opera et opuscula omnia</i> ("The Origin of Medicine, or Complete Works") in 1648.<sup id="cite_ref-Roberts_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roberts-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <i>Ortus medicinae</i> was based on, but not restricted to, the material of  <i>Dageraad ofte Nieuwe Opkomst der Geneeskunst</i> ("Daybreak, or the New Rise of Medicine"), which was published in 1644 in Van Helmont's native Dutch. His son Frans's writings, <i>Cabbalah Denudata</i> (1677) and <i>Opuscula philosophica</i> (1690) are a mixture of theosophy, mysticism and alchemy.
</p><p>Over and above the <i><a href="/wiki/Archeus" title="Archeus">archeus</a></i>, he believed that there is the sensitive <a href="/wiki/Soul" title="Soul">soul</a> which is the husk or shell of the immortal mind. Before <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Man" class="mw-redirect" title="Fall of Man">the Fall</a> the archeus obeyed the immortal mind and was directly controlled by it, but at the Fall men also received the sensitive soul and with it lost immortality, for when it perishes the immortal mind can no longer remain in the body.
</p><p>Van Helmont described the <i>archeus</i> as "aura vitalis seminum, vitae directrix" ("The chief Workman [Archeus] consists of the conjoyning of the vitall air, as of the matter, with the seminal likeness, which is the more inward spiritual kernel, containing the fruitfulness of the Seed; but the visible Seed is onely the husk of this.").<sup id="cite_ref-oriatrike_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oriatrike-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to the archeus, van Helmont believed in other governing agencies resembling the archeus which were not always clearly distinguished from it. From these he invented the term <i>blas</i> (motion), defined as the "vis motus tam alterivi quam localis" ("twofold motion, to wit, locall, and alterative"), that is, natural motion and motion that can be altered or voluntary. Of<i> blas</i> there were several kinds, e.g. blas humanum (blas of humans), blas of stars and blas meteoron (blas of meteors); of meteors he said "constare gas materiâ et blas efficiente" ("Meteors do consist of their matter Gas, and their efficient cause Blas, as well the Motive, as the altering").<sup id="cite_ref-oriatrike_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oriatrike-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Van Helmont "had frequent visions throughout his life and laid great stress upon them".<sup id="cite_ref-moon_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moon-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> His choice of a medical profession has been attributed to a conversation with the angel Raphael.,<sup id="cite_ref-jensen_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jensen-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and some of his writings described imagination as a celestial, and possibly magical, force.<sup id="cite_ref-Clericuzio_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clericuzio-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Though Van Helmont was skeptical of specific mystical theories and practices, he refused to discount magical forces as explanations for certain natural phenomena. This stance, reflected in a 1621 paper on sympathetic principles,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> may have contributed to his prosecution and subsequent house arrest.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observations_on_digestion">Observations on digestion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jan_Baptist_van_Helmont&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Observations on digestion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Van Helmont wrote extensively on the subject of digestion. In <i>Oriatrike or Physick Refined</i> (1662, an English translation of <i>Ortus medicinae</i>), van Helmont considered earlier ideas on the subject, such as food being digested through the body's internal heat. But if that were so, he asked, how could cold-blooded animals live? His own opinion was that digestion was aided by a chemical reagent, or "ferment", within the body, such as inside the stomach. Harré suggests that van Helmont's theory was "very near to our modern concept of an enzyme".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Van Helmont proposed and described six different stages of digestion.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disputed_portrait">Disputed portrait</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jan_Baptist_van_Helmont&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Disputed portrait">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2003, the historian <a href="/wiki/Lisa_Jardine" title="Lisa Jardine">Lisa Jardine</a> proposed that a portrait held in the collections of the <a href="/wiki/Natural_History_Museum,_London" title="Natural History Museum, London">Natural History Museum, London</a>, traditionally identified as <a href="/wiki/John_Ray" title="John Ray">John Ray</a>, might represent <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hooke" title="Robert Hooke">Robert Hooke</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-lisa_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lisa-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Jardine's hypothesis was subsequently disproved by William Jensen of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cincinnati" title="University of Cincinnati">University of Cincinnati</a><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and by the German researcher Andreas Pechtl of <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Gutenberg_University_of_Mainz" class="mw-redirect" title="Johannes Gutenberg University of Mainz">Johannes Gutenberg University of Mainz</a>, who showed that the portrait in fact depicts van Helmont.
</p>

<li>The <a href="/wiki/Moldovans" title="Moldovans">Moldavian</a> prince and scholar, <a href="/wiki/Dimitrie_Cantemir" title="Dimitrie Cantemir">Dimitrie Cantemir</a>, wrote a biography of Helmont, which is now difficult to locate. It is cited in Debus, Allen G. (2002) <i>The Chemical Philosophy: Paracelsian science and medicine in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries</i>. Courier Dover Publications, <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r861714446"/><a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Book_Number" title="International Standard Book Number">ISBN</a>&#160;<a href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0486421759" title="Special:BookSources/0486421759">0486421759</a> on pages 311 and 312, as Catemir, Dimitri (Demetrius) (1709); Ioannis Baptistae Van Helmont physices universalis doctrine et christianae fidei congrua et necessaria philosophia. Wallachia. Debus refers to a suggestion of his colleague William H. McNeill for this information and cites Badaru, Dan (1964); Filozofia lui Dilmitrie Cantemir. Editura Academici Republicii Popular Romine, Bucharest pages 394–410 for further information. Debus further remarks that the work of Cantemir contains merely a paraphrase and selection of "Ortus Medicinae", but it made the views of van Helmont available to Eastern Europe.</li>
