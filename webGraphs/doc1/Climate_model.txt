<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Climate model</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about the theories and mathematics of climate modeling. For computer-driven prediction of Earth's climate, see <a href="/wiki/General_circulation_model" title="General circulation model">General circulation model</a>.</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For a broader coverage of this topic, see <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_model" title="Atmospheric model">Atmospheric model</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Global_Climate_Model.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Global_Climate_Model.png/350px-Global_Climate_Model.png" width="350" height="231" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Global_Climate_Model.png/525px-Global_Climate_Model.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Global_Climate_Model.png/700px-Global_Climate_Model.png 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="660" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Global_Climate_Model.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Climate models are systems of <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equations</a> based on the basic laws of <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid motion</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>. To “run” a model, scientists divide the planet into a 3-dimensional grid, apply the basic equations, and evaluate the results. Atmospheric models calculate <a href="/wiki/Winds" class="mw-redirect" title="Winds">winds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">heat transfer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a>, and surface <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a> within each grid and evaluate interactions with neighboring points.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Climate models</b> use <a href="/wiki/Quantitative_method" class="mw-redirect" title="Quantitative method">quantitative methods</a> to simulate the interactions of the important drivers of climate, including <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth&#39;s atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Land_surface" class="mw-redirect" title="Land surface">land surface</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cryosphere" title="Cryosphere">ice</a>. They are used for a variety of purposes from study of the dynamics of the climate system to projections of future <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>.
</p><p>All climate models take account of incoming <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> from the sun as short wave <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">electromagnetic radiation</a>, chiefly <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible</a> and short-wave (near) <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a>, as well as outgoing long wave (far) <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> electromagnetic. Any imbalance results in a <a href="/wiki/First_law_of_thermodynamics" title="First law of thermodynamics">change in temperature</a>.
</p><p>Models vary in complexity:
</p>
<ul><li>A simple <a href="/wiki/Radiant_heat" class="mw-redirect" title="Radiant heat">radiant heat</a> transfer model treats the earth as a single point and averages outgoing energy</li>
<li>This can be expanded vertically (radiative-convective models) and/or horizontally</li>
<li>Finally, (coupled) atmosphere–ocean–<a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">sea ice</a> <b><a href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Global climate model">global climate models</a></b> solve the full equations for mass and <a href="/wiki/Energy_transfer" class="mw-redirect" title="Energy transfer">energy</a> transfer and radiant exchange.</li>
<li>Box models can treat flows across and within ocean basins.</li>
<li>Other types of modelling can be interlinked, such as <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a>, allowing researchers to predict the interaction between climate and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystems" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecosystems">ecosystems</a>.</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_models">Box models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Box models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Box models</b> are simplified versions of complex systems, reducing them to boxes (or <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoirs</a>) linked by fluxes. The boxes are assumed to be mixed homogeneously. Within a given box, the concentration of any <a href="/wiki/Chemical_species" title="Chemical species">chemical species</a> is therefore uniform. However, the abundance of a species within a given box may vary as a function of time due to the input to (or loss from) the box or due to the production, consumption or decay of this species within the box.
</p><p>Simple box models, i.e. box model with a small number of boxes whose properties (e.g. their volume) do not change with time, are often useful to derive analytical formulas describing the dynamics and steady-state abundance of a species. More complex box models are usually solved using numerical techniques.
</p><p>Box models are used extensively to model environmental systems or ecosystems and in studies of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_circulation" class="mw-redirect" title="Ocean circulation">ocean circulation</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sarmiento1984_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sarmiento1984-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>This yields an apparent effective average earth temperature of 288&#160;<a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">K</a> (15&#160;<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">°C</a>; 59&#160;<a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">°F</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This is because the above equation represents the effective <i>radiative</i> temperature of the Earth (including the clouds and atmosphere). The use of effective emissivity and albedo account for the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>.
</p><p>This very simple model is quite instructive, and the only model that could fit on a page. For example, it easily determines the effect on average earth temperature of changes in solar constant or change of albedo or effective earth emissivity.
</p><p>The average emissivity of the earth is readily estimated from available data. The emissivities of terrestrial surfaces are all in the range of 0.96 to 0.99<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> (except for some small desert areas which may be as low as 0.7). Clouds, however, which cover about half of the earth’s surface, have an average emissivity of about 0.5<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> (which must be reduced by the fourth power of the ratio of cloud absolute temperature to average earth absolute temperature) and an average cloud temperature of about 258&#160;K (−15&#160;°C; 5&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Taking all this properly into account results in an effective earth emissivity of about 0.64 (earth average temperature 285&#160;K (12&#160;°C; 53&#160;°F)).
</p><p>This simple model readily determines the effect of changes in solar output or change of earth albedo or effective earth emissivity on average earth temperature. It says nothing, however about what might cause these things to change. Zero-dimensional models do not address the temperature distribution on the earth or the factors that move energy about the earth.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Radiative-convective_models">Radiative-convective models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Radiative-convective models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The zero-dimensional model above, using the solar constant and given average earth temperature, determines the effective earth emissivity of long wave radiation emitted to space. This can be refined in the vertical to a one-dimensional radiative-convective model, which considers two processes of energy transport:
</p>
<ul><li>upwelling and downwelling radiative transfer through atmospheric layers that both absorb and emit infrared radiation</li>
<li>upward transport of heat by convection (especially important in the lower <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>).</li></ul>
<p>The radiative-convective models have advantages over the simple model: they can determine the effects of varying <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> concentrations on effective emissivity and therefore the surface temperature. But added parameters are needed to determine local emissivity and albedo and address the factors that move energy about the earth.
</p><p>Effect of ice-albedo feedback on global sensitivity in a one-dimensional radiative-convective climate model.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Higher-dimension_models">Higher-dimension models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Higher-dimension models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The zero-dimensional model may be expanded to consider the energy transported horizontally in the atmosphere. This kind of model may well be <a href="/wiki/Zonal_and_meridional" title="Zonal and meridional">zonally</a> averaged. This model has the advantage of allowing a rational dependence of local albedo and emissivity on temperature – the poles can be allowed to be icy and the equator warm – but the lack of true dynamics means that horizontal transports have to be specified.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="EMICs_.28Earth-system_models_of_intermediate_complexity.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="EMICs_(Earth-system_models_of_intermediate_complexity)">EMICs (Earth-system models of intermediate complexity)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: EMICs (Earth-system models of intermediate complexity)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Depending on the nature of questions asked and the pertinent time scales, there are, on the one extreme,  conceptual, more inductive models, and, on the other extreme, <a href="/wiki/General_circulation_model" title="General circulation model">general circulation models</a> operating at the highest spatial and temporal resolution currently feasible. Models of intermediate complexity bridge the gap. One example is the Climber-3 model. Its atmosphere is a 2.5-dimensional statistical-dynamical model with 7.5° × 22.5° resolution and time step of half a day;  the ocean is MOM-3 (<a href="/wiki/Modular_Ocean_Model" title="Modular Ocean Model">Modular Ocean Model</a>) with a 3.75° × 3.75° grid and 24 vertical levels.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="GCMs_.28global_climate_models_or_general_circulation_models.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="GCMs_(global_climate_models_or_general_circulation_models)">GCMs (global climate models or general circulation models)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: GCMs (global climate models or general circulation models)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/General_Circulation_Model" class="mw-redirect" title="General Circulation Model">General Circulation Model</a></div>
<p>General Circulation Models (GCMs) discretise the equations for fluid motion and energy transfer and integrate these over time. Unlike simpler models, GCMs divide the atmosphere and/or oceans into grids of discrete "cells", which represent computational units. Unlike simpler models which make mixing assumptions, processes internal to a cell—such as convection—that occur on scales too small to be resolved directly are parameterised at the cell level, while other functions govern the interface between cells.
</p><p>Atmospheric GCMs (AGCMs) model the atmosphere and impose <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a> as boundary conditions. Coupled atmosphere-ocean GCMs (AOGCMs, e.g. <a href="/wiki/HadCM3" title="HadCM3">HadCM3</a>, <a href="/wiki/EdGCM" title="EdGCM">EdGCM</a>, <a href="/wiki/GFDL_CM2.X" class="mw-redirect" title="GFDL CM2.X">GFDL CM2.X</a>, ARPEGE-Climat)<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> combine the two models. The first general circulation climate model that combined both oceanic and atmospheric processes was developed in the late 1960s at the <a href="/wiki/NOAA" class="mw-redirect" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> <a href="/wiki/Geophysical_Fluid_Dynamics_Laboratory" title="Geophysical Fluid Dynamics Laboratory">Geophysical Fluid Dynamics Laboratory</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> AOGCMs represent the pinnacle of complexity in climate models and internalise as many processes as possible. However, they are still under development and uncertainties remain.  They may be coupled to models of other processes, such as the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>, so as to better model feedback effects. Such integrated multi-system models are sometimes referred to as either "earth system models" or "global climate models."
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_and_development">Research and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Research and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are three major types of institution where climate models are developed, implemented and used:
</p>
<ul><li>National meteorological services. Most national weather services have a <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatology</a> section.</li>
<li>Universities. Relevant departments include atmospheric sciences, meteorology, climatology, and geography.</li>
<li>National and international research laboratories. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Atmospheric_Research" title="National Center for Atmospheric Research">National Center for Atmospheric Research</a> (NCAR, in <a href="/wiki/Boulder,_Colorado" title="Boulder, Colorado">Boulder, Colorado</a>, USA), the <a href="/wiki/Geophysical_Fluid_Dynamics_Laboratory" title="Geophysical Fluid Dynamics Laboratory">Geophysical Fluid Dynamics Laboratory</a> (GFDL, in <a href="/wiki/Princeton,_New_Jersey" title="Princeton, New Jersey">Princeton, New Jersey</a>, USA), the <a href="/wiki/Hadley_Centre_for_Climate_Prediction_and_Research" title="Hadley Centre for Climate Prediction and Research">Hadley Centre for Climate Prediction and Research</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a>, UK), the <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Institute_for_Meteorology" title="Max Planck Institute for Meteorology">Max Planck Institute for Meteorology</a> in Hamburg, Germany, or the <a href="/wiki/Laboratoire_des_sciences_du_climat_et_de_l%27environnement" title="Laboratoire des sciences du climat et de l&#39;environnement">Laboratoire des Sciences du Climat et de l'Environnement</a> (LSCE), France, to name but a few.</li></ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Climate_Research_Programme" title="World Climate Research Programme">World Climate Research Programme</a> (WCRP), hosted by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> (WMO), coordinates research activities on climate modelling worldwide.
</p><p>A 2012 <a href="/wiki/U.S._National_Research_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. National Research Council">U.S. National Research Council</a> report discussed how the large and diverse U.S. climate modeling enterprise could evolve to become more unified.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Efficiencies could be gained by developing a common software infrastructure shared by all U.S. climate researchers, and holding an annual climate modeling forum, the report found.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
