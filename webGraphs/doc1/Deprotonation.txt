<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Deprotonation</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Deprotonation.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Deprotonation.svg/300px-Deprotonation.svg.png" width="300" height="47" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Deprotonation.svg/450px-Deprotonation.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Deprotonation.svg/600px-Deprotonation.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="81" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Deprotonation.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Deprotonation of acetic acid by a hydroxide ion</div></div></div>
<p><b>Deprotonation</b> is the removal (transfer) of a <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Hydron_(chemistry)" title="Hydron (chemistry)">hydrogen cation</a>, H<sup>+</sup>) from a <a href="/wiki/Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid" class="mw-redirect" title="Brønsted–Lowry acid">Brønsted–Lowry acid</a> in an <a href="/wiki/Acid-base_reaction" class="mw-redirect" title="Acid-base reaction">acid-base reaction</a>. The species formed is the <a href="/wiki/Conjugate_base" class="mw-redirect" title="Conjugate base">conjugate base</a> of that acid. The complementary process, when a proton is added (transferred) to a <a href="/wiki/Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_base" class="mw-redirect" title="Brønsted–Lowry base">Brønsted–Lowry base</a>, is <a href="/wiki/Protonation" title="Protonation">protonation</a>. The species formed is the <a href="/wiki/Conjugate_acid" title="Conjugate acid">conjugate acid</a> of that base.
</p><p>A species that can either accept or donate a proton is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Amphiprotic" class="mw-redirect" title="Amphiprotic">amphiprotic</a>. An example is the H<sub>2</sub>O (water) molecule, which can gain a proton to form the <a href="/wiki/Hydronium" title="Hydronium">hydronium</a> ion, H<sub>3</sub>O<sup>+</sup>, or lose a proton, leaving the <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> ion, OH<sup>−</sup>.
</p><p>The relative ability of a molecule to give up a proton is measured by its <a href="/wiki/PKa" class="mw-redirect" title="PKa">p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub></a> value. A low p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> value indicates that the compound is acidic and will easily give up its proton to a <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a>. The p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> of a compound is determined by many things, but the most significant is the stability of the conjugate base. This is primarily determined by the ability (or inability) of the conjugated base to stabilize negative charge. One of the most important ways of assessing a conjugate base's ability to distribute negative charge is using <a href="/wiki/Resonance_structures" class="mw-redirect" title="Resonance structures">resonance</a>. Electron withdrawing groups (which can stabilize the molecule by increasing charge distribution) or electron donating groups (which destabilize by decreasing charge distribution) present on a molecule also determine its <a href="/wiki/PKa" class="mw-redirect" title="PKa">p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub></a>.  The solvent used can also assist in the stabilization of the negative charge on a conjugated base.
</p><p>Bases used to deprotonate depend on the p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> of the compound. When the compound is not particularly <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acidic</a>, and, as such, the molecule does not give up its proton easily, a base stronger than the commonly known hydroxides is required.  <a href="/wiki/Hydride" title="Hydride">Hydrides</a> are one of the many types of powerful deprotonating agents.  Common hydrides used are <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydride" title="Sodium hydride">sodium hydride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydride" title="Potassium hydride">potassium hydride</a>.  The hydride forms <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> with the liberated proton from the other molecule.  The hydrogen is dangerous and could ignite with the oxygen in the air, so the chemical procedure should be done in an <a href="/wiki/Inert_gas" title="Inert gas">inert</a> <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>).
</p><p>Deprotonation can be an important step in a chemical reaction. Acid/base reactions typically occur faster than any other step which may determine the product of a reaction. The conjugate base is more electron-rich than the molecule which can alter the reactivity of the molecule. For example, deprotonation of an alcohol forms the negatively charged alkoxide, which is a much stronger nucleophile.
</p>
