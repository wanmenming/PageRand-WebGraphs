<p><b>Dinitrogen trioxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.  This deep blue <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a><sup id="cite_ref-GW2_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GW2-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is one of the simple <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a>. It forms upon mixing equal parts of <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">nitric oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">nitrogen dioxide</a> and  cooling the mixture below &#8722;21&#160;°C (&#8722;6&#160;°F):<sup id="cite_ref-G&amp;E_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;E-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>NO + NO<sub>2</sub> ⇌ N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Dinitrogen trioxide is only isolable at low temperatures, i.e. in the liquid and solid <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phases</a>.  At higher temperatures the equilibrium favors the constituent gases, with  <i>K</i><sub>diss</sub>&#160;= 193&#160;kPa (25&#160;°C).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_bonding">Structure and bonding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dinitrogen_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure and bonding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Typically, N–N bonds are similar in length to that in <a href="/wiki/Hydrazine" title="Hydrazine">hydrazine</a> (145 pm). Dinitrogen trioxide, however, has an unusually long N–N bond at 186 pm. Some other nitrogen oxides do also possess long N–N bonds, including dinitrogen tetroxide (175 pm). The N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> molecule is planar and exhibits Cs symmetry. The dimensions displayed below come from microwave spectroscopy of low-temperature, gaseous N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>:<sup id="cite_ref-G&amp;E_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G&amp;E-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<center>
<p><a href="/wiki/File:Dinitrogen-trioxide-2D-geometry.png" class="image"><img alt="Dinitrogen-trioxide-2D-geometry.png" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Dinitrogen-trioxide-2D-geometry.png/400px-Dinitrogen-trioxide-2D-geometry.png" width="400" height="183" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Dinitrogen-trioxide-2D-geometry.png/600px-Dinitrogen-trioxide-2D-geometry.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Dinitrogen-trioxide-2D-geometry.png/800px-Dinitrogen-trioxide-2D-geometry.png 2x" data-file-width="1100" data-file-height="502" /></a>
</p>
</center>
<p>It is the anhydride of the unstable <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_acid" title="Nitrous acid">nitrous acid</a> (HNO<sub>2</sub>), and produces it when mixed into water. An alternative structure might be anticipated for the true anhydride, i.e. O=N–O–N=O, but this isomer is  observed. If the nitrous acid is not then used up quickly, it decomposes into nitric oxide and nitric acid. Nitrite salts are sometimes produced by adding N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> to solutions of bases:
</p>
<dl><dd>N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 2 NaOH →  2 NaNO<sub>2</sub>  +  H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
