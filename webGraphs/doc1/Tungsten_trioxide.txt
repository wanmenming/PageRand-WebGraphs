<p><b>Tungsten(VI) oxide</b>, also known as <b>tungsten trioxide</b> or <b>tungstic anhydride</b>, WO<sub>3</sub>, is a chemical compound containing <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and the transition metal <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>. It is obtained as an intermediate in the recovery of tungsten from its minerals.<sup id="cite_ref-handbook_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-handbook-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Tungsten ores are treated with <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkalis</a> to produce WO<sub>3</sub>. Further reaction with <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> gas reduces tungsten trioxide to the pure metal.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Primary reference for these reactions should be provided (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 WO<sub>3</sub> + 3 C → 2 W + 3 CO<sub>2</sub>  (high temperature)</dd>
<dd>WO<sub>3</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub> → W + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O  (550 - 850 °C)</dd></dl>
<p>Tungsten(VI) oxide occurs naturally in the form of <a href="/wiki/Hydrate" title="Hydrate">hydrates</a>, which include minerals: <a href="/wiki/Tungstite" title="Tungstite">tungstite</a> WO<sub>3</sub>·H<sub>2</sub>O, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Meymacite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Meymacite (page does not exist)">meymacite</a> WO<sub>3</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hydrotungstite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hydrotungstite (page does not exist)">hydrotungstite</a> (of the same composition as meymacite, however sometimes written as H<sub>2</sub>WO<sub>4</sub>). These minerals are rare to very rare secondary tungsten minerals.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tungsten has a rich history dating back to its discovery during the 18th century. <a href="/wiki/Peter_Woulfe" title="Peter Woulfe">Peter Woulfe</a> was the first to recognize a new element in the naturally occurring mineral <a href="/wiki/Wolframite" title="Wolframite">wolframite</a>. Tungsten was originally known as wolfram, explaining the choice of "W" for its elemental symbol. Swedish chemist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele" title="Carl Wilhelm Scheele">Carl Wilhelm Scheele</a> contributed to its discovery with his studies on the mineral <a href="/wiki/Scheelite" title="Scheelite">scheelite</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-handbook_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-handbook-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1841, a chemist named Robert Oxland gave the first procedures for preparing tungsten trioxide and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_tungstate" title="Sodium tungstate">sodium tungstate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tungsten_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tungsten-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He was granted patents for his work soon after, and is considered to be the founder of systematic tungsten chemistry.<sup id="cite_ref-tungsten_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tungsten-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tungsten trioxide can be prepared in several different ways. CaWO<sub>4</sub>, or <a href="/wiki/Scheelite" title="Scheelite">scheelite</a>, is allowed to react with <a href="/wiki/HCl" class="mw-redirect" title="HCl">HCl</a> to produce <a href="/wiki/Tungstic_acid" title="Tungstic acid">tungstic acid</a>, which decomposes to WO<sub>3</sub> and water at high temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-handbook_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-handbook-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>CaWO<sub>4</sub> + 2 HCl → CaCl<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>WO<sub>4</sub></dd>
<dd>H<sub>2</sub>WO<sub>4</sub> → H<sub>2</sub>O + WO<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Another common way to synthesize WO<sub>3</sub> is by <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcination</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_paratungstate" title="Ammonium paratungstate">ammonium paratungstate</a> (APT) under oxidizing conditions:<sup id="cite_ref-tungsten_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tungsten-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>(NH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>10</sub>[H<sub>2</sub>W<sub>12</sub>O<sub>42</sub>]•4H<sub>2</sub>O → 12 WO<sub>3</sub> + 10 NH<sub>3</sub> + 10 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The crystal structure of tungsten trioxide is temperature dependent. It is <a href="/wiki/Tetragonal" class="mw-redirect" title="Tetragonal">tetragonal</a> at temperatures above 740&#160;°C, <a href="/wiki/Orthorhombic" class="mw-redirect" title="Orthorhombic">orthorhombic</a> from 330 to 740&#160;°C, <a href="/wiki/Monoclinic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> from 17 to 330&#160;°C, and <a href="/wiki/Triclinic" class="mw-redirect" title="Triclinic">triclinic</a> from -50 to 17&#160;°C. The most common structure of WO<sub>3</sub> is monoclinic with <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> P2<sub>1</sub>/n.<sup id="cite_ref-tungsten_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tungsten-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tungsten_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tungsten trioxide is used for many purposes in everyday life. It is frequently used in industry to manufacture <a href="/wiki/Tungstate" title="Tungstate">tungstates</a> for <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">x-ray</a> screen <a href="/wiki/Phosphor" title="Phosphor">phosphors</a>, for <a href="/wiki/Fireproofing" title="Fireproofing">fireproofing</a> fabrics<sup id="cite_ref-merck_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-merck-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and in gas sensors.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Due to its rich yellow color, WO<sub>3</sub> is also used as a pigment in ceramics and paints.<sup id="cite_ref-handbook_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-handbook-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In recent years, tungsten trioxide has been employed in the production of <a href="/wiki/Electrochromic" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrochromic">electrochromic</a> windows, or <a href="/wiki/Smart_windows" class="mw-redirect" title="Smart windows">smart windows</a>. These windows are electrically switchable glass that change light transmission properties with an applied voltage.<sup id="cite_ref-journal_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journal-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> This allows the user to tint their windows, changing the amount of heat or light passing through.
</p><p>2010- <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Advanced_Industrial_Science_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Advanced Industrial Science and Technology">AIST</a> reports a quantum yield of 19% in <a href="/wiki/Photocatalytic_water_splitting" title="Photocatalytic water splitting">photocatalytic water splitting</a> with a caesium-enhanced tungsten oxide photocatalyst.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, highly photocatalytic active <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">titania</a>/tungsten (VI) oxide/noble metal (<a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">Au</a> and <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">Pt</a>) composites toward <a href="/wiki/Oxalic_acid" title="Oxalic acid">oxalic acid</a> were obtained by the means of selective <a href="/wiki/Noble_metal" title="Noble metal">noble metal</a> photodeposition on the desired oxide's surface (either on TiO<sub>2</sub> or on WO<sub>3</sub>). The composite showed a modest <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_production" title="Hydrogen production">hydrogen production</a> performance.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, shape controlled tungsten trioxide <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductors</a> were obtained by the means of <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_synthesis" title="Hydrothermal synthesis">hydrothermal synthesis</a>. From these semiconductors composite systems were prepared with commercial TiO<sub>2</sub>. These composite systems showed a higher <a href="/wiki/Photocatalytic" class="mw-redirect" title="Photocatalytic">photocatalysis</a> activity than the commercial TiO<sub>2</sub> (Evonik Aeroxide P25) towards <a href="/wiki/Phenol" title="Phenol">phenol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Methyl_orange" title="Methyl orange">methyl orange</a> degradation.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
