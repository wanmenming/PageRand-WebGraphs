<p>The <b>Jurassic</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/dʒ/: &#39;j&#39; in &#39;jam&#39;">dʒ</span><span title="/ʊəˈr/: &#39;ur&#39; in &#39;plurality&#39;">ʊəˈr</span><span title="/æ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">æ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span></span>/</a></span></span>; from <a href="/wiki/Jura_Mountains" title="Jura Mountains">Jura Mountains</a>) was a <a href="/wiki/Period_(geology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Period (geology)">geologic period and system</a> that spanned 56 million years from the end of the <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> Period 201.3 million years ago (<a href="/wiki/Annum" class="mw-redirect" title="Annum">Mya</a>) to the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> Period 145 Mya.<sup id="cite_ref-note_1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-note_1-7">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup> The Jurassic constitutes the middle period of the <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic_Era" class="mw-redirect" title="Mesozoic Era">Mesozoic Era</a>, also known as the Age of Reptiles. The start of the period was marked by the major <a href="/wiki/Triassic%E2%80%93Jurassic_extinction_event" title="Triassic–Jurassic extinction event">Triassic–Jurassic extinction event</a>. Two other <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">extinction</a> events occurred during the period: the <a href="/wiki/Toarcian_turnover" title="Toarcian turnover">Pliensbachian/Toarcian event</a> in the Early Jurassic, and the <a href="/wiki/Tithonian" title="Tithonian">Tithonian</a> event at the end<sup id="cite_ref-Hallam_1986_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hallam_1986-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>; however, neither event ranks among the <a href="/wiki/Extinction_event#Major_extinction_events" title="Extinction event">"Big Five" mass extinctions</a>.
</p><p>The Jurassic period is divided into three <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(geology)" title="Epoch (geology)">epochs</a>: Early, Middle, and Late. Similarly, in <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">stratigraphy</a>,  the Jurassic is divided into the <a href="/wiki/Lower_Jurassic" class="mw-redirect" title="Lower Jurassic">Lower Jurassic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middle_Jurassic" title="Middle Jurassic">Middle Jurassic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Upper_Jurassic" class="mw-redirect" title="Upper Jurassic">Upper Jurassic</a> <a href="/wiki/Series_(stratigraphy)" title="Series (stratigraphy)">series</a> of rock formations.
</p><p>The Jurassic is named after the <a href="/wiki/Jura_Mountains" title="Jura Mountains">Jura Mountains</a> within the <a href="/wiki/European_Alps" class="mw-redirect" title="European Alps">European Alps</a>, where <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> strata from the period were first identified.
By the beginning of the Jurassic, the <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinent</a> <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a> had begun <a href="/wiki/Rift" title="Rift">rifting</a> into two landmasses: <a href="/wiki/Laurasia" title="Laurasia">Laurasia</a> to the north, and <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a> to the south. This created more coastlines and shifted the continental climate from dry to humid, and many of the <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> deserts of the Triassic were replaced by lush rainforests.
</p><p>On land, the fauna transitioned from the Triassic fauna, dominated by both <a href="/wiki/Dinosauromorpha" title="Dinosauromorpha">dinosauromorph</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crocodylomorpha" title="Crocodylomorpha">crocodylomorph</a> <a href="/wiki/Archosaur" title="Archosaur">archosaurs</a>, to one dominated by <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a> alone. The first <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a> also appeared during the Jurassic, having evolved from a branch of <a href="/wiki/Theropod" class="mw-redirect" title="Theropod">theropod</a> dinosaurs. Other major events include the appearance of the earliest lizards, and the evolution of <a href="/wiki/Theria" title="Theria">therian</a> mammals, including primitive <a href="/wiki/Placental" class="mw-redirect" title="Placental">placentals</a>. Crocodilians made the transition from a terrestrial to an aquatic mode of life. The oceans were inhabited by <a href="/wiki/Marine_reptiles" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine reptiles">marine reptiles</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Ichthyosaur" title="Ichthyosaur">ichthyosaurs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plesiosaur" class="mw-redirect" title="Plesiosaur">plesiosaurs</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Pterosaur" title="Pterosaur">pterosaurs</a> were the dominant flying <a href="/wiki/Vertebrates" class="mw-redirect" title="Vertebrates">vertebrates</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_term">History of term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chronostratigraphy" title="Chronostratigraphy">chronostratigraphic</a> term "Jurassic" is directly linked to the <a href="/wiki/Jura_Mountains" title="Jura Mountains">Jura Mountains</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">mountain range</a>  mainly following the course of the <a href="/wiki/France%E2%80%93Switzerland_border" title="France–Switzerland border">France–Switzerland border</a>. During a tour of the region in 1795,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">Alexander von Humboldt</a> recognized the mainly <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> dominated mountain range of the Jura Mountains as a separate formation that had not been included in the established stratigraphic system defined by <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Gottlob_Werner" title="Abraham Gottlob Werner">Abraham Gottlob Werner</a>, and he named it "Jura-Kalkstein" ('Jura limestone') in 1799.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hölder,_1964_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hölder,_1964-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Arkell,_1956_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arkell,_1956-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pieńkowski_et_al.,_2008_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pieńkowski_et_al.,_2008-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name "Jura" is derived from the Celtic root <i>*jor</i> via Gaulish <i>*iuris</i> "wooded mountain", which, borrowed into Latin as a place name, evolved into <i>Juria</i> and finally <i>Jura</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hölder,_1964_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hölder,_1964-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Arkell,_1956_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arkell,_1956-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rollier,_1903_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rollier,_1903-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Divisions">Divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Divisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleogeography_and_tectonics">Paleogeography and tectonics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Paleogeography and tectonics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dilophosaurus_wetherilli.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Dilophosaurus_wetherilli.jpg/220px-Dilophosaurus_wetherilli.jpg" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Dilophosaurus_wetherilli.jpg/330px-Dilophosaurus_wetherilli.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Dilophosaurus_wetherilli.jpg/440px-Dilophosaurus_wetherilli.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1400" data-file-height="1001" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dilophosaurus_wetherilli.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Depiction of Early Jurassic environment preserved at the <a href="/wiki/St._George,_Utah" title="St. George, Utah">St. George</a> Dinosaur Discovery Site at Johnson Farm, with <i><a href="/wiki/Dilophosaurus" title="Dilophosaurus">Dilophosaurus wetherilli</a></i> in bird-like resting pose</div></div></div>
<p>During the early Jurassic period, the <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">supercontinent</a> <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">Pangaea</a> broke up into the northern supercontinent <a href="/wiki/Laurasia" title="Laurasia">Laurasia</a> and the southern supercontinent <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">Gondwana</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> opened in the new rift between <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and what is now Mexico's <a href="/wiki/Yucatan_Peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Yucatan Peninsula">Yucatan Peninsula</a>. The Jurassic North <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> was relatively narrow, while the South Atlantic did not open until the following Cretaceous period, when Gondwana itself rifted apart.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Tethys_Ocean" title="Tethys Ocean">Tethys Sea</a> closed, and the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Neotethys</a> basin appeared. Climates were warm, with no evidence of <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciation</a>. As in the Triassic, there was apparently no land over either pole, and no extensive ice caps existed.
</p><p>The Jurassic geological record is good in western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, where extensive marine sequences indicate a time when much of that future landmass was submerged under shallow tropical seas; famous locales include the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Coast" title="Jurassic Coast">Jurassic Coast</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" class="mw-redirect" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> in southern England and the renowned late Jurassic <i><a href="/wiki/Lagerst%C3%A4tte" title="Lagerstätte">lagerstätten</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Holzmaden" title="Holzmaden">Holzmaden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solnhofen_limestone" class="mw-redirect" title="Solnhofen limestone">Solnhofen</a> in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In contrast, the North American Jurassic record is the poorest of the Mesozoic, with few outcrops at the surface.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Though the <a href="/wiki/Epeiric_Sea" class="mw-redirect" title="Epeiric Sea">epicontinental</a> <a href="/wiki/Sundance_Sea" title="Sundance Sea">Sundance Sea</a> left marine deposits in parts of the northern plains of the United States and Canada during the late Jurassic, most exposed sediments from this period are continental, such as the <a href="/wiki/Alluvium" title="Alluvium">alluvial</a> deposits of the <a href="/wiki/Morrison_Formation" title="Morrison Formation">Morrison Formation</a>.
</p><p>The Jurassic was a time of <a href="/wiki/Calcite_sea" title="Calcite sea">calcite sea</a> geochemistry in which low-magnesium <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a> was the primary inorganic marine precipitate of calcium carbonate. <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_hardgrounds" title="Carbonate hardgrounds">Carbonate hardgrounds</a> were thus very common, along with calcitic <a href="/wiki/Ooids" class="mw-redirect" title="Ooids">ooids</a>, calcitic cements, and invertebrate faunas with dominantly calcitic skeletons (Stanley and Hardie, 1998, 1999).
</p><p>The first of several massive <a href="/wiki/Batholith" title="Batholith">batholiths</a> were emplaced in the northern <a href="/wiki/American_cordillera" class="mw-redirect" title="American cordillera">American cordillera</a> beginning in the mid-Jurassic, marking the <a href="/wiki/Nevadan_orogeny" title="Nevadan orogeny">Nevadan orogeny</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Important Jurassic exposures are also found in Russia, India, South America, Japan, Australasia and the United Kingdom.
</p><p>In Africa, Early Jurassic strata are distributed in a similar fashion to Late Triassic beds, with more common outcrops in the south and less common fossil beds which are predominated by tracks to the north.<sup id="cite_ref-dinopedia-african_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dinopedia-african-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> As the Jurassic proceeded, larger and more iconic groups of dinosaurs like sauropods and ornithopods proliferated in Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-dinopedia-african_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dinopedia-african-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Middle Jurassic strata are neither well represented nor well studied in Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-dinopedia-african_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dinopedia-african-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Late Jurassic strata are also poorly represented apart from the spectacular <a href="/wiki/Tendaguru_Formation" title="Tendaguru Formation">Tendaguru fauna</a> in Tanzania.<sup id="cite_ref-dinopedia-african_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dinopedia-african-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The Late Jurassic life of Tendaguru is very similar to <a href="/wiki/Paleobiota_of_the_Morrison_Formation" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleobiota of the Morrison Formation">that</a> found in western North America's <a href="/wiki/Morrison_Formation" title="Morrison Formation">Morrison Formation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dinopedia-african_20-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dinopedia-african-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aquatic_and_marine">Aquatic and marine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aquatic and marine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the Jurassic period, the primary vertebrates living in the sea were <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and marine <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">reptiles</a>. The latter include <a href="/wiki/Ichthyosaur" title="Ichthyosaur">ichthyosaurs</a>, which were at the peak of their diversity, <a href="/wiki/Plesiosauria" title="Plesiosauria">plesiosaurs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pliosaur" class="mw-redirect" title="Pliosaur">pliosaurs</a>, and marine <a href="/wiki/Crocodilia" title="Crocodilia">crocodiles</a> of the families <a href="/wiki/Teleosauridae" title="Teleosauridae">Teleosauridae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metriorhynchidae" title="Metriorhynchidae">Metriorhynchidae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Numerous <a href="/wiki/Turtle" title="Turtle">turtles</a> could be found in lakes and rivers.<sup id="cite_ref-nws_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nws-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ls_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ls-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrate</a> world, several new groups appeared, including <a href="/wiki/Rudists" title="Rudists">rudists</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Reef" title="Reef">reef</a>-forming variety of <a href="/wiki/Bivalvia" title="Bivalvia">bivalves</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Belemnitida" title="Belemnitida">belemnites</a>. Calcareous <a href="/wiki/Sabellidae" title="Sabellidae">sabellids</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Glomerula" title="Glomerula">Glomerula</a></i>) appeared in the Early Jurassic.<sup id="cite_ref-VinnMutvei2009_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VinnMutvei2009-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-VinnTenHoveMutvei2008_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VinnTenHoveMutvei2008-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The Jurassic also had diverse encrusting and <a href="/wiki/Bioerosion" title="Bioerosion">boring</a> (sclerobiont) communities, and it saw a significant rise in the <a href="/wiki/Bioerosion" title="Bioerosion">bioerosion</a> of carbonate shells and hardgrounds. Especially common is the <a href="/wiki/Ichnotaxa" class="mw-redirect" title="Ichnotaxa">ichnogenus</a> (<a href="/wiki/Trace_fossil" title="Trace fossil">trace fossil</a>) <i><a href="/wiki/Gastrochaenolites" title="Gastrochaenolites">Gastrochaenolites</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the Jurassic period, about four or five of the twelve <a href="/wiki/Clade" title="Clade">clades</a> of planktonic organisms that exist in the fossil record either experienced a massive evolutionary radiation or appeared for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-Palaeos_website_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palaeos_website-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terrestrial">Terrestrial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Terrestrial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Drzewica_Formation_Reconstruction.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Drzewica_Formation_Reconstruction.jpg/220px-Drzewica_Formation_Reconstruction.jpg" width="220" height="120" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Drzewica_Formation_Reconstruction.jpg/330px-Drzewica_Formation_Reconstruction.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Drzewica_Formation_Reconstruction.jpg/440px-Drzewica_Formation_Reconstruction.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4307" data-file-height="2344" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Drzewica_Formation_Reconstruction.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Example of Rare Early Jurassic (Pliensbachian) Ecosystem, the  <a href="/wiki/Drzewica_Formation" title="Drzewica Formation">Drzewica Formation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Szyd%C5%82owiec" title="Szydłowiec">Szydłowiec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>. This zone was characterised by a very damp ecosystem populated by dinosaur megafauna, more related in several aspects to Middle or Late Jurassic dinosaurs</div></div></div>
<p>On land, various <a href="/wiki/Archosaur" title="Archosaur">archosaurian</a> reptiles remained dominant. The Jurassic was a golden age for the large herbivorous dinosaurs known as the <a href="/wiki/Sauropod" class="mw-redirect" title="Sauropod">sauropods</a>—<i><a href="/wiki/Camarasaurus" title="Camarasaurus">Camarasaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Apatosaurus" title="Apatosaurus">Apatosaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Diplodocus" title="Diplodocus">Diplodocus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Brachiosaurus" title="Brachiosaurus">Brachiosaurus</a></i>, and many others—that roamed the land late in the period; their foraging grounds were either the <a href="/wiki/Prairie" title="Prairie">prairies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">ferns</a>, palm-like <a href="/wiki/Cycad" title="Cycad">cycads</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bennettitales" title="Bennettitales">bennettitales</a>, or the higher coniferous growth, according to their adaptations. The smaller <a href="/wiki/Ornithischia" title="Ornithischia">Ornithischian</a> herbivore dinosaurs, like <a href="/wiki/Stegosaur" class="mw-redirect" title="Stegosaur">stegosaurs</a> and small <a href="/wiki/Ornithopod" title="Ornithopod">ornithopods</a> were less predominant, but played important roles. They were preyed upon by large <a href="/wiki/Theropod" class="mw-redirect" title="Theropod">theropods</a>, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Ceratosaurus" title="Ceratosaurus">Ceratosaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Megalosaurus" title="Megalosaurus">Megalosaurus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Torvosaurus" title="Torvosaurus">Torvosaurus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Allosaurus" title="Allosaurus">Allosaurus</a></i>. All these belong to the 'lizard hipped' or <a href="/wiki/Saurischia" title="Saurischia">saurischian</a> branch of the <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
During the Late Jurassic, the first <a href="/wiki/Aves" class="mw-redirect" title="Aves">avialans</a>, like <i><a href="/wiki/Archaeopteryx" title="Archaeopteryx">Archaeopteryx</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolved</a> from small <a href="/wiki/Coelurosaur" class="mw-redirect" title="Coelurosaur">coelurosaurian</a> dinosaurs. In the air, <a href="/wiki/Pterosaur" title="Pterosaur">pterosaurs</a> were common; they ruled the skies, filling many ecological roles now taken by <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">birds</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and may have already produced some of the largest flying animals of all time.<sup id="cite_ref-witton2010_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-witton2010-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Within the undergrowth were various types of early <a href="/wiki/Mammals" class="mw-redirect" title="Mammals">mammals</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Tritylodontidae" title="Tritylodontidae">tritylodonts</a>, lizard-like <a href="/wiki/Sphenodontia" class="mw-redirect" title="Sphenodontia">sphenodonts</a>, and early <a href="/wiki/Lissamphibia" title="Lissamphibia">lissamphibians</a>. The rest of the <a href="/wiki/Lissamphibia" title="Lissamphibia">Lissamphibia</a> evolved in this period, introducing the first <a href="/wiki/Salamander" title="Salamander">salamanders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caecilian" title="Caecilian">caecilians</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora">Flora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jurassic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Flora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Douglas_fir_leaves_and_bud.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Douglas_fir_leaves_and_bud.jpg/120px-Douglas_fir_leaves_and_bud.jpg" width="120" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Douglas_fir_leaves_and_bud.jpg/180px-Douglas_fir_leaves_and_bud.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Douglas_fir_leaves_and_bud.jpg/240px-Douglas_fir_leaves_and_bud.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Douglas_fir_leaves_and_bud.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Conifer" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifer">Conifers</a> were the dominant land plants of the Jurassic</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Europasaurus_holgeri_Scene_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Europasaurus_holgeri_Scene_2.jpg/200px-Europasaurus_holgeri_Scene_2.jpg" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Europasaurus_holgeri_Scene_2.jpg/300px-Europasaurus_holgeri_Scene_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Europasaurus_holgeri_Scene_2.jpg/400px-Europasaurus_holgeri_Scene_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2576" data-file-height="1932" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Europasaurus_holgeri_Scene_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Various dinosaurs roamed forests of similarly large <a href="/wiki/Conifer" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifer">conifers</a> during the Jurassic period.</div></div></div>
<p>The arid, continental conditions characteristic of the <a href="/wiki/Triassic" title="Triassic">Triassic</a> steadily eased during the Jurassic period, especially at higher latitudes; the warm, humid climate allowed lush jungles to cover much of the landscape.<sup id="cite_ref-Haines,_2000_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haines,_2000-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gymnosperm" title="Gymnosperm">Gymnosperms</a> were relatively diverse during the Jurassic period.<sup id="cite_ref-Palaeos_website_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palaeos_website-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Conifers" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifers">Conifers</a> in particular dominated the flora, as during the Triassic; they were the most diverse group and constituted the majority of large trees.
</p><p>Extant conifer families that flourished during the Jurassic included the <a href="/wiki/Araucariaceae" title="Araucariaceae">Araucariaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cephalotaxaceae" title="Cephalotaxaceae">Cephalotaxaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pinaceae" title="Pinaceae">Pinaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Podocarpaceae" title="Podocarpaceae">Podocarpaceae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taxaceae" title="Taxaceae">Taxaceae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taxodiaceae" title="Taxodiaceae">Taxodiaceae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The extinct Mesozoic conifer family <a href="/wiki/Cheirolepidiaceae" title="Cheirolepidiaceae">Cheirolepidiaceae</a> dominated low latitude vegetation, as did the shrubby <a href="/wiki/Bennettitales" title="Bennettitales">Bennettitales</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Behrensmeyer_et_al.,_1992,_352_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Behrensmeyer_et_al.,_1992,_352-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cycad" title="Cycad">Cycads</a>, similar to palm trees, were also common, as were <a href="/wiki/Ginkgo" title="Ginkgo">ginkgos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dicksoniaceae" title="Dicksoniaceae">Dicksoniaceous</a> <a href="/wiki/Tree_ferns" class="mw-redirect" title="Tree ferns">tree ferns</a> in the forest.<sup id="cite_ref-Palaeos_website_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palaeos_website-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Smaller <a href="/wiki/Fern" title="Fern">ferns</a> were probably the dominant undergrowth. <a href="/wiki/Caytoniaceae" title="Caytoniaceae">Caytoniaceous seed ferns</a> were another group of important plants during this time and are thought to have been shrub to small-tree sized.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Ginkgo plants were particularly common in the mid- to high northern latitudes.<sup id="cite_ref-Palaeos_website_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palaeos_website-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In the Southern Hemisphere, <a href="/wiki/Podocarps" class="mw-redirect" title="Podocarps">podocarps</a> were especially successful, while <a href="/wiki/Ginkgo" title="Ginkgo">Ginkgos</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Czekanowskiales&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Czekanowskiales (page does not exist)">Czekanowskiales</a> were rare.<sup id="cite_ref-Haines,_2000_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haines,_2000-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Behrensmeyer_et_al.,_1992,_352_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Behrensmeyer_et_al.,_1992,_352-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the oceans, modern <a href="/wiki/Coralline_algae" title="Coralline algae">coralline algae</a> appeared for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-Palaeos_website_15-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palaeos_website-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> However, they were a part of another major extinction that happened within the next major time period.
</p>
