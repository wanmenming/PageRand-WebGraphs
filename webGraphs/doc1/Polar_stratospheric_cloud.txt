<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Polar stratospheric cloud</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>Polar stratospheric clouds</b> (<b>PSCs</b>), also known as <b>nacreous clouds</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/eɪ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;face&#39;">eɪ</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/i/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;happy&#39;">i</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>, from <i>nacre</i>, or <a href="/wiki/Mother_of_pearl" class="mw-redirect" title="Mother of pearl">mother of pearl</a>, due to its iridescence), are <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a> in the winter polar <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a> at altitudes of 15,000–25,000&#160;m (49,000–82,000&#160;ft). They are best observed during <a href="/wiki/Twilight#Civil_twilight" title="Twilight">civil twilight</a>, when the Sun is between 1 and 6 degrees below the horizon, as well as in <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">winter</a> and in more northerly latitudes.<sup id="cite_ref-AAD_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAD-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> They are implicated in the formation of <a href="/wiki/Ozone_hole" class="mw-redirect" title="Ozone hole">ozone holes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The effects on <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a> arise because they support chemical reactions that produce active <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> which catalyzes <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> destruction, and also because they remove gaseous <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a>, perturbing <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">nitrogen</a> and chlorine cycles in a way which increases ozone depletion.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polar_stratospheric_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The stratosphere is very dry; unlike the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, it rarely allows clouds to form. In the extreme cold of the polar winter, however, stratospheric clouds of different types may form, which are classified according to their <a href="/wiki/Physical_state" class="mw-redirect" title="Physical state">physical state</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_composition" title="Chemical composition">chemical composition</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to their high altitude and the curvature of the surface of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, these clouds will receive sunlight from below the horizon and reflect it to the ground, shining brightly well before <a href="/wiki/Dawn" title="Dawn">dawn</a> or after <a href="/wiki/Dusk" title="Dusk">dusk</a>.
</p><p>PSCs form at very low temperatures, below −78&#160;°C (−108&#160;°F). These temperatures can occur in the lower <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a> in polar winter. In the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a>, temperatures below −88&#160;°C (−126&#160;°F) frequently cause type II PSCs. Such low temperatures are rarer in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern hemisphere">Northern hemisphere</a>, the generation of <a href="/wiki/Lee_waves" class="mw-redirect" title="Lee waves">lee waves</a> by mountains may locally cool the lower stratosphere and lead to the formation of PSCs.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Forward_scatter" title="Forward scatter">Forward-scattering</a> of sunlight within the clouds produces a pearly-white appearance. Particles within the <a href="/wiki/Thin-film_optics" title="Thin-film optics">optically thin</a> clouds cause colored <a href="/wiki/Interference_fringe" class="mw-redirect" title="Interference fringe">interference fringes</a> by <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">diffraction</a>. The visibility of the colors may be enhanced with a <a href="/wiki/Polarising_filter" class="mw-redirect" title="Polarising filter">polarising filter</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AAD_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAD-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wegener_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wegener-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polar_stratospheric_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Polar_stratospheric_cloud_type_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Polar_stratospheric_cloud_type_2.jpg/220px-Polar_stratospheric_cloud_type_2.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Polar_stratospheric_cloud_type_2.jpg/330px-Polar_stratospheric_cloud_type_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Polar_stratospheric_cloud_type_2.jpg/440px-Polar_stratospheric_cloud_type_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Polar_stratospheric_cloud_type_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A type II (water) PSC showing iridescence</div></div></div>
<p>PSCs are classified into three types Ia, Ib and II according to their <a href="/wiki/Chemical_composition" title="Chemical composition">chemical composition</a> which can be measured using <a href="/wiki/LIDAR" class="mw-redirect" title="LIDAR">LIDAR</a>. The technique also determines the height and ambient temperature of the cloud.<sup id="cite_ref-wegener_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wegener-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Type I clouds contain water, <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> and they are a source of polar <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
<ul><li>Type Ia clouds consist of large, aspherical particles, consisting of nitric acid trihydrate (NAT).<sup id="cite_ref-wegener_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wegener-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Type Ib clouds contain small, spherical particles (non-depolarising), of a liquid <a href="/wiki/Supercooled" class="mw-redirect" title="Supercooled">supercooled</a> ternary solution (STS) of sulfuric acid, nitric acid and water.<sup id="cite_ref-wegener_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wegener-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Type Ic clouds consist of <a href="/wiki/Metastability" title="Metastability">metastable</a> water-rich nitric acid in a solid phase.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li>Type II clouds, which are very rarely observed in the Arctic, consist of <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">water ice</a> only.<sup id="cite_ref-wegener_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wegener-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Only Type II clouds are necessarily nacreous<sup id="cite_ref-AAD_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AAD-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> whereas Type I clouds can be iridescent under certain conditions, just as <a href="/wiki/Cloud_iridescence" title="Cloud iridescence">any other cloud</a>.
</p>
