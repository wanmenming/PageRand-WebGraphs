<p><b>Rubidium oxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> <b>Rb<sub>2</sub>O</b>. Rubidium oxide is highly reactive towards water, and therefore it would not be expected to occur naturally.  The rubidium content in <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> is often calculated and quoted in terms of Rb<sub>2</sub>O.  In reality, the rubidium is typically present as a component of (actually, an impurity in) <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">silicate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Aluminosilicate" title="Aluminosilicate">aluminosilicate</a>.  A major source of rubidium is <a href="/wiki/Lepidolite" title="Lepidolite">lepidolite</a>, KLi<sub>2</sub>Al(Al,Si)<sub>3</sub>O<sub>10</sub>(F,OH)<sub>2</sub>, wherein Rb sometimes replaces K.
</p><p>Rb<sub>2</sub>O is a yellow colored solid.  The related species Na<sub>2</sub>O, K<sub>2</sub>O, and Cs<sub>2</sub>O are colorless, pale-yellow, and orange, respectively.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Alkali_metal_oxide" title="Alkali metal oxide">alkali metal oxides</a> M<sub>2</sub>O (M = Li, Na, K, Rb) crystallise in the antifluorite structure. In the antifluorite motif the positions of the <a href="/wiki/Anion" class="mw-redirect" title="Anion">anions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cation" class="mw-redirect" title="Cation">cations</a> are reversed relative to their positions in <a href="/wiki/Calcium_fluoride" title="Calcium fluoride">CaF<sub>2</sub></a>, with rubidium ions 8 coordinate (cubic) and oxide ions 4 coordinate (tetrahedral).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubidium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like other alkali metal oxides, Rb<sub>2</sub>O is a strong <a href="/wiki/Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)">base</a>.  Thus, Rb<sub>2</sub>O reacts exothermically with water to form <a href="/wiki/Rubidium_hydroxide" title="Rubidium hydroxide">rubidium hydroxide</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd>Rb<sub>2</sub>O  +  H<sub>2</sub>O  →  2 RbOH</dd></dl>
<p>So reactive is Rb<sub>2</sub>O toward water that it is considered <a href="/wiki/Hygroscopic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hygroscopic">hygroscopic</a>.  Upon heating, Rb<sub>2</sub>O reacts with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> to rubidium hydroxide and <a href="/wiki/Rubidium_hydride" title="Rubidium hydride">rubidium hydride</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>Rb<sub>2</sub>O  +  H<sub>2</sub>  →  RbOH  +  RbH</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rubidium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For laboratory use, RbOH is usually used in place of the oxide. RbOH can be purchased for ca. <a href="/wiki/US$" class="mw-redirect" title="US$">US$</a>5/g (2006).  The hydroxide is more useful, less reactive toward atmospheric moisture, and less expensive than the oxide.
</p><p>As for most alkali metal oxides,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> the best synthesis of Rb<sub>2</sub>O does <i>not</i> entail oxidation of the metal but reduction of the anhydrous nitrate:
</p>
<dl><dd>10 Rb  +  2 RbNO<sub>3</sub>  →  6 Rb<sub>2</sub>O  +  N<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Typical for alkali metal hydroxides, RbOH <i>cannot</i> be dehydrated to the oxide.  Instead, the hydroxide can be decomposed to the oxide (by reduction of the hydrogen ion) using Rb metal:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Rb  +  2 RbOH  →  2 Rb<sub>2</sub>O  +  H<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Metallic Rb reacts with O<sub>2</sub>, as indicated by its tendency to rapidly <a href="/wiki/Tarnish" title="Tarnish">tarnish</a> in air.  The tarnishing process is relatively colorful as it proceeds via bronze-colored Rb<sub>6</sub>O and copper-colored Rb<sub>9</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/Suboxide" title="Suboxide">suboxides</a> of rubidium that have been characterized by <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">X-ray crystallography</a> include Rb<sub>9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and Rb<sub>6</sub>O,  as well as the mixed <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">Cs</a>-Rb suboxides Cs<sub>11</sub>O<sub>3</sub>Rb<sub><i>n</i></sub> (<i>n</i> = 1, 2, 3).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The final product of oxygenation of Rb is principally RbO<sub>2</sub>, rubidium <a href="/wiki/Superoxide" title="Superoxide">superoxide</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>Rb  +  O<sub>2</sub>  →  RbO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>This superoxide can then be reduced to Rb<sub>2</sub>O using excess rubidium metal:
</p>
<dl><dd>3 Rb  +  RbO<sub>2</sub>  →  2 Rb<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
