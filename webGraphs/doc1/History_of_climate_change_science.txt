<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">History of climate change science</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Global_Temperature_Trends.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Global_Temperature_Trends.png/350px-Global_Temperature_Trends.png" width="350" height="186" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Global_Temperature_Trends.png/525px-Global_Temperature_Trends.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Global_Temperature_Trends.png/700px-Global_Temperature_Trends.png 2x" data-file-width="904" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Global_Temperature_Trends.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Global Temperature Trends 1880–2017</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>history of the scientific discovery of climate change</b> began in the early 19th century when <a href="/wiki/Ice_ages" class="mw-redirect" title="Ice ages">ice ages</a> and other natural changes in <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimate" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleoclimate">paleoclimate</a> were first suspected and the natural <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a> first identified. In the late 19th century, scientists first argued that human emissions of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a> could change the <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>. Many other theories of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> were advanced, involving forces from <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanism</a> to <a href="/wiki/Solar_variation" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar variation">solar variation</a>. In the 1960s, the warming effect of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> gas became increasingly convincing. Some scientists also pointed out that human activities that generated atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Aerosols" class="mw-redirect" title="Aerosols">aerosols</a> (e.g., "pollution") could have cooling effects as well. During the 1970s, scientific opinion increasingly favored the warming viewpoint. By the 1990s, as a result of improving fidelity of <a href="/wiki/Computer_models" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer models">computer models</a> and observational work confirming the <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Milankovitch theory">Milankovitch theory</a> of the ice ages, a consensus position formed: greenhouse gases were deeply involved in most climate changes and human-caused emissions were bringing discernible <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. Since the 1990s, scientific research on climate change has included multiple disciplines and has expanded. Research has expanded our understanding of causal relations, links with historic data and ability to model climate change numerically.  Research during this period has been summarized in the Assessment Reports by the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a>.
</p><p>Climate change is a significant and lasting change in the statistical distribution of weather patterns over periods ranging from decades to millions of years. It may be a change in average weather conditions, or in the distribution of weather around the average conditions (such as more or fewer <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming#Extreme_weather" title="Effects of global warming">extreme weather</a> events). Climate change is caused by factors that include oceanic processes (such as oceanic circulation), biotic processes (e.g., plants), variations in <a href="/wiki/Solar_irradiance" title="Solar irradiance">solar radiation</a> received by Earth, <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanic eruptions</a>, and human-induced alterations of the natural world. The latter effect is currently causing global warming, and "climate change" is often used to describe human-specific impacts.
</p>

<h2><span id="Regional_changes.2C_antiquity_through_19th_century"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_changes,_antiquity_through_19th_century">Regional changes, antiquity through 19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_climate_change_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Regional changes, antiquity through 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a></div>
<p>From ancient times, people suspected that the climate of a region could change over the course of centuries. For example, <a href="/wiki/Theophrastus" title="Theophrastus">Theophrastus</a>, a pupil of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, told how the draining of marshes had made a particular locality more susceptible to freezing, and speculated that lands became warmer when the clearing of forests exposed them to sunlight. Renaissance and later scholars saw that deforestation, irrigation, and grazing had altered the lands around the Mediterranean since ancient times; they thought it plausible that these human interventions had affected the local weather.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a>, in the first century BC, wrote about climate in relation to housing architecture and how to choose locations for cities.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The most striking change came in the 18th and 19th centuries, obvious within a single lifetime: the conversion of Eastern North America from forest to croplands. By the early 19th century many believed the transformation was altering the region's climate—probably for the better.  When farmers, called "sodbusters", took over the Great Plains they were told that "rain follows the plough."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Not everyone agreed. Some experts reported that deforestation not only caused rainwater to run off rapidly in useless floods, but reduced rainfall itself. European professors, alert to any proof that their nations were wiser than others, claimed that the Orientals of the Ancient Near East had heedlessly converted their once lush lands into impoverished deserts.<sup id="cite_ref-aiphist2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aiphist2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>	
</p><p>Meanwhile, national weather agencies had begun to compile masses of reliable observations of temperature, rainfall, and the like. When the figures were analyzed they showed many rises and dips, but no steady long-term change. By the end of the 19th century, scientific opinion had turned decisively against any belief in a human influence on climate. And whatever the regional effects, few imagined that humans could affect the climate of the planet as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-aiphist2_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aiphist2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Paleoclimate_change_and_theories_of_its_causes.2C_19th_century"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleoclimate_change_and_theories_of_its_causes,_19th_century">Paleoclimate change and theories of its causes, 19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_climate_change_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Paleoclimate change and theories of its causes, 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Siebenschneiderstein_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Siebenschneiderstein_1.jpg/220px-Siebenschneiderstein_1.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Siebenschneiderstein_1.jpg/330px-Siebenschneiderstein_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Siebenschneiderstein_1.jpg/440px-Siebenschneiderstein_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2499" data-file-height="1876" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Siebenschneiderstein_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Glacial_erratic" title="Glacial erratic">Erratics</a>, boulders deposited by glaciers far from any existing glaciers, led geologists to the conclusion that climate had changed in the past.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">paleoclimatology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a></div>
<p>Prior to the 18th century, scientists had not suspected that prehistoric climates were different from the modern period.  By the late 18th century, geologists found evidence of a succession of <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time_scale" title="Geologic time scale">geological ages</a> with changes in climate. There were various competing theories about these changes, and <a href="/wiki/James_Hutton" title="James Hutton">James Hutton</a>, whose ideas of cyclic change over huge periods of time were later dubbed <a href="/wiki/Uniformitarianism" title="Uniformitarianism">uniformitarianism</a>, was among those who found signs of past glacial activity in places too warm for <a href="/wiki/Glaciers" class="mw-redirect" title="Glaciers">glaciers</a> in modern times.<sup id="cite_ref-Davis_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davis-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1815 <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Perraudin" class="mw-redirect" title="Jean-Pierre Perraudin">Jean-Pierre Perraudin</a> described for the first time how glaciers might be responsible for the giant boulders seen in alpine valleys. As he hiked in the <a href="/wiki/Val_de_Bagnes" class="mw-redirect" title="Val de Bagnes">Val de Bagnes</a>, he noticed giant granite rocks that were scattered around the narrow valley. He knew that it would take an exceptional force to move such large rocks. He also noticed how glaciers left stripes on the land, and concluded that it was the ice that had carried the boulders down into the valleys.<sup id="cite_ref-nasa-paleo_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nasa-paleo-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>His idea was initially met with disbelief. <a href="/wiki/Jean_de_Charpentier" title="Jean de Charpentier">Jean de Charpentier</a> wrote, "I found his hypothesis so extraordinary and even so extravagant that I considered it as not worth examining or even considering."<sup id="cite_ref-Imbrie_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Imbrie-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Despite Charpentier's initial rejection, Perraudin eventually convinced <a href="/wiki/Ignaz_Venetz" title="Ignaz Venetz">Ignaz Venetz</a> that it might be worth studying. Venetz convinced Charpentier, who in turn convinced the influential scientist <a href="/wiki/Louis_Agassiz" title="Louis Agassiz">Louis Agassiz</a> that the glacial theory had merit.<sup id="cite_ref-nasa-paleo_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nasa-paleo-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Agassiz developed a theory of what he termed "<a href="/wiki/Ice_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Ice Age">Ice Age</a>" — when glaciers covered Europe and much of North America. In 1837 Agassiz was the first to scientifically propose that the Earth had been subject to a past <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">ice age</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-evans_1887_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-evans_1887-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_Buckland" title="William Buckland">William Buckland</a> had led attempts in Britain to adapt the geological theory of <a href="/wiki/Catastrophism" title="Catastrophism">catastrophism</a> to account for erratic boulders and other "diluvium" as relics of the <a href="/wiki/Deluge_myth" class="mw-redirect" title="Deluge myth">Biblical flood</a>. This was strongly opposed by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lyell" title="Charles Lyell">Charles Lyell</a>'s version of Hutton's uniformitarianism, and was gradually abandoned by Buckland and other catastrophist geologists. A field trip to the Alps with Agassiz in October 1838 convinced Buckland that features in Britain had been caused by glaciation, and both he and Lyell strongly supported the ice age theory which became widely accepted by the 1870s.<sup id="cite_ref-Davis_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davis-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fourier2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Fourier2.jpg/220px-Fourier2.jpg" width="220" height="278" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Fourier2.jpg/330px-Fourier2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Fourier2.jpg/440px-Fourier2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="484" data-file-height="612" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fourier2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Joseph Fourier</div></div></div>
<p>In the same general period that scientists first suspected climate change and ice ages, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Fourier" title="Joseph Fourier">Joseph Fourier</a>, in 1824, found that Earth's atmosphere kept the planet warmer than would be the case in a vacuum.  Fourier recognized that the atmosphere transmitted <a href="/wiki/Visible_light" class="mw-redirect" title="Visible light">visible light</a> waves efficiently to the earth's surface. The earth then absorbed visible light and emitted <a href="/wiki/Infrared_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Infrared radiation">infrared radiation</a> in response, but the atmosphere did not transmit infrared efficiently, which therefore increased surface temperatures.  He also suspected that human activities could influence climate, although he focused primarily on land use changes.  In an 1827 paper Fourier stated, <i>"The establishment and progress of human societies, the action of natural forces, can notably change, and in vast regions, the state of the surface, the distribution of water and the great movements of the air. Such effects are able to make to vary, in the course of many centuries, the average degree of heat; because the analytic expressions contain coefficients relating to the state of the surface and which greatly influence the temperature."</i><sup id="cite_ref-fourier1_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fourier1-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Eunice_Newton_Foote" title="Eunice Newton Foote">Eunice Newton Foote</a> studied the warming effect of the sun, including how this warming was increased by the presence of carbonic acid gas (carbon dioxide), and suggested that the surface of an Earth whose atmosphere was rich in this gas would have a higher temperature. Her work was presented by Prof. <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Henry" title="Joseph Henry">Joseph Henry</a> at the <a href="/wiki/American_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="American Association for the Advancement of Science">American Association for the Advancement of Science</a> meeting in August 1856 and published as a brief note, but scientists failed to take notice.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Foote: “The highest effect of the sun’s rays I have found to be in carbonic acid gas. ... An atmosphere of that gas would give to our earth a high temperature; and if, as some suppose, at one period of its history, the air had mixed with it a larger proportion than at present, an increased temperature from its own action, as well as from increased weight, must have necessarily resulted.”<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Tyndall" title="John Tyndall">John Tyndall</a> took Fourier's work one step further in 1859 when he investigated the absorption of infrared radiation in different gases. He found that water vapor, <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> like <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (CH<sub>4</sub>), and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) strongly block the radiation.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Some scientists suggested that ice ages and other great climate changes were due to changes in the amount of gases emitted in <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">volcanism</a>. But that was only one of many possible causes. Another obvious possibility was <a href="/wiki/Solar_variation" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar variation">solar variation</a>. Shifts in <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean currents</a> also might explain many climate changes. For changes over millions of years, the raising and lowering of mountain ranges would change patterns of both winds and ocean currents. Or perhaps the climate of a continent had not changed at all, but it had grown warmer or cooler because of <a href="/wiki/True_polar_wander" title="True polar wander">polar wander</a> (the North Pole shifting to where the Equator had been or the like). There were dozens of theories.
</p><p>For example, in the mid 19th century, <a href="/wiki/James_Croll" title="James Croll">James Croll</a> published calculations of how the gravitational pulls of the Sun, Moon, and planets subtly affect the Earth's motion and orientation. The inclination of the Earth’s axis and the shape of its orbit around the Sun oscillate gently in cycles lasting tens of thousands of years. During some periods the Northern Hemisphere would get slightly less sunlight during the winter than it would get during other centuries. Snow would accumulate, reflecting sunlight and leading to a self-sustaining ice age.<sup id="cite_ref-Imbrie_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Imbrie-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Most scientists, however, found Croll’s ideas—and every other theory of climate change—unconvincing.
</p><p>In 1876, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Kropotkin" title="Peter Kropotkin">Peter Kropotkin</a> wrote about his observations that since the Industrial Revolution, Siberian glaciers were melting.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="First_calculations_of_human-induced_climate_change.2C_1896"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="First_calculations_of_human-induced_climate_change,_1896">First calculations of human-induced climate change, 1896</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_climate_change_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First calculations of human-induced climate change, 1896">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Arrhenius2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Arrhenius2.jpg/220px-Arrhenius2.jpg" width="220" height="276" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Arrhenius2.jpg/330px-Arrhenius2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Arrhenius2.jpg/440px-Arrhenius2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="479" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Arrhenius2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In 1896 <a href="/wiki/Svante_Arrhenius" title="Svante Arrhenius">Svante Arrhenius</a> calculated the effect of a doubling atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> to be an increase in surface temperatures of 5–6 degrees Celsius.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:T.C.Chamberlin.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/T.C.Chamberlin.gif/220px-T.C.Chamberlin.gif" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/T.C.Chamberlin.gif/330px-T.C.Chamberlin.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/T.C.Chamberlin.gif/440px-T.C.Chamberlin.gif 2x" data-file-width="639" data-file-height="850" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:T.C.Chamberlin.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>T.C. Chamberlin</div></div></div>
<p>By the late 1890s, American scientist <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Pierpoint_Langley" class="mw-redirect" title="Samuel Pierpoint Langley">Samuel Pierpoint Langley</a> had attempted to determine the surface temperature of the Moon by measuring infrared radiation leaving the Moon and reaching the Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-longthaw_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-longthaw-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The angle of the Moon in the sky when a scientist took a measurement determined how much CO<sub>2</sub> and water vapor the Moon's radiation had to pass through to reach the Earth's surface, resulting in weaker measurements when the Moon was low in the sky.  This result was unsurprising given that scientists had known about <a href="/wiki/Absorption_spectroscopy" title="Absorption spectroscopy">infrared radiation absorption</a> for decades.
</p><p>In 1896, Swedish scientist, <a href="/wiki/Svante_Arrhenius" title="Svante Arrhenius">Svante Arrhenius</a>, used Langley's observations of increased infrared absorption where Moon rays pass through the atmosphere at a low angle, encountering more <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), to estimate an atmospheric cooling effect from a future decrease of CO<sub>2</sub>.  He realized that the cooler atmosphere would hold less water vapor (another <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>) and calculated the additional cooling effect. He also realized the cooling would increase snow and ice cover at high latitudes, making the planet reflect more sunlight and thus further cool down, as <a href="/wiki/James_Croll" title="James Croll">James Croll</a> had hypothesized. Overall Arrhenius calculated that cutting CO<sub>2</sub> in half would suffice to produce an ice age. He further calculated that a doubling of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> would give a total warming of 5–6 degrees Celsius.
</p><p>Further, Arrhenius' colleague Professor <a href="/wiki/Arvid_H%C3%B6gbom" title="Arvid Högbom">Arvid Högbom</a>, who was quoted in length in Arrhenius' 1896 study <i>On the Influence of Carbonic Acid in the Air upon the Temperature of the Earth</i><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> had been attempting to quantify natural sources of emissions of CO<sub>2</sub> for purposes of understanding the global <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a>.  Högbom found that estimated carbon production from industrial sources in the 1890s (mainly coal burning) was comparable with the natural sources.<sup id="cite_ref-aiphistory_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aiphistory-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
Arrhenius saw that this human emission of carbon would eventually lead to warming. However, because of the relatively low rate of CO<sub>2</sub> production in 1896, Arrhenius thought the warming would take thousands of years, and he expected it would be beneficial to humanity.<sup id="cite_ref-aiphistory_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aiphistory-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1899 <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Chrowder_Chamberlin" title="Thomas Chrowder Chamberlin">Thomas Chrowder Chamberlin</a> developed at length the idea that changes in climate could result from changes in the concentration of atmospheric carbon dioxide.<sup id="cite_ref-WH1965_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WH1965-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Chamberlin wrote in his 1899 book, <i>An Attempt to Frame a Working Hypothesis of the Cause of Glacial Periods on an Atmospheric Basis</i>:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Previous advocacy of an atmospheric hypothesis, — The general doctrine that the glacial periods may have been due to a change in the atmospheric content of carbon dioxide is not new. It was urged by Tyndall a half century ago and has been urged by others since. Recently it has been very effectively advocated by Dr. Arrhenius, who has taken a great step in advance of his predecessors in reducing his conclusions to definite quantitative terms deduced from observational data. [..] The functions of carbon dioxide. — By the investigations of Tyndall, Lecher and Pretner, Keller, Roentgen, and Arrhenius, it has been shown that the carbon dioxide and water vapor of the atmosphere have remarkable power of absorbing and temporarily retaining heat rays, while the oxygen, nitrogen, and argon of the atmosphere possess this power in a feeble degree only. It follows that the effect of the carbon dioxide and water vapor is to blanket the earth with a thermally absorbent envelope. [..] The general results assignable to a greatly increased or a greatly reduced quantity of atmospheric carbon dioxide and water may be summarized as follows:
</p><ul><li>a. An increase, by causing a larger absorption of the sun's radiant energy, raises the average temperature, while a reduction lowers it. The estimate of Dr. Arrhenius, based upon an elaborate mathematical discussion of the observations of Professor Langley, is that an increase of the carbon dioxide to the amount of two or three times the present content would elevate the average temperature 8° or 9° C. and would bring on a mild climate analogous to that which prevailed in the Middle Tertiary age.  On the other hand, a reduction of the quantity of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere to an amount ranging from 55 to 62 per cent, of the present content, would reduce the average temperature 4 or 5 C, which would bring on a glaciation comparable to that of the Pleistocene period.</li>
<li>b. A second effect of increase and decrease in the amount of atmospheric carbon dioxide is the equalization, on the one hand, of surface temperatures, or their differentiation on the other. The temperature of the surface of the earth varies with latitude, altitude, the distribution of land and water, day and night, the seasons, and some other elements that may here be neglected. It is postulated that an increase in the thermal absorption of the atmosphere equalizes the temperature, and tends to eliminate the variations attendant on these contingencies. Conversely, a reduction of thermal atmospheric absorption tends to intensify all of these variations. A secondary effect of intensification of differences of temperature is an increase of atmospheric movements in the effort to restore equilibrium. Increased atmospheric movements, which are necessarily convectional, carry the warmer air to the surface of the atmosphere, and facilitate the discharge of the heat and thus intensify the primary effect. [..]</li></ul>
<p>In the case of the outgoing rays, which are absorbed in much larger proportions than the incoming rays because they are more largely long-wave rays, the tables of Arrhenius show that the absorption is augmented by increase of carbonic acid in greater proportions in high latitudes than in low; for example, the increase of temperature for three times the present content of carbonic acid is 21.5 per cent, greater between 60° and 70° N. latitude than at the equator.
</p><p>It now becomes necessary to assign agencies capable of removing carbon dioxide from the atmosphere at a rate sufficiently above the normal rate of supply, at certain times, to produce glaciation; and on the other hand, capable of restoring it to the atmosphere at certain other times in sufficient amounts to produce mild climates.
</p><p>When the temperature is rising after a glacial episode, dissociation is promoted, and the ocean gives forth its carbon dioxide at an increased rate, and thereby assists in accelerating the amelioration of climate.
</p><p>A study of the life of the geological periods seems to indicate that there were very notable fluctuations in the total mass of living matter. To be sure there was a reciprocal relation between the life of the land and that of the sea, so that when the latter was extended upon the continental platforms and greatly augmented, the former was contracted, but notwithstanding this it seems clear that the sum of life activity fluctuated notably during the ages. It is believed that on the whole it was greatest at the periods of sea extension and mild climates, and least at the times of disruption and climatic intensification. This factor then acted antithetically to the carbonic acid freeing previously noted, and, so far as it went, tended to offset its effects.
</p><p>In periods of sea extension and of land reduction (base-level periods in particular), the habitat of shallow water lime-secreting life is concurrently extended, giving to the agencies that set carbon dioxide free accelerated activity, which is further aided by the consequent rising temperature which reduces the absorptive power of the ocean and increases dissociation. At the same time, the area of the land being diminished, a low consumption of carbon dioxide both in original decomposition of the silicates and in the solution of the limestones and dolomites obtains.
</p><p>Thus the reciprocating agencies again conjoin, but now to increase the carbon dioxide of the air. These are the great and essential factors. They are modified by several subordinate agencies already mentioned, but the quantitative effect of these is thought to be quite insufficient to prevent very notable fluctuations in the atmospheric constitution.
</p><p>As a result, it is postulated that geological history has been accentuated by an alternation of climatic episodes embracing, on the one hand, periods of mild, equable, moist climate nearly uniform for the whole globe; and on the other, periods when there were extremes of aridity and precipitation, and of heat and cold; these last denoted by deposits of salt and gypsum, of subaerial conglomerates, of red sandstones and shales, of arkose deposits, and occasionally by glaciation in low latitudes.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span id="Paleoclimates_and_sunspots.2C_early_1900s_to_1950s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleoclimates_and_sunspots,_early_1900s_to_1950s">Paleoclimates and sunspots, early 1900s to 1950s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_climate_change_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Paleoclimates and sunspots, early 1900s to 1950s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Arrhenius's calculations were disputed and subsumed into a larger debate over whether atmospheric changes had caused the ice ages.  Experimental attempts to measure infrared absorption in the laboratory seemed to show little differences resulted from increasing CO<sub>2</sub> levels, and also found significant overlap between absorption by CO<sub>2</sub> and absorption by water vapor, all of which suggested that increasing carbon dioxide emissions would have little climatic effect. These early experiments were later found to be insufficiently accurate, given the instrumentation of the time. Many scientists also thought that the oceans would quickly absorb any excess carbon dioxide.<sup id="cite_ref-aiphistory_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aiphistory-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other theories of the causes of climate change fared no better. The principal advances were in observational <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">paleoclimatology</a>, as scientists in various fields of <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> worked out methods to reveal ancient climates.  <a href="/wiki/Wilmot_H._Bradley" class="mw-redirect" title="Wilmot H. Bradley">Wilmot H. Bradley</a> found that annual <a href="/wiki/Varve" title="Varve">varves</a> of clay laid down in lake beds showed climate cycles. An Arizona astronomer, <a href="/wiki/A._E._Douglass" title="A. E. Douglass">Andrew Ellicott Douglass</a>, saw strong indications of climate change in <a href="/wiki/Tree_rings" class="mw-redirect" title="Tree rings">tree rings</a>. Noting that the rings were thinner in dry years, he reported climate effects from solar variations, particularly in connection with the 17th-century dearth of <a href="/wiki/Sunspots" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunspots">sunspots</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Maunder_Minimum" title="Maunder Minimum">Maunder Minimum</a>) noticed previously by <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">William Herschel</a> and others. Other scientists, however, found good reason to doubt that tree rings could reveal anything beyond random regional variations. The value of tree rings for climate study was not solidly established until the 1960s.<sup id="cite_ref-aipSun_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aipSun-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hufbauer_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hufbauer-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Through the 1930s the most persistent advocate of a solar-climate connection was astrophysicist <a href="/wiki/Charles_Greeley_Abbot" title="Charles Greeley Abbot">Charles Greeley Abbot</a>. By the early 1920s, he had concluded that the <a href="/wiki/Solar_constant" title="Solar constant">solar "constant"</a> was misnamed: his observations showed large variations, which he connected with <a href="/wiki/Sunspots" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunspots">sunspots</a> passing across the face of the Sun. He and a few others pursued the topic into the 1960s, convinced that sunspot variations were a main cause of climate change. Other scientists were skeptical.<sup id="cite_ref-aipSun_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aipSun-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hufbauer_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hufbauer-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, attempts to connect the <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">solar cycle</a> with climate cycles were popular in the 1920s and 1930s. Respected scientists announced correlations that they insisted were reliable enough to make predictions. Sooner or later, every prediction failed, and the subject fell into disrepute.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Meanwhile, the Serbian engineer <a href="/wiki/Milutin_Milankovitch" class="mw-redirect" title="Milutin Milankovitch">Milutin Milankovitch</a>, building on <a href="/wiki/James_Croll" title="James Croll">James Croll</a>'s theory, improved the tedious calculations of the varying distances and angles of the Sun's radiation as the Sun and Moon gradually perturbed the Earth's orbit. Some observations of <a href="/wiki/Varves" class="mw-redirect" title="Varves">varves</a> (layers seen in the mud covering the bottom of lakes) matched the prediction of a <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Milankovitch cycle">Milankovitch cycle</a> lasting about 21,000 years. However, most geologists dismissed the astronomical theory. For they could not fit Milankovitch’s timing to the accepted sequence, which had only four ice ages, all of them much longer than 21,000 years.<sup id="cite_ref-aipCycles_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aipCycles-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1938 a British engineer, <a href="/wiki/Guy_Stewart_Callendar" title="Guy Stewart Callendar">Guy Stewart Callendar</a>, attempted to revive Arrhenius's greenhouse-effect theory. Callendar presented evidence that both temperature and the CO<sub>2</sub> level in the atmosphere had been rising over the past half-century, and he argued that newer <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopic" class="mw-redirect" title="Spectroscopic">spectroscopic</a> measurements showed that the gas was effective in absorbing infrared in the atmosphere. Nevertheless, most scientific opinion continued to dispute or ignore the theory.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Increasing_concern.2C_1950s_.E2.80.93_1960s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Increasing_concern,_1950s_–_1960s">Increasing concern, 1950s – 1960s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_climate_change_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Increasing concern, 1950s – 1960s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Charles_David_Keeling_2001.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Charles_David_Keeling_2001.jpg/220px-Charles_David_Keeling_2001.jpg" width="220" height="277" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Charles_David_Keeling_2001.jpg/330px-Charles_David_Keeling_2001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Charles_David_Keeling_2001.jpg/440px-Charles_David_Keeling_2001.jpg 2x" data-file-width="801" data-file-height="1008" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Charles_David_Keeling_2001.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Charles Keeling, receiving the <a href="/wiki/National_Medal_of_Science" title="National Medal of Science">National Medal of Science</a> from <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>, in 2001.</div></div></div>
<p>Better <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">spectrography</a> in the 1950s showed that CO<sub>2</sub> and water vapor absorption lines did not overlap completely.  Climatologists also realized that little water vapor was present in the upper atmosphere.  Both developments showed that the CO<sub>2</sub> greenhouse effect would not be overwhelmed by water vapor.<sup id="cite_ref-aiphistory_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aiphistory-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1955 <a href="/wiki/Hans_Suess" title="Hans Suess">Hans Suess</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Carbon-14" title="Carbon-14">carbon-14</a> isotope analysis showed that CO<sub>2</sub> released from fossil fuels was not immediately absorbed by the ocean. In 1957, better understanding of ocean chemistry led <a href="/wiki/Roger_Revelle" title="Roger Revelle">Roger Revelle</a> to a realization that the ocean surface layer had limited ability to absorb carbon dioxide, also predicting the rise in levels of CO<sub>2</sub> and later being proven by <a href="/wiki/Charles_David_Keeling" title="Charles David Keeling">Charles David Keeling</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>  By the late 1950s, more scientists were arguing that carbon dioxide emissions could be a problem, with some projecting in 1959 that CO<sub>2</sub> would rise 25% by the year 2000, with potentially "radical" effects on climate.<sup id="cite_ref-aiphistory_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aiphistory-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In 1960 <a href="/wiki/Charles_David_Keeling" title="Charles David Keeling">Charles David Keeling</a> demonstrated that the level of CO<sub>2</sub> in the atmosphere was in fact rising. Concern mounted year by year along with the rise of the "<a href="/wiki/Keeling_Curve" title="Keeling Curve">Keeling Curve</a>" of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p><p>Another clue to the nature of climate change came in the mid-1960s from analysis of deep-sea cores by <a href="/wiki/Cesare_Emiliani" title="Cesare Emiliani">Cesare Emiliani</a> and analysis of ancient corals by <a href="/wiki/Wallace_Broecker" class="mw-redirect" title="Wallace Broecker">Wallace Broecker</a> and collaborators. Rather than four long <a href="/wiki/Ice_ages" class="mw-redirect" title="Ice ages">ice ages</a>, they found a large number of shorter ones in a regular sequence. It appeared that the timing of ice ages was set by the small orbital shifts of the <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a>. While the matter remained controversial, some began to suggest that the climate system is sensitive to small changes and can readily be flipped from a stable state into a different one.<sup id="cite_ref-aipCycles_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aipCycles-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Scientists meanwhile began using computers to develop more sophisticated versions of Arrhenius's calculations. In 1967, taking advantage of the ability of digital computers to integrate absorption curves numerically, <a href="/wiki/Syukuro_Manabe" title="Syukuro Manabe">Syukuro Manabe</a> and Richard Wetherald made the first detailed calculation of the greenhouse effect incorporating convection (the "Manabe-Wetherald one-dimensional radiative-convective model").<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> They found that, in the absence of unknown feedbacks such as changes in clouds, a doubling of carbon dioxide from the current level would result in approximately 2&#160;°C increase in global temperature.
</p><p>By the 1960s, <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosol</a> pollution ("smog") had become a serious local problem in many cities, and some scientists began to consider whether the cooling effect of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">particulate</a> pollution could affect global temperatures.  Scientists were unsure whether the cooling effect of particulate pollution or warming effect of greenhouse gas emissions would predominate, but regardless, began to suspect that human emissions could be disruptive to climate in the 21st century if not sooner.  In his 1968 book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Population_Bomb" title="The Population Bomb">The Population Bomb</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_R._Ehrlich" title="Paul R. Ehrlich">Paul R. Ehrlich</a> wrote, "the greenhouse effect is being enhanced now by the greatly increased level of carbon dioxide... [this] is being countered by low-level clouds generated by contrails, dust, and other contaminants... At the moment we cannot predict what the overall climatic results will be of our using the atmosphere as a garbage dump."<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1965, the landmark report, "Restoring the Quality of Our Environment" by U.S. President Lyndon B. Johnson’s Science Advisory Committee warned of the harmful effects of fossil fuel emissions:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The part that remains in the atmosphere may have a significant effect on climate; carbon dioxide is nearly transparent to visible light, but it is a strong absorber and back radiator of infrared radiation, particularly in the wave lengths from 12 to 18 microns; consequently, an increase of atmospheric carbon dioxide could act, much like the glass in a greenhouse, to raise the temperature of the lower air.<sup id="cite_ref-WH1965_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WH1965-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>A 1968 study by the <a href="/wiki/Stanford_Research_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Stanford Research Institute">Stanford Research Institute</a> for the <a href="/wiki/American_Petroleum_Institute" title="American Petroleum Institute">American Petroleum Institute</a> noted:<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>If the earth's temperature increases significantly, a number of events might be expected to occur, including the melting of the Antarctic ice cap, a rise in sea levels, warming of the oceans, and an increase in photosynthesis. [..]  Revelle makes the point that man is now engaged in a vast geophysical experiment with his environment, the earth. Significant temperature changes are almost certain to occur by the year 2000 and these could bring about climatic changes.
</p></blockquote>
<p>In 1969, <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> was the first candidate to deal with climate change on an international level. It was planned then to establish a hub of research and initiatives of the organization in the civil area, dealing with environmental topics<sup id="cite_ref-Hüne_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hüne-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> as <a href="/wiki/Acid_Rain" class="mw-redirect" title="Acid Rain">Acid Rain</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">Greenhouse effect</a>. The suggestion of US President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> was not very successful with the administration of German Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Georg_Kiesinger" title="Kurt Georg Kiesinger">Kurt Georg Kiesinger</a>. But the topics and the preparation work done on the NATO proposal by the German authorities gained international momentum, (see e.g. the Stockholm <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_the_Human_Environment" title="United Nations Conference on the Human Environment">United Nations Conference on the Human Environment</a> 1970) as the government of <a href="/wiki/Willy_Brandt" title="Willy Brandt">Willy Brandt</a> started to apply them on the civil sphere instead.<sup id="cite_ref-Hüne_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hüne-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="very unclear English, not sure what is being said after some effort to understand. (December 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Also in 1969, <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Budyko" title="Mikhail Budyko">Mikhail Budyko</a> published a theory on the <a href="/wiki/Ice-albedo_feedback" class="mw-redirect" title="Ice-albedo feedback">ice-albedo feedback</a>, a center piece of what is today known as <a href="/wiki/Arctic_amplification" class="mw-redirect" title="Arctic amplification">Arctic amplification</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The same year a similar model was published by <a href="/wiki/William_D._Sellers" title="William D. Sellers">William D. Sellers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Both studies attracted significant attention since they hinted at the possibility for a runaway positive feedback within the global climate system.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Scientists_increasingly_predict_warming.2C_1970s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientists_increasingly_predict_warming,_1970s">Scientists increasingly predict warming, 1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_climate_change_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Scientists increasingly predict warming, 1970s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Global_Cooling_Map.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Global_Cooling_Map.png/220px-Global_Cooling_Map.png" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Global_Cooling_Map.png/330px-Global_Cooling_Map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Global_Cooling_Map.png/440px-Global_Cooling_Map.png 2x" data-file-width="758" data-file-height="492" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Global_Cooling_Map.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mean temperature anomalies during the period 1965 to 1975 with respect to the average temperatures from 1937 to 1946. This dataset was not available at the time.</div></div></div>
<p>In the early 1970s, evidence that aerosols were increasing worldwide encouraged <a href="/wiki/Reid_Bryson" title="Reid Bryson">Reid Bryson</a> and some others to warn of the possibility of severe cooling. Meanwhile, the new evidence that the timing of ice ages was set by predictable orbital cycles suggested that the climate would gradually cool, over thousands of years. For the century ahead, however, a survey of the scientific literature from 1965 to 1979 found 7 articles predicting cooling and 44 predicting warming (many other articles on climate made no prediction); the warming articles were cited much more often in subsequent scientific literature.<sup id="cite_ref-globalcoolingmyth_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globalcoolingmyth-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Several scientific panels from this time period concluded that more research was needed to determine whether warming or cooling was likely, indicating that the trend in the scientific literature had not yet become a consensus.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/John_Sawyer_(meteorologist)" title="John Sawyer (meteorologist)">John Sawyer</a> published the study <i>Man-made Carbon Dioxide and the “Greenhouse” Effect</i> in 1972.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> He summarized the knowledge of the science at the time, the anthropogenic attribution of the carbon dioxide greenhouse gas, distribution and exponential rise, findings which still hold today. Additionally he accurately predicted the rate of global warming for the period between 1972 and 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p><i>The increase of 25% CO2 expected by the end of the century therefore corresponds to an increase of 0.6°C in the world temperature – an amount somewhat greater than the climatic variation of recent centuries</i>. – John Sawyer, 1972
</p></blockquote>
<p>The mainstream news media at the time exaggerated the warnings of the minority who expected imminent cooling. For example, in 1975, <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i> magazine published a story that warned of "ominous signs that the Earth's weather patterns have begun to change."<sup id="cite_ref-newsweek-1975_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newsweek-1975-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> The article continued by stating that evidence of global cooling was so strong that meteorologists were having "a hard time keeping up with it."<sup id="cite_ref-newsweek-1975_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newsweek-1975-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> On 23 October 2006, <i>Newsweek</i> issued an update stating that it had been "spectacularly wrong about the near-term future".<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the first two "Reports for the Club of Rome" in 1972<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> and 1974,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> the anthropogenic climate changes by CO<sub>2</sub> increase as well as by <a href="/wiki/Waste_heat" title="Waste heat">Waste heat</a> were mentioned. About the latter <a href="/wiki/John_Holdren" title="John Holdren">John Holdren</a> wrote in a study<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> cited in the 1st report, “… that global thermal pollution is hardly our most immediate environmental threat. It could prove to be the most inexorable, however, if we are fortunate enough to evade all the rest.” Simple global-scale estimates<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> that recently have been actualized<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> and confirmed by more refined model calculations<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-flanner_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-flanner-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> show noticeable contributions from waste heat to global warming after the year 2100, if its growth rates are not strongly reduced (below the averaged 2% p.a. which occurred since 1973).
</p><p>Evidence for warming accumulated. By 1975, Manabe and Wetherald had developed a three-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Global_climate_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Global climate model">Global climate model</a> that gave a roughly accurate representation of the current climate. Doubling CO<sub>2</sub> in the model's atmosphere gave a roughly 2&#160;°C rise in global temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> Several other kinds of computer models gave similar results: it was impossible to make a model that gave something resembling the actual climate and not have the temperature rise when the CO<sub>2</sub> concentration was increased.
</p><p>In a separate development, an analysis of deep-sea cores published in 1976 by <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Shackleton" title="Nicholas Shackleton">Nicholas Shackleton</a> and colleagues showed that the dominating influence on ice age timing came from a 100,000-year Milankovitch orbital change. This was unexpected, since the change in sunlight in that cycle was slight. The result emphasized that the climate system is driven by feedbacks, and thus is strongly susceptible to small changes in conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-Imbrie_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Imbrie-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1979 <a href="/wiki/World_Climate_Conference" title="World Climate Conference">World Climate Conference</a> (12 to 23 February) of the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> concluded "it appears plausible that an increased amount of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere can contribute to a gradual warming of the lower atmosphere, especially at higher latitudes....It is possible that some effects on a regional and global scale may be detectable before the end of this century and become significant before the middle of the next century."<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 1979 the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Research Council">United States National Research Council</a> published a report,<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
concluding (in part):
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>When it is assumed that the CO<sub>2</sub> content of the atmosphere is doubled and statistical thermal equilibrium is achieved, the more realistic of the modeling efforts predict a global surface warming of between 2°C and 3.5°C, with greater increases at high latitudes.
</p><p>… we have tried but have been unable to find any overlooked or underestimated physical effects that could reduce the currently estimated global warmings due to a doubling of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> to negligible proportions or reverse them altogether.
</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span id="Consensus_begins_to_form.2C_1980.E2.80.931988"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Consensus_begins_to_form,_1980–1988">Consensus begins to form, 1980–1988</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_climate_change_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Consensus begins to form, 1980–1988">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Attribution_of_recent_climate_change" title="Attribution of recent climate change">Attribution of recent climate change</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:James_Hansen_Crop1.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/James_Hansen_Crop1.png/220px-James_Hansen_Crop1.png" width="220" height="196" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fc/James_Hansen_Crop1.png 1.5x" data-file-width="270" data-file-height="240" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:James_Hansen_Crop1.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>James Hansen during his 1988 testimony to Congress, which alerted the public to the dangers of global warming.</div></div></div>
<p>By the early 1980s, the slight cooling trend from 1945–1975 had stopped.  Aerosol pollution had decreased in many areas due to environmental legislation and changes in fuel use, and it became clear that the cooling effect from aerosols was not going to increase substantially while carbon dioxide levels were progressively increasing.
</p><p>
Hansen et al. 1981, published the study <i>Climate impact of increasing atmospheric carbon dioxide</i>, and noted: </p><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>It is shown that the anthropogenic carbon dioxide warming should emerge from the noise level of natural climate variability by the end of the century, and there is a high probability of warming in the 1980s. Potential effects on climate in the 21st century include the creation of drought-prone regions in North America and central Asia as part of a shifting of climatic zones, erosion of the West Antarctic ice sheet with a consequent worldwide rise in sea level, and opening of the fabled Northwest Passage.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>In 1982, <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">ice cores</a> drilled by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Oeschger" title="Hans Oeschger">Hans Oeschger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willi_Dansgaard" title="Willi Dansgaard">Willi Dansgaard</a>, and collaborators revealed dramatic temperature oscillations in the space of a century in the distant past.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> The most prominent of the changes in their record corresponded to the violent <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a> climate oscillation seen in shifts in types of pollen in lake beds all over Europe. Evidently drastic climate changes were possible within a human lifetime.
</p><p>In 1973, British scientist <a href="/wiki/James_Lovelock" title="James Lovelock">James Lovelock</a> speculated that <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">chlorofluorocarbons</a> (CFCs) could have a global warming effect.  In 1975, <a href="/wiki/Veerabhadran_Ramanathan" title="Veerabhadran Ramanathan">V. Ramanathan</a> found that a CFC molecule could be 10,000 times more effective in absorbing infrared radiation than a carbon dioxide molecule, making CFCs potentially important despite their very low concentrations in the atmosphere.  While most early work on CFCs focused on their role in <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a>, by 1985 Ramanathan and others showed that CFCs together with methane and other trace gases could have nearly as important a climate effect as increases in CO<sub>2</sub>. In other words, global warming would arrive twice as fast as had been expected.<sup id="cite_ref-aiphistoryother_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aiphistoryother-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1985 a joint UNEP/WMO/ICSU Conference on the "Assessment of the Role of Carbon Dioxide and Other Greenhouse Gases in Climate Variations and Associated Impacts"  concluded that greenhouse gases "are expected" to cause significant warming in the next century and that some warming is inevitable.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Meanwhile, ice cores drilled by a Franco-Soviet team at the <a href="/wiki/Vostok_Station" title="Vostok Station">Vostok Station</a> in <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> showed that CO<sub>2</sub> and temperature had gone up and down together in wide swings through past ice ages. This confirmed the CO<sub>2</sub>-temperature relationship in a manner entirely independent of computer climate models, strongly reinforcing the emerging scientific consensus. The findings also pointed to powerful biological and geochemical feedbacks.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 1988, <a href="/wiki/James_E._Hansen" class="mw-redirect" title="James E. Hansen">James E. Hansen</a> made one of the first assessments that human-caused warming had already measurably affected global climate.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Shortly after, a "World Conference on the Changing Atmosphere: Implications for Global Security" gathered hundreds of scientists and others in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>. They concluded that the changes in the atmosphere due to human pollution "represent a major threat to international security and are already having harmful consequences over many parts of the globe," and declared that by 2005 the world should push its emissions some 20% below the 1988 level.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1980s saw important breakthroughs with regard to global environmental challenges. E.g. <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">Ozone depletion</a> was mitigated by the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_the_Ozone_Layer" title="Vienna Convention for the Protection of the Ozone Layer">Vienna Convention</a> (1985) and the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a> (1987). <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">Acid rain</a> was mainly regulated on the national and regional level.
</p>

<p>In 1988 the WMO established the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> with the support of the UNEP. The IPCC continues its work through the present day, and issues a series of Assessment Reports and supplemental reports that describe the state of scientific understanding at the time each report is prepared. Scientific developments during this period are summarized about once every five to six years in the IPCC Assessment Reports which were published in 1990 (<a href="/wiki/IPCC_First_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC First Assessment Report">First Assessment Report</a>), 1995 (<a href="/wiki/IPCC_Second_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Second Assessment Report">Second Assessment Report</a>), 2001 (<a href="/wiki/IPCC_Third_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Third Assessment Report">Third Assessment Report</a>), 2007 (<a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fourth Assessment Report">Fourth Assessment Report</a>), and 2013/2014 (<a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fifth Assessment Report">Fifth Assessment Report</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the 1990s, research on climate change has expanded and grown, linking many fields such as atmospheric sciences, numeric modeling, behavioral sciences, geology and economics, or <a href="/wiki/Climate_security" title="Climate security">security</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_of_other_climate_changing_factors">Discovery of other climate changing factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_climate_change_science&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Discovery of other climate changing factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b><a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">Methane</a>:</b> In 1859, <a href="/wiki/John_Tyndall" title="John Tyndall">John Tyndall</a> determined that <a href="/wiki/Coal_gas" title="Coal gas">coal gas</a>, a mix of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and other gases, strongly absorbed infrared radiation.  Methane was subsequently detected in the atmosphere in 1948, and in the 1980s scientists realized that human emissions were having a substantial impact.<sup id="cite_ref-aiphistoryother_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aiphistoryother-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">Chlorofluorocarbon</a>:</b> In 1973, British scientist <a href="/wiki/James_Lovelock" title="James Lovelock">James Lovelock</a> speculated that chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) could have a global warming effect.  In 1975, <a href="/wiki/Veerabhadran_Ramanathan" title="Veerabhadran Ramanathan">V. Ramanathan</a> found that a CFC molecule could be 10,000 times more effective in absorbing infrared radiation than a carbon dioxide molecule, making CFCs potentially important despite their very low concentrations in the atmosphere.  While most early work on CFCs focused on their role in <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a>, by 1985 scientists had concluded that CFCs together with methane and other trace gases could have nearly as important a climate effect as increases in CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-aiphistoryother_63-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aiphistoryother-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
