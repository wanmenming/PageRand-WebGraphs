<p><b>Iron(II) oxide</b> or <b>ferrous oxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the formula FeO.  Its mineral form is known as <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCstite" title="Wüstite">wüstite</a>. One of several <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxides</a>, it is a black-colored powder that is sometimes confused with <a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">rust</a>, the latter of which consists of hydrated <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide" title="Iron(III) oxide">iron(III) oxide</a> (ferric oxide). Iron(II) oxide also refers to a family of related <a href="/wiki/Non-stoichiometric_compound" title="Non-stoichiometric compound">non-stoichiometric compounds</a>, which are typically iron deficient with compositions ranging from Fe<sub>0.84</sub>O to Fe<sub>0.95</sub>O.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>FeO can be prepared by the thermal decomposition of <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_oxalate" title="Iron(II) oxalate">iron(II) oxalate</a>. 
</p>
<dl><dd>FeC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>   →   FeO  +  CO<sub>2</sub>  +  CO</dd></dl>
<p>The procedure is conducted under an inert atmosphere to avoid the formation of <a href="/wiki/Ferric_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferric oxide">ferric oxide</a>.  A similar procedure can also be used for the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Manganous_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Manganous oxide">manganous oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stannous_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Stannous oxide">stannous oxide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Stoichiometric FeO can be prepared by heating Fe<sub>0.95</sub>O with metallic iron at 770&#160;°C and 36&#160;kbar.<sup id="cite_ref-Wells_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wells-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>FeO is thermodynamically unstable below 575&#160;°C, tending to disproportionate to metal and <a href="/wiki/Iron(II,III)_oxide" title="Iron(II,III) oxide">Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub></a>:<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>4FeO &#8594; Fe + Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Iron(II) oxide adopts the cubic, <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">rock salt</a> structure, where iron atoms are octahedrally coordinated by oxygen atoms and the oxygen atoms octahedrally coordinated by iron atoms. The non-stoichiometry occurs because of the ease of oxidation of Fe<sup>II</sup> to Fe<sup>III</sup> effectively replacing a small portion of Fe<sup>II</sup> with two thirds their number of Fe<sup>III</sup>, which take up tetrahedral positions in the close packed oxide lattice.<sup id="cite_ref-Wells_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wells-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Below 200&#160;K there is a minor change to the structure which changes the symmetry to rhombohedral and samples become <a href="/wiki/Antiferromagnetism" title="Antiferromagnetism">antiferromagnetic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wells_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wells-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_in_nature">Occurrence in nature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Occurrence in nature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Iron(II) oxide makes up approximately 9% of the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>. Within the mantle, it may be electrically conductive, which is a possible explanation for perturbations in Earth's rotation not accounted for by accepted models of the mantle's properties.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Limestone_building_with_pollution.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Limestone_building_with_pollution.jpg/220px-Limestone_building_with_pollution.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Limestone_building_with_pollution.jpg/330px-Limestone_building_with_pollution.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Limestone_building_with_pollution.jpg/440px-Limestone_building_with_pollution.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1598" data-file-height="1199" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Limestone_building_with_pollution.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Iron III hydroxide staining caused by oxidation of dissolved iron II and precipitation, <a href="/wiki/Perth" title="Perth">Perth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Iron dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> is in the reduced iron II form. If this groundwater comes in contact with oxygen at the surface, e.g. in natural <a href="/wiki/Spring_(hydrology)" title="Spring (hydrology)">springs</a>, iron II is oxidised to iron III and forms insoluble hydroxides in water.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Iron(II) oxide is used as a <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a>. It is <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">FDA</a>-approved for use in cosmetics and it is used in some <a href="/wiki/Tattoo" title="Tattoo">tattoo</a> inks.  It can also be used as a phosphate remover from home aquaria.
</p>
