<p><b>Manganese(II) oxide</b>  is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> MnO.<sup id="cite_ref-Reidies_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reidies-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It forms green crystals.  The compound is produced on a large scale as a component of <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a> and food additives.
</p>

<h2><span id="Structure.2C_stoichiometry.2C_reactivity"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure,_stoichiometry,_reactivity">Structure, stoichiometry, reactivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure, stoichiometry, reactivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like many monoxides, MnO adopts the rock salt structure, where cations and anions are both octahedrally coordinated. Also like many oxides, manganese(II) oxide is often <a href="/wiki/Nonstoichiometric" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonstoichiometric">nonstoichiometric</a>: its composition can vary from MnO to MnO<sub>1.045</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Below 118 K MnO is <a href="/wiki/Antiferromagnetism" title="Antiferromagnetism">antiferromagnetic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> MnO has the distinction of being one of the first compounds<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> to have its magnetic structure determined by neutron diffraction, the report appearing in 1951.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This study  showed that the Mn<sup>2+</sup> ions form a face centered cubic magnetic sub-lattice where there are ferromagnetically coupled sheets that are anti-parallel with adjacent sheets.
</p><p>Manganese(II) oxide undergoes the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_reaction" title="Chemical reaction">chemical reactions</a> typical of an ionic oxide. Upon treatment with acids, it converts to the corresponding manganese(II) salt and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Oxidation of manganese(II) oxide gives <a href="/wiki/Manganese(III)_oxide" title="Manganese(III) oxide">manganese(III) oxide</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation_and_occurrence">Preparation and occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation and occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>MnO occurs in nature as the rare mineral <a href="/wiki/Manganosite" title="Manganosite">manganosite</a>.<br />  Commercially it is prepared by reduction of MnO<sub>2</sub> with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> or <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, e.g.:<sup id="cite_ref-Reidies_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reidies-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>MnO<sub>2</sub>  +  H<sub>2</sub>  →  MnO  +  H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
<dd>MnO<sub>2</sub> + CO → MnO + CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>MnO can also be prepared by decarbonation of the carbonate:<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>MnCO<sub>3</sub>  →   MnO + CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Calcining" class="mw-redirect" title="Calcining">calcining</a> process is conducted anaerobically, lest <a href="/wiki/Manganese(III)_oxide" title="Manganese(III) oxide">Mn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></a> form.
</p><p>An alternative route, mostly for demonstration purposes, is the oxalate method, which also applicable to the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Ferrous_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferrous oxide">ferrous oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stannous_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Stannous oxide">stannous oxide</a>. Upon heating in an oxygen-free atmosphere (usually CO<sub>2</sub>), manganese(II) oxalate decomposes into MnO:<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>MnC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O   →   MnO  +  CO<sub>2</sub>  +  CO  +  2 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese(II)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Together with manganese sulfate, MnO is a component of <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizers</a> and food additives.  Many thousands of tons are consumed annually for this purpose. Other uses include: a <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Allyl_alcohol" title="Allyl alcohol">allyl alcohol</a>, ceramics, paints, colored glass, bleaching tallow and textile printing.<sup id="cite_ref-Reidies_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reidies-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
