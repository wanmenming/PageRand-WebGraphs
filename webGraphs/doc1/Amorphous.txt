<p>In <a href="/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics" title="Condensed matter physics">condensed matter physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a>, an <b>amorphous</b> (from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <i>a</i>, without, <i>morphé</i>, shape, form) or <b>non-crystalline solid</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">solid</a> that lacks the <a href="/wiki/Long-range_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Long-range order">long-range order</a> that is characteristic of a <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystal</a>. In some older books, the term has been used synonymously with <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>. Nowadays, "glassy solid" or "amorphous solid" is considered to be the overarching concept, and glass the more special case: Glass is an amorphous solid that exhibits a <a href="/wiki/Glass_transition" title="Glass transition">glass transition</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Polymers are often amorphous. Other types of amorphous solids include <a href="/wiki/Gel" title="Gel">gels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thin_film" title="Thin film">thin films</a>, and nanostructured materials such as glass.
</p>

<p>Amorphous materials have an internal structure made of interconnected structural blocks. These blocks can be similar to the basic structural units found in the corresponding crystalline phase of the same compound.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Whether a material is <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">liquid</a> or solid depends primarily on the connectivity between its elementary building blocks so that solids are characterized by a high degree of connectivity whereas structural blocks in fluids have lower connectivity.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In pharmaceutical industry, the amorphous drugs were shown to have higher bioavailability than their crystalline counterparts due to the high solubility of amorphous phase. Moreover, certain compounds can undergo precipitation in their amorphous form <i><a href="/wiki/In_vivo_supersaturation" title="In vivo supersaturation">in vivo</a></i>, and they can decrease each other's bioavailability if administered together.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nano-structured_materials">Nano-structured materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amorphous_solid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nano-structured materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Even amorphous materials have some shortrange order at the atomic length scale due to the nature of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bonding</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Structure_of_liquids_and_glasses" title="Structure of liquids and glasses">structure of liquids and glasses</a> for more information on non-crystalline material structure). Furthermore, in very small <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystals</a> a large fraction of the <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> are the crystal; relaxation of the surface and interfacial effects distort the atomic positions, decreasing the structural order. Even the most advanced structural characterization techniques, such as x-ray diffraction and transmission electron microscopy, have difficulty in distinguishing between amorphous and crystalline structures on these length scales.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amorphous_thin_films">Amorphous thin films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amorphous_solid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Amorphous thin films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-Context" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/60px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/80px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="48" data-file-height="48" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>provides insufficient context for those unfamiliar with the subject</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Amorphous_solid&amp;action=edit">improve the article</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Writing_better_articles#Lead_section" title="Wikipedia:Writing better articles">good introductory style</a>.</span>  <small><i>(January 2014)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Amorphous phases are important constituents of <a href="/wiki/Thin_film" title="Thin film">thin films</a>, which are solid layers of a few <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">nanometres</a> to some tens of <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">micrometres</a> thickness deposited upon a substrate. So-called structure zone models were developed to describe the micro structure and ceramics of thin films as a function of the <a href="/wiki/Homologous_temperature" title="Homologous temperature">homologous temperature</a> <i>T<sub>h</sub></i> that is the ratio of deposition temperature over melting temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-MoDe1969_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoDe1969-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Thor1974_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thor1974-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> According to these models, a necessary (but not sufficient) condition for the occurrence of amorphous phases is that <i>T<sub>h</sub></i> has to be smaller than 0.3, that is the deposition temperature must be below 30% of the melting temperature. For higher values, the surface diffusion of deposited atomic species would allow for the formation of crystallites with long range atomic order.
</p><p>Regarding their applications, amorphous metallic layers played an important role in the discussion of a suspected <a href="/wiki/Superconductivity" title="Superconductivity">superconductivity</a> in <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_metal" title="Amorphous metal">amorphous metals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Buck1961_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Buck1961-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Today, <a href="/wiki/Optical_coating" title="Optical coating">optical coatings</a> made from <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">TiO<sub>2</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">SiO<sub>2</sub></a>, <a href="/wiki/Tantalum_pentoxide" title="Tantalum pentoxide">Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub></a> etc. and combinations of them in most cases consist of amorphous phases of these compounds. Much research is carried out into thin amorphous films as a gas separating <a href="/wiki/Biological_membrane" title="Biological membrane">membrane</a> layer.<sup id="cite_ref-Vos1998_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vos1998-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The technologically most important thin amorphous film is probably represented by few nm thin SiO<sub>2</sub> layers serving as isolator above the conducting channel of a metal-oxide semiconductor field-effect transistor (<a href="/wiki/MOSFET" title="MOSFET">MOSFET</a>). Also, hydrogenated <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_silicon" title="Amorphous silicon">amorphous silicon</a>, a-Si:H in short, is of technical significance for <a href="/wiki/Thin-film_solar_cells" class="mw-redirect" title="Thin-film solar cells">thin-film solar cells</a>. In case of a-Si:H the missing long-range order between silicon atoms is partly induced by the presence by hydrogen in the percent range.
</p><p>The occurrence of amorphous phases turned out as a phenomenon of particular interest for studying thin-film growth.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Remarkably, the growth of polycrystalline films is often used and preceded by an initial amorphous layer, the thickness of which may amount to only a few nm. The most investigated example is represented by thin multicrystalline silicon films, where such as the unoriented molecule. An initial amorphous layer was observed in many studies.<sup id="cite_ref-Birk2001_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birk2001-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Wedge-shaped polycrystals were identified by <a href="/wiki/Transmission_electron_microscopy" title="Transmission electron microscopy">transmission electron microscopy</a> to grow out of the amorphous phase only after the latter has exceeded a certain thickness, the precise value of which depends on deposition temperature, background pressure and various other process parameters. The phenomenon has been interpreted in the framework of <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)#Ostwald&#39;s_rule" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">Ostwald's rule</a> of stages<sup id="cite_ref-Ostw1897_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ostw1897-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> that predicts the formation of phases to proceed with increasing condensation time towards increasing stability.<sup id="cite_ref-Buck1961_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Buck1961-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Birk2001_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Birk2001-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Experimental studies of the phenomenon require a clearly defined state of the substrate surface and its contaminant density etc., upon which the thin film is deposited.
</p>
