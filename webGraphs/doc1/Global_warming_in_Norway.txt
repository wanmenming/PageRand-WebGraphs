<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>The Kingdom of Norway</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign state</a> and <a href="/wiki/Unitary_state" title="Unitary state">unitary</a> <a href="/wiki/Monarchy" title="Monarchy">monarchy</a> whose territory comprises the western portion of the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Peninsula" title="Scandinavian Peninsula">Scandinavian Peninsula</a> plus the island <a href="/wiki/Jan_Mayen" title="Jan Mayen">Jan Mayen</a> and the archipelago of <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">Svalbard</a>.
</p><p>The country maintains a combination of <a href="/wiki/Market_economy" title="Market economy">market economy</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Nordic_welfare_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Nordic welfare model">Nordic welfare model</a> with <a href="/wiki/Universal_health_care" title="Universal health care">universal health care</a> and a comprehensive <a href="/wiki/Social_security" title="Social security">social security</a> system. Norway has extensive reserves of <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minerals" class="mw-redirect" title="Minerals">minerals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lumber" title="Lumber">lumber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seafood" title="Seafood">seafood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a>. The petroleum industry accounts for around a quarter of the country's <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> (GDP). On a <a href="/wiki/Per_capita" title="Per capita">per-capita</a> basis, Norway is the world's largest producer of oil and natural gas outside the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_emissions">Carbon emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Carbon emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:373px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Energy_balance_for_Norway_in_2014.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Energy_balance_for_Norway_in_2014.png/371px-Energy_balance_for_Norway_in_2014.png" width="371" height="262" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Energy_balance_for_Norway_in_2014.png/557px-Energy_balance_for_Norway_in_2014.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6d/Energy_balance_for_Norway_in_2014.png 2x" data-file-width="610" data-file-height="431" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Energy_balance_for_Norway_in_2014.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Figure 1.</b> Illustrates the energy balance for Norway in 2014.</div></div></div>
<p>Despite Norway maintaining its ranks among the 20 highest EPI countries, achieving a score of 86.9% and rank of 17th out of the 180 analysed in 2016,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> it is one of the world's largest oil exporter and has the largest sovereign fund of any country. In 2015, Norway produced 53.9 million tonnes of greenhouse gases (GHGs) noted as carbon dioxide emissions - 15.1 million tonnes were attributed to oil and gas extraction<sup id="cite_ref-:14_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:14-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> - accounting for the largest proportion of emissions than the other sources, e.g. energy supply, agriculture, road traffic. The total emissions of GHGs increased by 600,000 tonnes since 2014, with emissions from oil and gas extraction increasing by 83.3% since 1990. In more detail, a 25% increase CO<sub>2</sub> emissions, 10% decrease in methane, 38% decrease in nitrous oxide; 44.7 million tones (Mt) was CO2, 5.5 Mt of CH4, 2.6 Mt of N20 (Figure 1).<sup id="cite_ref-:14_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:14-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_supply_and_demand">Energy supply and demand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Energy supply and demand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2015, Norway's energy supply reached 1.7 million tonnes – a 311.3% increase since 1990 <sup id="cite_ref-:15_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> - and their total domestic consumption was 213 TWh in 2015, of which 89 TWh were used by households and services. This was a 2% increase in household consumption, which has been attributed to lower temperatures causing a rise in demand for heating,<sup id="cite_ref-:14_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:14-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> which also led to a 7% rise in biofuel use from 2014. Due to rising global demand in natural gas and oil, 56 new licences were issued in January 2016 to allow more oil exploration near the <a href="/wiki/Lofoten_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Lofoten Islands">Lofoten islands</a>, in addition to the North and Barents seas.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This poses a threat to biodiversity and fish stocks in these areas despite numerous promises to improve their environmental ratings and the Paris pledge. On the other hand, 98% of Norway's electricity demand is supplied by renewable energies, 95% of this comes from <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectric power</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Because of the knowledge that their electricity is supplied by renewable sources and its very low cost as it is produced domestically, consumption in Norway is three times higher than the average European.<sup id="cite_ref-:15_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Electricity consumption equates to approximately 77% of household energy usage in an average detached house.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Transport_in_Norway" title="Transport in Norway">Norway’s transport</a> mix is heavily influenced by its low population density, narrow shape and long coastline with many small islands. The Norwegian Ministry of Transport and Communications has overall responsibility for the civil aviation, public roads and rail transport sector, ferry services forming part of the national road system (i.e. coastal regions), for coastal management, the marine environment and port and sea transport policy. They also have the ability to delegate tasks related to public transport and roads to the designated counties and municipalities. The majority of infrastructure in Norway is publicly owned, with operations often contracted out to private firms.
</p><p>Public transport in and around urban populations is well developed, particularly is <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a> which has one of the most advanced public transportation systems in Europe boasting metro, bus, tram and ferry networks that are all integrated on a zone-based far system with the latest technology. However, regions with low populations do often lack <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">public transport</a> infrastructure, forcing inhabitants to have their own car. It must also be noted that public transport is subsidised by the government.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, the <b>National Transport Plan 2018-2029 (NTP)</b> identified that the transportation sector accounts for one-third of total <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse gas emissions">greenhouse gas emissions</a> produced in Norway (~16.5 million tons of CO2), with road traffic accounting for ~10 million tons of CO2. Hence, leading to government agencies outlining these specific goals in order to achieve an emission-free transport system;<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By 2025, all new private cars, buses and light commercial vehicles should be zero emission vehicles.
</p><p>By 2030, new heavier vans, 75% of new long-distance buses and 50% of new trucks should be zero emission vehicles
</p><p>By 2030, 40% of all ships in short sea shipping using biofuels or be low- or zero-emission ships
</p><p>Greenhouse gas emissions from equipment and raw materials for construction, operation and maintenance of the infrastructure is reduced by 40% by 2030.
</p><p>Biofuels will annually replace 1.7 billion litres of fossil fuel by 2030. This provides a theoretical reduction in GHGs of ~5 million tons of CO2 equivalents.
</p><p>According to the World Economic Forum’s Travel and Tourism Competitiveness Report 2015 (biannual report), Norway placed 9/141 in air transport infrastructure, 35/141 in quality of railroad infrastructure, 56/141 in ground and port infrastructure and 74/141 regarding quality of roads.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Rail transport</b> (~18-36g/km of CO2 produced depending on capacity of the train)<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
The main railway network in Norway consists of 4,087&#160;km (2,556&#160;mi) of standard gauge lines, of which 242&#160;km (150&#160;mi) is double track and 64&#160;km (40&#160;mi) is high-speed rail (with speeds up to 210&#160;km/hr).
2,622&#160;km (64%) is electrified through a 15kV 162/3&#160;Hz AC with overhead wires. This enables significant reductions in greenhouse gas emissions given that 98% (134TWh) of Norway’s electricity sector is power by renewable energy (129TWh or 95% of which is produced by hydroelectricity).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The only sections that are not electrified are the lines north of Miøsa (except the Dovre and Ofoten Line). Diesel locomotives run the non-electrified sections. All of the urban railways use 750V DC via overhead wires on the tramways and third rail on the Oslo T-bane.
The railways transported 73,836,237 passengers’ 3,555 million km in 2015, with the transport of goods seeing 31,585,437 tons of cargo 3,498 million km.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Civil Aviation</b> (~220-455g/km of CO2 produced depending on capacity of the plane)<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Norway has 98 airports, of which 51 facilitate public flights, including one heliport. 45/51 are owned by the government through it airport operator, Avinor. Norway is the country in Europe with the most airline trips per capita, and the routes from Oslo to Trondheim, Bergen and Stavanger are all amongst the ten busiest in Europe. Contributing factors include poor rail and road infrastructure in areas with a low population density, a rugged geography and limited population in the interior and north.
The main gateway by air to Norway is Oslo Airport (Gardermoen), located 50&#160;km north of Oslo and primarily services both major Norwegian airlines; Scandinavian Airlines System and Norwegian Air Shuttle.
</p><p><b>Road transport</b>
</p><p>o <b><a href="/wiki/Electric_car" title="Electric car">Electric Cars</a></b>
Norway fleet of electric cars is one of the cleanest in the world due to its high abundance of electricity generated by hydropower (98%). This has steadily grown in interest, with the end of 2016 seeing 5% (135,000) of all passenger cars on Norwegian roads being a plug-in (Figure 2).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Government incentives include being exempt from all non-recurring vehicle fees (including purchase tax and 25% VAT on purchase), a tax reduction for plug-in hybrids and free access to road ferries. These in certain municipalities they can park for free and use public transport lanes. This successful integration of policies has seen electric vehicles widely accepted in Norway and the public even had the chance to discuss and propose ideas for the governments NTP. This led to the NTP setting the goal that all new cars; buses and light commercial vehicles should be zero emission vehicles (i.e. all-electric or hydrogen powered) by 2025. However, there have been some side effects with excessively high public subsidies, increased traffic congestion in public transport lanes, shortage of parking spaces for conventional cars (intentional) and loss of revenue for ferry operators.
</p><p>o <b>Bus transport</b>
Each county is responsible for the public bus and boat transport in their area, with railways, regional airlines and the Coastal boat all being financed by the state. In 2015, buses transported 356 million passengers over 4 billion passenger km. In an attempt to meet their plan to be carbon-neutral by 2050 (conditional for 2030) Oslo is also converting municipal buses to run on biomethane captured from human waste in order to cut CO2 emissions (saves 44 tons of CO2 per bus per year compared to gas alternatives).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Water transport</b>
</p><p>o <b>Ferries</b>
The Norwegian Coastal Administration operates the coastal infrastructure that spans over Norway’s 90,000 (56,000&#160;mi) of shoreline. Car ferries are vital links across fjords and to islands where there is no fixed connection. There are currently over one hundred car ferry connections in Norway. In 2015, boats transported 11 million passengers to their destination, a 10% growth from 2014. Norway even has started to install battery-electric ferries and has plans to expand the current fleet, powered by the large amount of hydroelectricity.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The Coastal Express (known as Hurtigruten) operates daily from Bergen to Kirkenes, stopping at 35 ports. This is pleasing news on a regional and national level but fails to address their huge international fleet as shipping and aircraft regulations where notably absent from the Paris Agreement.
</p><p>o <b>Pipelines</b>
The petroleum and natural gas <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_continental_shelf" title="Norwegian continental shelf">production on the Norwegian continental shelf</a> utilizes pipelines to transport products to processing plants and onwards to other European countries (9,481&#160;km long).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry_and_agriculture">Industry and agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Industry and agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>12 million tonnes of CO2 equivalents and 66 TWh were used by the manufacturing mining and quarrying industry in 2015 - a 39% emissions reduction since 1990, second only to oil and gas extraction.<sup id="cite_ref-:15_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:15-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This industry is showing a downward trend in emissions, but there was an increase between 2014 and 2015 of 3.1%. Higher production and use of fertilizer in 2015 contributed notably to higher CO2 and nitrous oxide emissions,<sup id="cite_ref-:14_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:14-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> which was also the largest proportion of agricultural emission causes. The agricultural sector emitted 4.5 million tonnes of CO2 equivalents, but these emissions have been steadily decreasing since 1990.
</p>
<h3><span id="Carbon_capture_and_storage_.28CCS.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_capture_and_storage_(CCS)">Carbon capture and storage (CCS)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Carbon capture and storage (CCS)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CCS is a potential means of mitigating the effects of fossil fuel emissions to global warming and ocean acidification. However, given Norway’s power supply is almost 100% renewable (majority coming from hydroelectricity) it is odd that they could also be depicted as world leaders when it comes to CCS technology. This can be explained by several key factors;<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>- Conflict between the large offshore oil and gas industry whose emissions are growing and the relatively high ambition in environmental protection expected by civil society and outlined in <a href="/wiki/Climate_and_energy" title="Climate and energy">climate and energy</a> policy targets
</p><p>- During 1997-2005 there was discussions to introduce natural gas plants into the country’s previously emissions-free power supply. This led to CCS becoming the only viable solution to overcome this political conflict.
</p><p>- The realisation of <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_Oil_Recovery_(EOR)" class="mw-redirect" title="Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR)">enhanced oil recovery (EOR)</a> upon installation of CCS technology led the oil and gas industry-led companies to start up CCS initiatives from the early 1990s (i.e. Statoil’s pioneering storage project, separating CO2 from natural gas, at the <a href="/wiki/Sleipner_gas_field" title="Sleipner gas field">Sleipner gas field</a> in the North Sea).
</p><p>Currently, the Norwegian government has set the main goal of its CCS policy to identify measures that can contribute to technology development and cost reductions. Furthermore, they seek to construct at least one full-scale carbon capture demonstration plant by 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This was made evident in their recent feasibility studies whereby the Minister of Petroleum and Energy (overall responsibility), Gassnova SF (project coordinator and capture storage) and Gassco AS (transport) identified three potential sites for full-scale CCS projects; a cement factory in Brevik (Norcem AS), an ammonia plan at Herøys in Porsgrunn (Yara Norge AS) and a waste recovery plant at Klemetsrud (Waste-to-Energy Agency in Oslo).<sup id="cite_ref-:1_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> However, both Statoil and Gassnova consider an onshore facility, accessed by ship, and a pipeline to ‘Smeaheia’ to be the best solution for CO2 storage. In their statement they highlight that “the costs for planning and investment for such a chain is estimated at between 7.2-12.6 billion kroner (~US$852-1492 million) with an uncertainty of +/- 40% or better”. Hence, a full-scale project will not be realised until at least 2022.
</p><p>The Norwegian Government is expected to outline further plans for CCS in the 2017 state budget, released in October.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Short-term_and_long-term_effects:_Current_and_changes_through_2100">Short-term and long-term effects: Current and changes through 2100</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Short-term and long-term effects: Current and changes through 2100">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture_and_forestry">Agriculture and forestry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Agriculture and forestry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Agricultural areas account for 3% of the mainland, while the forests are about 37%. Around 47% of the land is located above the tree line.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Studies have demonstrated that future long-term warming trends may lead to a longer growing season and hence increasing agricultural yields.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> This effect would increase progressively from south to north. In Northern Norway this is projected to be in the order of an increase of 1–4 weeks for the period 2021–2050, compared with 1961–1990.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> A longer season could also increase the use of legumes and more productive perennial forage grasses, vegetables and grains.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The relationship between a longer growing season and agriculture is not linear.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> An extended growing season is still limited by the reduced photoperiod, which terminates growth irrespective of the temperature increase. Thus, there needs to be both a prolonged autumn and earlier spring to promote longer growing seasons, whilst taking the risk of frost into account. Frost on snow-free soil leads to thick layers of frozen soil that can prolong lower soil temperatures irrespective of other factors that favour an early start to the season. Increased precipitation in autumn could also complicate harvesting and agricultural practices.
</p><p>The agricultural industry is already exposed to several other issues that could be compounded by global warming. Those are that the farming population is ageing with the younger generation flocking to cities for education and other forms of employment.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, any reductions in <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_subsidy" title="Agricultural subsidy">agricultural subsidies</a> and lack of increases in real incomes for farming may exacerbate the problem even further.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The most obvious<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (June 2017)">according to whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> change in forestry will be the expansion of coniferous forests. They will spread northwards and to higher elevations in the next century due to increasing temperatures. <a href="/wiki/Birch" title="Birch">Birch</a> forests are expected to exhibit similar trends. This will result in a considerable increase in forested area in northern Norway. A temperature increase of 2 degrees Celsius can move the tree line up the mountainside by about 300 m.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Biodiversity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Norwegian <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> is getting warmer and wetter, with large local variations.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> This is already having observed effects on almost all ecosystems. One is a terrestrial ecosystem, which has led to the earlier migration of birds, earlier sexual maturation in some animals, higher production and reproduction in both plants and animals, and earlier budding and pollen production.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> This is also evident in forests as warming temperatures leads to a higher tree line. The result of this is an expansion both northwards and upwards of species, particularly coniferous and birch forests.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> This movement will also cause northern boreal forests to invade tundra ecosystems in the long-term future.
</p><p>Whilst heat-stress is not expected to become a large issue on land, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Norway" title="Northern Norway">Northern Norway</a>, warmer conditions will support the spread of disease-bearing insects (especially those limited by the cold temperatures) and invasive species into Norway, thus increasing the vulnerability of native species, livestock and the human population alike.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Rising temperatures have affected local Norwegian ecosystems in many ways. Sea ice is diminishing, threatening ice-dependent species quicker than first imagined.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The absence of sea ice leads to more rapid warming, due to the feedback mechanisms associated with the absorbance of sunlight.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> It also leads to a reduction in biodiversity, as several species are dependent on sea ice. For example, ice algae that grow in and under the ice, seals that need sea ice to give birth to their young, polar bears that prey on seals and several species of bird too.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The rising temperatures are having direct impacts on freshwater and wetlands biodiversity. Atlantic salmon is a keystone species in rivers along the coast of Norway. Salmon have an upper temperature limit in the twenties so future warming may make it increasingly difficult to maintain current population levels. Higher initial temperatures could lead to increased growth and production in the short-term but ultimately there could be a massive collapse if warming trends continue.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_23-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> This has been made evident by the recent decline in mean individual mass and annual mean length of the fish.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> It is suggested that the size change of Atlantic salmon is made by the collapse and rebuilding of the pelagic fish abundance in the North Atlantic Ocean, a gradual decrease in zooplankton abundance and climate change. It could also promote genetic abnormalities and the spread of disease such as Pancreas Disease (PD) and <a href="/wiki/Infectious_salmon_anemia_virus" title="Infectious salmon anemia virus">Infectious Salmon Anemia Virus</a> (ISA).<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, lake and river surface water temperatures are projected to increase further, resulting in a longer summer stratification period and more <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacterial_blooms" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyanobacterial blooms">cyanobacterial blooms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, both the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_salmon" title="Atlantic salmon">Atlantic salmon</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_char" title="Arctic char">Arctic charr</a> have experienced changes in abundance.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Whilst both species coexist it has been the Arctic charr who seems to be more vulnerable to environmental changes, hence leading to its overall decline in numbers.
</p><p>A rise in sea temperatures will also affect on marine, <a href="/wiki/Estuarine" class="mw-redirect" title="Estuarine">estuarine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Intertidal_ecosystem" class="mw-redirect" title="Intertidal ecosystem">intertidal ecosystems</a>. Warmer seawater can lead to more phytoplankton and zooplankton but it is not known if other species can utilise this increase in food stocks.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_23-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> This change also favours species that prefer warmer waters and they will start to out-compete local species. Additionally, increased CO2 concentrations in the atmosphere are leading to <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a>, which is expected to continue over the next century to levels not witnessed in the last 20 million years.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_23-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> This may cause the extinction of coral species as the changing water chemistry makes it increasingly difficult for organisms with calcareous shells to form with calcium.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glacial_retreat">Glacial retreat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Glacial retreat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most of the <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> in the Norwegian Arctic are found in <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">Svalbard</a>, where glaciers have a total volume of ~7,000km3 and asn area of 36,000km2. On the mainland glaciers only have a volume of 64km3 and an area of 1,000km2.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_23-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The glaciers in Svalbard are key contributors to sea level rise as the archipelago accounts for 11% of Arctic land ice, apart from <a href="/wiki/Greenland_ice_sheet" title="Greenland ice sheet">Greenland</a>. Melting in Svalbard is extensive and is in line with both Arctic and global trends.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_23-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Knowledge of glacier volume and ice thickness distribution is important for assessing the cryospheric contribution to sea-level rise, glacier response to global warming and water resource management at the local to national level in Norway.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> When glaciers melt the white surface of the glaciers that normally reflects solar radiation becomes exposed (underlying dark surfaces), causing a positive feedback mechanism and hence further melting and increase in temperatures.
</p><p>Having experienced a brief period of expansion between 1940-90s as a response to higher winter accumulation, Norwegian glaciers have continued retreating as a result of less snowfall and higher summer temperatures (=more melting).<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:7_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> This has led to long-term forecasts where it is expected to see a summer temperature increase of at least 2.3oC and a significant increase (~16%) by the end of the 21st century. As a result, ~98% of the Norwegian glaciers are likely to disappear and the glacier area may be reduced by ~34% by 2100.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> This is in line with global glacier volume dramatically decreasing over the rest of the 21st century.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precipitation_patterns">Precipitation patterns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Precipitation patterns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Strong westerly winds bring moist air masses from the ocean and fall in the form of rain/snow to most of Norway. However, this does vary greatly from coastal areas that could receive more than 3500mm annually down to 300mm in south-eastern Norway and <a href="/wiki/Finnmarksvidda" title="Finnmarksvidda">Finnmarksvidda</a> where they are on the leeward side of mountain ranges.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Modelled climate data expects mainland Norway to experience annual precipitation increases of around 18% (5-30%) up to 2100, with respect to 1961-1990.<sup id="cite_ref-:9_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:8_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The largest variation is expected in autumn (+23%) as precipitation starts to fall as rain rather than snow, with the lowest being in summer 9% (-3 to 17%) as nearly all precipitation already falls in the form of rain.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:9_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Projections also indicate more days with heavy rainfall and precipitation values in the extreme events all over Norway and in all seasons.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> This is particularly relevant during winter and autumn where the number of days with heavy rainfall is expected to double.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_38-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The long-term outlook for the snow season is that it will become increasingly shorter over the century. A reduction of 2-3 month is estimated for low-elevation and coastal areas in west, mid and north Norway (when comparing current (1961-1990) and future climate data (2071-2100).<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> As winters become shorter, snowfall in autumn and spring will reduce. The decrease in total annual snowfall is smaller with increasing altitude and distance from the coast. Highly mountainous areas may experience slight increases in snowfall.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It must be noted that the current trends over the last 40 years is unprecedented and if continued Norway would experience annual precipitation changes of 30% over a century. This is 2-3 times higher than predicted.<sup id="cite_ref-:9_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sea-level">Sea-level</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sea-level">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In comparison to other parts of the world, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">Svalbard</a> will not experience any dramatic effects from a rise in sea level, as the land is still rising after the previous Ice Age and the coast is relatively steep.
</p><p>At the end of the previous Ice Age, a layer of ice up to 3&#160;km thick covered parts of northern Europe and North America. When the ice melted, the significant weight of the ice layer that pushed the Earth’s crust down into the mantle began to rise again. Land uplift was greatest immediately after the ice melted, however, it is estimated to continue rising for another 10,000 years.
</p><p>Studies suggest that Norway will experience an approximately 10&#160;cm greater rise in sea level than the global average within the year 2100.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_23-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Despite great uncertainty from all data, the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">IPCC</a> calculated a global increase of 10–90&#160;cm during this century.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Other studies conducted by the NOU Climate Adaptation in 2009 suggest a 40–95&#160;cm rise in sea level in northern Norway up until 2100, corrected for land uplift. This makes infrastructure long the coast more vulnerable to damage, especially during storm surges.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_23-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seasons_and_temperature_trends">Seasons and temperature trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Seasons and temperature trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>All climate scenarios indicate that all season in all regions of Norway will become warmer this century.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:6_26-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Low, mean and high projections depict the annual mean temperature rising by 2.3oC, 3.4oC and 4.6oC respectively by 2100 (Table 1). For the mainland, the smallest increase is expected in Western Norway 3.1oC (1.9-4.2oC), with the highest being in the northernmost country (Finnmark) of 4.2oC (3.0-5.4oC). This is expected to be even larger in offshore territories such as <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">Svalbard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jan_Mayen" title="Jan Mayen">Jan Mayen</a>, some predictions as high at 8oC.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_38-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The largest increase is projected during winter, whilst the smallest will occur during summer. This will see an increase in the growing season and corresponding decrease in snow cover over large parts of the country.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_38-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Hence, warmer seasons will increase in length, whilst winter will become shorter and more sporadic depending on given temperature regions.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_19-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thawing_permafrost">Thawing permafrost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Thawing permafrost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:470px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carbon_cycle-cute_diagram.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Carbon_cycle-cute_diagram.svg/468px-Carbon_cycle-cute_diagram.svg.png" width="468" height="361" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Carbon_cycle-cute_diagram.svg/702px-Carbon_cycle-cute_diagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Carbon_cycle-cute_diagram.svg/936px-Carbon_cycle-cute_diagram.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="540" data-file-height="417" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Carbon_cycle-cute_diagram.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Figure 2.</b> This <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> diagram shows the storage and annual exchange of carbon between the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth&#39;s atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geosphere" title="Geosphere">geosphere</a> in gigatons - or billions of tons - of Carbon (GtC).</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">Permafrost</a> is defined as ground, soil or rock, including ice or organic material that remains at or below zero degrees Celsius for at least two consecutive years. The regions in which permafrost occurs occupy ~24% (23 million km2) of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a>. Modelling suggests that permafrost covers have been warming and thawing since the end of the <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a> c. 120 years ago. Access the current global distribution of permafrost here.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Permafrost plays three important roles in the context of climate change; a mechanism for temperature archives, a translator of global warming through subsidence and related impacts and a facilitator of further change through its effect on the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">global carbon cycle</a> (Figure 2).<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to its climate setting (mild winters, cool summers), mountain permafrost is the dominating permafrost type. In Southern Norway, the lower permafrost extends from 1300–1600 metres above sea level (masl). Whilst in the north, mountain permafrost begins around 900masl in the west and as low as 400masl in the east (Finnimark county). The archipelago of <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">Svalbard</a> is also covered by approximately 60% of continuous permafrost and is the only landscape in Scandinavia where people are living directly on permafrost.
</p><p>Ground temperature measurements taken by The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oslo" title="University of Oslo">University of Oslo</a> and the Meteorological Institute have shown an increase of 1oC since 1999, with clear evidence of permafrost degradation in test sites shown by the Norwegian Permafrost Database (NORPERM).<sup id="cite_ref-:10_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The lower limit of mountain permafrost are highly sensitive to global warming as their permafrost temperatures are already just below 0oC and will thaw if current trends continue.
</p><p>Already the wetland areas of northern Norway (palsas and peat plateaus) have air photo and field analysis showcasing reductions of up to 50% in ground ice cover since the 1950s.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_44-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> This results in a considerable loss of permafrost and may trigger an increased emission of greenhouse gases (positive-feedback mechanism) from previously frozen, but now degrading organic material.
</p><p>Most of the permafrost in Norway is situated in uninhabited areas making the impact on society limited. However, glaciations and glacier erosion helped have sculpted mountain areas in Norway, revealing many steep and unstable slopes (i.e. Mt.Nordnes northeast of <a href="/wiki/Troms%C3%B8" title="Tromsø">Tromsø</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-:10_44-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> These slopes tend to lie in the permafrost area and failure of such slopes could affect roads, towns and even trigger localised tsunamis if large rock masses hit fjords or lakes.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_44-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Melting has even recently caused the <a href="/wiki/Svalbard_Global_Seed_Vault" title="Svalbard Global Seed Vault">Global Seed Vault</a>, buried in a mountain deep inside the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a>, to be breached after global warming produced extraordinary temperatures over the winter, sending meltwater gushing into the entrance tunnel.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Continued melting will cause gas and oil pipelines to crack and buildings to slowly fall apart due to unstable ground.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Arctic">The Arctic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: The Arctic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:467px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Arctic_melt_trends_graph.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Arctic_melt_trends_graph.png/465px-Arctic_melt_trends_graph.png" width="465" height="373" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Arctic_melt_trends_graph.png/698px-Arctic_melt_trends_graph.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5a/Arctic_melt_trends_graph.png 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="578" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Arctic_melt_trends_graph.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Figure 3.</b> The icy cap over Earth's <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">North Pole</a> reaches its summer minimum in September and its winter maximum in late February or early March. Satellite observations since 1979 have shown that amount of ice that survives the summer is getting smaller; declines have been especially dramatic in the past decade. Recently, scientists from NASA and the National Snow and Ice Data Center described another way Arctic sea ice is changing: the summer melt season is getting significantly longer, particularly in the Arctic region.</div></div></div>
<p>The Arctic region will warm more rapidly than the global mean, and mean warming over land will be larger than over the ocean, with studies indicating somewhere between 3-12 degrees Celsius with a high degree of uncertainty.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_26-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Over the last two decades Arctic sea ice and Northern Hemisphere spring snow cover has continued to decrease in extent not exhibited in at least the last 1,450 years.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_26-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> This is expected to continue as global mean surface temperatures increase.
</p><p>The annual mean sea ice extent has decreased in the range of 3.5 to 4.1% per decade (0.45 to 0.51 million square km per decade) over the period 1979-2012. This rate increases to 9.4 to 13.6% per decade (0.73 to 1.07 million square km per decade) for the summer sea ice minimum; hence it is most rapid in summer (Figure 3). Furthermore, IPCCs 5th Synthesis Report illustrates the continued reduction in extent of Arctic July–August–September (summer) average sea ice between 1900-2100.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_26-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Year-round reductions in <a href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice" class="mw-redirect" title="Arctic sea ice">Arctic sea ice</a> extent are projected by the end of the 21st century from multi-model averages. These reductions range from 43-94% in September and from 8-34% in February. Thus, it is very likely that we will see a nearly ice-free <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> in September before mid-century or towards the end of the 21st century depending on our ability to cut greenhouse gas emissions to the atmosphere. This is as anthropogenic influences have very likely contributed to Arctic sea ice loss since 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_26-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This is very worrying as sea ice plays a crucial role in regulating the temperature of Earth. Sea ice prevents warming to its high <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> and its ability to reflect the sunlight’s rays. However, in the event of less sea ice the ocean then absorbs this heat and continues to increase further warming (<a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback loop</a>). This affects those animals that rely on sea ice (i.e. polar bears and some seal species).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind">Wind</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Wind">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Future projections to the year 2100 (compared with 1961-1990) indicate small or no changes for average wind speed.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_38-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Changes are expected to stay within the range of natural variability and have different effects depending on the scenario.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Extreme geostrophic wind speeds are expected to experience a 2-6% decrease over the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Sea" title="Norwegian Sea">Norwegian Sea</a>, whereas southern and eastern parts of Northern Europe will see an increase of 2-4%.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Observed_and_expected_economic_effects">Observed and expected economic effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Observed and expected economic effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Positive_effects">Positive effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Positive effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A warmer climate will have its pros and cons for the Norwegian agriculture. Higher temperatures combined with new types of plants adapted to the milder climate may yield larger harvests and possibly making two harvests possible per year. The impact of climate change will vary between regions as there are already today a lot of local differences in precipitation etc. An earlier time of snow melting in areas with a dry climate may lead to crops drying out and dying. In wetter regions, further increased precipitation may cause outbreaks of fungus invasion on crops.
</p><p>The productive forest in Norway is expected to increase considerably due to climate change, but not without complications. Mild winters will reduce the resistance of trees and their frost tolerance. <a href="/wiki/Freeze_thaw" class="mw-redirect" title="Freeze thaw">Freeze-thaw</a> cycles will also be more frequent during mild winters, damaging the trees. Pest invasions and diseases are expected to be more frequent as new pests can move rapidly northwards. It is also possible that insects will be able to reproduce one more generation per summer due to higher temperatures, so that for instance the European spruce bark beetle may damage spruce trees with an extra invasion per summer.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sovereign_funding">Sovereign funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Sovereign funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Government Pension Fund Global (GPFG) is a fund where the surplus profit produced by Norwegian petroleum industry (oil and gas) is deposited. Previously called ‘<a href="/wiki/The_Petroleum_Fund_of_Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="The Petroleum Fund of Norway">The Petroleum Fund of Norway</a>’ when it was established in 1990, the fund changed its name in 2006. Norges Bank Investment Management (NBIM) manages the fund, which is part of the Norwegian Central Bank and on behalf of the Ministry of Finance. It is not a normal pension fund in the sense as its financial backing comes from oil profits rather than pensioners. This makes continued investment dependent on the survival of the oil industry, despite the world realising fossil fuels directly attribute to global warming.
</p><p>As of April 2017, the fund was valued at US$916.9 billion (NOK 7.827 trillion).<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> This makes it the third largest pension fund in the world behind the Social Security Trust Fund (US – value US$2.837 trillion) and the Government Pension Investment Fund (Japan – US$1.103 trilion).<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to the large size of the fund compared to the relative low population of Norway (~5.3 million in 2017), the fund has become a hot political issue. This includes whether the petroleum revenues should be used now rather than save for the future and if carrying out spending would cause inflation. Furthermore, there are arguments over whether the high level of exposure (62.5%) to the highly volatile stock market is financially safe or simply appropriate diversification. More importantly, in regards to global warming and ethical issues, the fund has been question on its investment policy.
</p><p>There is large controversy over the investment policy as current and previous investments have included industries such as arms production, tobacco and fossil fuels. Despite having ethical guidelines that prohibit the investment in companies that directly or indirectly attribute to killing, torture, deprivation of freedom or other violations of human rights, the fund is still allowed to fossil fuel companies and a number of arms-producing companies (excluding nuclear weapons).
</p><p>In 2014, there was significant pressure leading to a parliament investigation as to whether the fund should divest its coal assets in line with it ethical investment mandate. This resulted the fund divesting from energy companies that derive more than 30% of their revenues from coal, 53 companies in total. There is evidence however, that investment into coal actually grew during this period by simply shifting money to those companies who derive &lt;70% of their revenues from coal (i.e. Glencore, BHP and Rio Tinto).<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> In the same year the fund also increased its stake in 59/90 oil and gas companies in which it holds shares in excess of US$30 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> This gravely disappointed campaigners who argue it should sell of all investments in the fossil fuel industry as they continue to drive global warming and climate change.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_Sami_and_reindeer_herding">Effects on Sami and reindeer herding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Effects on Sami and reindeer herding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the climate change progresses, the winters in <a href="/wiki/Sami_people" title="Sami people">Sámi</a> have become less and less predictable. Increased temperatures lead to more frequent icing on the ground, leaving food inaccessible for the reindeer. Having to move the reindeer to new grazing areas is problematic as there's often conflicts in respect of area usage. Unstable early winters present difficulties when moving the reindeer from the winter grazing areas to the summer grazing areas as lakes and rivers do not properly freeze. The increased humidity and temperature can favour insects and parasitic pests that target reindeer. However, the increased temperatures could bring some positives sides to reindeer herding, with increased plant growth and better food availability during summer grazing. Early springs can also lead to an extended summer grazing.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discourse_on_global_warming">Discourse on global warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Discourse on global warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political">Political</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Political">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:368px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Norway%27s_climate_change_commitments.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Norway%27s_climate_change_commitments.png/366px-Norway%27s_climate_change_commitments.png" width="366" height="493" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Norway%27s_climate_change_commitments.png/549px-Norway%27s_climate_change_commitments.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/03/Norway%27s_climate_change_commitments.png 2x" data-file-width="704" data-file-height="948" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Norway%27s_climate_change_commitments.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><b>Figure 4.</b> Highlights Norway’s ongoing commitments to international climate change agreements. Norway ratified the Kyoto Protocol (CP1) on 30 May 2002 and became a Party when the Protocol entered into force on 16 February 2005. Furthermore, it ratified the Doha Amendments and second period (CP2) of the Kyoto Protocol on 12 June 2014. Following its willingness to be associated with the non-binding Copenhagen Accord on 25 January, Norway became the first developed nation to ratify the Paris Agreement on 20 June 2016 with a target of a 40% by 2030 on 1990 levels<sup id="cite_ref-Tracker_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tracker-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Tracker_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tracker-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Norwegian government">Norwegian government</a> is attempting to tackle global warming directly through an array of national and international plans and policies. Norway has long pledged to play a leading role in negotiations towards a more ambitious international climate change agreement, using their starting point as limiting the average rise in global temperatures to no more than 2 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels (Figure 4).
</p><p>This became apparent when almost all countries in the world first became parties to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC)</a> in 1992. Despite global emissions rising since then,<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> Norway's long-term goal has remained to become a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_neutrality" title="Carbon neutrality">carbon neutral</a> country by 2050 (with a conditional target of 2030) through the assistance of the EU emissions trading market, international cooperation on emissions reductions, emissions trading and project-based cooperation. This sentiment has been reflected through their ongoing commitments in international agreements as depicted below. However, this has not come without scrutiny as the country is often questioned for buying itself out of burdensome domestic environmental obligations by purchasing international CO2 quotas and offsetting emissions through the <a href="/wiki/European_Union_Emission_Trading_Scheme" title="European Union Emission Trading Scheme">EU trading scheme</a> (despite not being a member of the EU).
</p><p>There seems to be two stories, one about Norway wanting to be a world leader in global climate change and environmental issues whilst the other tends to favour Norway’s oil and gas reserves, claiming that its necessary to extract more oil and gas because of high demand and in order to help the poor who in some parts of the world have no access to energy.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> This duality therefore sends a very polarised message to the Norwegian public and may be part of the reason why there is a lack of engagement or enthusiasm currently observed around the issue of climate change.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Developed nations like Norway have been instructed to take the lead in reducing their emissions and investing heavily in climate commitments as part of its partnership schemes with developing nations - particularly focusing on clean, renewable energy resources, climate change mitigation/adaptation and food security, primarily funded through the Norwegian Clean Energy for Development initiative launched in 2007 and the International Energy and Climate Change Initiative Energy+ (launched in 2011). For example, in 2010 Norway supported household installation of 80,000 solar home systems in Nepal.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific">Scientific</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Scientific">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scientific evidence and concern about <a href="/wiki/Anthropogenic_global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Anthropogenic global warming">anthropogenic global warming</a> has gained momentum over recent decades.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_26-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> This is in spite of recognising that scientific findings carry uncertainty and the very nature of climate change is an extremely complex natural phenomenon that takes centuries to observe and measure, and that manifests itself differently across the world. These facts make it difficult for scientists to give clear recommendations about political actions related to adaptation and mitigation.<sup id="cite_ref-:11_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The scientific debate in Norway is not centred on weather or not climate change exists as it is considered a certainty. Rather issues arise on the timescales at which human actions are affecting the planet and how quickly our planet responds to significant increases in greenhouse gas emissions, i.e. global warming of surface temperatures. The scientific community has even debated the sustainability of different climate technological solutions in the press, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bioenergy" title="Bioenergy">bioenergy</a><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Offshore_wind_power" title="Offshore wind power">offshore wind power</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Increasingly, it is Norwegian climate researchers that are depicted as world leaders in several areas and have produced the most number of publications in the world (per capita).<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> This is also demonstrated by the high numbers of Norwegian researchers serving as authors for the <a href="/wiki/Intergovenmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change_(IPCC)" class="mw-redirect" title="Intergovenmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC)">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC)</a> working group reports and other prominent international research organisations.
</p><p>Research on global warming is often being portrayed through the same journalistic principles as other news stories; newsworthiness and contested phenomena. Despite previous attempts at balanced reporting giving rise to a skewed perception of climate scepticism, the debate over anthropogenic climate change in Norway is quite progressive compared to others. So much so that you don't even see conservative politicians or media commentators question mainstream climate science anymore as to them it is clear that the planet is warming. Moreover, the main debate is centred on the timescale of change due to our impacts.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_perception">Public perception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_warming_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Public perception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Norway is a small, politically stable Northern European country with a substantial welfare scheme. The Norwegian media landscape is also based on public and government-funded broadcasting where high uptake is considered important for citizen’s knowledge on political issues.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> This, combined with Norway's access to energy resources, makes it a particularly interesting field of study. This is evident by the huge economic interests associated with the oil and gas industry, leading to the popularity of the Norwegian petro-industrial complex and a public discourse around scepticism towards climate science.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> On the other hand, Norwegians have had a long history of environmental concern given their stunning nature and widespread popular perception of renewable energy affluence thanks to large hydroelectric resources.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> This dualism has led to an undercurrent of doubt towards climate change and can pose the question: If the climate issue is such a threat, why aren’t the politicians doing anything about it? Nonetheless, the government also gets very little credit for its climate policy.<sup id="cite_ref-:12_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the past, most people believed that climate change was real.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> However, perceptions started to shift thanks to an emphasis on ‘balanced reporting’, whereby accounts of scientific controversy made the public ambiguous as to the urgency of the issue. Shifts in public attitude towards climate change have also been shaped by many other key factors. These include news media coverage of changes in nature (nature drama), coverage of presumed experts’ disagreement about global warming (science drama), critical attitudes toward media, observations of political inaction and consideration with respect to everyday life.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> This lead some to conclude that there is not a lack of public knowledge surrounding global warming,<sup id="cite_ref-:13_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> rather that translating this knowledge into action can be regarded as problematic. People often indicated that their behaviour was constrained by a lack of infrastructure and mechanisms, higher prices of environmentally friendly goods, current design promoted private car usage and a lack of disincentives to pollute.<sup id="cite_ref-:12_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Furthermore, a lack of strong proactive policies by the government has fostered widespread frustration within the public arena, as messages of how to address global warming and climate change are often inconsistent.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> On the one hand it advocated for geographically remote technical fixes (i.e. CCS and <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a>), whilst on the other hand, the public was asked to take on the prime responsibility of reducing emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-:13_75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> This mentality that there is a lack of visible political action is then often hard to change.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Examples of this include public calls for comprehensive policies for electric road transport (currently in force), better and cheaper modes of public transport, political guidance concerning energy efficiency in buildings and willingness to develop renewable energy technologies.<sup id="cite_ref-:12_73-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> This led a study on young people to conclude that individual actions did “mot matter much in the global context” and that authorities did not facilitate “contributions from ordinary citizens”.<sup id="cite_ref-:11_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, they highlighted that they think Norway does have a responsibility to help poor countries but also must mitigate the problem and reduce its own oil production simultaneously.<sup id="cite_ref-:11_60-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another strand of research related to climate policy analysed whether support for international climate action is conditional on perceptions of reciprocity. Some studies also suggest that public support for international climate change is more conditional in Norway than in the US or Canada, leading one to suggest that country size and dependence on fossil fuels may be more important than national traditions for multilateral cooperation in predicting unilateral climate action support.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> The latest opinion polls in Norway however have seen climate change jump to be the second-most important issue on the public’s agenda. This is up from sixth place in 2010–2014.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
