<p><b>Copper(I) oxide</b> or <b>cuprous oxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the formula Cu<sub>2</sub>O. It is one of the principal <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxides</a> of <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a>, the other being CuO or <a href="/wiki/Copper(II)_oxide" title="Copper(II) oxide">cupric oxide</a>. This red-coloured solid is a component of some <a href="/wiki/Antifouling" class="mw-redirect" title="Antifouling">antifouling</a> paints.  The compound can appear either yellow or red, depending on the size of the particles.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Copper(I) oxide is found as the reddish <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> <a href="/wiki/Cuprite" title="Cuprite">cuprite</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper(I)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Copper(I) oxide may be produced by several methods.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Most straightforwardly, it arises via the oxidation of copper metal:
</p>
<dl><dd>4 Cu  +  O<sub>2</sub>   →  2 Cu<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Additives such as water and acids affect the rate of this process as well as the further oxidation to copper(II) oxides. It is also produced commercially by reduction of copper(II) solutions with <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>.  Aqueous cuprous chloride solutions react with base to give the same material.  In all cases, the color is highly sensitive to the procedural details.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cu-pourbaix-diagram.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Cu-pourbaix-diagram.svg/220px-Cu-pourbaix-diagram.svg.png" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Cu-pourbaix-diagram.svg/330px-Cu-pourbaix-diagram.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Cu-pourbaix-diagram.svg/440px-Cu-pourbaix-diagram.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="420" data-file-height="300" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cu-pourbaix-diagram.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Pourbaix_diagram" title="Pourbaix diagram">Pourbaix diagram</a> for copper in uncomplexed media (anions other than OH<sup>−</sup> not considered). Ion concentration 0.001 m (mol/kg water). Temperature 25°C.</div></div></div>
<p>Formation of copper(I) oxide is the basis of the <a href="/wiki/Fehling%27s_solution" title="Fehling&#39;s solution">Fehling's test</a> and <a href="/wiki/Benedict%27s_reagent" title="Benedict&#39;s reagent">Benedict's test</a> for reducing <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugars</a>. These sugars reduce an <a href="/wiki/Alkaline" class="mw-redirect" title="Alkaline">alkaline</a> solution of a copper(II) salt, giving a bright red <a href="/wiki/Precipitate" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitate">precipitate</a> of Cu<sub>2</sub>O.
</p><p>It forms on <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>-plated copper parts exposed to moisture when the silver layer is porous or damaged.  This kind of <a href="/wiki/Corrosion" title="Corrosion">corrosion</a> is known as <a href="/wiki/Red_plague_(corrosion)" title="Red plague (corrosion)">red plague</a>.
</p><p>Little  evidence exists for cuprous hydroxide, which is expected to rapidly undergo dehydration.  A similar situation applies to the hydroxides of gold(I) and silver(I).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper(I)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The solid is <a href="/wiki/Diamagnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Diamagnetic">diamagnetic</a>.  In terms of their coordination spheres, copper centres are 2-coordinated and the oxides are tetrahedral.  The structure thus resembles in some sense the main polymorphs of SiO<sub>2</sub>, and both structures feature interpenetrated lattices.
</p><p>Copper(I) oxide dissolves in concentrated <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> solution to form the colourless <a href="/wiki/Complex_(chemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Complex (chemistry)">complex</a> [Cu(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>]<sup>+</sup>, which is easily <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidized</a> in air to the blue [Cu(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>4</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>2</sub>]<sup>2+</sup>. It dissolves in <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> to give solutions of CuCl<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>.  Dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> produce <a href="/wiki/Copper(II)_sulfate" title="Copper(II) sulfate">copper(II) sulfate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Copper(II)_nitrate" title="Copper(II) nitrate">copper(II) nitrate</a>, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cu<sub>2</sub>O degrades to <a href="/wiki/Copper(II)_oxide" title="Copper(II) oxide">copper(II) oxide</a> in moist air.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper(I)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

One sublattice is shifted by a quarter of the body diagonal. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Semiconducting_properties">Semiconducting properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper(I)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Semiconducting properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the history of <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> physics, Cu<sub>2</sub>O is one of the most studied materials, and many experimental observations and semiconductor applications have been demonstrated first in this material:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">Semiconductor</a></li>
<li>Semiconductor <a href="/wiki/Diode" title="Diode">diodes</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Phonoritons <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The lowest excitons in Cu<sub>2</sub>O are extremely long lived; absorption lineshapes have been demonstrated with <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">neV</a> linewidths, which is the narrowest bulk exciton resonance ever observed.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  The associated quadrupole <a href="/wiki/Polariton" title="Polariton">polaritons</a> have low <a href="/wiki/Group_velocity" title="Group velocity">group velocity</a> approaching the speed of sound. Thus, light moves almost as slowly as sound in this medium, which results in high polariton densities.
Another unusual feature of the <a href="/wiki/Ground_state" title="Ground state">ground state</a> excitons is that all primary scattering mechanisms are known quantitatively.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Cu<sub>2</sub>O was the first substance where an entirely parameter-free model of <a href="/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation)" title="Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)">absorption</a> <a href="/wiki/Linewidth" class="mw-redirect" title="Linewidth">linewidth</a> broadening by <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> could be established, allowing the corresponding <a href="/wiki/Absorption_coefficient" class="mw-redirect" title="Absorption coefficient">absorption coefficient</a> to be deduced. It can be shown using Cu<sub>2</sub>O that the <a href="/wiki/Kramers%E2%80%93Kronig_relation" class="mw-redirect" title="Kramers–Kronig relation">Kramers–Kronig relations</a> do not apply to polaritons.<sup id="cite_ref-Hopfield1958_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hopfield1958-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Copper(I)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cuprous oxide is commonly used as a <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Fungicide" title="Fungicide">fungicide</a>, and an anti<a href="/wiki/Fouling" title="Fouling">fouling</a> agent for marine paints.  <a href="/wiki/Rectifier" title="Rectifier">Rectifier diodes</a> based on this material have been used industrially as early as 1924, long before <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> became the standard.  Copper(I) oxide is also responsible for the pink color in a positive <a href="/wiki/Benedict%27s_reagent" title="Benedict&#39;s reagent">Benedict's test</a>.
</p>
