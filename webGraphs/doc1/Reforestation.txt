<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Reforestation</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><b>Reforestation</b> is the natural or intentional restocking of existing <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a> and <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodlands</a> (<a href="/wiki/Forestation" title="Forestation">forestation</a>) that have been depleted, usually through <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Reforestation can be used to rectify or improve the quality of human life by soaking up <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> and dust from the air, rebuild natural <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, mitigate <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> since forests facilitate <a href="/wiki/Biosequestration" title="Biosequestration">biosequestration</a> of atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">harvest</a> for resources, particularly <a href="/wiki/Timber" class="mw-redirect" title="Timber">timber</a>, but also <a href="/wiki/Non-timber_forest_product" title="Non-timber forest product">non-timber forest products</a>.
</p><p>A similar concept, <a href="/wiki/Afforestation" title="Afforestation">afforestation</a>, another type of forestation, refers to the process of restoring and recreating areas of <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodlands</a> or <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a> that may have existed long ago but were <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforested</a> or otherwise removed at some point in the past or lacked it naturally (e.g., natural <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grasslands</a>). Sometimes the term "re-afforestation" is used to distinguish between the <a href="/wiki/Old_growth_forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Old growth forest">original forest cover</a> and the later re-growth of forest to an area. Special tools, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Tree_planting_bar" title="Tree planting bar">tree planting bars</a>, are used to make planting of trees easier and faster.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reforestation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A debated issue in managed reforestation is whether or not the succeeding forest will have the same <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> as the original forest. If the forest is replaced with only one species of tree and all other vegetation is prevented from growing back, a <a href="/wiki/Monoculture" title="Monoculture">monoculture</a> forest similar to agricultural crops would be the result. However, most reforestation involves the planting of different <a href="/w/index.php?title=Seedlot&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Seedlot (page does not exist)">lots of seed</a> of seedlings taken from the area, often of multiple species.<sup id="cite_ref-kennethmarendefoundation.com_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kennethmarendefoundation.com-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Another important factor is the natural regeneration of a wide variety of plant and animal species that can occur on a <a href="/wiki/Clearcutting" title="Clearcutting">clear cut</a>. In some areas the suppression of <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">forest fires</a> for hundreds of years has resulted in large single aged and single species <a href="/wiki/Stand_level_modelling" title="Stand level modelling">forest stands</a>. The logging of small clear cuts and/or prescribed burning actually increases the biodiversity in these areas by creating a greater variety of tree stand ages and species.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="For_harvesting">For <a href="/wiki/Harvesting" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvesting">harvesting</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reforestation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: For harvesting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Reforestation need not be only used for recovery of accidentally destroyed forests. In some countries, such as <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, many of the forests are <i>managed</i> by the wood products and <a href="/wiki/Pulp_and_paper_industry" title="Pulp and paper industry">pulp and paper industry</a>. In such an arrangement, like other crops, trees are planted to replace those that have been cut. In such circumstances, the industry can cut the trees in a way to allow easier reforestation. The wood products industry systematically replaces many of the trees it cuts, employing large numbers of summer workers for <a href="/wiki/Tree_planting" title="Tree planting">tree planting</a> work. For example, in 2010, <a href="/wiki/Weyerhaeuser" title="Weyerhaeuser">Weyerhaeuser</a> reported planting 50 million seedlings.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However replanting an <a href="/wiki/Old-growth_forest" title="Old-growth forest">old-growth forest</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantation</a> is <a href="/wiki/Plantation#Natural_forest_loss" title="Plantation">not replacing the old with the same</a> characteristics in the new.
</p><p>In just 20 years, a <a href="/wiki/Teak" title="Teak">teak</a> plantation in <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a> can produce up to about 400 m³ of wood per hectare. As the natural teak forests of Asia become more scarce or difficult to obtain, the prices commanded by plantation-grown teak grows higher every year. Other species such as <a href="/wiki/Mahogany" title="Mahogany">mahogany</a> grow more slowly than teak in Tropical America but are also extremely valuable. Faster growers include <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">eucalyptus</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Gmelina_arborea" title="Gmelina arborea">Gmelina</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Reforestation, if several <a href="/wiki/Native_plant" title="Native plant">indigenous species</a> are used, can provide other benefits in addition to financial returns, including <a href="/wiki/Soil_regeneration" title="Soil regeneration">restoration of the soil</a>, rejuvenation of local flora and fauna, and the capturing and sequestering of 38 <a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">tons</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> per <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">hectare</a> per year.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The reestablishment of forests is not just simple tree planting. Forests are made up of a <a href="/wiki/Plant_community" title="Plant community">community of species</a> and they build <a href="/wiki/Humus" title="Humus">dead organic matter</a> into soils over time. A major tree-planting program could enhance the local climate and reduce the demands of burning large amounts of fossil fuels for cooling in the summer.<sup id="cite_ref-Wood_well_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wood_well-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="For_climate_change_mitigation">For climate change mitigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reforestation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: For climate change mitigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Forests are an important part of the <a href="/wiki/Global_carbon_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Global carbon cycle">global carbon cycle</a> because trees and plants absorb <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> through <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>. By removing this <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> from the air, forests function as terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sink" title="Carbon sink">carbon sinks</a>, meaning they store large amounts of carbon. At any time, forests account for as much as double the amount of carbon in the atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-Canadell_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canadell-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1456</span></sup> Even as more anthropogenic carbon is produced, forests remove around three billion tons of anthropogenic carbon every year. This amounts to about 30% of all carbon dioxide emissions from <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>. Therefore, an increase in the overall <a href="/wiki/Forest_cover" title="Forest cover">forest cover</a> around the world would tend to mitigate <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.
</p><p>There are four major strategies available to mitigate carbon emissions through forestry activities: increase the amount of forested land through a reforestation process; increase the carbon density of existing forests at a stand and landscape scale; expand the use of forest products that will sustainably replace fossil-fuel emissions; and reduce carbon emissions that are caused from deforestation and degradation.<sup id="cite_ref-Canadell_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canadell-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1456</span></sup>
</p><p>Achieving the first strategy would require enormous and wide-ranging efforts. However, there are many organizations around the world that encourage tree-planting as a way to offset carbon emissions for the express purpose of fighting climate change. For example, in China, the <a href="/wiki/Jane_Goodall_Institute" title="Jane Goodall Institute">Jane Goodall Institute</a>, through their Shanghai <a href="/wiki/Roots_%26_Shoots" title="Roots &amp; Shoots">Roots &amp; Shoots</a> division, launched the Million Tree Project in Kulun Qi, <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolia</a> to plant one million trees to stop <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a> and help curb climate change.<sup id="cite_ref-Shanghai_Roots_&amp;_Shoots_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shanghai_Roots_&amp;_Shoots-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_Million_Tree_Project_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Million_Tree_Project-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> China has used 24 billion metres squared of new forest plantation and natural forest regrowth to offset 21% of Chinese fossil fuel emissions in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-Canadell_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canadell-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1456</span></sup> In Java, Indonesia each newlywed couple is to give whoever is sermonizing their wedding 5 seedlings to combat global warming. Each couple that wishes to have a divorce has to give 25 seedlings to whoever divorces them.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The second strategy has to do with selecting species for tree-planting. In theory, planting any kind of tree to produce more forest cover would absorb more carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. On the other hand, a <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified" class="mw-redirect" title="Genetically modified">genetically modified</a> tree specimen might grow much faster than any other regular tree.<sup id="cite_ref-Economist_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>93</span></sup> Some of these trees are already being developed in the <a href="/wiki/Lumber_industry" class="mw-redirect" title="Lumber industry">lumber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a> industries. These fast-growing trees would not only be planted for those industries but they can also be planted to help absorb carbon dioxide faster than slow-growing trees.<sup id="cite_ref-Economist_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Economist-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>93</span></sup>
</p><p>Extensive forest resources placed anywhere in the world will not always have the same impact. For example, large reforestation programs in boreal or <a href="/wiki/Subarctic" title="Subarctic">subarctic</a> regions have a limited impact on climate mitigation. This is because it substitutes a bright snow-dominated region that reflects the sunlight with dark forest canopies. On the other hand, a positive example would be reforestation projects in tropical regions, which would lead to a positive biophysical change such as the formation of <a href="/wiki/Clouds" class="mw-redirect" title="Clouds">clouds</a>. These clouds would then <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">reflect the sunlight</a>, creating a positive impact on climate mitigation.<sup id="cite_ref-Canadell_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canadell-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1457</span></sup>
</p><p>There is an advantage to planting trees in <a href="/wiki/Tropical_climate" title="Tropical climate">tropical climates</a> with <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">wet seasons</a>. In such a setting, trees have a quicker <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/growth" class="extiw" title="wikt:growth">growth</a> rate because they can grow year-round. Trees in tropical climates have, on average, larger, brighter, and more abundant leaves than non-tropical climates. A study of the <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/girth" class="extiw" title="wikt:girth">girth</a> of 70,000 trees across <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> has shown that tropical forests are soaking up more carbon dioxide pollution than previously realized. The research suggests almost one fifth of fossil fuel emissions are absorbed by forests across <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amazonia" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazonia">Amazonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>. Simon Lewis, a climate expert at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Leeds" title="University of Leeds">University of Leeds</a>, who led the study, said: "Tropical forest trees are absorbing about 18% of the carbon dioxide added to the atmosphere each year from burning fossil fuels, substantially buffering the rate of change."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is also important to deal with the rate of deforestation. At this point, there are 13 billion metres squared of tropical regions that are deforested every year. There is potential for these regions to reduce rates of deforestation by 50% by 2050, which would be a huge contribution to stabilize the global climate.<sup id="cite_ref-Canadell_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canadell-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1456</span></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Incentives">Incentives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reforestation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Incentives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some incentives for reforestation can be as simple as a financial compensation. Streck and Scholz (2006) explain how a group of scientists from various institutions have developed a compensated reduction of deforestation approach which would reward developing countries that disrupt any further act of deforestation. Countries that participate and take the option to reduce their emissions from deforestation during a committed period of time would receive financial compensation for the carbon dioxide emissions that they avoided.<sup id="cite_ref-Streck_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Streck-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>875</span></sup> To raise the payments, the host country would issue government bonds or negotiate some kind of loan with a financial institution that would want to take part in the compensation promised to the other country. The funds received by the country could be invested to help find alternatives to the extensive cutdown of forests. This whole process of cutting emissions would be voluntary, but once the country has agreed to lower their emissions they would be obligated to reduce their emissions. However, if a country was not able to meet their obligation, their target would get added to their next commitment period. The authors of these proposals see this as a solely government-to-government agreement; private entities would not participate in the compensation trades.<sup id="cite_ref-Streck_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Streck-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>876</span></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sub-Saharan_Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reforestation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sub-Saharan Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One plan in this region involves planting a nine-mile width of trees on the Southern Border of the Sahara Desert.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Great_Green_Wall" title="Great Green Wall">Great Green Wall</a> initiative is a <a href="/wiki/Pan-Africanism" title="Pan-Africanism">pan-African</a> proposal to “green” the continent from west to east in order to battle desertification. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reforestation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Natural Resources Canada (The Department of Natural Resources) states that the national forest cover was decreased by 0.34% from 1990 to 2015, and Canada has the lowest deforestation rate in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The forest industry is one of the main industries in Canada which contributes about 7% of Canadian economy<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>, and about 9% of the forests on earth are in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Therefore, Canada has many policies and laws to commit to sustainable forest management. For example, 94% of Canadian forests are public land, and the government obligates planting trees after harvesting to public forests.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reforestation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In China, extensive replanting programs have existed since the 1970s. Programs have had overall success. The forest cover has increased from 12% of China's land area to 16%.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> However, specific programs have had limited success. The "<a href="/wiki/Green_Wall_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Green Wall of China">Green Wall of China</a>", an attempt to limit the expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Gobi_Desert" title="Gobi Desert">Gobi Desert</a> is planned to be 2,800 miles (4,500&#160;km) long and to be completed in 2050. China plans to plant 26 billion trees in the next decade that is two trees for every Chinese citizen per year.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> China requires that students older than 11 years old plant one tree a year until their high school graduation.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the years 2013-2018, China planted 338,000 square kilometres of forests, what cost 82.88 billions dollars<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>. In 2018 21.7% of China's territory is covered by forests. Until 2035 China wants to expand it to 26%. The total area of China is 9,596,961 km2(see <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>), so it means plant 412,669 square kilometres more<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>.
</p>

<p>The Ministry of Agriculture, Forestry and Fishery explain that about two-thirds of Japanese land is covered with forests<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>, and it was almost unchanged from 1966 to 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Japan needs to reduce 26% of green house gas emission from 2013 by 2030 to accomplish Paris Agreement and is trying to reduce 2% of them by forestry.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>

Reforestation was conducted as a part of <a href="/wiki/Afforestation" title="Afforestation">afforestation</a> due to inabilities of self-recovering by the natural land itself due to serious soil pollution and loss of woods consequence in loss of soils for plants to grow, thus needing artificial efforts involving introducing of healthy soils from outside. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lebanon">Lebanon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reforestation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Lebanon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For thousands of years, Lebanon was covered by forests, one particular species of interest, <i><a href="/wiki/Cedrus_libani" title="Cedrus libani">Cedrus libani</a></i> was exceptionally valuable and was almost eliminated  due to lumbering operations for such purposes as the construction of King Solomon’s great temple and palace<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>. Virtually every ancient culture that shared the Mediterranean Sea harvested these trees from the Phoenicians who used cedar, pine and juniper to build their famous boats to the Romans, who cut them down for lime-burning kilns, to early in the 20th century when the Ottomans used much of the remaining cedar forests of Lebanon as fuel in steam trains<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>. Despite two millennia of deforestation,  forests in Lebanon still cover 13.6% of the country, and other wooded lands represent 11%<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>.  
</p><p>Law No. 558, which was ratified by the Lebanese Parliament on April 19, 1996, aims to protect and expand existing forests, classifying all forests of cedar, <a href="/wiki/Fir" title="Fir">fir</a>, high juniper (juniperus excelsa), evergreen cypress (cupressus sempervirens) and other trees, whether diverse or homogeneous, whether state-owned or not as conserved forests.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>.
</p><p>Since 2011, more than 600,000 trees, including cedars and other native species, have been planted throughout Lebanon as part of the Lebanon Reforestation Initiative, which aims to restore Lebanon's native forests<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>. Projects financed locally and by international charity are performing extensive reforestation of cedar being carried out in the Mediterranean region, particularly in Lebanon and Turkey, where over 50 million young cedars are being planted annually.
</p><p>The Lebanon Reforestation Initiative has been working since 2012 with tree nurseries throughout Lebanon to help grow stronger tree seedlings that are better suited to survive once planted<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkey">Turkey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reforestation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Turkey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>4000 years ago <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a> was 60% to 70% forested:<sup id="cite_ref-Colak_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Colak-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> although the <a href="/wiki/Flora_of_Turkey" title="Flora of Turkey">flora of Turkey</a> remains more biodiverse than many European countries deforestation occurred during both prehistoric<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> and historic times, including the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> periods.
</p><p>Since the first forest code of 1937 the official government definition of 'forest' has varied,<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> but according to the current definition 21 million hectares are forested (but only about half 'productive'), an increase of about 1 million hectares over the past 30 years.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> However according to the United Nations <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> definition of forest<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> about 12 million hectares was forested in 2015<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>, about 15% of the land surface.
</p><p>However according to the <a href="/wiki/World_Resources_Institute" title="World Resources Institute">World Resources Institute</a> “Atlas of Forest Landscape Restoration Opportunities” the reforestation opportunities are vastly greater.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> This could help limit <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_in_Turkey" class="mw-redirect" title="Global warming in Turkey">global warming in Turkey</a>. 50 million hectares are potential forest land, and to help preserve the <a href="/wiki/Turkey#Biodiversity" title="Turkey">biodiversity of Turkey</a> more sustainable forestry has been suggested.<sup id="cite_ref-Colak_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Colak-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reforestation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Organizations">Organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reforestation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Trees_for_the_Future" title="Trees for the Future">Trees for the Future</a> has assisted more than 170,000 families, in 6,800 villages of Asia, Africa and the Americas, to plant over 35 million trees.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Wangari_Maathai" title="Wangari Maathai">Wangari Maathai</a>, 2004 <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a> recipient, founded the <a href="/wiki/Green_Belt_Movement" title="Green Belt Movement">Green Belt Movement</a> which planted over 47 million trees to restore the Kenyan environment.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Shanghai <a href="/wiki/Roots_%26_Shoots" title="Roots &amp; Shoots">Roots &amp; Shoots</a>, a division of the <a href="/wiki/Jane_Goodall_Institute" title="Jane Goodall Institute">Jane Goodall Institute</a>, launched The Million Tree Project in Kulun Qi, <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolia</a> to plant one million trees to stop <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a> and alleviate global warming.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Reforestation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Reforestation&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span>  <small><i>(April 2014)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Reforestation competes with other land uses such as food production, livestock grazing and living space for further economic growth. Reforestation often has the tendency to create large fuel loads, resulting in significantly hotter combustion than fires involving low brush or grasses.  Reforestation can divert large amounts of water from other activities.  Reforesting sometimes results in extensive canopy creation that prevents growth of diverse vegetation in the shadowed areas and generating soil conditions that hamper other types of vegetation. Trees used in some reforesting efforts (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_globulus" title="Eucalyptus globulus">Eucalyptus globulus</a></i>) tend to extract large amounts of moisture from the soil, preventing the growth of other plants.
</p><p>There is also the risk that through a <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">forest fire</a> or <a href="/wiki/Forest_pathology" title="Forest pathology">insect outbreak</a> much of the stored carbon in a reforested area could make its way back to the atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-Canadell_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canadell-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1456</span></sup> Reduced harvesting rates and fire suppression have caused an increase in the forest biomass in the western United States over the past century. This causes an increase of about a factor of four in the frequency of fires due to longer and hotter dry seasons.<sup id="cite_ref-Canadell_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canadell-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1456</span></sup>
</p>
