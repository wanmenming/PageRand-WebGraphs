<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gas_emissions">Greenhouse gas emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_biodiesel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Greenhouse gas emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BioDieselFootprint.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/BioDieselFootprint.jpg/350px-BioDieselFootprint.jpg" width="350" height="215" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/BioDieselFootprint.jpg/525px-BioDieselFootprint.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/BioDieselFootprint.jpg/700px-BioDieselFootprint.jpg 2x" data-file-width="958" data-file-height="588" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BioDieselFootprint.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Calculation of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Intensity" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon Intensity">Carbon Intensity</a> of Soy biodiesel grown in the US and burnt in the UK, using figures calculated by the UK government for the purposes of the <a href="/wiki/Renewable_transport_fuel_obligation" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewable transport fuel obligation">Renewable transport fuel obligation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-UKRTFO_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UKRTFO-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BiodieselsCountryOfOrigin.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/BiodieselsCountryOfOrigin.jpg/350px-BiodieselsCountryOfOrigin.jpg" width="350" height="201" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/BiodieselsCountryOfOrigin.jpg/525px-BiodieselsCountryOfOrigin.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/BiodieselsCountryOfOrigin.jpg/700px-BiodieselsCountryOfOrigin.jpg 2x" data-file-width="998" data-file-height="573" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BiodieselsCountryOfOrigin.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Graph of UK figures for the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_Intensity" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon Intensity">Carbon Intensity</a> of <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">Biodiesels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>. This graph assumes that all biodiesel is used in its country of origin. It also assumes that the diesel is produced from pre-existing croplands rather than by changing land use<sup id="cite_ref-UKBioDiesel_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UKBioDiesel-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>An often mentioned incentive for using <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> is its capacity to lower <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions compared to those of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a>. Whether this is true or not depends on many factors. A general critic to biodiesel is the <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a> change, which have potential to cause even more emissions than what would be caused by using fossil fuels alone.<sup id="cite_ref-Science,_Land_Use_Change_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science,_Land_Use_Change-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Yet this problem would be fixed with <a href="/wiki/Algal_biofuel" class="mw-redirect" title="Algal biofuel">algal biofuel</a> which can use land unsuitable for agriculture. 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a> is one of the major <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>. Although the burning of biodiesel produces carbon dioxide emissions similar to those from ordinary fossil fuels, the <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> feedstock used in the production absorbs carbon dioxide from the atmosphere when it grows. Plants absorb carbon dioxide through a process known as <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> which allows it to store energy from sunlight in the form of sugars and starches. After the <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> is converted into biodiesel and burned as fuel the energy and carbon is released again. Some of that energy can be used to power an engine while the carbon dioxide is released back into the atmosphere.
</p><p>When considering the total amount of greenhouse gas emissions it is therefore important to consider the whole production process and what indirect effects such production might cause. The effect on carbon dioxide emissions is highly dependent on production methods and the type of feedstock used. Calculating the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_intensity" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon intensity">carbon intensity</a> of biofuels is a complex and inexact process, and is highly dependent on the assumptions made in the calculation. A calculation usually includes:
</p>
<ul><li>Emissions from growing the feedstock (e.g. Petrochemicals used in fertilizers)</li>
<li>Emissions from transporting the feedstock to the factory</li>
<li>Emissions from processing the feedstock into biodiesel</li>
<li>Absorption of CO2 Emissions from growing the feedstock</li></ul>
<p>Other factors can be very significant but are sometimes not considered. These include:
</p>
<ul><li>Emissions from the change in land use of the area where the fuel feedstock is grown.</li>
<li>Emissions from transportation of the biodiesel from the factory to its point of use</li>
<li>The efficiency of the biodiesel compared with standard diesel</li>
<li>The amount of Carbon Dioxide produced at the tail pipe. (Biodiesel can produce 4.7% more)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>
<li>The benefits due to the production of useful by-products, such as cattle feed or <a href="/wiki/Glycerine" class="mw-redirect" title="Glycerine">glycerine</a></li></ul>
<p>If land use change is not considered and assuming today's production methods, biodiesel from rapeseed and sunflower oil produce 45%-65% lower greenhouse gas emissions than petrodiesel.<sup id="cite_ref-SHU_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SHU-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EEA_report_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EEA_report-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> However, there is ongoing research to improve the efficiency of the production process.<sup id="cite_ref-SHU_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SHU-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EEA_report_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EEA_report-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Biodiesel produced from used cooking oil or other waste fat could reduce CO<sub>2</sub> emissions by as much as 85%.<sup id="cite_ref-UKRTFO_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UKRTFO-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> As long as the feedstock is grown on existing cropland, land use change has little or no effect on greenhouse gas emissions. However, there is concern that increased feedstock production directly affects the rate of deforestation. Such <a href="/wiki/Clearcutting" title="Clearcutting">clearcutting</a> cause carbon stored in the forest, soil and <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> layers to be released. The amount of greenhouse gas emissions from deforestation is so large that the benefits from lower emissions (caused by biodiesel use alone) would be negligible for hundreds of years.<sup id="cite_ref-UKRTFO_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UKRTFO-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Science,_Land_Use_Change_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science,_Land_Use_Change-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Biofuel produced from feedstock such as palm oil could therefore cause much higher carbon dioxide emissions than some types of fossil fuels.<sup id="cite_ref-greenpeace_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenpeace-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollution">Pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_biodiesel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the United States, biodiesel is the only <a href="/wiki/Alternative_fuel" title="Alternative fuel">alternative fuel</a> to have successfully completed the Health Effects Testing requirements (Tier I and Tier II) of the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(1990)" class="mw-redirect" title="Clean Air Act (1990)">Clean Air Act (1990)</a>.
</p><p>Biodiesel can reduce the direct tailpipe-emission of <a href="/wiki/Particulate" class="mw-redirect" title="Particulate">particulates</a>, small particles of solid combustion products, on vehicles with particulate filters by as much as 20 percent compared with low-sulfur (&lt; 50 ppm) diesel. Particulate emissions as the result of production are reduced by around 50 percent compared with fossil-sourced diesel.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeer_et_al.2004_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeer_et_al.2004-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodegradation">Biodegradation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_biodiesel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Biodegradation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/University_of_Idaho" title="University of Idaho">University of Idaho</a> study compared biodegradation rates of biodiesel, neat vegetable oils, biodiesel and petroleum diesel blends, and neat 2-D diesel fuel. Using low concentrations of the product to be degraded (10 ppm) in nutrient and sewage sludge amended solutions, they demonstrated that biodiesel degraded at the same rate as a dextrose control and 5 times as quickly as petroleum diesel over a period of 28 days, and that biodiesel blends doubled the rate of petroleum diesel degradation through <a href="/wiki/Co-metabolism" class="mw-redirect" title="Co-metabolism">co-metabolism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-uidaho04_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uidaho04-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
The same study examined <a href="/wiki/Soil_retrogression_and_degradation" title="Soil retrogression and degradation">soil degradation</a> using 10 000 ppm of biodiesel and petroleum diesel, and found biodiesel degraded at twice the rate of petroleum diesel in soil. In all cases, it was determined biodiesel also degraded more completely than petroleum diesel, which produced poorly degradable undetermined intermediates. Toxicity studies for the same project demonstrated no mortalities and few toxic effects on rats and rabbits with up to 5000&#160;mg/kg of biodiesel. Petroleum diesel showed no mortalities at the same concentration either, however toxic effects such as hair loss and urinary discolouring were noted with concentrations of &gt;2000&#160;mg/l in rabbits.:<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodegradation_in_Aquatic_Environments">Biodegradation in Aquatic Environments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_biodiesel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Biodegradation in Aquatic Environments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As biodiesel becomes more widely used, it is important to consider how consumption affects water quality and aquatic ecosystems. Research examining the biodegradability of different biodiesel fuels found that all of the biofuels studied (including Neat Rapeseed oil, Neat Soybean oil, and their modified ester products) were “readily biodegradable” compounds, and had a relatively high biodegradation rate in water.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, the presence of biodiesel can increase the rate of diesel biodegradation via co-metabolism. As the ratio of biodiesel is increased in biodiesel/diesel mixtures, the faster the diesel is degraded. Another study using controlled experimental conditions also showed that fatty acid methyl esters, the primary molecules in biodiesel, degraded much faster than petroleum diesel in sea water.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbonyl_Emissions">Carbonyl Emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_biodiesel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Carbonyl Emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When considering the emissions from fossil fuel and biofuel use, research typically focuses on major pollutants such as hydrocarbons. It is generally recognized that using biodiesel in place of diesel results in a substantial reduction in regulated gas emissions, but there has been a lack of information in research literature about the non-regulated compounds which also play a role in air pollution.<sup id="cite_ref-Tan,_J._2009_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tan,_J._2009-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> One study focused on the emissions of non-criteria carbonyl compounds from the burning of pure diesel and biodiesel blends in heavy-duty diesel engines. The results found that carbonyl emissions of formaldehyde, acetaldehyde, acrolein, acetone, propionaldehyde and butyraldehyde, were higher in biodiesel mixtures than emissions from pure diesel. Biodiesel use results in higher carbonyl emissions but lower total hydrocarbon emissions, which may be better as an alternative fuel source. Other studies have been done which conflict with these results, but comparisons are difficult to make due to various factors that differ between studies (such as types of fuel and engines used). In a paper which compared 12 research articles on carbonyl emissions from biodiesel fuel use, it found that 8 of the papers reported increased carbonyl compound emissions while 4 showed the opposite.<sup id="cite_ref-Tan,_J._2009_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tan,_J._2009-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> This is evidence that there is still much research required on these compounds.
</p>
