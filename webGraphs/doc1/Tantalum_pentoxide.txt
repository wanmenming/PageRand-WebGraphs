<p><b>Tantalum pentoxide</b>, also known as <a href="/wiki/Tantalum" title="Tantalum">tantalum</a>(V) oxide, is the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">Ta<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span></span>. It is a white solid that is insoluble in all solvents but is attacked by strong bases and hydrofluoric acid. <span class="chemf nowrap">Ta<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span></span> is an inert material with a high <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a> and low absorption (i.e. colourless), which makes it useful for coatings.<sup id="cite_ref-book_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is also extensively used in the production of <a href="/wiki/Tantalum_capacitor" title="Tantalum capacitor">capacitors</a>, due to its high <a href="/wiki/Dielectric_constant" class="mw-redirect" title="Dielectric constant">dielectric constant</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tantalum occurs in the minerals <a href="/wiki/Tantalite" title="Tantalite">tantalite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Columbite" title="Columbite">columbite</a> (columbium being an archaic name for niobium), which occur in <a href="/wiki/Pegmatite" title="Pegmatite">pegmatites</a>, an igneous rock formation. Mixtures of columbite and tantalite are called <a href="/wiki/Coltan" title="Coltan">coltan</a>.  Tantalite was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Anders_Gustaf_Ekeberg" title="Anders Gustaf Ekeberg">Anders Gustaf Ekeberg</a> at <a href="/wiki/Ytterby" title="Ytterby">Ytterby</a>, Sweden, and Kimoto, Finland. The minerals <a href="/wiki/Microlite" title="Microlite">microlite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pyrochlore" title="Pyrochlore">pyrochlore</a> contain approximately 70% and 10% Ta, respectively.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Refining">Refining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Refining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tantalum ores often contain significant amounts of <a href="/wiki/Niobium" title="Niobium">niobium</a>, which is itself a valuable metal. As such, both metals are extracted so that they may be sold. The overall process is one of <a href="/wiki/Hydrometallurgy" title="Hydrometallurgy">hydrometallurgy</a> and begins with a <a href="/wiki/Leaching_(metallurgy)" title="Leaching (metallurgy)">leaching</a> step; in which the ore is treated with <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to produce water-soluble <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride" title="Hydrogen fluoride">hydrogen fluorides</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Potassium_heptafluorotantalate" title="Potassium heptafluorotantalate">heptafluorotantalate</a>. This allows the metals to be separated from the various non-metallic impurities in the rock.
</p>
<dl><dd>(FeMn)(NbTa)<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> + 16 <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">HF</a> → H<sub>2</sub>[TaF<sub>7</sub>] + H<sub>2</sub>[NbOF<sub>5</sub>] + <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_fluoride" title="Iron(II) fluoride">FeF<sub>2</sub></a> + <a href="/wiki/Manganese(II)_fluoride" title="Manganese(II) fluoride">MnF<sub>2</sub></a> + 6 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>The tantalum and niobium hydrogenflorides are then removed from the <a href="/wiki/Aqueous" class="mw-redirect" title="Aqueous">aqueous</a> solution by <a href="/wiki/Liquid%E2%80%93liquid_extraction" title="Liquid–liquid extraction">liquid-liquid extraction</a> using <a href="/wiki/Organic_solvents" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic solvents">organic solvents</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Cyclohexanone" title="Cyclohexanone">cyclohexanone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Methyl_isobutyl_ketone" title="Methyl isobutyl ketone">methyl isobutyl ketone</a>. This step allows the simple removal of various metal impurities (e.g. iron and manganese) which remain in the aqueous phase in the form of <a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">fluorides</a>. Separation of the tantalum and niobium is then achieved by <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> adjustment. Niobium requires a higher level of acidity to remain soluble in the organic phase and can hence be selectively removed by extraction into less acidic water.
The pure tantalum hydrogen fluoride solution is then neutralised with aqueous <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> to give <a href="/wiki/Water_of_hydration" class="mw-redirect" title="Water of hydration">hydrated</a> tantalum oxide (Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>x</sub>), which is <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcinated</a> to tantalum pentoxide (Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>) as described in these idealized equations:<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>H<sub>2</sub>[TaF<sub>7</sub>] + 5 H<sub>2</sub>O + 7 <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">NH<sub>3</sub></a> → 0.5 Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>5</sub> + 7 <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_fluoride" title="Ammonium fluoride">NH<sub>4</sub>F</a></dd>
<dd>Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>5</sub>  →   Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> + 5 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Natural pure tantalum oxide is known as the mineral <a href="/wiki/Tantite" title="Tantite">tantite</a>, although it is exceedingly rare.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="From_alkoxides">From alkoxides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: From alkoxides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tantalum oxide is frequently used in electronics, often in the form of <a href="/wiki/Thin_film" title="Thin film">thin films</a>. For these applications it can be produced by <a href="/wiki/MOCVD" class="mw-redirect" title="MOCVD">MOCVD</a> (or related techniques), which involves the <a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolysis</a> of its volatile <a href="/wiki/Halide" title="Halide">halides</a> or <a href="/wiki/Alkoxide" title="Alkoxide">alkoxides</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/Tantalum(V)_ethoxide" title="Tantalum(V) ethoxide">Ta<sub>2</sub>(OEt)<sub>10</sub></a> + 5 H<sub>2</sub>O → Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> + 10 EtOH</dd>
<dd>2 <a href="/wiki/Tantalum_chloride" class="mw-redirect" title="Tantalum chloride">TaCl<sub>5</sub></a> + 5 H<sub>2</sub>O → Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> + 10 HCl</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_properties">Structure and properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Structure and properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The crystal structure of tantalum pentoxide has been the matter of some debate. The bulk material is <a href="/wiki/Disordered_phase" class="mw-redirect" title="Disordered phase">disordered</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-temp_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-temp-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> being either <a href="/wiki/Amorphous" class="mw-redirect" title="Amorphous">amorphous</a> or <a href="/wiki/Polycrystalline" class="mw-redirect" title="Polycrystalline">polycrystalline</a>; with <a href="/wiki/Single_crystal" title="Single crystal">single crystals</a> being difficult to grow. As such <a href="/wiki/Xray_crystallography" class="mw-redirect" title="Xray crystallography">Xray crystallography</a> has largely been limited to <a href="/wiki/Powder_diffraction" title="Powder diffraction">powder diffraction</a>, which provides less structural information.
</p><p>At least 2 <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a> are known to exist. A low temperature form, known as L- or β-Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>, and the high temperature form known as H- or α-Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>. The transition between these two forms is slow and reversible; taking place between 1000-1360&#160;°C, with a mixture of structures existing at intermediate temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-temp_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-temp-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The structures of both polymorphs consist of chains built from octahedral TaO<sub>6</sub> and pentagonal bipyramidal TaO<sub>7</sub> polyhedra sharing opposite vertices; which are further joined by edge-sharing.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The overall crystal system is <a href="/wiki/Orthorhombic" class="mw-redirect" title="Orthorhombic">orthorhombic</a> in both cases, with the <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> of β-Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> being identified as <i>Pna2</i> by single crystal X-ray diffraction.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
A high pressure form (<i>Z</i>-Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>) has also been reported, in which the Ta atoms adopt a 7 coordinate geometry to give a <a href="/wiki/Monoclinic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> structure (space group C2).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The difficulty in forming material with a uniform structure has led to variations in its reported properties. Like many metal oxides Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is an <a href="/wiki/Insulator_(electricity)" title="Insulator (electricity)">insulator</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Band_gap" title="Band gap">band gap</a> has variously been reported as being between 3.8 and 5.3 eV, depending on the method of manufacture.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In general the more <a href="/wiki/Amorphous" class="mw-redirect" title="Amorphous">amorphous</a> the material the greater its observed band gap.
These observed values are significantly higher than those predicted by <a href="/wiki/Computational_chemistry" title="Computational chemistry">computational chemistry</a> (2.3 - 3.8 eV).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Its <a href="/wiki/Dielectric_constant" class="mw-redirect" title="Dielectric constant">dielectric constant</a> is typically about ~25<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> although values of over 50 have been reported.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In general tantalum pentoxide is considered to be a <a href="/wiki/High-k_dielectric" class="mw-redirect" title="High-k dielectric">high-k dielectric</a> material.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> does not react appreciably with either HCl or HBr, however it will dissolve in <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a>, and reacts with <a href="/wiki/Potassium_bifluoride" title="Potassium bifluoride">potassium bifluoride</a> and HF according to the following equation:<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>  +  4 KHF<sub>2</sub> + 6 HF   →   2 <a href="/wiki/Potassium_heptafluorotantalate" title="Potassium heptafluorotantalate">K<sub>4</sub>[TaF<sub>7</sub>] </a> +  5 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> can be reduced to metallic Ta via the use of metallic reductants such as calcium and aluminium.
</p>
<dl><dd>Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> + 5 Ca →  2 Ta + 5 <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">CaO</a></dd></dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_electronics">In electronics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In electronics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Owing to its high <a href="/wiki/Band_gap" title="Band gap">band gap</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dielectric_constant" class="mw-redirect" title="Dielectric constant">dielectric constant</a>, tantalum pentoxide has found a variety of uses in electronics, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Tantalum_capacitor" title="Tantalum capacitor">tantalum capacitors</a>. These are used in <a href="/wiki/Automotive_electronics" title="Automotive electronics">automotive electronics</a>, cell phones, and pagers, electronic circuitry; thin-film components; and high-speed tools. In the 1990s, interest grew in the use of tantalum oxide as a <a href="/wiki/High-k_dielectric" class="mw-redirect" title="High-k dielectric">high-k dielectric</a> for <a href="/wiki/DRAM" class="mw-redirect" title="DRAM">DRAM</a> capacitor applications.,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is used in on-chip metal-insulator-metal capacitors for high frequency <a href="/wiki/CMOS" title="CMOS">CMOS</a> integrated circuits. Tantalum oxide may have applications as the charge trapping layer for <a href="/wiki/Non-volatile_memory" title="Non-volatile memory">Non-volatile memories</a>.,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> There are applications  of tantalum oxide in <a href="/wiki/Resistive_random-access_memory" title="Resistive random-access memory">resistive switching memories</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tantalum_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to its high <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a>, Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> has been utilized in the fabrication of the <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a> of <a href="/wiki/Photographic_lens" class="mw-redirect" title="Photographic lens">photographic lenses</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-book_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-book-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
