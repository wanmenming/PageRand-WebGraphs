<p><b>Neptunium(IV) oxide</b>, or <b>neptunium dioxide</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Radioactive" class="mw-redirect" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a>, olive green<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> cubic<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> crystalline solid with the formula NpO<sub>2</sub>. It is a common product of plutonium fission, and emits both α- and γ-particles.<sup id="cite_ref-enth_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enth-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neptunium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Industrially, neptunium dioxide is formed from the <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry)" title="Precipitation (chemistry)">precipitation</a> of neptunium(IV) oxalate from a neptunium feed solution with oxalic acid, followed by <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcination</a> to neptunium dioxide. The neptunium feed solution (which includes varying oxidation states of neptunium) is <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduced</a> to a predominately neptunium(IV) solution via ascorbic acid prior to the addition of oxalic acid. A hydrazine inhibitor is initially added to the neptunium feed solution to protect the neptunium and ascorbic acid from decay.<sup id="cite_ref-Porter1964_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Porter1964-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Extrapolated and balanced from "The Production of Neptunium Dioxide" by J. A. Porter</b><sup id="cite_ref-Porter1964_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Porter1964-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Np<sup>4+</sup> + Np<sup>5+</sup> + Np<sup>6+</sup> + HNO<sub>3</sub> + C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>8</sub>O<sub>6</sub> → 3 Np<sup>4+</sup> + C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> + H<sub>2</sub> + HNO<sub>3</sub>
</p><p>Np<sup>4+</sup> + C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>H<sub>2</sub> → Np(C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>) • 6H<sub>2</sub>O + 2H<sup>−</sup>
</p><p>Np(C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>) • 6H<sub>2</sub>O + Δ → Np(C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>)
</p><p>Np(C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>) + Δ → NpO<sub>2</sub> + 2CO<sub>2</sub>
</p><p>Neptunium dioxide can also be formed effectively from precipitation of neptunium(IV) peroxide, but the oxalate reduction has been found to be more industrially efficient.<sup id="cite_ref-Porter1964_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Porter1964-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Purification">Purification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neptunium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Purification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a byproduct of nuclear waste, neptunium dioxide can be purified by <a href="/wiki/Fluorination" class="mw-redirect" title="Fluorination">fluorination</a>, followed by reduction with excess calcium in the presence of iodine.<sup id="cite_ref-enth_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enth-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> However, the aforementioned synthesis yields a quite pure solid, with less than 0.3% weight of impurities. Generally, further purification is unnecessary.<sup id="cite_ref-Porter1964_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Porter1964-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_properties">Other properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neptunium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Neptunium dioxide contributes to the α-decay of <sup>241</sup>Am, reducing its usual <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> an untested but appreciable amount.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The compound has a low <a href="/wiki/Specific_heat_capacity" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific heat capacity">specific heat capacity</a> (900 K, compared with <a href="/wiki/Uranium_dioxide" title="Uranium dioxide">uranium dioxide</a>'s specific head capacity of 1400 K), an abnormality theorized to stem from its 5f electron count.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Another unique trait of neptunium dioxide is its "mysterious low-temperature ordered phase". Mentioned above, it references an abnormal level of order for an actinitde dioxide complex at low temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Further discussion of such topics could indicate useful physical trends in the actinides.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Neptunium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The neptunium dioxide complex is used as a means of stabilizing, and decreasing the "long term environmental burden"<sup id="cite_ref-Collie2011_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collie2011-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> of neptunium as a nuclear fission byproduct. Actinide-containing nuclear waste will commonly be treated so that various AnO<sub>2</sub> (where An = U, Np, Pu, Am, etc.) complexes form. In neptunium dioxide, the neptunium is of reduced radio toxicity compared with pure neptunium metal, and is thus more desirable for storage and disposal. Neptunium dioxide has also been show to contribute to increased decay rates of radioactive metals, an application which is currently being explored.<sup id="cite_ref-Collie2011_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collie2011-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Neptunium dioxide is also used experimentally for research into nuclear chemistry and physics, and it is speculated that neptunium dioxide could be used to make efficient nuclear weapons. In nuclear reactors, neptunium dioxide can also be used as the target metal for plutonium bombardment.<sup id="cite_ref-Collie2011_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collie2011-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Furthermore, a patent for a rocket powered by neptunium dioxide is held by Shirakawa Toshihisa © 2007,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> but there is little information available into research and production associated with such a product.
</p>
