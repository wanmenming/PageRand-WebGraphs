<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Individual action on climate change</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>

Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span>  <small><i>(March 2011)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table> 
<p>Making various personal choices has been advocated as a means of fighting <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/IPCC" class="mw-redirect" title="IPCC">IPCC</a> <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fifth Assessment Report">Fifth Assessment Report</a> emphasises that behaviour, lifestyle and cultural change have a high mitigation potential in some sectors, particularly when complementing technological and structural change.<sup id="cite_ref-edenhofer-etal-2014_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edenhofer-etal-2014-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>20</span></sup> In general, <a href="/wiki/Overconsumption" title="Overconsumption">higher consumption</a> lifestyles have a greater environmental impact. Several scientific studies have shown that when people, especially those living in <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed countries</a> but more generally including all countries, wish to reduce their carbon footprint, there are four key "high-impact" actions they can take:<sup id="cite_ref-Wynes-Nicholas-2017_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wynes-Nicholas-2017-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ceballos-Ehrlich-2017-05_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ceballos-Ehrlich-2017-05-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PimmJenkins_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PimmJenkins-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>1. <a href="/wiki/Human_overpopulation" title="Human overpopulation">Not having</a> an additional child (58.6 tonnes CO<sub>2</sub>-equivalent emission reductions per year)</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>2. Living <a href="/wiki/Car-free_movement" title="Car-free movement">car-free</a> (2.4 tonnes CO2)</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>3. Avoiding one round-trip transatlantic flight (1.6 tonnes)</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>4. Eating a <a href="/wiki/Plant-based_diet" title="Plant-based diet">plant-based diet</a> (0.8 tonnes)</dd></dl></dd></dl>
<dl><dd>These appear to differ significantly from the popular advice for “greening” one’s lifestyle, which seem to fall mostly into the “low-impact” category: Replacing a typical car with a hybrid (0.52 tonnes); Washing clothes in cold water (0.25 tonnes); Recycling (0.21 tonnes); Upgrading light bulbs (0.10 tonnes); etc. The researchers found that public discourse on reducing one’s carbon footprint overwhelmingly focuses on low-impact behaviors, and that mention of the high-impact behaviors is almost non-existent in the mainstream media, government publications, K-12 school textbooks, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-Wynes-Nicholas-2017_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wynes-Nicholas-2017-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ceballos-Ehrlich-2017-05_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ceballos-Ehrlich-2017-05-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PimmJenkins_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PimmJenkins-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  The researchers added that “Our recommended high-impact actions are more effective than many more commonly discussed options (e.g. eating a plant-based diet saves eight times more emissions than upgrading light bulbs). More significantly, a US family who chooses to have one fewer child would provide the same level of emissions reductions as 684 teenagers who choose to adopt comprehensive recycling for the rest of their lives.”<sup id="cite_ref-Wynes-Nicholas-2017_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wynes-Nicholas-2017-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ceballos-Ehrlich-2017-05_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ceballos-Ehrlich-2017-05-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PimmJenkins_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PimmJenkins-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<ul><li>A <a href="/wiki/Carbon_diet" title="Carbon diet">carbon diet</a> is an effective way to understand the amount of impact on the environment and how to make meaningful changes.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Low_carbon_diet" class="mw-redirect" title="Low carbon diet">low carbon diet</a> is a way of reducing impact by choosing food that causes much less pollution.</li>
<li>Trees: Protecting forests and planting new trees contributes to the absorption of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> from the air. There are many opportunities to plant trees in the yard, along roads, in parks, and in public gardens. In addition, some charities plant fast-growing trees—for as little as $US0.10 per tree—to help people in tropical developing countries restore the productivity of their lands.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.treesftf.org/about.htm">[1]</a>  Conversely, clearing old-growth forests adds to the carbon in the atmosphere, so buying non-old-growth paper is good for the climate as well as the forest.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>
<li>Labels: The <a href="/wiki/Energy_Star" title="Energy Star">Energy Star</a> label can be seen on many household appliances, home electronics, office equipment, heating and cooling equipment, windows, residential light fixtures, and other products. Energy Star products use less energy.</li>
<li>Travel:
<ul><li>In the United States, <a href="/wiki/Public_opinion_on_climate_change" class="mw-redirect" title="Public opinion on climate change">perception towards climate change</a> influenced people to change their travel lifestyle. More than a third of Americans have changed their travel habits in 2008, versus only 14% in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Cars: Purchasing a vehicle which gets high <a href="/wiki/Gas_mileage" class="mw-redirect" title="Gas mileage">gas mileage</a> helps to reduce emissions of carbon dioxide.</li>
<li>Flying: Avoiding air travel and particularly <a href="/wiki/Frequent-flyer_program#Climate_and_environmental_issues" title="Frequent-flyer program">frequent flyer programs</a> has a high benefit because the convenience makes <a href="/wiki/Hypermobility_(travel)" title="Hypermobility (travel)">frequent, long distance travel</a> easy, and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_aviation#Mechanisms_and_Cumulative_Effects_of_aviation_on_Climate" title="Environmental impact of aviation">high-altitude emissions</a> are more potent for the climate than the same emissions made at ground level.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Walking" title="Walking">Walking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Running" title="Running">running</a> are among the least environmentally harmful modes of transportation,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> followed by the <a href="/wiki/Bicycle" title="Bicycle">bicycle</a>.</li></ul></li>
<li>Many energy suppliers in various countries worldwide have options to purchase part or pure "<a href="/wiki/Green_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Green energy">green energy</a>." The wind energy produced in Denmark, for example, provides about 20 percent of the country's total electricity needs.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> These methods of energy production emit no greenhouse gases once they are up and running.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carbon_offset" title="Carbon offset">Carbon offsets</a>: The principle of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_offset" title="Carbon offset">carbon offset</a> is thus: one decides that they don't want to be responsible for accelerating climate change, and they've already made efforts to reduce their <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_emissions" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon dioxide emissions">carbon dioxide emissions</a>, so they decide to pay someone else to further reduce their net emissions by planting trees or by taking up low-carbon technologies. Every unit of carbon that is absorbed by trees—or not emitted due to your funding of renewable energy deployment—offsets the emissions from their fossil fuel use. In many cases, funding of renewable energy, energy efficiency, or <a href="/wiki/Tree_planting" title="Tree planting">tree planting</a> — particularly in developing nations—can be a relatively cheap way of making an individual "carbon neutral".  Carbon offset providers—some as inexpensive as US$0.11 per metric ton (<a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a> 0.10 per US ton) of carbon dioxide—are referenced below under Lifestyle Action.</li>
<li>Low carbon investments and <a href="/wiki/Ethical_banking" title="Ethical banking">ethical banking</a>: If coupled with a charitably funded low-carbon investment fund to pay for long-term adaptation needs, and ethical investments to accelerate global development, ethical low-carbon investments can allow a global consumer-led response to climate change<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>. With roughly 5% of OECD disposable income saved<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="https://data.oecd.org/hha/household-savings.htm">[2]</a>, ethical investments have tremendous potential.</li></ul>
