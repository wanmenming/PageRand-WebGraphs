<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Fisheries management</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>
<b>Fisheries management</b> is the activity of protecting <a href="/wiki/Fishery" title="Fishery">fishery</a> resources so <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_fishery" title="Sustainable fishery">sustainable exploitation</a> is possible, drawing on <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_science" title="Fisheries science">fisheries science</a>, and including the <a href="/wiki/Precautionary_principle" title="Precautionary principle">precautionary principle</a>.  Modern fisheries management is often referred to as a governmental system of appropriate management rules based on defined objectives and a mix of management means to implement the rules, which are put in place by a system of <a href="/wiki/Monitoring_control_and_surveillance" title="Monitoring control and surveillance">monitoring control and surveillance</a>. A popular approach is the ecosystem approach to fisheries management.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization_of_the_United_Nations" class="mw-redirect" title="Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations">Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations</a> (FAO), there are "no clear and generally accepted definitions of fisheries management".<sup id="cite_ref-FAOFM_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAOFM-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> However, the working definition used by the FAO and much cited elsewhere is: 
</p>
<blockquote><p>The integrated process of information gathering, analysis, planning, consultation, decision-making, allocation of resources and formulation and implementation, with enforcement as necessary, of regulations or rules which govern fisheries activities in order to ensure the continued productivity of the resources and the accomplishment of other fisheries objectives.<sup id="cite_ref-FAOFM_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAOFM-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fisheries have been explicitly managed in some places for hundreds of years. More than 80 percent of the world's commercial exploitation of fish and shellfish are harvested from natural occurring populations in the oceans and freshwater areas.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> For example, the <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Māori people</a>, New Zealand residents for about 700 years, had prohibitions against taking more than what could be eaten and about giving back the first fish caught as an offering to sea god <a href="/wiki/Tangaroa" title="Tangaroa">Tangaroa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Starting in the 18th century attempts were made to regulate fishing in the <a href="/wiki/Nord-Norge" class="mw-redirect" title="Nord-Norge">North Norwegian</a> fishery. This resulted in the enactment of a law in 1816 on the <a href="/wiki/Lofoten" title="Lofoten">Lofoten</a> fishery, which established in some measure what has come to be known as territorial use rights.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"The fishing banks were divided into areas belonging to the nearest fishing base on land and further subdivided into fields where the boats were allowed to fish. The allocation of the fishing fields was in the hands of local governing committees, usually headed by the owner of the onshore facilities which the fishermen had to rent for accommodation and for drying the fish."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Governmental resource protection-based fisheries management is a relatively new idea, first developed for North European fisheries after the first <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">Overfishing</a> Conference held in London in 1936. In 1957 British fisheries researchers <a href="/wiki/Ray_Beverton" title="Ray Beverton">Ray Beverton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Holt" title="Sidney Holt">Sidney Holt</a> published a seminal work on <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> commercial fisheries dynamics.<sup id="cite_ref-Beverton_and_Holt,_1957_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Beverton_and_Holt,_1957-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In the 1960s the work became the theoretical platform for North European management schemes.
</p><p>After some years away from the field of fisheries management, Beverton criticized his earlier work in a paper given at the first World Fisheries Congress in Athens in 1992. "The Dynamics of Exploited Fish Populations" expressed his concerns, including the way his and Sidney Holt's work had been misinterpreted and misused by fishery biologists and managers during the previous 30 years.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, the institutional foundation for modern fishery management had been laid.
</p><p>In 1996, the <a href="/wiki/Marine_Stewardship_Council" title="Marine Stewardship Council">Marine Stewardship Council</a> was founded to set standards for <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_fishing" class="mw-redirect" title="Sustainable fishing">sustainable fishing</a>. In 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture_Stewardship_Council" title="Aquaculture Stewardship Council">Aquaculture Stewardship Council</a> was created to do the same for <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a>.
</p><p>A report by <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">Prince Charles</a>' International Sustainability Unit, the New York-based <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Defense_Fund" title="Environmental Defense Fund">Environmental Defense Fund</a> and 50in10 published in July 2014 estimated global fisheries were adding $270 billion a year to global GDP, but by full implementation of sustainable fishing, that figure could rise by  an extra amount of as much as $50 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-GlobalFisheries_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GlobalFisheries-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_objectives">Political objectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political objectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/FAO" class="mw-redirect" title="FAO">FAO</a>, fisheries management should be based explicitly on political objectives, ideally with transparent priorities.<sup id="cite_ref-FAOGuidebook_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAOGuidebook-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Typical political objectives when exploiting a fish resource are to:<sup id="cite_ref-Duzgunes_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duzgunes-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustainable_yield" title="Maximum sustainable yield">maximize sustainable biomass yield</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Optimum_sustainable_yield" title="Optimum sustainable yield">maximize sustainable economic yield</a></li>
<li>secure and increase employment</li>
<li>secure protein production and food supplies</li>
<li>increase export income</li></ul>
<p>Such political goals can also be a weak part of fisheries management, since the objectives can conflict with each other.<sup id="cite_ref-Duzgunes_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duzgunes-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_objectives">International objectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: International objectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fisheries objectives need to be expressed in concrete management rules. In most countries fisheries management rules should be based on the internationally agreed, though non-binding, Code of Conduct for Responsible Fisheries,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> agreed at a meeting of the U.N.'s Food and Agriculture Organization <a href="/wiki/FAO" class="mw-redirect" title="FAO">FAO</a> session in 1995. The <a href="/wiki/Precautionary_principle" title="Precautionary principle">precautionary approach</a> it prescribes is typically implemented in concrete management rules as minimum <a href="/wiki/Spawn_(biology)" title="Spawn (biology)">spawning</a> <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a>, maximum fishing mortality rates, etc. In 2005 the <a href="/wiki/UBC_Fisheries_Centre" title="UBC Fisheries Centre">UBC Fisheries Centre</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_British_Columbia" title="University of British Columbia">University of British Columbia</a> comprehensively reviewed the performance of the world's major fishing nations against the Code.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>International agreements are required in order to regulate fisheries in international waters. The desire for agreement on this and other maritime issues led to three conferences on the Law of the Sea, and ultimately to the treaty known as the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea" title="United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea">United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea</a> (UNCLOS). Concepts such as <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone" title="Exclusive economic zone">exclusive economic zones</a> (EEZ, extending 200 nautical miles (370&#160;km) from a nation's coasts) allocate certain sovereign rights and responsibilities for resource management to individual countries.
</p><p>Other situations need additional intergovernmental coordination. For example, in the Mediterranean Sea and other relatively narrow bodies of water, EEZ of 200 nautical miles (370&#160;km) are irrelevant. International waters beyond 12-nautical-mile (22&#160;km) from shore require explicit agreements.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Straddling_stocks" class="mw-redirect" title="Straddling stocks">Straddling fish stocks</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Fish_migration" title="Fish migration">migrate</a> through more than one EEZ also present challenges. Here sovereign responsibility must be agreed with neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Coastal_states" class="mw-redirect" title="Coastal states">coastal states</a> and fishing entities. Usually this is done through the medium of a regional organisation set up for the purpose of <a href="/wiki/Straddling_Fish_Stocks_Agreement" title="Straddling Fish Stocks Agreement">coordinating the management</a> of that stock.
</p><p>UNCLOS does not prescribe precisely how fisheries confined only to international waters should be managed. Several new fisheries (such as high seas <a href="/wiki/Bottom_trawling" title="Bottom trawling">bottom trawling</a> fisheries) are not (yet) subject to international agreement across their entire range. In November 2004 the <a href="/wiki/UN_General_Assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="UN General Assembly">UN General Assembly</a> issued a resolution on Fisheries that prepared for further development of international fisheries management law.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management_mechanisms">Management mechanisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Management mechanisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many countries have set up Ministries/Government Departments, named "<a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Fisheries_(New_Zealand)" title="Ministry of Fisheries (New Zealand)">Ministry of Fisheries</a>" or similar, controlling aspects of fisheries within their <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zones" class="mw-redirect" title="Exclusive economic zones">exclusive economic zones</a>.
Four categories of management means have been devised, regulating either input/investment, or output, and operating either directly or indirectly:
</p>

<th>Inputs</th>

<p>Technical means may include:
</p>
<ul><li>prohibiting devices such as bows and arrows, and spears, or firearms</li>
<li>prohibiting nets</li>
<li>setting minimum mesh sizes</li>
<li>limiting the average potential catch of a vessel in the fleet (vessel and crew size, gear, electronic gear and other physical "inputs".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>prohibiting bait</li>
<li>snagging</li>
<li>limits on fish traps</li>
<li>limiting the number of poles or lines per fisherman</li>
<li>restricting the number of simultaneous fishing vessels</li>
<li>limiting a vessel's average operational intensity per unit time at sea</li>
<li>limiting average time at sea</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Catch_quotas">Catch quotas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Catch quotas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Individual_transferable_quota" class="mw-redirect" title="Individual transferable quota">Individual transferable quota</a></div>
<p>Systems that use <i>individual transferable quotas</i> (ITQ), also called individual fishing quota limit the total catch and allocate shares of that quota among the fishers who work that fishery. Fishers can buy/sell/trade shares as they choose.
</p><p>A large scale study in 2008 provided strong evidence that ITQ's can help to prevent fishery collapse and even restore fisheries that appear to be in decline.<sup id="cite_ref-costello2008_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-costello2008-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Other studies have shown negative socio-economic consequences of ITQs, especially on small-sclale fisheries.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> These consequences include concentration of quota in that hands of few fishers; increased number of inactive fishers leasing their quotas to others (a phenomenon known as armchair fishermen); and detrimental effects on coastal communities.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precautionary_principle">Precautionary principle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Precautionary principle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>A Fishery Manager's Guidebook</i> issued in 2002 by the <a href="/wiki/FAO" class="mw-redirect" title="FAO">FAO</a> advises that a set of working principles should be applied to "highlight the underlying key issues" of fisheries management."<sup id="cite_ref-FAOGuidebook_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAOGuidebook-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>130</span></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FAO2002_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAO2002-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> There are 8 principles that should be considered as a whole in order to best manage a fishery. The first principle focuses on the finite nature of fish stocks and how potential yields must be estimated based on the biological constraints of the population.
</p><p>In a paper published in 2007, Shertzer and Prager suggested that there can be significant benefits to <a href="/wiki/Fish_stock" title="Fish stock">stock biomass</a> and fishery yield if management is stricter and more prompt.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> This is supported by recent work on the management of North Sea fisheries in accordance with ranges of acceptable fishing, where fishing at the top of the "acceptable" ranges is many times more risky than fishing near the bottom, but delivers only 20% more yield.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fisheries_law">Fisheries law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fisheries law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_law" title="Fisheries law">Fisheries law</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fisheries_law" title="Fisheries law">Fisheries law</a> is an emerging and specialized area of law which includes the study and analysis of different fisheries management approaches, including seafood safety regulations and aquaculture regulations. Despite its importance, this area is rarely taught at law schools around the world, which leaves a vacuum of advocacy and research.
</p><p>Fisheries legislation on a national level differs greatly between countries <sup id="cite_ref-FAOGuidebook_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAOGuidebook-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>130</span></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FAO2002_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAO2002-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Fisheries may also me managed on an international level. One the first laws enacted was the "United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea of 10 December 1982 (LOS Convention)", which entered into force in 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-FAOGuidebook_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAOGuidebook-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>130</span></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FAO2002_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAO2002-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> This law set the foundation for all international agreements related to oceans that followed.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">Climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_and_climate_change" title="Fisheries and climate change">Fisheries and climate change</a></div>
<p>In the past, changing <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> has affected inland and offshore fisheries and such changes are likely to continue.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>  From a fisheries perspective, the specific driving factors of climate change include rising water temperature, alterations in the <a href="/wiki/Hydrologic_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrologic cycle">hydrologic cycle</a>, changes in nutrient fluxes, and relocation of <a href="/wiki/Spawning" class="mw-redirect" title="Spawning">spawning</a> and nursery habitat.  Further, changes in such factors would affect resources at all levels of biological organization, including the genetic, organism, population, and <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> levels.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Understanding how these factors affect fisheries at a more nuanced level stand as challenges that fish biologists, across multiple fields, still need to face.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_dynamics">Population dynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Population dynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics_of_fisheries" title="Population dynamics of fisheries">Population dynamics of fisheries</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics_of_fisheries" title="Population dynamics of fisheries">Population dynamics</a> describes the growth and decline of a given fishery <a href="/wiki/Fish_stock" title="Fish stock">stock</a> over time, as controlled by birth, death and migration. It is the basis for understanding changing fishery patterns and issues such as habitat destruction, predation and optimal harvesting rates. The population dynamics of fisheries has been traditionally used by <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_science" title="Fisheries science">fisheries scientists</a> to determine <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_yield_in_fisheries" title="Sustainable yield in fisheries">sustainable yields</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The basic accounting relation for population dynamics is the <a href="/wiki/Matrix_population_models" title="Matrix population models">BIDE</a> model:<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><i>N</i><sub>1</sub> = <i>N</i><sub>0</sub> + <i>B</i> − <i>D</i> + <i>I</i> − <i>E</i></dd></dl>
<p>where <i>N</i><sub>1</sub> is the number of individuals at time 1, <i>N</i><sub>0</sub> is the number of individuals at time 0, <i>B</i> is the number of individuals born, <i>D</i> the number that died, <i>I</i> the number that immigrated, and <i>E</i> the number that emigrated between time 0 and time 1. While immigration and emigration can be present in <a href="/wiki/Wild_fisheries_of_the_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Wild fisheries of the world">wild fisheries</a>, they are usually not measured.
</p><p>Care is needed when applying population dynamics to real world fisheries. In the past, over-simplistic modelling, such as ignoring the size, age and reproductive status of the fish, focusing solely on a single species, ignoring <a href="/wiki/Bycatch" title="Bycatch">bycatch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_effects_of_fishing" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental effects of fishing">physical damage</a> to the ecosystem, has accelerated the collapse of key <a href="/wiki/Fish_stock" title="Fish stock">stocks</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Epitaph_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Epitaph-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lessons_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lessons-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elderly_maternal_fish">Elderly maternal fish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Elderly maternal fish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sebastes_mystinus_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Sebastes_mystinus_2.jpg/220px-Sebastes_mystinus_2.jpg" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Sebastes_mystinus_2.jpg/330px-Sebastes_mystinus_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Sebastes_mystinus_2.jpg/440px-Sebastes_mystinus_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="823" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sebastes_mystinus_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Old, fat, female rockfish are the best producers.</div></div></div>
<p>Traditional management practices aim to reduce the number of old, slow-growing fish, leaving more room and resources for younger, faster-growing fish. Most marine fish produce huge numbers of eggs. The assumption was that younger spawners would produce plenty of viable <a href="/wiki/Larvae" class="mw-redirect" title="Larvae">larvae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ASSS_2005_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASSS_2005-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, 2005 research on <a href="/wiki/Sebastes" title="Sebastes">rockfish</a> shows that large, elderly females are far more important than younger fish in maintaining productive fisheries. The larvae produced by these older maternal fish grow faster, survive starvation better, and are much more likely to survive than the offspring of younger fish. Failure to account for the role of older fish may help explain recent collapses of some major US West Coast fisheries. Recovery of some stocks is expected to take decades. One way to prevent such collapses is to establish marine reserves, where fishing is not allowed and fish populations age naturally.<sup id="cite_ref-ASSS_2005_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ASSS_2005-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_quality">Data quality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Data quality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to fisheries scientist Milo Adkison, the primary limitation in fisheries management decisions is the absence of quality data. Fisheries management decisions are often based on population models, but the models need quality data to be effective. He asserts that scientists and fishery managers would be better served with simpler models and improved data.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The most reliable source for summary statistics is the <a href="/wiki/FAO" class="mw-redirect" title="FAO">FAO</a> Fisheries Department.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecopath">Ecopath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ecopath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ecopath" title="Ecopath">Ecopath</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Ecosim" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecosim">Ecosim</a> (EwE), is an <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> modelling <a href="/wiki/Software_suite" title="Software suite">software suite</a>. It was initially a <a href="/wiki/NOAA" class="mw-redirect" title="NOAA">NOAA</a> initiative led by Jeffrey Polovina, later primarily developed at the <a href="/wiki/UBC_Fisheries_Centre" title="UBC Fisheries Centre">UBC Fisheries Centre</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_British_Columbia" title="University of British Columbia">University of British Columbia</a>. In 2007, it was named as one of the ten biggest scientific breakthroughs in NOAA’s 200-year history. The citation states that Ecopath "revolutionized scientists’ ability worldwide to understand complex marine ecosystems". Behind this lies two decades of development work by <a href="/wiki/Villy_Christensen" title="Villy Christensen">Villy Christensen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carl_Walters" title="Carl Walters">Carl Walters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Pauly" title="Daniel Pauly">Daniel Pauly</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_scientist" class="mw-redirect" title="Fisheries scientist">fisheries scientists</a>. As of 2010 there are 6000 registered users in 155 countries. Ecopath is widely used in fisheries management as a tool for modelling and visualising the complex relationships that exist in real world marine ecosystems.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_factors">Human factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Human factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Managing fisheries is about managing people and businesses, and not about managing fish. Fish populations are managed by regulating the actions of people.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> If fisheries management is to be successful, then associated human factors, such as the reactions of fishermen, are of key importance, and need to be understood.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Management regulations must also consider the implications for stakeholders. Commercial fishermen rely on catches to provide for their families just as farmers rely on crops. Commercial fishing can be a traditional trade passed down from generation to generation. Most commercial fishing is based in towns built around the fishing industry; regulation changes can impact an entire town’s economy. Cuts in harvest quotas can have adverse effects on the ability of fishermen to compete with the tourism industry.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Effective management of fisheries includes involving all stakeholders in the fishery. To do this successfully, stakeholders need to feel empowered enough to make meaningful contributions to the management process.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Empowerment has a wide application but in this context it refers to a tool that gives people within the fishing communities an opportunity to shape their own future in order to cope with the impacts from large-scale commercial fishing, competition of resources, and other threats that impact fishing communities.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, there are limits to empowerment in the fisheries management process. Empowerment maintains an involvement on the part of the state in fisheries management and no matter how empowered the other stakeholders are, the success of fisheries isn't possible without the legislative powers, financial resources, educational support, and research the government provides.<sup id="cite_ref-svein1_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-svein1-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This concept is not accepted by all, as some communities and individuals argue that the state should withdraw completely and let the local communities handle their own fishery management based on cultural traditions and established practices.<sup id="cite_ref-svein1_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-svein1-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, others have argued that co-management only empowers the wealthy and powerful which in turn solidifies and validates the already existing inequalities of fisheries management. <sup id="cite_ref-:0_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Empowerment working as a function of co-management, carried out correctly, will not only enable but it will authorize individuals and communities to make meaningful contributions to fisheries management. It is a mechanism that works in a loop, where an individual gains empowerment and encouragement from being a part of the group and the collective action is only successful because of its empowered individuals. <sup id="cite_ref-:1_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> In order to effectively and successfully use empowerment as co-management, it is imperative that study programs, guidelines, reading materials, manuals, and checklists are developed and incorporated into all fisheries management. 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Performance">Performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fisheries_management&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a> of certain global <a href="/wiki/Fish_stock" title="Fish stock">fish stocks</a> have been allowed to run down.  The biomass of many species have now diminished to the point where it is no longer possible to <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustainable_yield" title="Maximum sustainable yield">sustainably</a> catch the amount of fish that could be caught. According to a 2008 UN report, titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sunken_Billions" title="The Sunken Billions">The Sunken Billions: The Economic Justification for Fisheries Reform</a></i>, the world's <a href="/wiki/Fishing_fleet" title="Fishing fleet">fishing fleets</a> incur a "$US 50 billion annual economic loss" through depleted stocks and poor fisheries management. The report, produced jointly by the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> and the UN <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> (FAO), asserts that half the world's <a href="/wiki/Fishing_fleet" title="Fishing fleet">fishing fleet</a> could be scrapped with no change in catch.
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"By improving governance of marine fisheries, society could capture a substantial part of this $50 billion annual economic loss. Through comprehensive reform, the fisheries sector could become a basis for economic growth and the creation of alternative livelihoods in many countries. At the same time, a nation’s natural capital in the form of fish stocks could be greatly increased and the negative impacts of the fisheries on the marine environment reduced."<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The most prominent failure of fisheries management in recent times has perhaps been the events that lead to the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Cod_fisheries#Collapse_of_the_northern_cod_fishery" title="Cod fisheries">northern cod fisheries</a>. More recently, the <a href="/wiki/International_Consortium_of_Investigative_Journalists" title="International Consortium of Investigative Journalists">International Consortium of Investigative Journalists</a> produced a series of journalistic investigations called <i><a href="/wiki/Looting_the_seas" class="mw-redirect" title="Looting the seas">Looting the seas</a></i>. These detail investigations into the black market for <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_bluefin_tuna" title="Atlantic bluefin tuna">bluefin tuna</a>, the subsidies propping up the Spanish fishing industry, and the overfishing of the <a href="/wiki/Chilean_jack_mackerel" title="Chilean jack mackerel">Chilean jack mackerel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
