<p><b>Zirconium dioxide</b> (<span class="chemf nowrap">ZrO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>), sometimes known as <b>zirconia</b> (not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">zircon</a>), is a white crystalline <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> of <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a>. Its most naturally occurring form, with a <a href="/wiki/Monoclinic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> crystalline structure, is the <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> <a href="/wiki/Baddeleyite" title="Baddeleyite">baddeleyite</a>. A dopant stabilized cubic structured zirconia, <a href="/wiki/Cubic_zirconia" title="Cubic zirconia">cubic zirconia</a>, is synthesized in various colours for use as a <a href="/wiki/Gemstone" title="Gemstone">gemstone</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Diamond_simulant" title="Diamond simulant">diamond simulant</a>.
</p>

<h2><span id="Production.2C_chemical_properties.2C_occurrence"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Production,_chemical_properties,_occurrence">Production, chemical properties, occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production, chemical properties, occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Zirconia is produced by <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcining</a> zirconium compounds, exploiting its high <a href="/wiki/Thermal_stability" title="Thermal stability">thermal stability</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Three phases are known: monoclinic below 1170&#8239;°C, tetragonal between 1170&#8239;°C and 2370&#8239;°C, and cubic above 2370&#8239;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  The trend is for higher symmetry at higher temperatures, as is usually the case. A small percentage of the oxides of calcium or yttrium stabilize in the cubic phase.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  The very rare mineral tazheranite (Zr,Ti,Ca)O<sub>2</sub> is <a href="/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system" title="Cubic crystal system">cubic</a>.  Unlike TiO<sub>2</sub>, which features six-coordinate Ti in all phases, monoclinic zirconia consists of seven-coordinate zirconium centres.  This difference is attributed to the larger size of Zr atom relative to the Ti atom.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_reactions">Chemical reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Zirconia is chemically unreactive.  It is slowly attacked by concentrated <a href="/wiki/Hydrofluoric_acid" title="Hydrofluoric acid">hydrofluoric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a>.  When heated with carbon, it converts to <a href="/wiki/Zirconium_carbide" title="Zirconium carbide">zirconium carbide</a>.  When heated with carbon in the presence of chlorine, it converts to <a href="/wiki/Zirconium_tetrachloride" class="mw-redirect" title="Zirconium tetrachloride">zirconium tetrachloride</a>.  This conversion is the basis for the purification of zirconium metal and is analogous to the <a href="/wiki/Kroll_process" title="Kroll process">Kroll process</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Engineering_properties">Engineering properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Engineering properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Zirconium dioxide is one of the most studied <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a> materials. ZrO<sub>2</sub> adopts a <a href="/wiki/Monoclinic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">crystal structure</a> at room temperature and transitions to <a href="/wiki/Tetragonal" class="mw-redirect" title="Tetragonal">tetragonal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system" title="Cubic crystal system">cubic</a> at higher temperatures. The change of volume caused by the structure transitions from tetragonal to monoclinic to cubic induces large stresses, causing it to crack upon cooling from high temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> When the zirconia is <a href="/wiki/Doping_(semiconductor)" title="Doping (semiconductor)">blended with</a> some other oxides, the tetragonal and/or cubic phases are stabilized.  Effective dopants include <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">magnesium oxide</a> (MgO), <a href="/wiki/Yttrium_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Yttrium oxide">yttrium oxide</a> (Y<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, yttria), <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">calcium oxide</a> (CaO), and <a href="/wiki/Cerium(III)_oxide" title="Cerium(III) oxide">cerium(III) oxide</a> (Ce<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>).<sup id="cite_ref-evans_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-evans-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Zirconia is often more useful in its phase 'stabilized' state. Upon heating, zirconia undergoes disruptive phase changes. By adding small percentages of yttria, these phase changes are eliminated, and the resulting material has superior thermal, mechanical, and electrical properties. In some cases, the tetragonal phase can be <a href="/wiki/Metastable" class="mw-redirect" title="Metastable">metastable</a>. If sufficient quantities of the metastable tetragonal phase is present, then an applied stress, magnified by the <a href="/wiki/Stress_concentration" title="Stress concentration">stress concentration</a> at a crack tip, can cause the tetragonal phase to convert to monoclinic, with the associated volume expansion. This phase transformation can then put the crack into compression, retarding its growth, and enhancing the <a href="/wiki/Fracture_toughness" title="Fracture toughness">fracture toughness</a>. This mechanism is known as transformation toughening, and significantly extends the reliability and lifetime of products made with stabilized zirconia.<sup id="cite_ref-evans_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-evans-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The ZrO<sub>2</sub> <a href="/wiki/Band_gap" title="Band gap">band gap</a> is dependent on the phase (cubic, tetragonal, monoclinic, or amorphous) and preparation methods, with typical estimates from 5–7&#8239;eV.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A special case of zirconia is that of <a href="/wiki/Tetragonal_polycrystalline_zirconia" title="Tetragonal polycrystalline zirconia">tetragonal zirconia polycrystal</a>, or TZP, which is indicative of polycrystalline zirconia composed of only the metastable tetragonal phase.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main use of zirconia is in the production of hard ceramics, such as in dentistry (see below),<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> with other uses including as a protective coating on particles of <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">titanium dioxide</a> pigments,<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  as a <a href="/wiki/Refractory" title="Refractory">refractory</a> material, in <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">insulation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abrasive" title="Abrasive">abrasives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">enamels</a>. Stabilized zirconia is used in <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_sensor" title="Oxygen sensor">oxygen sensors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cell</a> membranes because it has the ability to allow <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ions</a> to move freely through the crystal structure at high temperatures. This high <a href="/wiki/Ionic_conductivity" class="mw-redirect" title="Ionic conductivity">ionic conductivity</a> (and a low electronic conductivity) makes it one of the most useful <a href="/wiki/Electroceramics" title="Electroceramics">electroceramics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Zirconium dioxide is also used as the <a href="/wiki/Solid_electrolyte" class="mw-redirect" title="Solid electrolyte">solid electrolyte</a> in <a href="/wiki/Electrochromic_devices" title="Electrochromic devices">electrochromic devices</a>.
</p><p>Zirconia is a precursor to the electroceramic <a href="/wiki/Lead_zirconate_titanate" title="Lead zirconate titanate">lead zirconate titanate</a> (<i>PZT</i>), which is a high-K dielectric, which is found in myriad components.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Niche_uses">Niche uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Niche uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The very low <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cubic_zirconia" title="Cubic zirconia">cubic phase of zirconia</a> also has led to its use as a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_barrier_coating" title="Thermal barrier coating">thermal barrier coating</a>, or TBC, in <a href="/wiki/Jet_engine" title="Jet engine">jet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engines</a> to allow operation at higher temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  Thermodynamically, the higher the operation temperature of an engine, <a href="/wiki/Carnot_heat_engine" title="Carnot heat engine">the greater the possible efficiency</a>. Another low thermal conductivity use is a ceramic fiber insulation for crystal growth furnaces, fuel cell stack insulation and infrared heating systems.
</p><p>This material is also used in dentistry in the manufacture of 1) subframes for the construction of <a href="/wiki/Dental_restoration" title="Dental restoration">dental restorations</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Crown_(dentistry)" title="Crown (dentistry)">crowns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bridge_(dentistry)" title="Bridge (dentistry)">bridges</a>, which are then veneered with a conventional <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">feldspathic</a> <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a> for aesthetic reasons, or of 2) strong, extremely durable dental prostheses constructed entirely from monolithic zirconia, with limited but constantly improving aesthetics.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Zirconia stabilized with <a href="/wiki/Yttria" class="mw-redirect" title="Yttria">yttria</a> (yttrium oxide), known as <a href="/wiki/Yttria-stabilized_zirconia" title="Yttria-stabilized zirconia">yttria-stabilized zirconia</a>, can be used as a strong base material in some full ceramic crown restorations.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>   
</p>
 <div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Zirconia_bridge.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Zirconia_bridge.jpg/220px-Zirconia_bridge.jpg" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Zirconia_bridge.jpg/330px-Zirconia_bridge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Zirconia_bridge.jpg/440px-Zirconia_bridge.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="1280" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Zirconia_bridge.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>High translucent Zirconia bridge layered by porcelain and stained with luster paste</div></div></div>
<p>Transformation toughened zirconia is used to make ceramic knives. Because of the hardness, ceramic-edged cutlery stays sharp longer than steel edged products.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to its infusibility and brilliant luminosity when <a href="/wiki/Incandescence" title="Incandescence">incandescent</a>, it was used as an ingredient of sticks for <a href="/wiki/Limelight" title="Limelight">limelight</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Zirconia has been proposed to <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolyze</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> and oxygen from the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars" title="Atmosphere of Mars">atmosphere of Mars</a> to provide both fuel and oxidizer that could be used as a store of chemical energy for use with surface transportation on Mars.  <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide/oxygen_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon monoxide/oxygen engine">Carbon monoxide/oxygen engines</a>  have been suggested for early surface transportation use as both carbon monoxide and oxygen can be straightforwardly produced by zirconia electrolysis without requiring use of any of the Martian water resources to obtain hydrogen, which would be needed for the production of methane or any hydrogen-based fuels.<sup id="cite_ref-landis2001_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-landis2001-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Zirconia is also a potential <a href="/wiki/High-k_dielectric" class="mw-redirect" title="High-k dielectric">high-k dielectric</a> material with potential applications as an insulator in <a href="/wiki/Transistor" title="Transistor">transistors</a>.
</p><p>Zirconia is also employed in the deposition of <a href="/wiki/Optical_coating" title="Optical coating">optical coatings</a>; it is a high-index material usable from the <a href="/wiki/Near_UV#Subtypes" class="mw-redirect" title="Near UV">near-UV</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Mid-infrared#CIE_division_scheme" class="mw-redirect" title="Mid-infrared">mid-IR</a>, due to its low absorption in this spectral region. In such applications, it is typically deposited by <a href="/wiki/Physical_vapor_deposition" title="Physical vapor deposition">PVD</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In jewelry making, some watch cases are advertised as being "black zirconium oxide".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In 2015 Omega released a fully ZrO<sub>2</sub> watch named "The Dark Side of The Moon" <sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> with ceramic case, bezel, pushers and clasp, advertising it as four times harder than stainless steel and therefore much more resistant to scratches during everyday use.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diamond_simulant">Diamond simulant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zirconium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diamond simulant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cubic_zirconia" title="Cubic zirconia">Cubic zirconia</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CZ_brilliant.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/CZ_brilliant.jpg/220px-CZ_brilliant.jpg" width="220" height="182" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bd/CZ_brilliant.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="298" data-file-height="247" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CZ_brilliant.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Brilliant-cut cubic zirconia</div></div></div>
<p>Single crystals of the cubic phase of zirconia are commonly used as <a href="/wiki/Diamond_simulant" title="Diamond simulant">diamond simulant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jewellery" title="Jewellery">jewellery</a>. Like diamond, cubic zirconia has a cubic crystal structure and a high <a href="/wiki/Index_of_refraction" class="mw-redirect" title="Index of refraction">index of refraction</a>. Visually discerning a good quality cubic zirconia gem from a diamond is difficult, and most jewellers will have a thermal conductivity tester to identify cubic zirconia by its low <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a> (diamond is a very good thermal conductor). This state of zirconia is commonly called <i>cubic zirconia</i>, <i>CZ</i>, or <i>zircon</i> by <a href="/wiki/Jewellery" title="Jewellery">jewellers</a>, but the last name is not chemically accurate. <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">Zircon</a> is actually the mineral name for naturally occurring <a href="/wiki/Zirconium_silicate" class="mw-redirect" title="Zirconium silicate">zirconium silicate</a> (ZrSiO<sub>4</sub>).
</p>
