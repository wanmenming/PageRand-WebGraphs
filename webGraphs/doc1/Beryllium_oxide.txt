<p><b><a href="/wiki/Beryllium" title="Beryllium">Beryllium</a> oxide</b> (<b>BeO</b>), also known as <b>beryllia</b>, is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> BeO. This colourless solid is a notable electrical insulator with a higher thermal conductivity than any other non-metal except <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamond</a>, and exceeds that of most metals.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> As an <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_solid" title="Amorphous solid">amorphous solid</a>, beryllium oxide is white. Its high melting point leads to its use as a <a href="/wiki/Refractory" title="Refractory">refractory</a> material.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It occurs in nature as the mineral <a href="/wiki/Bromellite" title="Bromellite">bromellite</a>. Historically and in materials science, beryllium oxide was called <b>glucina</b> or glucinium oxide. Formation of BeO from beryllium and oxygen releases <a href="/wiki/Energy_density" title="Energy density">the highest energy per mass of reactants</a> for any chemical reaction, close to 24&#160;MJ/kg.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation_and_chemical_properties">Preparation and chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beryllium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation and chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beryllium oxide can be prepared by calcining (roasting) beryllium carbonate, dehydrating <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_hydroxide" title="Beryllium hydroxide">beryllium hydroxide</a>, or igniting metallic beryllium:
</p>
<dl><dd>BeCO<sub>3</sub> → BeO + CO<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd>Be(OH)<sub>2</sub> → BeO + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd>
<dd>2 Be + O<sub>2</sub> → 2 BeO</dd></dl>
<p>Igniting beryllium in air gives a mixture of BeO and the nitride <a href="/wiki/Beryllium_nitride" title="Beryllium nitride">Be<sub>3</sub>N<sub>2</sub></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  Unlike the oxides formed by the other group 2 elements (alkaline earth metals), beryllium oxide is <a href="/wiki/Amphoterism" title="Amphoterism">amphoteric</a> rather than basic.
</p><p>Beryllium oxide formed at high temperatures (&gt;800&#160;°C) is inert, but dissolves easily in hot aqueous <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_bifluoride" title="Ammonium bifluoride">ammonium bifluoride</a> (NH<sub>4</sub>HF<sub>2</sub>) or a solution of hot concentrated <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> (H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_sulfate" title="Ammonium sulfate">ammonium sulfate</a> ((NH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beryllium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>BeO crystallizes in the hexagonal <a href="/wiki/Wurtzite_crystal_structure" title="Wurtzite crystal structure">wurtzite</a> structure, featuring tetrahedral Be<sup>2+</sup> and O<sup>2−</sup> centres, like <a href="/wiki/Lonsdaleite" title="Lonsdaleite">lonsdaleite</a> and w-<a href="/wiki/Boron_nitride" title="Boron nitride">BN</a> (with both of which it is <a href="/wiki/Isoelectronicity" title="Isoelectronicity">isoelectronic</a>).  In contrast, the oxides of the larger group-2 metals, i.e., <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide" title="Magnesium oxide">MgO</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium_oxide" title="Calcium oxide">CaO</a>, <a href="/wiki/Strontium_oxide" title="Strontium oxide">SrO</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barium_oxide" title="Barium oxide">BaO</a>, crystallize in the cubic <a href="/wiki/Rock_salt" class="mw-redirect" title="Rock salt">rock salt</a> motif with octahedral geometry about the dications and dianions.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> At high temperature the structure transforms to a tetragonal form.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the vapour phase, beryllium oxide is present as discrete <a href="/wiki/Diatomic_molecule" title="Diatomic molecule">diatomic molecules</a>. In the language of <a href="/wiki/Valence_bond_theory" title="Valence bond theory">valence bond theory</a>, these molecules can be described as adopting sp orbital hybridisation, featuring two <a href="/wiki/%CE%A3_bond" class="mw-redirect" title="Σ bond">σ</a> and two <a href="/wiki/%CE%A0_bond" class="mw-redirect" title="Π bond">π bonds</a>. The corresponding ground state is ...(2sσ)<sup>2</sup>(2sσ*)<sup>2</sup>(2pπ)<sup>4</sup>, where both degenerate π orbitals can be considered as dative bonds from oxygen towards beryllium.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beryllium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>High-quality crystals may be grown <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_synthesis" title="Hydrothermal synthesis">hydrothermally</a>, or otherwise by the <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Victor_Louis_Verneuil" title="Auguste Victor Louis Verneuil">Verneuil method</a>. For the most part, beryllium oxide is produced as a white amorphous powder, <a href="/wiki/Sintered" class="mw-redirect" title="Sintered">sintered</a> into larger shapes. Impurities, like carbon, can give a variety of colours to the otherwise colourless host crystals.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sintered" class="mw-redirect" title="Sintered">Sintered</a> beryllium oxide is a very stable <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Beryllium oxide is used in rocket engines<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and as a transparent <a href="/wiki/Anti-corrosion" title="Anti-corrosion">protective over-coating</a> on <a href="/wiki/Silvering" title="Silvering">aluminised</a> <a href="/wiki/Curved_mirror" title="Curved mirror">telescope mirrors</a>.
</p><p>Beryllium oxide is used in many high-performance <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> parts for applications such as radio equipment because it has good <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a> while also being a good electrical insulator. It is used as a filler in some thermal interface materials such as <a href="/wiki/Thermal_grease" title="Thermal grease">thermal grease</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-apmag_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-apmag-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Some <a href="/wiki/Power_semiconductor_device" title="Power semiconductor device">power semiconductor devices</a> have used beryllium oxide ceramic between the <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> chip and the metal mounting base of the package to achieve a lower value of <a href="/wiki/Thermal_resistance_in_electronics" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermal resistance in electronics">thermal resistance</a> than a similar construction of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">aluminium oxide</a>. It is also used as a structural <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a> for high-performance microwave devices, <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">vacuum tubes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magnetron" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnetron">magnetrons</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gas_laser" title="Gas laser">gas lasers</a>. BeO has been proposed as a <a href="/wiki/Neutron_moderator" title="Neutron moderator">neutron moderator</a> for naval marine high-temperature <a href="/wiki/Gas-cooled_reactor" title="Gas-cooled reactor">gas-cooled reactors</a> (MGCR), as well as NASA's <a href="/wiki/Kilopower" title="Kilopower">Kilopower</a> nuclear reactor for space applications.<sup id="cite_ref-KiloPower_Reactor_Materials_Study_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KiloPower_Reactor_Materials_Study-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beryllium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>BeO is <a href="/wiki/Carcinogen" title="Carcinogen">carcinogenic</a> in powdered form <sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and may cause chronic <a href="/wiki/Berylliosis" title="Berylliosis">beryllium disease</a>. Once fired into solid form, it is safe to handle if not subjected to machining that generates dust.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  Beryllium oxide ceramic is not a hazardous waste under federal law in the USA.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
