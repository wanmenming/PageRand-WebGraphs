<p><b>Terbium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Tb</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 65. It is a silvery-white, <a href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" class="mw-redirect" title="Rare earth element">rare earth</a> <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> that is <a href="/wiki/Malleable" class="mw-redirect" title="Malleable">malleable</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ductile" class="mw-redirect" title="Ductile">ductile</a>, and soft enough to be cut with a knife. The ninth member of the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a> series, terbium is a fairly <a href="/wiki/Electropositive" class="mw-redirect" title="Electropositive">electropositive</a> metal that reacts with water, evolving <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> gas. Terbium is never found in nature as a free element, but it is contained in many <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Cerite" title="Cerite">cerite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gadolinite" title="Gadolinite">gadolinite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xenotime" title="Xenotime">xenotime</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Euxenite" title="Euxenite">euxenite</a>.
</p><p>Swedish chemist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustaf_Mosander" title="Carl Gustaf Mosander">Carl Gustaf Mosander</a> discovered terbium as a chemical element in 1843. He detected it as an impurity in <a href="/wiki/Yttrium_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Yttrium oxide">yttrium oxide</a>, Y<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. Yttrium and terbium are named after the village of <a href="/wiki/Ytterby" title="Ytterby">Ytterby</a> in Sweden. Terbium was not isolated in pure form until the advent of <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a> techniques.
</p><p>Terbium is used to <a href="/wiki/Dopant" title="Dopant">dope</a> <a href="/wiki/Calcium_fluoride" title="Calcium fluoride">calcium fluoride</a>, calcium <a href="/wiki/Tungstate" title="Tungstate">tungstate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Strontium" title="Strontium">strontium</a> <a href="/wiki/Molybdate" title="Molybdate">molybdate</a>, materials that are used in <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_device" class="mw-redirect" title="Solid-state device">solid-state devices</a>, and as a crystal stabilizer of <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cells</a> which operate at elevated temperatures. As a component of <a href="/wiki/Terfenol-D" title="Terfenol-D">Terfenol-D</a> (an alloy that expands and contracts when exposed to magnetic fields more than any other alloy), terbium is of use in  <a href="/wiki/Actuator" title="Actuator">actuators</a>, in naval <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">sonar</a> systems and in <a href="/wiki/Sensor" title="Sensor">sensors</a>.
</p><p>Most of the world's terbium supply is used in green phosphors. Terbium <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> is in <a href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamp" title="Fluorescent lamp">fluorescent lamps</a> and television and monitor <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">cathode ray tubes</a> (CRTs). Terbium green <a href="/wiki/Phosphor" title="Phosphor">phosphors</a> are combined with divalent <a href="/wiki/Europium" title="Europium">europium</a> blue phosphors and trivalent europium red phosphors to provide <a href="/wiki/Trichromatic" class="mw-redirect" title="Trichromatic">trichromatic</a> lighting technology, a high-efficiency white light used for standard illumination in indoor lighting.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Terbium is a silvery-white <a href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" class="mw-redirect" title="Rare earth element">rare earth</a> <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> that is <a href="/wiki/Malleable" class="mw-redirect" title="Malleable">malleable</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ductile" class="mw-redirect" title="Ductile">ductile</a> and soft enough to be cut with a knife.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is relatively stable in air compared to the earlier, more reactive lanthanides in the first half of the lanthanide series.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Terbium exists in two crystal <a href="/wiki/Allotropy" title="Allotropy">allotropes</a> with a transformation temperature of 1289&#160;°C between them.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The 65 electrons of a terbium atom are arranged in the <a href="/wiki/Electron_configuration" title="Electron configuration">electron configuration</a> [Xe]4f<sup>9</sup>6s<sup>2</sup>; normally, only three electrons can be removed before the nuclear charge becomes too great to allow further ionization, but in the case of terbium, the stability of the half-filled [Xe]4f<sup>7</sup> configuration allows further ionization of a fourth electron in the presence of very strong oxidizing agents such as <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> gas.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The terbium(III) cation is brilliantly <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence" title="Fluorescence">fluorescent</a>, in a bright lemon-yellow color that is the result of a strong green <a href="/wiki/Emission_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Emission line">emission line</a> in combination with other lines in the orange and red. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yttrofluorite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Yttrofluorite (page does not exist)">yttrofluorite</a> variety of the mineral <a href="/wiki/Fluorite" title="Fluorite">fluorite</a> owes its creamy-yellow fluorescence in part to terbium. Terbium easily oxidizes, and is therefore used in its elemental form specifically for research. Single terbium atoms have been isolated by implanting them into <a href="/wiki/Fullerene" title="Fullerene">fullerene</a> molecules.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Terbium has a simple <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferromagnetic">ferromagnetic</a> ordering at temperatures below 219 K. Above 219 K, it turns into a helical <a href="/wiki/Antiferromagnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Antiferromagnetic">antiferromagnetic</a> state in which all of the atomic moments in a particular <a href="/wiki/Basal_plane" class="mw-redirect" title="Basal plane">basal plane</a> layer are parallel, and oriented at a fixed angle to the moments of adjacent layers. This unusual antiferromagnetism transforms into a disordered <a href="/wiki/Paramagnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Paramagnetic">paramagnetic</a> state at 230 K.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most common oxidation state of terbium is +3, as in <a href="/wiki/Terbium(III)_oxide" title="Terbium(III) oxide"><span class="chemf nowrap">Tb<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span></a>. The +4 state is known in TbO<sub>2</sub> and TbF<sub>4</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-patnaik_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
Terbium burns readily to form a mixed <a href="/wiki/Terbium(III,IV)_oxide" title="Terbium(III,IV) oxide">terbium(III,IV) oxide</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-reactions_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reactions-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>8&#160;Tb + 7&#160;O<sub>2</sub> → 2&#160;Tb<sub>4</sub>O<sub>7</sub></dd></dl>
<p>In solution, terbium forms only trivalent ions. Terbium is quite electropositive and reacts slowly with cold water and quite quickly with hot water to form terbium hydroxide:<sup id="cite_ref-reactions_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reactions-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2&#160;Tb + 6&#160;H<sub>2</sub>O → 2 Tb(OH)<sub>3</sub> + 3&#160;H<sub>2</sub>↑</dd></dl>
<p>Terbium metal reacts with all the halogens, forming white trihalides:<sup id="cite_ref-reactions_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reactions-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2&#160;Tb + 3&#160;X<sub>2</sub> → 2&#160;TbX<sub>3</sub> (X = <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">F</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">Cl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bromine" title="Bromine">Br</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">I</a>)</dd></dl>
<p>Terbium dissolves readily in dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to form solutions containing the pale pink terbium(III) ions, which exist as [Tb(OH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>9</sub>]<sup>3+</sup> complexes:<sup id="cite_ref-reactions_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reactions-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2&#160;Tb (s) + 3&#160;H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> → 2&#160;Tb<sup>3+</sup> + 3&#160;<span class="chemf nowrap">SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2−</sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> + 3&#160;H<sub>2</sub>↑</dd></dl>

<p>Terbium combines with nitrogen, carbon, sulfur, phosphorus, boron, selenium, silicon and arsenic at elevated temperatures, forming various binary compounds such as TbH<sub>2</sub>, TbH<sub>3</sub>, TbB<sub>2</sub>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Terbium(III)_sulfide&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Terbium(III) sulfide (page does not exist)">Tb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub></a>, TbSe, TbTe and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Terbium(III)_nitride&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Terbium(III) nitride (page does not exist)">TbN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-patnaik_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In those compounds, Tb mostly exhibits the oxidation states +3 and sometimes +2. Terbium(II) halogenides are obtained by annealing Tb(III) halogenides in presence of metallic Tb in tantalum containers. Terbium also forms sesquichloride Tb<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>3</sub>, which can be further reduced to TbCl by annealing at 800&#160;°C. This terbium(I) chloride forms platelets with layered graphite-like structure.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other compounds include 
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Chloride" title="Chloride">Chlorides</a>: <a href="/wiki/Terbium(III)_chloride" title="Terbium(III) chloride">TbCl<sub>3</sub></a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bromide" title="Bromide">Bromides</a>: <a href="/wiki/Terbium(III)_bromide" title="Terbium(III) bromide">TbBr<sub>3</sub></a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Iodide" title="Iodide">Iodides</a>: <a href="/wiki/Terbium(III)_iodide" title="Terbium(III) iodide">TbI<sub>3</sub></a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fluoride" title="Fluoride">Fluorides</a>: <a href="/w/index.php?title=Terbium(III)_fluoride&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Terbium(III) fluoride (page does not exist)">TbF<sub>3</sub></a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Terbium(IV)_fluoride&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Terbium(IV) fluoride (page does not exist)">TbF<sub>4</sub></a></li></ul>
<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terbium(IV)_fluoride&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Terbium(IV) fluoride (page does not exist)">Terbium(IV) fluoride</a> is a strong fluorinating agent, emitting relatively pure atomic fluorine when heated<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> rather than the mixture of fluoride vapors emitted from CoF<sub>3</sub> or CeF<sub>4</sub>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_terbium" title="Isotopes of terbium">isotopes of terbium</a></div>
<p>Naturally occurring terbium is composed of its only stable <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a>, terbium-159; the element is thus called <a href="/wiki/Mononuclidic_element" title="Mononuclidic element">mononuclidic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monoisotopic_element" title="Monoisotopic element">monoisotopic</a>. Thirty-six <a href="/wiki/Radioisotope" class="mw-redirect" title="Radioisotope">radioisotopes</a> have been characterized, with the heaviest being terbium-171 (with <a href="/wiki/Atomic_mass" title="Atomic mass">atomic mass</a> of 170.95330(86) <a href="/wiki/Dalton_(unit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dalton (unit)">u</a>) and lightest being terbium-135 (exact mass unknown).<sup id="cite_ref-iso_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iso-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The most stable <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_radioisotope" title="Synthetic radioisotope">synthetic radioisotopes</a> of terbium are terbium-158, with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 180 years, and terbium-157, with a half-life of 71 years. All of the remaining <a href="/wiki/Radioactive" class="mw-redirect" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> isotopes have half-lives that are much less than a quarter of a year, and the majority of these have half-lives that are less than half a minute.<sup id="cite_ref-iso_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iso-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The primary <a href="/wiki/Decay_mode" class="mw-redirect" title="Decay mode">decay mode</a> before the most abundant stable isotope, <sup>159</sup>Tb, is <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a>, which results in production of <a href="/wiki/Gadolinium" title="Gadolinium">gadolinium</a> isotopes, and the primary mode after is <a href="/wiki/Beta_minus_decay" class="mw-redirect" title="Beta minus decay">beta minus decay</a>, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Dysprosium" title="Dysprosium">dysprosium</a> isotopes.<sup id="cite_ref-iso_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iso-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The element also has 27 <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">nuclear isomers</a>, with masses of 141–154, 156, and 158 (not every mass number corresponds to only one isomer). The most stable of them are terbium-156m, with half-life of 24.4 hours and terbium-156m2, with half-life of 22.7 hours; this is longer than half-lives of most ground states of radioactive terbium isotopes, except only those with mass numbers 155–161.<sup id="cite_ref-iso_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iso-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemist" title="Chemist">chemist</a> <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustaf_Mosander" title="Carl Gustaf Mosander">Carl Gustaf Mosander</a> discovered terbium in 1843. He detected it as an impurity in <a href="/wiki/Yttrium_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Yttrium oxide">yttrium oxide</a>, Y<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. Yttrium is named after the village of <a href="/wiki/Ytterby" title="Ytterby">Ytterby</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>. Terbium was not isolated in pure form until the advent of <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a> techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-history_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mosander first separated yttria into three fractions, all named for the ore: yttria, erbia, and terbia. "Terbia" was originally the fraction that contained the pink color, due to the element now known as <a href="/wiki/Erbium" title="Erbium">erbium</a>. "Erbia" (containing what we now call terbium) originally was the fraction that was essentially colorless in solution. The insoluble oxide of this element was noted to be tinged brown.
</p><p>Later workers had difficulty in observing the minor colorless "erbia", but the soluble pink fraction was impossible to miss. Arguments went back and forth as to whether erbia even existed. In the confusion, the original names got reversed, and the exchange of names stuck, so that the pink fraction referred eventually to the solution containing erbium (which in solution, is pink). It is now thought that workers using double sodium or <a href="/wiki/Potassium_sulfate" title="Potassium sulfate">potassium sulfates</a> to remove ceria from yttria inadvertently lost the terbium into the ceria-containing precipitate. What is now known as terbium was only about 1% of the original yttria, but that was sufficient to impart a yellowish color to the yttrium oxide. Thus, terbium was a minor component in the original fraction containing it, where it was dominated by its immediate neighbors, <a href="/wiki/Gadolinium" title="Gadolinium">gadolinium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dysprosium" title="Dysprosium">dysprosium</a>.
</p><p>Thereafter, whenever other rare earths were teased apart from this mixture, whichever fraction gave the brown oxide retained the terbium name, until at last, the brown oxide of terbium was obtained in pure form. The 19th century investigators did not have the benefit of the UV fluorescence technology to observe the brilliant yellow or green Tb(III) fluorescence that would have made terbium easier to identify in solid mixtures or solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-history_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Xenotim_mineralogisches_museum_bonn.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Xenotim_mineralogisches_museum_bonn.jpg/220px-Xenotim_mineralogisches_museum_bonn.jpg" width="220" height="182" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Xenotim_mineralogisches_museum_bonn.jpg/330px-Xenotim_mineralogisches_museum_bonn.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Xenotim_mineralogisches_museum_bonn.jpg/440px-Xenotim_mineralogisches_museum_bonn.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="1656" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Xenotim_mineralogisches_museum_bonn.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Xenotime</div></div></div>
<p>Terbium is contained along with other rare earth elements in many minerals, including <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> ((Ce,La,Th,Nd,Y)PO<sub>4</sub> with up to 0.03% terbium), <a href="/wiki/Xenotime" title="Xenotime">xenotime</a> (YPO<sub>4</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Euxenite" title="Euxenite">euxenite</a> ((Y,Ca,Er,La,Ce,U,Th)(Nb,Ta,Ti)<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> with 1% or more terbium). The crust abundance of terbium is estimated as 1.2&#160;mg/kg.<sup id="cite_ref-patnaik_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> No terbium-dominant mineral has yet been found.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Currently, the richest commercial sources of terbium are the ion-adsorption <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clays</a> of <a href="/wiki/Southern_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Southern China">southern China</a>; the concentrates with about two-thirds yttrium oxide by weight have about 1% terbia. Small amounts of terbium occur in <a href="/wiki/Bastn%C3%A4site" title="Bastnäsite">bastnäsite</a> and monazite; when these are processed by solvent extraction to recover the valuable heavy lanthanides as <a href="/wiki/Samarium" title="Samarium">samarium</a>-<a href="/wiki/Europium" title="Europium">europium</a>-<a href="/wiki/Gadolinium" title="Gadolinium">gadolinium</a> concentrate, terbium is recovered therein. Due to the large volumes of bastnäsite processed relative to the ion-adsorption clays, a significant proportion of the world's terbium supply comes from bastnäsite.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Crushed terbium-containing minerals are treated with hot concentrated <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to produce water-soluble sulfates of rare earths. The acidic filtrates are partially neutralized with caustic soda to pH 3–4. <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">Thorium</a> precipitates out of solution as hydroxide and is removed. After that the solution is treated with <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_oxalate" title="Ammonium oxalate">ammonium oxalate</a> to convert rare earths into their insoluble <a href="/wiki/Oxalate" title="Oxalate">oxalates</a>. The oxalates are decomposed to oxides by heating. The oxides are dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> that excludes one of the main components, cerium, whose oxide is insoluble in HNO<sub>3</sub>. Terbium is separated as a double salt with <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_nitrate" title="Ammonium nitrate">ammonium nitrate</a> by crystallization.<sup id="cite_ref-patnaik_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The most efficient separation routine for terbium salt from the rare-earth salt solution is <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a>. In this process, rare-earth ions are sorbed onto suitable ion-exchange resin by exchange with hydrogen, ammonium or cupric ions present in the resin. The rare earth ions are then selectively washed out by suitable complexing agent. As with other rare earths, terbium metal is produced by reducing the anhydrous chloride or fluoride with calcium metal. Calcium and <a href="/wiki/Tantalum" title="Tantalum">tantalum</a> impurities can be removed by vacuum remelting, distillation, amalgam formation or <a href="/wiki/Zone_melting" title="Zone melting">zone melting</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-patnaik_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Terbium is used as a <a href="/wiki/Dopant" title="Dopant">dopant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Calcium_fluoride" title="Calcium fluoride">calcium fluoride</a>, calcium <a href="/wiki/Tungstate" title="Tungstate">tungstate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Strontium" title="Strontium">strontium</a> <a href="/wiki/Molybdate" title="Molybdate">molybdate</a>, materials that are used in solid-state devices, and as a crystal stabilizer of <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cells</a> which operate at elevated temperatures, together with <a href="/wiki/Zirconium(IV)_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Zirconium(IV) oxide">ZrO<sub>2</sub></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Terbium is also used in <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> and in the production of electronic devices. As a component of <a href="/wiki/Terfenol-D" title="Terfenol-D">Terfenol-D</a>, terbium is used in  <a href="/wiki/Actuator" title="Actuator">actuators</a>, in naval <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">sonar</a> systems, <a href="/wiki/Sensor" title="Sensor">sensors</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/SoundBug" title="SoundBug">SoundBug</a> device (its first commercial application), and other magnetomechanical devices. Terfenol-D is a terbium alloy that expands or contracts in the presence of a magnetic field. It has the highest <a href="/wiki/Magnetostriction" title="Magnetostriction">magnetostriction</a> of any <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Terbium <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> is used in green <a href="/wiki/Phosphor" title="Phosphor">phosphors</a> in fluorescent lamps and color TV tubes. <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">Sodium</a> terbium <a href="/wiki/Borate" title="Borate">borate</a> is used in <a href="/wiki/Solid_state_(electronics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Solid state (electronics)">solid state</a> devices. The brilliant fluorescence allows terbium to be used as a probe in biochemistry, where it somewhat resembles <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> in its behavior. Terbium "green" phosphors (which fluoresce a brilliant lemon-yellow) are combined with divalent europium blue phosphors and trivalent europium red phosphors to provide the <a href="/wiki/Trichromatic" class="mw-redirect" title="Trichromatic">trichromatic</a> lighting technology which is by far the largest consumer of the world's terbium supply. Trichromatic lighting provides much higher light output for a given amount of electrical energy than does <a href="/wiki/Incandescent_light" class="mw-redirect" title="Incandescent light">incandescent lighting</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Terbium is also used to detect <a href="/wiki/Endospores" class="mw-redirect" title="Endospores">endospores</a>, as it acts as an assay of <a href="/wiki/Dipicolinic_acid" title="Dipicolinic acid">dipicolinic acid</a> based on <a href="/wiki/Photoluminescence" title="Photoluminescence">photoluminescence</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precautions">Precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terbium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As with the other <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanides</a>, terbium compounds are of low to moderate toxicity, although their toxicity has not been investigated in detail. Terbium has no known biological role.<sup id="cite_ref-CRC_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRC-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
