<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Biosphere</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Biosphere_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Biosphere (disambiguation)">Biosphere (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">The global sum of all ecosystems on Earth</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:362px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg/360px-Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg" width="360" height="230" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg/540px-Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg/720px-Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="819" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/False-color" class="mw-redirect" title="False-color">false-color</a> composite of global oceanic and terrestrial photoautotroph abundance, from September 2001 to August 2017. Provided by the <a href="/wiki/SeaWiFS" title="SeaWiFS">SeaWiFS</a> Project, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>/<a href="/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center" title="Goddard Space Flight Center">Goddard Space Flight Center</a> and <a href="/wiki/ORBIMAGE" class="mw-redirect" title="ORBIMAGE">ORBIMAGE</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></div></div></div>
<p>The <b>biosphere</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> βίος <i>bíos</i> "life" and σφαῖρα <i>sphaira</i> "sphere") also known as the <b>ecosphere</b> (from Greek οἶκος <i>oîkos</i> "environment" and σφαῖρα), is the worldwide sum of all <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>. It can also be termed the zone of <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a> on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, a closed system (apart from <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">solar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Cosmic radiation">cosmic radiation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> from the interior 
of the Earth), and largely self-regulating.<sup id="cite_ref-ColumbiaEncyc_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ColumbiaEncyc-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> By the most general <a href="/wiki/Geophysiology" class="mw-redirect" title="Geophysiology">biophysiological</a> definition, the biosphere is the global <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecological</a> system integrating all <a href="/wiki/Living_beings" class="mw-redirect" title="Living beings">living beings</a> and their relationships, including their interaction with the elements of the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geosphere" title="Geosphere">geosphere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth&#39;s atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. The biosphere is postulated to have <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolved</a>, beginning with a process of <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_life" class="mw-redirect" title="Origin of life">biopoiesis</a> (life created naturally from non-living matter, such as simple organic compounds) or <a href="/wiki/Biogenesis" title="Biogenesis">biogenesis</a> (life created from living matter), at least some 3.5 billion years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell_2006_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell_2006-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT-20131003_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20131003-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In a general sense, biospheres are any closed, self-regulating systems containing ecosystems. This includes artificial biospheres such as <a href="/wiki/Biosphere_2" title="Biosphere 2">Biosphere 2</a> and <a href="/wiki/BIOS-3" title="BIOS-3">BIOS-3</a>, and potentially ones on other planets or moons.<sup id="cite_ref-webdictionary.co.uk_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webdictionary.co.uk-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_use_of_the_term">Origin and use of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin and use of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:90_mile_beach.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/90_mile_beach.jpg/220px-90_mile_beach.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/90_mile_beach.jpg/330px-90_mile_beach.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/90_mile_beach.jpg/440px-90_mile_beach.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:90_mile_beach.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Beach" title="Beach">beach</a> scene on Earth, simultaneously showing the lithosphere (ground), hydrosphere (ocean) and atmosphere (air)</div></div></div>
<p>The term "biosphere" was coined by geologist <a href="/wiki/Eduard_Suess" title="Eduard Suess">Eduard Suess</a> in 1875, which he defined as the place on Earth's surface where life dwells.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While the concept has a geological origin, it is an indication of the effect of both <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matthew_F._Maury" class="mw-redirect" title="Matthew F. Maury">Matthew F. Maury</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Earth_sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth sciences">Earth sciences</a>. The biosphere's ecological context comes from the 1920s (see <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_I._Vernadsky" class="mw-redirect" title="Vladimir I. Vernadsky">Vladimir I. Vernadsky</a>), preceding the 1935 introduction of the term "<a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>" by Sir <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Tansley" title="Arthur Tansley">Arthur Tansley</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Ecology_(history)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecology (history)">ecology history</a>). Vernadsky defined <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a> as the science of the biosphere. It is an <a href="/wiki/Interdisciplinary" class="mw-redirect" title="Interdisciplinary">interdisciplinary</a> concept for integrating <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">geophysics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">evolution</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">geochemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a> and, generally speaking, all life and Earth sciences.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Narrow_definition">Narrow definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Narrow definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Geochemists define the biosphere as being the total sum of living organisms (the "<a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a>" or "<a href="/wiki/Biota_(ecology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Biota (ecology)">biota</a>" as referred to by biologists and ecologists). In this sense, the biosphere is but one of four separate components of the geochemical model, the other three being <i><a href="/wiki/Geosphere" title="Geosphere">geosphere</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth&#39;s atmosphere">atmosphere</a></i>. When these four component spheres are combined into one system, it is known as the <a href="/wiki/Ecosphere_(planetary)" title="Ecosphere (planetary)">Ecosphere</a>. This term was coined during the 1960s and encompasses both biological and physical components of the planet.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Second International Conference on Closed Life Systems defined <i>biospherics</i> as the science and technology of analogs and <a href="/wiki/Model_(abstract)" class="mw-redirect" title="Model (abstract)">models</a> of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s biosphere; i.e., artificial Earth-like biospheres.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Others may include the creation of artificial non-Earth biospheres&#8212;for example, human-centered biospheres or a native <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Martian</a> biosphere&#8212;as part of the topic of biospherics.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Earth.27s_biosphere"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth's_biosphere">Earth's biosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Earth&#039;s biosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Age">Age</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Age">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stromatolithe_Pal%C3%A9oarch%C3%A9en_-_MNHT.PAL.2009.10.1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Stromatolithe_Pal%C3%A9oarch%C3%A9en_-_MNHT.PAL.2009.10.1.jpg/200px-Stromatolithe_Pal%C3%A9oarch%C3%A9en_-_MNHT.PAL.2009.10.1.jpg" width="200" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Stromatolithe_Pal%C3%A9oarch%C3%A9en_-_MNHT.PAL.2009.10.1.jpg/300px-Stromatolithe_Pal%C3%A9oarch%C3%A9en_-_MNHT.PAL.2009.10.1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Stromatolithe_Pal%C3%A9oarch%C3%A9en_-_MNHT.PAL.2009.10.1.jpg/400px-Stromatolithe_Pal%C3%A9oarch%C3%A9en_-_MNHT.PAL.2009.10.1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5185" data-file-height="3655" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stromatolithe_Pal%C3%A9oarch%C3%A9en_-_MNHT.PAL.2009.10.1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Stromatolite fossil estimated at 3.2&#8211;3.6 billion years old</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms" title="Earliest known life forms">earliest evidence</a> for <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life on Earth</a> includes <a href="/wiki/Biogenic_substance" title="Biogenic substance">biogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> found in 3.7 billion-year-old <a href="/wiki/Metasediment" class="mw-redirect" title="Metasediment">metasedimentary rocks</a> from <a href="/wiki/Western_Greenland" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Greenland">Western Greenland</a><sup id="cite_ref-NG-20131208_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NG-20131208-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Microbial_mat" title="Microbial mat">microbial mat</a> <a href="/wiki/Fossils" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossils">fossils</a> found in 3.48 billion-year-old <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> from <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AP-20131113_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AP-20131113-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AST-20131108_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AST-20131108-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  More recently, in 2015, "remains of <a href="/wiki/Biotic_material" title="Biotic material">biotic life</a>" were found in 4.1 billion-year-old rocks in Western Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-AP-20151019_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AP-20151019-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PNAS-20151014-pdf_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PNAS-20151014-pdf-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In 2017, putative fossilized <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Micropaleontology#Microfossils" title="Micropaleontology">microfossils</a>) were announced to have been discovered in <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent" title="Hydrothermal vent">hydrothermal vent precipitates</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Nuvvuagittuq_Greenstone_Belt" title="Nuvvuagittuq Greenstone Belt">Nuvvuagittuq Belt</a> of Quebec, Canada that were as old as 4.28 billion years, the oldest record of life on earth, suggesting "an almost instantaneous emergence of life" after <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_water_on_Earth#Water_in_the_development_of_Earth" title="Origin of water on Earth">ocean formation 4.4 billion years ago</a>, and not long after the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_Earth" title="Age of the Earth">formation of the Earth</a> 4.54 billion years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-NAT-20170301_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NAT-20170301-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT-20170301_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20170301-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BBC-20170301_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-20170301-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4.3b_oldest_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4.3b_oldest-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> According to biologist <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Blair_Hedges" title="Stephen Blair Hedges">Stephen Blair Hedges</a>, "If life arose relatively quickly on Earth ... then it could be common in the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-AP-20151019_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AP-20151019-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extent">Extent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Extent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ruppelsvulture.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Ruppelsvulture.jpg/170px-Ruppelsvulture.jpg" width="170" height="256" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Ruppelsvulture.jpg/255px-Ruppelsvulture.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Ruppelsvulture.jpg/340px-Ruppelsvulture.jpg 2x" data-file-width="398" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ruppelsvulture.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rüppell's vulture</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:XenophyophoreNOAA.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/XenophyophoreNOAA.jpg/220px-XenophyophoreNOAA.jpg" width="220" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/XenophyophoreNOAA.jpg/330px-XenophyophoreNOAA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/XenophyophoreNOAA.jpg/440px-XenophyophoreNOAA.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="514" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:XenophyophoreNOAA.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Xenophyophore" title="Xenophyophore">Xenophyophore</a>, a barophilic organism, from the <a href="/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_hotspot" title="Galápagos hotspot">Galapagos Rift</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Every part of the planet, from the <a href="/wiki/Polar_region" class="mw-redirect" title="Polar region">polar</a> ice caps to the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, features life of some kind. Recent advances in <a href="/wiki/Microbiology" title="Microbiology">microbiology</a> have demonstrated that microbes live deep beneath the Earth's terrestrial surface, and that the total mass of <a href="/wiki/Microbial" class="mw-redirect" title="Microbial">microbial</a> life in so-called "uninhabitable zones" may, in <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">biomass</a>, exceed all animal and plant life on the surface. The actual thickness of the biosphere on earth is difficult to measure. Birds typically fly at altitudes as high as 1,800&#160;m (5,900&#160;ft; 1.1&#160;mi) and fish live as much as 8,372&#160;m (27,467&#160;ft; 5.202&#160;mi) underwater in the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Trench" title="Puerto Rico Trench">Puerto Rico Trench</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell_2006_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell_2006-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are more extreme examples for life on the planet: <a href="/wiki/R%C3%BCppell%27s_vulture" title="Rüppell&#39;s vulture">Rüppell's vulture</a> has been found at <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitudes</a> of 11,300&#160;m (37,100&#160;ft; 7.0&#160;mi); <a href="/wiki/Bar-headed_goose" title="Bar-headed goose">bar-headed geese</a> migrate at altitudes of at least 8,300&#160;m (27,200&#160;ft; 5.2&#160;mi); <a href="/wiki/Yak" class="mw-redirect" title="Yak">yaks</a> live at elevations as high as 5,400&#160;m (17,700&#160;ft; 3.4&#160;mi) above sea level; <a href="/wiki/Mountain_goat" title="Mountain goat">mountain goats</a> live up to 3,050&#160;m (10,010&#160;ft; 1.90&#160;mi). Herbivorous animals at these elevations depend on lichens, grasses, and herbs.
</p><p>Life forms live in every part of the Earth's biosphere, including <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hot_spring" title="Hot spring">hot springs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Endolith" title="Endolith">inside rocks</a> at least 19&#160;km (12&#160;mi) deep underground, the deepest parts of the ocean, and at least 64&#160;km (40&#160;mi) high in the atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-SD-19980625-UG_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SD-19980625-UG-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ABM-20150112_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABM-20150112-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BBC-20151124_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-20151124-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">Microorganisms</a>, under certain test conditions, have been observed to <a href="/wiki/Panspermia#Research_in_outer_space" title="Panspermia">survive the vacuum of outer space</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dose_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dose-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Horneck_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Horneck-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The total amount of soil and subsurface bacterial <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> is estimated as 5 × 10<sup>17</sup> g, or the "weight of the United Kingdom".<sup id="cite_ref-SD-19980625-UG_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SD-19980625-UG-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The mass of <a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">prokaryote</a> microorganisms—which includes bacteria and archaea, but not the nucleated <a href="/wiki/Microorganism#Eukaryotes" title="Microorganism">eukaryote microorganisms</a>—may be as much as 0.8 trillion tons of carbon (of the total biosphere <a href="/wiki/Biomass_(ecology)" title="Biomass (ecology)">mass</a>, estimated at between 1 and 4 trillion tons).<sup id="cite_ref-AGCI-2014_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AGCI-2014-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Piezophile" title="Piezophile">Barophilic</a> marine microbes have been found at more than a depth of 10,000&#160;m (33,000&#160;ft; 6.2&#160;mi) in the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Trench" title="Mariana Trench">Mariana Trench</a>, the deepest spot in the Earth's oceans.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In fact, single-celled life forms have been found in the deepest part of the Mariana Trench, by the <a href="/wiki/Challenger_Deep" title="Challenger Deep">Challenger Deep</a>, at depths of 11,034&#160;m (36,201&#160;ft; 6.856&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LS-20130317_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LS-20130317-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NG-20130317_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NG-20130317-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Other researchers reported related studies that microorganisms thrive inside rocks up to 580&#160;m (1,900&#160;ft; 0.36&#160;mi) below the sea floor under 2,590&#160;m (8,500&#160;ft; 1.61&#160;mi) of ocean off the coast of the northwestern <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-LS-20130317_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LS-20130317-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LS-20130314_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LS-20130314-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> as well as 2,400&#160;m (7,900&#160;ft; 1.5&#160;mi) beneath the seabed off Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC-20141215-RM_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-20141215-RM-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>  Culturable thermophilic microbes have been extracted from cores drilled more than 5,000&#160;m (16,000&#160;ft; 3.1&#160;mi) into the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth&#39;s crust">Earth's crust</a> in Sweden,<sup id="cite_ref-Szewzyketal1994_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Szewzyketal1994-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> from rocks between 65–75&#160;°C (149–167&#160;°F). Temperature <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_gradient" title="Geothermal gradient">increases with increasing depth</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_crust" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth&#39;s crust">Earth's crust</a>. The rate at which the temperature increases depends on many factors, including type of crust (continental vs. oceanic), rock type, geographic location, etc. The greatest known temperature at which microbial life can exist is 122&#160;°C (252&#160;°F) (<i><a href="/wiki/Methanopyrus_kandleri" class="mw-redirect" title="Methanopyrus kandleri">Methanopyrus kandleri</a></i> Strain 116), and it is likely that the limit of life in the "deep biosphere" is defined by temperature rather than absolute depth.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> On 20 August 2014, scientists confirmed the existence of microorganisms living 800&#160;m (2,600&#160;ft; 0.50&#160;mi) below the ice of <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NAT-20140820_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NAT-20140820-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FRB-20140820_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FRB-20140820-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> According to one researcher, "You can find microbes everywhere — they're extremely adaptable to conditions, and survive wherever they are."<sup id="cite_ref-LS-20130317_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LS-20130317-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Our biosphere is divided into a number of <a href="/wiki/Biome" title="Biome">biomes</a>, inhabited by fairly similar <a href="/wiki/Flora_(plants)" class="mw-redirect" title="Flora (plants)">flora</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fauna_(animals)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fauna (animals)">fauna</a>. On land, biomes are separated primarily by <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>. Terrestrial biomes lying within the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">Antarctic Circles</a> are relatively barren of <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a> life, while most of the more populous biomes lie near the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artificial_biospheres">Artificial biospheres</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Artificial biospheres">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Biosphere_2_4888964549.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Biosphere_2_4888964549.jpg/220px-Biosphere_2_4888964549.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Biosphere_2_4888964549.jpg/330px-Biosphere_2_4888964549.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Biosphere_2_4888964549.jpg/440px-Biosphere_2_4888964549.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2312" data-file-height="1542" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Biosphere_2_4888964549.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Biosphere 2 in Arizona.</div></div></div>
<p>Experimental biospheres, also called <a href="/wiki/Closed_ecological_system" title="Closed ecological system">closed ecological systems</a>, have been created to study ecosystems and the potential for supporting life outside the earth. These include spacecraft and the following terrestrial laboratories:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Biosphere_2" title="Biosphere 2">Biosphere 2</a> in <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>, United States, 3.15 acres (13,000 m<sup>2</sup>).</li>
<li>BIOS-1, BIOS-2 and <a href="/wiki/BIOS-3" title="BIOS-3">BIOS-3</a> at the Institute of Biophysics in <a href="/wiki/Krasnoyarsk" title="Krasnoyarsk">Krasnoyarsk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, in what was then the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Biosphere J (CEEF, Closed Ecology Experiment Facilities), an experiment in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Micro-Ecological Life Support System Alternative (<a href="/wiki/MELiSSA" title="MELiSSA">MELiSSA</a>) at <a href="/wiki/Universitat_Aut%C3%B2noma_de_Barcelona" class="mw-redirect" title="Universitat Autònoma de Barcelona">Universitat Autònoma de Barcelona</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_biospheres">Extraterrestrial biospheres</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial biospheres">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>No biospheres have been detected beyond the Earth; therefore, the existence of extraterrestrial biospheres remains hypothetical. The <a href="/wiki/Rare_Earth_hypothesis" title="Rare Earth hypothesis">rare Earth hypothesis</a> suggests they should be very rare, save ones composed of <a href="/wiki/Microbe" class="mw-redirect" title="Microbe">microbial</a> life only.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> On the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Earth_analog" title="Earth analog">Earth analogs</a> may be quite numerous, at least in the <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way_galaxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Milky Way galaxy">Milky Way galaxy</a>, given the large number of planets.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Three of the planets discovered orbiting <a href="/wiki/TRAPPIST-1" title="TRAPPIST-1">TRAPPIST-1</a> could possibly contain biospheres.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>  Given limited understanding of <a href="/wiki/Abiogenesis" title="Abiogenesis">abiogenesis</a>, it is currently unknown what percentage of these planets actually develop biospheres.
</p><p>Based on observations by the <a href="/wiki/Kepler_Space_Telescope" class="mw-redirect" title="Kepler Space Telescope">Kepler Space Telescope</a> team, it has been calculated that provided the probability of abiogenesis is higher than 1 to 1000, the closest alien biosphere should be within 100 light-years from the Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is also possible that artificial biospheres will be created during the future, for example on <a href="/wiki/Terraforming_of_Mars" title="Terraforming of Mars">Mars</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The process of creating an uncontained system that mimics the function of Earth's biosphere is called <a href="/wiki/Terraforming" title="Terraforming">terraforming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Fogg1998_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fogg1998-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
