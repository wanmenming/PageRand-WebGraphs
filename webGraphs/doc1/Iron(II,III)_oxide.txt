<p><b>Iron(II,III) oxide</b> is ferrous oxide, the chemical compound with formula Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>. It occurs in nature as the mineral <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a>. It is one of a number of <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxides" class="mw-redirect" title="Iron oxides">iron oxides</a>, the others being <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide" title="Iron(II) oxide">iron(II) oxide</a> (FeO), which is rare, and <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide" title="Iron(III) oxide">iron(III) oxide</a> (Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) which is ferric oxide, also known as <a href="/wiki/Hematite" title="Hematite">hematite</a> or rust. It contains both Fe<sup>2+</sup> and Fe<sup>3+</sup> ions and is sometimes formulated as FeO ∙ Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. This iron oxide is encountered in the laboratory as a black powder. It exhibits permanent magnetism and is <a href="/wiki/Ferrimagnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferrimagnetic">ferrimagnetic</a>, but is sometimes incorrectly described as <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferromagnetic">ferromagnetic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Its most extensive use is as a black pigment which is synthesised rather than being extracted from the naturally occurring mineral as the particle size and shape can be varied by the method of production.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(II,III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Under <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">anaerobic</a> conditions, <a href="/wiki/Ferrous_hydroxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferrous hydroxide">ferrous hydroxide</a> (Fe(OH)<sub>2</sub>) can be oxidized by water to form magnetite and molecular <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>. <p>The well-crystallized magnetite (Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>) is thermodynamically more stable than the ferrous hydroxide (Fe(OH)<sub>2</sub> ).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Magnetite can be prepared in the laboratory as a <a href="/wiki/Ferrofluid" title="Ferrofluid">ferrofluid</a> in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Massart_method&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Massart method (page does not exist)">Massart method</a> by mixing <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_chloride" title="Iron(II) chloride">iron(II) chloride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_chloride" title="Iron(III) chloride">iron(III) chloride</a> in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Magnetite can also be prepared by the chemical co-precipitation in presence of ammonia, which consist in a mixture of a solution 0.1 M of FeCl<sub>3</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O and FeCl<sub>2</sub>·4H<sub>2</sub>O with mechanic agitation of about 2000 rpm. The molar ratio of FeCl<sub>3</sub>:FeCl<sub>2</sub> can be 2:1; heating this solution at 70&#160;°C, and immediately the speed is elevated to 7500 rpm and adding quickly a solution of NH<sub>4</sub>OH (10 volume&#160;%), immediately a dark precipitate will be formed, which consists of nanoparticles of magnetite.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In both cases, the precipitation reaction rely on a quick transformation of acidic hydrolyzed iron ions into the spinel iron oxide structure, by hydrolysis at elevated pH values (above ca. 10).
</p><p>Considerable efforts has been devoted towards controlling the particle formation process of magnetite nanoparticles due to the challenging and complex chemistry reactions involved in the phase transformations prior to the formation of the magnetite spinel structure.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Magnetite particles are of interests in bioscience applications such as in <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">magnetic resonance imaging</a> (MRI) since iron oxide magnetite nanoparticles represent a non-toxic alternative to currently employed gadolinium-based <a href="/wiki/Contrast_agents" class="mw-redirect" title="Contrast agents">contrast agents</a>. However, due to lack of control over the specific transformations involved in the formation of the particles, truly superparamagnetic particles have not yet been prepared from magnetite, i.e. magnetite nanoparticles that completely lose their permanent magnetic characteristic in the absence of an external magnetic field (which by definition show a coercivity of 0 A/m). The smallest values currently reported for nanosized magnetite particles is <i>Hc</i> = 8.5 A m<sup>−1</sup>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> whereas the largest reported magnetization value is 87 Am<sup>2</sup> kg<sup>−1</sup> for synthetic magnetite.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pigment quality Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, so called synthetic magnetite, can be prepared using processes that use industrial wastes, scrap iron or solutions containing iron salts (e.g. those produced as by-products in industrial processes such as the acid vat treatment (<a href="/wiki/Pickling_(metal)" title="Pickling (metal)">pickling</a>) of steel):
</p>
<ul><li>Oxidation of Fe metal in the Laux process where <a href="/wiki/Nitrobenzene" title="Nitrobenzene">nitrobenzene</a> is treated with iron metal using FeCl<sub>2</sub> as a catalyst to produce <a href="/wiki/Aniline" title="Aniline">aniline</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<dl><dd>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>NO<sub>2</sub> + 3 Fe + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O   →   C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>NH<sub>2</sub> + Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub></dd></dl>
<ul><li>Oxidation of Fe<sup>II</sup> compounds, e.g. the precipitation of iron(II) salts as hydroxides followed by oxidation by aeration where careful control of the pH determines the oxide produced.<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Reduction of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> with hydrogen:<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>3Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub> → 2Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> +H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Reduction of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> with CO:<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>3Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + CO → 2Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> + CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Production of nano-particles can be performed chemically by taking for example mixtures of Fe<sup>II</sup> and Fe<sup>III</sup> salts and mixing them with alkali to precipitate colloidal Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>. The reaction conditions are critical to the process and determine the particle size.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(II,III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> has a cubic inverse <a href="/wiki/Spinel_group" title="Spinel group">spinel group</a> structure which consists of a cubic close packed array of oxide ions where all of the Fe<sup>2+</sup> ions occupy half of the octahedral sites and the Fe<sup>3+</sup> are split evenly across the remaining octahedral sites and the tetrahedral sites.
</p><p>Both <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide" title="Iron(II) oxide">FeO</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide" title="Iron(III) oxide">γ-Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></a> have a similar cubic close packed array of oxide ions and this accounts for the ready interchangeability between the three compounds on oxidation and reduction as these reactions entail a relatively small change to the overall structure.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> samples can be <a href="/wiki/Non-stoichiometric" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-stoichiometric">non-stoichiometric</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ferrimagnetism" title="Ferrimagnetism">ferrimagnetism</a> of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> arises because the electron spins of the Fe<sup>II</sup> and Fe<sup>III</sup> ions in the octahedral sites are coupled and the spins of the Fe<sup>III</sup> ions in the tetrahedral sites are coupled but anti-parallel to the former. The net effect is that the magnetic contributions of both sets are not balanced and there is a permanent magnetism.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(II,III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Magnetite.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Magnetite.jpg/240px-Magnetite.jpg" width="240" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Magnetite.jpg/360px-Magnetite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Magnetite.jpg/480px-Magnetite.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5977" data-file-height="3953" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Magnetite.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sample of <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a>, naturally occurring Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>.</div></div></div>
<p>Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> is <a href="/wiki/Ferrimagnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferrimagnetic">ferrimagnetic</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Curie_point" class="mw-redirect" title="Curie point">Curie temperature</a> of 858 K. There is a phase transition at 120K, the so-called <a href="/wiki/Verwey_transition" title="Verwey transition">Verwey transition</a> where there is a discontinuity in the structure, conductivity and magnetic properties.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> This effect has been extensively investigated and whilst various explanations have been proposed, it does not appear to be fully understood.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> is an electrical conductor with a conductivity significantly higher (X 10<sup>6</sup>) than <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide" title="Iron(III) oxide">Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></a>, and this is ascribed to electron exchange between the Fe<sup>II</sup> and Fe<sup>III</sup> centres.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(II,III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> is used as a black pigment and is known as <i>C.I pigment black 11</i> (C.I. No.77499) or <a href="/wiki/Mars_Black_(pigment)" title="Mars Black (pigment)">Mars Black</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Buxbaum_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Buxbaum-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> is used as a catalyst in the <a href="/wiki/Haber_process" title="Haber process">Haber process</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Water_gas_shift_reaction" class="mw-redirect" title="Water gas shift reaction">water gas shift reaction</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lee_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The latter uses an HTS (high temperature shift catalyst) of iron oxide stabilised by chromium oxide.<sup id="cite_ref-Lee_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> This iron-chrome catalyst is reduced at reactor start up to generate Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> from α-Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>  and Cr<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> to CrO<sub>3</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lee_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Nano particles of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> are used as contrast agents in <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">MRI scanning</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ferumoxytol, also known as Feraheme and Rienso,<sup id="cite_ref-amagpr2014_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amagpr2014-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> is an intravenous Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> preparation for treatment of <a href="/wiki/Anemia" title="Anemia">anemia</a> resulting from <a href="/wiki/Chronic_kidney_disease" title="Chronic kidney disease">chronic kidney disease</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-schwenk2010_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schwenk2010-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  Ferumoxytol is manufactured and globally distributed by <a href="/wiki/AMAG_Pharmaceuticals" title="AMAG Pharmaceuticals">AMAG Pharmaceuticals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-amagpr2014_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amagpr2014-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Along with <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, it is an ingredient in a specific type of <a href="/wiki/Thermite" title="Thermite">thermite</a> useful for cutting steel.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bluing_(steel)" title="Bluing (steel)">Bluing</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">passivation</a> process that produces a layer of Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> on the surface of steel to protect it from rust.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_occurrence">Biological occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(II,III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Biological occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Magnetite has been found as nano-crystals in <a href="/wiki/Magnetotactic_bacteria" title="Magnetotactic bacteria">magnetotactic bacteria</a> (42-45&#160;nm)<sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and in homing pigeon beak tissue<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
