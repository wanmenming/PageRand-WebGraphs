<p><b>Barium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol&#160;<b>Ba</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a>&#160;56. It is the fifth element in group 2 and is a soft, silvery <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_earth_metal" title="Alkaline earth metal">alkaline earth metal</a>. Because of its high chemical <a href="/wiki/Reactivity_(chemistry)" title="Reactivity (chemistry)">reactivity</a>, barium is never found in nature as a free element. Its hydroxide, known in pre-modern times as <a href="/wiki/Barium_hydroxide" title="Barium hydroxide">baryta</a>, does not occur as a <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a>, but can be prepared by heating barium carbonate.
</p><p>The most common naturally occurring minerals of barium are <a href="/wiki/Baryte" title="Baryte">barite</a> (now called baryte<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>) (<a href="/wiki/Barium_sulfate" title="Barium sulfate">barium sulfate</a>, BaSO<sub>4</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Witherite" title="Witherite">witherite</a> (<a href="/wiki/Barium_carbonate" title="Barium carbonate">barium carbonate</a>, BaCO<sub>3</sub>), both insoluble in water. The name <i>barium</i> originates from the alchemical derivative "baryta", from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> βαρύς (<i>barys</i>), meaning "heavy." <i>Baric</i> is the adjectival form of barium. Barium was identified as a new element in 1774, but not reduced to a metal until 1808 with the advent of <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a>.
</p><p>Barium has few industrial applications.  Historically, it was used as a <a href="/wiki/Getter" title="Getter">getter</a> for <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_tube" title="Vacuum tube">vacuum tubes</a> and in oxide form as the emissive coating on <a href="/wiki/Hot_cathode#Oxide-coated_cathodes" title="Hot cathode">indirectly heated cathodes</a>. It is a component of <a href="/wiki/Yttrium_barium_copper_oxide" title="Yttrium barium copper oxide">YBCO</a> (<a href="/wiki/High-temperature_superconductivity" title="High-temperature superconductivity">high-temperature superconductors</a>) and electroceramics, and is added to steel and cast iron to reduce the size of carbon grains within the microstructure. Barium compounds are added to fireworks to impart a green color. <a href="/wiki/Barium_sulfate" title="Barium sulfate">Barium sulfate</a> is used as an insoluble additive to <a href="/wiki/Oil_well" title="Oil well">oil well</a> <a href="/wiki/Drilling_fluid" title="Drilling fluid">drilling fluid</a>, as well as in a purer form, as X-ray <a href="/wiki/Radiocontrast_agent" title="Radiocontrast agent">radiocontrast agents</a> for imaging the human gastrointestinal tract. The soluble barium <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ion</a> and soluble compounds are poisonous, and have been used as <a href="/wiki/Rodenticide" title="Rodenticide">rodenticides</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Barium_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Barium_1.jpg/220px-Barium_1.jpg" width="220" height="296" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Barium_1.jpg/330px-Barium_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9e/Barium_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="371" data-file-height="500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Barium_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Oxidized barium</div></div></div>
<p>Barium is a soft, silvery-white metal, with a slight golden shade when ultrapure.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>2</span></sup> The silvery-white color of barium metal rapidly vanishes upon <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">oxidation</a> in air yielding a dark gray <a href="/wiki/Barium_oxide" title="Barium oxide">oxide</a> layer. Barium has a medium <a href="/wiki/Specific_weight" title="Specific weight">specific weight</a> and good electrical conductivity. Ultrapure barium is very difficult to prepare, and therefore many properties of barium have not been accurately measured yet.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>2</span></sup>
</p><p>At room temperature and pressure, barium has a <a href="/wiki/Body-centered_cubic" class="mw-redirect" title="Body-centered cubic">body-centered cubic</a> structure, with a barium–barium distance of 503 <a href="/wiki/Picometer" class="mw-redirect" title="Picometer">picometers</a>, expanding with heating at a rate of approximately 1.8<span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">×</span>10<sup>−<span style="white-space:nowrap">5</span></sup>/°C.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>2</span></sup> It is a very soft metal with a <a href="/wiki/Mohs_hardness" class="mw-redirect" title="Mohs hardness">Mohs hardness</a> of 1.25.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>2</span></sup> Its melting temperature of 1,000&#160;K (730&#160;°C; 1,340&#160;°F)<sup id="cite_ref-Lide2004_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lide2004-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4–43</span></sup> is intermediate between those of the lighter strontium (1,050&#160;K or 780&#160;°C or 1,430&#160;°F)<sup id="cite_ref-Lide2004_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lide2004-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4–86</span></sup> and heavier radium (973&#160;K or 700&#160;°C or 1,292&#160;°F);<sup id="cite_ref-Lide2004_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lide2004-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4–78</span></sup> however, its boiling point of 2,170&#160;K (1,900&#160;°C; 3,450&#160;°F) exceeds that of strontium (1,655&#160;K or 1,382&#160;°C or 2,519&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-Lide2004_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lide2004-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4–86</span></sup> The density (3.62&#160;g/cm<sup>3</sup>)<sup id="cite_ref-Lide2004_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lide2004-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4–43</span></sup> is again intermediate between those of strontium (2.36&#160;g/cm<sup>3</sup>)<sup id="cite_ref-Lide2004_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lide2004-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4–86</span></sup> and radium (~5&#160;g/cm<sup>3</sup>).<sup id="cite_ref-Lide2004_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lide2004-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4–78</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_reactivity">Chemical reactivity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical reactivity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Barium is chemically similar to magnesium, calcium, and strontium, but even more reactive. It always exhibits the oxidation state of +2, except in a few rare and unstable molecular species that are only characterised in the gas phase such as BaF.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>2</span></sup> Reactions with <a href="/wiki/Chalcogen" title="Chalcogen">chalcogens</a> are highly <a href="/wiki/Exothermic_reaction" title="Exothermic reaction">exothermic</a> (release energy); the reaction with oxygen or air occurs at room temperature, and therefore barium is stored under oil or in an inert atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>2</span></sup> Reactions with other <a href="/wiki/Nonmetal" title="Nonmetal">nonmetals</a>, such as carbon, nitrogen, phosphorus, silicon, and hydrogen, are generally exothermic and proceed upon heating.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>2–3</span></sup> Reactions with water and alcohols are very exothermic and release hydrogen gas:<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>3</span></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Ba + 2 ROH → Ba(OR)<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>↑ (R is an alkyl group or a hydrogen atom)</dd></dl>
<p>Barium reacts with <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> to form complexes such as Ba(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>6</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>3</span></sup>
</p><p>The metal is readily attacked by most acids. <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">Sulfuric acid</a> is a notable exception because <a href="/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry)" title="Passivation (chemistry)">passivation</a> stops the reaction by forming the insoluble <a href="/wiki/Barium_sulfate" title="Barium sulfate">barium sulfate</a> on the surface.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Barium combines with several metals, including <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, forming <a href="/wiki/Intermetallics" class="mw-redirect" title="Intermetallics">intermetallic phases</a> and alloys.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Barium salts are typically white when solid and colorless when dissolved, and barium ions provide no specific coloring.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> They are denser than the <a href="/wiki/Strontium" title="Strontium">strontium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> analogs, except for the <a href="/wiki/Halide" title="Halide">halides</a> (see table; <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> is given for comparison).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Barium_hydroxide" title="Barium hydroxide">Barium hydroxide</a> ("baryta") was known to alchemists, who produced it by heating barium carbonate. Unlike calcium hydroxide, it absorbs very little CO<sub>2</sub> in aqueous solutions and is therefore insensitive to atmospheric fluctuations. This property is used in calibrating pH equipment.
</p><p>Volatile barium compounds burn with a green to pale green <a href="/wiki/Flame_test" title="Flame test">flame</a>, which is an efficient test to detect a barium compound. The color results from <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">spectral lines</a> at 455.4, 493.4, 553.6, and 611.1&#160;nm.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>3</span></sup> 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Group_2_organometallic_chemistry#Organobarium" title="Group 2 organometallic chemistry">Organobarium compounds</a> are a growing field of knowledge: recently discovered are dialkylbariums and alkylhalobariums.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>3</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_barium" title="Isotopes of barium">Isotopes of barium</a></div>
<p>Barium found in the Earth's crust is a mixture of seven primordial nuclides, barium-130, 132, and 134 through 138.<sup id="cite_ref-iso_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iso-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Barium-130 undergoes very slow <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> to <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">xenon</a>-130 by double <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta plus decay</a>, and barium-132 theoretically decays similarly to xenon-132, with half-lives a thousand times greater than the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_the_Universe" class="mw-redirect" title="Age of the Universe">age of the Universe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NUBASE_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NUBASE-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The abundance is ~0.1% that of natural barium.<sup id="cite_ref-iso_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iso-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The radioactivity of these isotopes is so weak that they pose no danger to life.
</p><p>Of the stable isotopes, barium-138 composes 71.7% of all barium, and the lighter the isotope, the less abundant.<sup id="cite_ref-iso_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iso-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In total, barium has about 50 known isotopes, ranging in mass between 114 and 153. The most stable metastable isotope is barium-133 with a half-life of approximately 10.51&#160;years.  Five other isotopes have half-lives longer than a day.<sup id="cite_ref-NUBASE_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NUBASE-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Barium also has 10 <a href="/wiki/Meta_state" class="mw-redirect" title="Meta state">meta states</a>, out of which barium-133m1 is the most stable with a half-life of about 39 hours.<sup id="cite_ref-NUBASE_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NUBASE-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Humphry_Davy_Engraving_1830.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Humphry_Davy_Engraving_1830.jpg/170px-Humphry_Davy_Engraving_1830.jpg" width="170" height="216" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Humphry_Davy_Engraving_1830.jpg/255px-Humphry_Davy_Engraving_1830.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Humphry_Davy_Engraving_1830.jpg/340px-Humphry_Davy_Engraving_1830.jpg 2x" data-file-width="469" data-file-height="597" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Humphry_Davy_Engraving_1830.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Sir Humphry Davy</a>, who first isolated barium metal</div></div></div>
<p>Alchemists in the early Middle Ages knew about some barium minerals. Smooth pebble-like stones of mineral baryte were found in volcanic rock near <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">Bologna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, and so were called "Bologna stones." Alchemists were attracted to them because after exposure to light they would glow for years.<sup id="cite_ref-history_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The phosphorescent properties of baryte heated with organics were described by V. Casciorolus in 1602.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>5</span></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Carl_Scheele" class="mw-redirect" title="Carl Scheele">Carl Scheele</a> determined that baryte contained a new element in 1774, but could not isolate barium, only <a href="/wiki/Barium_oxide" title="Barium oxide">barium oxide</a>. <a href="/wiki/Johan_Gottlieb_Gahn" title="Johan Gottlieb Gahn">Johan Gottlieb Gahn</a> also isolated <a href="/wiki/Barium_oxide" title="Barium oxide">barium oxide</a> two years later in similar studies. Oxidized barium was at first called "barote" by <a href="/wiki/Guyton_de_Morveau" class="mw-redirect" title="Guyton de Morveau">Guyton de Morveau</a>, a name that was changed by <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Lavoisier">Antoine Lavoisier</a> to <i>baryta.</i> Also in the 18th century, English mineralogist <a href="/wiki/William_Withering" title="William Withering">William Withering</a> noted a heavy mineral in the lead mines of <a href="/wiki/Cumberland" title="Cumberland">Cumberland</a>, now known to be <a href="/wiki/Witherite" title="Witherite">witherite</a>. Barium was first isolated by electrolysis of molten barium salts in 1808 by Sir <a href="/wiki/Humphry_Davy" title="Humphry Davy">Humphry Davy</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Davy, by analogy with <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, named "barium" after baryta, with the "-ium" ending signifying a metallic element.<sup id="cite_ref-history_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bunsen" title="Robert Bunsen">Robert Bunsen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Augustus_Matthiessen" title="Augustus Matthiessen">Augustus Matthiessen</a> obtained pure barium by electrolysis of a molten mixture of <a href="/wiki/Barium_chloride" title="Barium chloride">barium chloride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The production of pure oxygen in the <a href="/wiki/Brin_process" title="Brin process">Brin process</a> was a large-scale application of barium peroxide in the 1880s, before it was replaced by electrolysis and <a href="/wiki/Fractional_distillation" title="Fractional distillation">fractional distillation</a> of liquefied air in the early 1900s. In this process barium oxide reacts at 500–600&#160;°C (932–1,112&#160;°F) with air to form barium peroxide, which decomposes above 700&#160;°C (1,292&#160;°F) by releasing oxygen:<sup id="cite_ref-O2_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-O2-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 BaO + O<sub>2</sub> ⇌ 2 BaO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Barium sulfate was first applied as a <a href="/wiki/Radiocontrast" class="mw-redirect" title="Radiocontrast">radiocontrast</a> agent in <a href="/wiki/Medical_imaging" title="Medical imaging">X-ray imaging</a> of the digestive system in 1908.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_and_production">Occurrence and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Occurrence and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The abundance of barium is 0.0425% in the Earth's crust and 13&#160;µg/L in sea water. The primary commercial source of barium is baryte (also called barytes or heavy spar), a barium sulfate mineral.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>5</span></sup> with deposits in many parts of the world. Another commercial source, far less important than baryte, is witherite, a barium carbonate mineral. The main deposits are located in England, Romania, and the former USSR.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>5</span></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303569">@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .tmulti>.thumbinner{width:100%!important;max-width:none!important}.mw-parser-output .tmulti <p>The baryte reserves are estimated between 0.7 and 2 billion <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a>. The maximum production, 8.3 million tonnes, was produced in 1981, but only 7–8% was used for barium metal or compounds.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>5</span></sup> Baryte production has risen since the second half of the 1990s from 5.6 million tonnes in 1996 to 7.6 in 2005 and 7.8 in 2011. China accounts for more than 50% of this output, followed by India (14% in 2011), Morocco (8.3%), US (8.2%), Turkey (2.5%), Iran and Kazakhstan (2.6% each).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The mined ore is washed, crushed, classified, and separated from quartz. If the quartz penetrates too deeply into the ore, or the iron, zinc, or lead content is abnormally high, then <a href="/wiki/Froth_flotation" title="Froth flotation">froth flotation</a> is used. The product is a 98% pure baryte (by mass); the purity should be no less than 95%, with a minimal content of iron and <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">silicon dioxide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>7</span></sup> It is then reduced by carbon to barium sulfide:<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>6</span></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>BaSO<sub>4</sub> + 2 C → BaS + 2 CO<sub>2</sub>↑</dd></dl>
<p>The water-soluble barium sulfide is the starting point for other compounds: reacting BaS with oxygen produces the sulfate, with nitric acid the nitrate, with carbon dioxide the carbonate, and so on.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>6</span></sup> The nitrate can be thermally decomposed to yield the oxide.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>6</span></sup> Barium metal is produced by reduction with <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> at 1,100&#160;°C (2,010&#160;°F). The <a href="/wiki/Intermetallic_compound" class="mw-redirect" title="Intermetallic compound">intermetallic compound</a> BaAl<sub>4</sub> is produced first:<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>3</span></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>3 BaO + 14 Al → 3 BaAl<sub>4</sub> + Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>BaAl<sub>4</sub> is an intermediate reacted with barium oxide to produce the metal. Note that not all barium is reduced.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>3</span></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>8 BaO + BaAl<sub>4</sub> → Ba↑ + 7 BaAl<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The remaining barium oxide reacts with the formed aluminium oxide:<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>3</span></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>BaO + Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> → BaAl<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub></dd></dl>
<p>and the overall reaction is<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>3</span></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>4 BaO + 2 Al → 3 Ba↑ + BaAl<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Barium vapor is condensed and packed into molds in an atmosphere of argon.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>3</span></sup> This method is used commercially, yielding ultrapure barium.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>3</span></sup> Commonly sold barium is about 99% pure, with main impurities being strontium and calcium (up to 0.8% and 0.25%) and other contaminants contributing less than 0.1%.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4</span></sup>
</p><p>A similar reaction with silicon at 1,200&#160;°C (2,190&#160;°F) yields barium and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Barium_metasilicate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Barium metasilicate (page does not exist)">barium metasilicate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>3</span></sup> Electrolysis is not used because barium readily dissolves in molten halides and the product is rather impure.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>3</span></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Benitoite_HD.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Benitoite_HD.jpg/220px-Benitoite_HD.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Benitoite_HD.jpg/330px-Benitoite_HD.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Benitoite_HD.jpg/440px-Benitoite_HD.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5623" data-file-height="3737" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Benitoite_HD.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Benitoite crystals on natrolite. The mineral is named for the <a href="/wiki/San_Benito_River" title="San Benito River">San Benito River</a> in <a href="/wiki/San_Benito_County" class="mw-redirect" title="San Benito County">San Benito County</a> where it was first found.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gemstone">Gemstone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Gemstone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The barium mineral, <a href="/wiki/Benitoite" title="Benitoite">benitoite</a> (barium titanium silicate), occurs as a very rare blue fluorescent gemstone, and is the official state gem of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metal_and_alloys">Metal and alloys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Metal and alloys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Barium, as a metal or when alloyed with aluminium, is used to remove unwanted gases (<a href="/wiki/Getter" title="Getter">gettering</a>) from vacuum tubes, such as TV picture tubes.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4</span></sup> Barium is suitable for this purpose because of its low <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">vapor pressure</a> and reactivity towards oxygen, nitrogen, carbon dioxide, and water; it can even partly remove noble gases by dissolving them in the crystal lattice. This application is gradually disappearing due to the rising popularity of the tubeless LCD and plasma sets.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4</span></sup>
</p><p>Other uses of elemental barium are minor and include an additive to <a href="/wiki/Silumin" title="Silumin">silumin</a> (aluminium–silicon alloys) that refines their structure, as well as<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4</span></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bearing_alloy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bearing alloy (page does not exist)">bearing alloys</a>;</li>
<li>lead–tin <a href="/wiki/Solder" title="Solder">soldering</a> alloys – to increase the creep resistance;</li>
<li>alloy with nickel for <a href="/wiki/Spark_plug" title="Spark plug">spark plugs</a>;</li>
<li>additive to steel and cast iron as an inoculant;</li>
<li>alloys with calcium, manganese, silicon, and aluminium as high-grade steel deoxidizers.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Barium_sulfate_and_baryte">Barium sulfate and baryte</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Barium sulfate and baryte">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BariumXray.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/BariumXray.jpg/220px-BariumXray.jpg" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/BariumXray.jpg/330px-BariumXray.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/BariumXray.jpg/440px-BariumXray.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BariumXray.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Amoebiasis" title="Amoebiasis">Amoebiasis</a> as seen in a radiograph of a barium-filled colon</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Barium_sulfate" title="Barium sulfate">Barium sulfate</a> (the mineral baryte, BaSO<sub>4</sub>) is important to the petroleum industry as a <a href="/wiki/Drilling_fluid" title="Drilling fluid">drilling fluid</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oil_well" title="Oil well">oil and gas wells</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lide2004_8-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lide2004-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4–5</span></sup> The precipitate of the compound (called "blanc fixe", from the French for "permanent white") is used in paints and varnishes; as a filler in ringing ink, plastics, and rubbers; as a paper coating pigment; and in <a href="/wiki/Nanoparticle" title="Nanoparticle">nanoparticles</a>, to improve physical properties of some polymers, such as epoxies.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>9</span></sup>
</p><p>Barium sulfate has a low toxicity and relatively high density of ca. 4.5&#160;g/cm<sup>3</sup> (and thus opacity to X-rays). For this reason it is used as a <a href="/wiki/Radiocontrast" class="mw-redirect" title="Radiocontrast">radiocontrast</a> agent in <a href="/wiki/Medical_imaging" title="Medical imaging">X-ray imaging</a> of the digestive system ("<a href="/wiki/Barium_meal" class="mw-redirect" title="Barium meal">barium meals</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Lower_gastrointestinal_series" title="Lower gastrointestinal series">barium enemas</a>").<sup id="cite_ref-Lide2004_8-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lide2004-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4–5</span></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lithopone" title="Lithopone">Lithopone</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a> that contains barium sulfate and <a href="/wiki/Zinc_sulfide" title="Zinc sulfide">zinc sulfide</a>, is a permanent white with good covering power that does not darken when exposed to sulfides.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_barium_compounds">Other barium compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other barium compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2006_Fireworks_1.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/2006_Fireworks_1.JPG/220px-2006_Fireworks_1.JPG" width="220" height="229" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/2006_Fireworks_1.JPG/330px-2006_Fireworks_1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/2006_Fireworks_1.JPG/440px-2006_Fireworks_1.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1371" data-file-height="1430" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2006_Fireworks_1.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Green barium fireworks</div></div></div>
<p>Other compounds of barium find only niche applications, limited by the toxicity of Ba<sup>2+</sup> ions (barium carbonate is a <a href="/wiki/Rat_poison" class="mw-redirect" title="Rat poison">rat poison</a>), which is not a problem for the insoluble BaSO<sub>4</sub>.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Barium_oxide" title="Barium oxide">Barium oxide</a> coating on the <a href="/wiki/Electrode" title="Electrode">electrodes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lamp" title="Fluorescent lamp">fluorescent lamps</a> facilitates the release of <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a>.</li>
<li>By its great atomic density, <a href="/wiki/Barium_carbonate" title="Barium carbonate">barium carbonate</a> increases the <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a> and luster of glass<sup id="cite_ref-Lide2004_8-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lide2004-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4–5</span></sup> and reduces leaks of X-rays from <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tubes" class="mw-redirect" title="Cathode ray tubes">cathode ray tubes</a> (CRT) TV sets.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>12–13</span></sup></li>
<li>Barium, typically as <a href="/wiki/Barium_nitrate" title="Barium nitrate">barium nitrate</a> imparts a yellow or "apple" green color to fireworks;<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> for brilliant green barium monochloride is used.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Barium_peroxide" title="Barium peroxide">Barium peroxide</a> is a catalyst in the <a href="/wiki/Aluminothermic_reaction" title="Aluminothermic reaction">aluminothermic reaction</a> (<a href="/wiki/Thermite" title="Thermite">thermite</a>) for welding rail tracks. It is also a green flare in <a href="/wiki/Tracer_ammunition" title="Tracer ammunition">tracer ammunition</a> and a bleaching agent.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Barium_titanate" title="Barium titanate">Barium titanate</a> is a promising <a href="/wiki/Electroceramic" class="mw-redirect" title="Electroceramic">electroceramic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Barium_fluoride" title="Barium fluoride">Barium fluoride</a> is used for optics in infrared applications because of its wide transparency range of 0.15–12&#160;micrometers.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yttrium_barium_copper_oxide" title="Yttrium barium copper oxide">YBCO</a> was the first <a href="/wiki/High-temperature_superconductivity" title="High-temperature superconductivity">high-temperature superconductor</a> cooled by liquid nitrogen, with a transition temperature of 93&#160;K (−180.2&#160;°C; −292.3&#160;°F) that exceeded the boiling point of nitrogen (77&#160;K or −196.2&#160;°C or −321.1&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ferrite_(magnet)" title="Ferrite (magnet)">Ferrite</a>, a type of <a href="/wiki/Sintering" title="Sintering">sintered</a> ceramic composed of Iron Oxide (Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) and barium oxide (BaO), is both <a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical conductivity">electrically nonconductive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ferrimagnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferrimagnetic">ferrimagnetic</a>, and can be temporarily or permanently magnetized.</li></ul>

<p>Because of the high reactivity of the metal, toxicological data are available only for compounds.<sup id="cite_ref-bariumtoxic_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bariumtoxic-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Soluble barium compounds are poisonous. In low doses, barium ions act as a muscle stimulant, and higher doses affect the <a href="/wiki/Nervous_system" title="Nervous system">nervous system</a>, causing cardiac irregularities, tremors, weakness, <a href="/wiki/Anxiety_(mood)" class="mw-redirect" title="Anxiety (mood)">anxiety</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dyspnea" class="mw-redirect" title="Dyspnea">shortness of breath</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paralysis" title="Paralysis">paralysis</a>. This toxicity may be caused by Ba<sup>2+</sup> <a href="/wiki/Potassium_channel_blocker" title="Potassium channel blocker">blocking</a> <a href="/wiki/Potassium_ion_channels" class="mw-redirect" title="Potassium ion channels">potassium ion channels</a>, which are critical to the proper function of the nervous system.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Other organs damaged by water-soluble barium compounds (i.e., barium ions) are the eyes, immune system, heart, respiratory system, and skin<sup id="cite_ref-bariumtoxic_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bariumtoxic-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> causing, for example, blindness and sensitization.<sup id="cite_ref-bariumtoxic_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bariumtoxic-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Barium is not carcinogenic<sup id="cite_ref-bariumtoxic_29-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bariumtoxic-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and it does not <a href="/wiki/Bioaccumulation" title="Bioaccumulation">bioaccumulate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Inhaled dust containing insoluble barium compounds can accumulate in the lungs, causing a <a href="/wiki/Benign" class="mw-redirect" title="Benign">benign</a> condition called <a href="/wiki/Baritosis" title="Baritosis">baritosis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid1257935_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid1257935-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The insoluble sulfate is nontoxic and is not classified as a dangerous goods in transport regulations.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullman2005_7-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullman2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>9</span></sup>
</p><p>To avoid a potentially vigorous chemical reaction, barium metal is kept in an argon atmosphere or under mineral oils. Contact with air is dangerous and may cause ignition. Moisture, friction, heat, sparks, flames, shocks, static electricity, and exposure to oxidizers and acids should be avoided. Anything that may contact with barium should be electrically grounded. Anyone who works with the metal should wear pre-cleaned non-sparking shoes, flame-resistant rubber clothes, rubber gloves, apron, goggles, and a gas mask. Smoking in the working area is forbidden. Thorough washing is required after handling barium.<sup id="cite_ref-bariumtoxic_29-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bariumtoxic-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
