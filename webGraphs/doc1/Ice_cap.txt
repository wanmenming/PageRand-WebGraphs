<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Ice cap</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about the glacial feature. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Icecap_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Icecap (disambiguation)">Icecap (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vatnaj%C3%B6kull.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Vatnaj%C3%B6kull.jpeg/300px-Vatnaj%C3%B6kull.jpeg" width="300" height="231" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Vatnaj%C3%B6kull.jpeg/450px-Vatnaj%C3%B6kull.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Vatnaj%C3%B6kull.jpeg/600px-Vatnaj%C3%B6kull.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="619" data-file-height="477" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vatnaj%C3%B6kull.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Vatnaj%C3%B6kull" title="Vatnajökull">Vatnajökull</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a></div></div></div>
<p>An <b> ice cap</b> is a mass of <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> that covers less than 50,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup> of land area (usually covering a highland area). Larger ice masses covering more than 50,000&#160;km<sup>2</sup> are termed <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">ice sheets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DougBenn_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DougBenn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bennett_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bennett-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GreveBlatter2009_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GreveBlatter2009-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ice caps are not constrained by <a href="/wiki/Topographical" class="mw-redirect" title="Topographical">topographical</a> features (i.e., they will lie over the top of mountains). By contrast, ice masses of similar size that <i>are</i> constrained by topographical features are known as <a href="/wiki/Ice_field" title="Ice field">ice fields</a>. The <i>dome</i> of an ice cap is usually centred on the highest point of a <a href="/wiki/Massif" title="Massif">massif</a>. Ice flows away from this high point (the <a href="/wiki/Ice_divide" title="Ice divide">ice divide</a>) towards the ice cap's periphery.<sup id="cite_ref-DougBenn_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DougBenn-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GreveBlatter2009_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GreveBlatter2009-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Ice caps have significant effects on the <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphology</a> of the area that they occupy. Plastic moulding, gouging and other <a href="/wiki/Glacial" class="mw-redirect" title="Glacial">glacial</a> <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosional</a> features become present upon the glacier's retreat. Many lakes, such as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> in North America, as well as numerous valleys have been formed by glacial action over hundreds of thousands of years.
</p><p>On Earth, there are about 30 million km<sup>3</sup> of total ice mass.  The average temperature of an ice mass ranges between −20&#160;°C and −30&#160;°C. The core of an ice cap exhibits a constant temperature that ranges between −15&#160;°C and −20&#160;°C.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>A high-latitude region covered in ice, though strictly not an ice cap (since they exceed the maximum area specified in the definition above), are called <a href="/wiki/Polar_ice_caps" class="mw-redirect" title="Polar ice caps">polar ice caps</a>; the usage of this designation is widespread in the mass media<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and arguably recognized by experts.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Vatnaj%C3%B6kull" title="Vatnajökull">Vatnajökull</a> is an example of an ice cap in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
