<p><b>Xenon trioxide</b> is an unstable <a href="/wiki/Noble_gas_compounds" class="mw-redirect" title="Noble gas compounds">compound</a> of <a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">xenon</a> in its +6 <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_state" title="Oxidation state">oxidation state</a>. It is a very powerful <a href="/wiki/Oxidizing_agent" title="Oxidizing agent">oxidizing agent</a>, and liberates oxygen from water slowly, accelerated by exposure to sunlight. It is dangerously explosive upon contact with organic materials. When it detonates, it releases xenon and oxygen gas.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xenon_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Xenon trioxide is a strong oxidising agent and can oxidise most substances that are at all oxidisable. However, it is slow-acting and this reduces its usefulness.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Above 25 °C, xenon trioxide is very prone to violent explosion:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 XeO<sub>3</sub> → 2 Xe + 3 O<sub>2</sub> with ΔH = −402 k<a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">J</a>/<a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mol</a>.</dd></dl>
<p>When it dissolves in water, an acidic solution of <a href="/wiki/Xenic_acid" title="Xenic acid">xenic acid</a> is formed:
</p>
<dl><dd>XeO<sub>3</sub> (aq) + H<sub>2</sub>O → H<sub>2</sub>XeO<sub>4</sub> ⇌ H<sup>+</sup> + HXeO<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup></dd></dl>
<p>This solution is stable at room temperature and lacks the explosive properties of xenon trioxide. It oxidises <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acids</a> quantitatively to <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" class="mw-redirect" title="Water (molecule)">water</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Alternatively, it dissolves in alkaline solutions to form <i>xenates</i>. The HXeO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">&#8722;</sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span> anion is the predominant species in xenate solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> These are not stable and begin to <a href="/wiki/Disproportionation" title="Disproportionation">disproportionate</a> into <a href="/wiki/Perxenate" title="Perxenate">perxenates</a> (+8 oxidation state) and xenon and oxygen gas.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Solid perxenates containing <span class="chemf nowrap">XeO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4−</sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span></span> have been isolated by reacting <span class="chemf nowrap">XeO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> with an aqueous solution of hydroxides. Xenon trioxide reacts with inorganic fluorides such as KF, RbF, or CsF to form stable solids of the form <span class="chemf nowrap">MXeO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>F</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xenon_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hydrolysis of <a href="/wiki/Xenon_hexafluoride" title="Xenon hexafluoride">xenon hexafluoride</a> or <a href="/wiki/Xenon_tetrafluoride" title="Xenon tetrafluoride">xenon tetrafluoride</a> yields a solution from which colorless XeO<sub>3</sub> crystals can be obtained by evaporation.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The crystals are stable for days in dry air, but readily absorb water from humid air to form a concentrated solution. The crystal structure is <a href="/wiki/Orthorhombic" class="mw-redirect" title="Orthorhombic">orthorhombic</a> with <i>a</i> = 6.163 Å, <i>b</i> = 8.115 Å, <i>c</i> = 5.234 Å, and 4 molecules per unit cell. The density is 4.55 g/cm<sup>3</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-xe_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xe-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xenon_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>XeO<sub>3</sub> should be handled with great caution. Samples have detonated when undisturbed at room temperature. Dry crystals react explosively with cellulose.<sup id="cite_ref-xe_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-xe-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
