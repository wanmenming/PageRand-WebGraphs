<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Human migration</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Human migration</b> is the movement by people from one place to another with the intentions of settling, permanently or temporarily in a new location. The movement is often over long distances and from one country to another, but <a href="/wiki/Internal_migration" title="Internal migration">internal migration</a> is also possible; indeed, this is the dominant form globally. People may migrate as individuals, in family units or in <a href="/wiki/Mass_migration" title="Mass migration">large groups</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> A person who moves from their home to another place because of natural disaster or civil disturbance may be described as a <a href="/wiki/Refugee" title="Refugee">refugee</a> or, especially within the same country, a <a href="/wiki/Displaced_person" class="mw-redirect" title="Displaced person">displaced person</a>. A person seeking refuge from political, religious, or other forms of persecution is usually described as an <a href="/wiki/Asylum_seeker" title="Asylum seeker">asylum seeker</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nomad" title="Nomad">Nomadic</a> movements are normally not regarded as migrations as there is no intention to settle in the new place and because the movement is generally <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_human_migration" title="Seasonal human migration">seasonal</a>. Only a few nomadic people have retained this form of lifestyle in modern times. Also, the temporary movement of people for the purpose of travel, tourism, pilgrimages, or the commute is not regarded as migration, in the absence of an intention to live and settle in the visited places.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Migrants_in_the_world_1960-2015-en.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Migrants_in_the_world_1960-2015-en.png/400px-Migrants_in_the_world_1960-2015-en.png" width="400" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Migrants_in_the_world_1960-2015-en.png/600px-Migrants_in_the_world_1960-2015-en.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/27/Migrants_in_the_world_1960-2015-en.png 2x" data-file-width="780" data-file-height="520" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Migrants_in_the_world_1960-2015-en.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The number of migrants in the world 1960–2015.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Many estimates of statistics in worldwide migration patterns exist.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> has published its <i>Migration and Remittances Factbook</i> annually since 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Organisation_for_Migration" class="mw-redirect" title="International Organisation for Migration">International Organisation for Migration</a> (IOM) has published a yearly <i>World Migration Report</i> since 1999. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Statistics_Division" title="United Nations Statistics Division">United Nations Statistics Division</a> also keeps a database on worldwide migration.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Recent advances in research on migration via the Internet promise better understanding of migration patterns and migration motives.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Substantial internal migration can also take place within a country, either <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_human_migration" title="Seasonal human migration">seasonal human migration</a> (mainly related to agriculture and to tourism to urban places), or shifts of population into cities (<a href="/wiki/Urbanisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Urbanisation">urbanisation</a>) or out of cities (<a href="/wiki/Suburbanisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Suburbanisation">suburbanisation</a>). Studies of worldwide migration patterns, however, tend to limit their scope to <a href="/wiki/International_migration" title="International migration">international migration</a>.
</p><p>The World Bank's <i>Migration and Remittances Factbook</i> of 2011 lists the following estimates for the year 2010: total number of immigrants: 215.8 million or 3.2% of world population.  In 2013, the percentage of international migrants worldwide increased by 33% with 59% of migrants targeting developed regions.<sup id="cite_ref-UNFPA_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNFPA-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  Almost half of these migrants are women, which is one of the most significant migrant-pattern changes in the last half century.<sup id="cite_ref-UNFPA_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNFPA-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Women migrate alone or with their family members and community. Even though female migration is largely viewed as associations rather than independent migration, emerging studies argue complex and manifold reasons for this.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Often<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="This term requires quantification. (September 2015)">quantify</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> a distinction is made<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (September 2015)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> between voluntary and involuntary migration, or between <a href="/wiki/Refugee" title="Refugee">refugees</a> fleeing political conflict or natural disaster vs. economic or <a href="/wiki/Labour_migration" class="mw-redirect" title="Labour migration">labor migration</a>, but these distinctions are difficult to make and partially subjective, as the motivators for migration are often correlated. The World Bank's report estimates that, as of 2010, 16.3 million or 7.6% of migrants qualified as refugees. At the end of 2012, approximately 15.4 million people were refugees and persons in refugee-like situations - 87% of them found asylum in developing countries.<sup id="cite_ref-UNFPA_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNFPA-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Structurally, there is substantial South-South and North-North migration, i.e., most emigrants from high-income O.E.C.D. countries migrate to other high-income countries, and a substantial part (estimated at 43%) of emigrants from developing countries migrate to other developing countries. The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Population_Fund" title="United Nations Population Fund">United Nations Population Fund</a> says that "[while the North has experienced a higher absolute increase in the migrant stock since 2000 (32 million) compared to the South (25 million), the South recorded a higher growth rate.<sup id="cite_ref-UNFPA_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNFPA-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Between 2000 and 2013 the average annual rate of change of the migrant population in the developing regions (2.3%) slightly exceeded that of the developed regions (2.1%).<sup id="cite_ref-UNFPA_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNFPA-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>

</p><p><a href="/wiki/Remittance" title="Remittance">Remittances</a>, i.e., funds transferred by migrant workers to their home country, form a substantial part of the economy of some countries. The top ten remittance recipients in 2010 were (estimates in billion US dollar) 1. India (55; 2.7% of GDP), 2. China (51; 0.5% of GNP), Mexico (22.6; 1.8% of GDP), <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> (21.3; 7.8% of GDP), France (15.9; 0.5% of GDP), Germany (11.6; 0.2% of GDP), <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> (11.1; 7.2% of GDP), Belgium (10.4; 1.9% of GDP), Spain (10.2; 0.7% of GDP), <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a> (10.0; 1.9% of GDP).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The Global Commission on International Migration (GCIM), launched in 2003, published a report in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> International migration challenges at the global level are addressed through the <a href="/wiki/Global_Forum_on_Migration_and_Development" title="Global Forum on Migration and Development">Global Forum on Migration and Development</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Global_Migration_Group" title="Global Migration Group">Global Migration Group</a>, both established in 2006.
</p><p>The United Nations reported that 2014 had the highest level of forced migration on record: 59.5 million individuals, caused by "persecution, conflict, generalized violence, or human rights violations", as compared with 51.2 million in 2013 (an increase of 8.3 million) and with 37.5 million a decade prior. As of  2015<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> one of every 122 humans is a refugee, internally displaced, or seeking asylum.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic</a> has published 5 maps showing human migrations in progress in 2015 based on the UN report.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Labor_migration_theories_in_the_21st_century">Labor migration theories in the 21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Labor migration theories in the 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are countless of reasons why immigrants choose to migrate to another country. For instance, globalization has increased the demand for workers from other countries in order to sustain national economies. Known as "economic migrants," these individuals are generally from impoverished developing countries migrating to obtain sufficient income for survival.<sup id="cite_ref-unfpa.org_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unfpa.org-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> This income is usually sent home to family members in the form of <a href="/wiki/Remittances" class="mw-redirect" title="Remittances">economic remittances</a> and has become an economic staple in a number of developing countries.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> People also move or are forced to move as a result of conflict, human rights violations, violence, or to escape persecution. In 2013, it was estimated that around 51.2 million people fell into this category.<sup id="cite_ref-unfpa.org_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unfpa.org-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Another reason people move is to gain access to opportunities and services or to escape extreme weather. This type of movement is usually from rural to urban areas and is known as "internal migration." <sup id="cite_ref-unfpa.org_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unfpa.org-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Socio-cultural and geo-historical factors also play a major role. In North Africa, for example, being an immigrant in Europe is considered a sign of social prestige. Moreover, there are many countries which were former European colonies. This means that many have relatives that live legally in Europe, who often constitute an important help for immigrants who have just arrived in a European country. Relatives might help with job research and accommodation. The geographical proximity of Africa to Europe and the long historical ties between Northern and Southern Mediterranean countries also prompt many to migrate.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are a number of theories to explain the international flow of capital and people from one country to another.<sup id="cite_ref-Jennissen,_R_2007_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jennissen,_R_2007-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neoclassical_economic_theory">Neoclassical economic theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Neoclassical economic theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_economics" title="Neoclassical economics">Neoclassical economics</a></div><p>This theory of migration states that the main reason for labor migration is wage difference between two geographic locations. These wage differences are usually linked to geographic labor demand and supply. It can be said that areas with a shortage of labor but an excess of capital have a high relative wage while areas with a high labor supply and a dearth of capital have a low relative wage. Labor tends to flow from low-wage areas to high-wage areas. Often, with this flow of labor comes changes in the sending as well as the receiving country. Neoclassical economic theory is best used to describe transnational migration, because it is not confined by international immigration laws and similar governmental regulations.<sup id="cite_ref-Jennissen,_R_2007_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jennissen,_R_2007-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dual_labor_market_theory">Dual labor market theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dual labor market theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dual labor market theory states that migration is mainly caused by pull factors in more developed countries. This theory assumes that the labor markets in these developed countries consist of two segments: the primary market, which requires high-skilled labor, and the secondary market, which is very labor-intensive requiring low-skilled workers. This theory assumes that migration from less developed countries into more developed countries is a result of a pull created by a need for labor in the developed countries in their secondary market. <a href="/wiki/Migrant_worker" title="Migrant worker">Migrant workers</a> are needed to fill the lowest rung of the labor market because the native laborers do not want to do these jobs as they present a lack of mobility. This creates a need for migrant workers. Furthermore, the initial dearth in available labor pushes wages up, making migration even more enticing.<sup id="cite_ref-Jennissen,_R_2007_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jennissen,_R_2007-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_economics_of_labor_migration">New economics of labor migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: New economics of labor migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This theory states that migration flows and patterns can't be explained solely at the level of individual workers and their economic incentives, but that wider social entities must be considered as well. One such social entity is the household. Migration can be viewed as a result of risk aversion on the part of a household that has insufficient income. The household, in this case, is in need of extra capital that can be achieved through remittances sent back by family members who participate in migrant labor abroad. These <a href="/wiki/Remittances" class="mw-redirect" title="Remittances">remittances</a> can also have a broader effect on the economy of the sending country as a whole as they bring in capital.<sup id="cite_ref-Jennissen,_R_2007_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jennissen,_R_2007-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Recent research has examined a decline in U.S. interstate migration from 1991 to 2011, theorizing that the reduced interstate migration is due to a decline in the geographic specificity of occupations and an increase in workers’ ability to learn about other locations before moving there, through both information technology and inexpensive travel.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Other researchers find that the location-specific nature of housing is more important than moving costs in determining labour reallocation.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relative_deprivation_theory">Relative deprivation theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Relative deprivation theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Relative_deprivation" title="Relative deprivation">Relative deprivation</a></div>
<p>Relative deprivation theory states that awareness of the income difference between neighbors or other households in the migrant-sending community is an important factor in migration. The incentive to migrate is a lot higher in areas that have a high level of economic inequality. In the short run, remittances may increase inequality, but in the long run, they may actually decrease it. There are two stages of migration for a worker: first, they invest in human capital formation, and then they try to capitalize on their investments. In this way, successful migrants may use their new capital to provide for better schooling for their children and better homes for their families. Successful high-skilled emigrants may serve as an example for neighbors and potential migrants who hope to achieve that level of success.<sup id="cite_ref-Jennissen,_R_2007_16-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jennissen,_R_2007-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_systems_theory">World systems theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: World systems theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/World-systems_theory" title="World-systems theory">World-systems theory</a> looks at migration from a global perspective. It explains that interaction between different societies can be an important factor in social change within societies.  Trade with one country, which causes economic decline in another, may create incentive to migrate to a country with a more vibrant economy. It can be argued that even after decolonization, the economic dependence of former colonies still remains on mother countries. This view of <a href="/wiki/International_trade" title="International trade">international trade</a> is controversial, however, and some argue that free trade can actually reduce migration between developing and developed countries. It can be argued that the developed countries import labor-intensive goods, which causes an increase in employment of unskilled workers in the less developed countries, decreasing the outflow of migrant workers. The export of capital-intensive goods from rich countries to poor countries also equalizes income and employment conditions, thus also slowing migration. In either direction, this theory can be used to explain migration between countries that are geographically far apart.<sup id="cite_ref-Jennissen,_R_2007_16-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jennissen,_R_2007-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Osmosis:_the_unifying_theory_of_human_migration">Osmosis: the unifying theory of human migration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Osmosis: the unifying theory of human migration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Old migration theories are generally embedded in geography, sociology or economics. They explain migration in specific periods and spaces. In fact, Osmosis theory explains the whole phenomenon of human migration. Based on the <a href="/wiki/History_of_human_migration" title="History of human migration">history of human migration</a>, Djelti (2017a)<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> studies the evolution of its natural determinants. According to him, human migration is divided into two main types: the simple migration and the complicated one. The simple migration is divided, in its turn, into diffusion, stabilisation and concentration periods. During these periods, water availability, adequate climate, security and population density represent the natural determinants of human migration. For the complicated migration, it is characterised by the speedy evolution and the emergence of new sub-determinants notably earning, unemployment, networks and migration policies. Osmosis theory (Djelti, 2017b)<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> explains analogically human migration by the biophysical phenomenon of <a href="/wiki/Osmosis" title="Osmosis">osmosis</a>. In this respect, the countries are represented by <a href="/wiki/Animal_cells" class="mw-redirect" title="Animal cells">animal cells</a>, the borders by the <a href="/wiki/Semipermeable_membranes" class="mw-redirect" title="Semipermeable membranes">semipermeable membranes</a> and the humans by <a href="/wiki/Ions" class="mw-redirect" title="Ions">ions</a> of water. As to osmosis phenomenon, according to the theory, humans migrate from countries with less migration pressure to countries with high migration pressure. In order to measure the latter, the natural determinants of human migration replace the variables of the <a href="/wiki/Second_principle_of_thermodynamics" class="mw-redirect" title="Second principle of thermodynamics">second principle of thermodynamics</a> used to measure the <a href="/wiki/Osmotic_pressure" title="Osmotic pressure">osmotic pressure</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociological_and_Political_Science_theories">Sociological and Political Science theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sociological and Political Science theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociology">Sociology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sociology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sociology_of_immigration" title="Sociology of immigration">Sociology of immigration</a></div><p>A number of social scientists have examined immigration from a <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociological</a> perspective, paying particular attention to how immigration affects, and is affected by, matters of <a href="/wiki/Race_(human_categorization)" title="Race (human categorization)">race</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnicity</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Social_structure" title="Social structure">social structure</a>. They have produced three main sociological perspectives: <a href="/wiki/Symbolic_interactionism" title="Symbolic interactionism">symbolic interactionism</a>, which aims to understand migration via face-to-face interactions on a micro-level; <a href="/wiki/Social_conflict_theory" title="Social conflict theory">social conflict theory</a> examines migration through the prism of competition for power and resources; <a href="/wiki/Structural_functionalism" title="Structural functionalism">structural functionalism</a>, based on the ideas of <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Durkheim" title="Émile Durkheim">Émile Durkheim</a>, examines the role of migration in fulfilling certain functions within each society, such as the decrease of <a href="/wiki/Anomie" title="Anomie">despair and aimlessness</a> and the consolidation of <a href="/wiki/Social_network" title="Social network">social networks</a>.
</p><p>More recently, as attention shifted away from countries of destination, sociologists have attempted to understand how <a href="/wiki/Transnationalism" title="Transnationalism">transnationalism</a> allows us to understand the interplay between migrants, their countries of destination, and their countries of origins.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In this framework, work on social remittances by <a href="/wiki/Peggy_Levitt" title="Peggy Levitt">Peggy Levitt</a> and others has led to a stronger conceptualisation of how migrants affect socio-political processes in their countries of origin.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_Science">Political Science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Political Science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Political scientists have put forth a number of theoretical frameworks on migration, offering different perspectives on processes of <a href="/wiki/International_security" title="International security">security</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Citizenship" title="Citizenship">citizenship</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">international relations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The political importance of <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">diasporas</a> has also become a growing field of interest, as scholars examine questions of diaspora <a href="/wiki/Activism" title="Activism">activism</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> state-diaspora relations,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> out-of-country <a href="/wiki/Voting" title="Voting">voting</a> processes,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and states' <a href="/wiki/Soft_power" title="Soft power">soft power</a> strategies.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In this field, the majority of work has focused on immigration politics, viewing migration from the perspective of the country of destination.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> With regard to <a href="/wiki/Emigration" title="Emigration">emigration</a> processes, political scientists have expanded on <a href="/wiki/Albert_O._Hirschman" title="Albert O. Hirschman">Albert Hirschman's</a> framework on 'voice' vs. 'exit' to discuss how emigration affects the politics within the countries of origin.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_theories">Historical theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Historical theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ravenstein">Ravenstein</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ravenstein">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Certain laws of <a href="/wiki/Social_sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Social sciences">social science</a> have been proposed to describe human migration. The following was a standard list after <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Georg_Ravenstein" title="Ernst Georg Ravenstein">Ravenstein's</a> (1834–1913) proposal in the 1880s. The laws are as follows:
</p>
<ol><li><i>every migration flow generates a return or counter migration.</i></li>
<li><i>the majority of migrants move a short distance.</i></li>
<li><i>migrants who move longer distances tend to choose big-city destinations.</i></li>
<li><i>urban residents are often less migratory than inhabitants of rural areas.</i></li>
<li><i>families are less likely to make international moves than young adults.</i></li>
<li><i>most migrants are adults.</i></li>
<li><i>large towns grow by migration rather than natural increase.</i></li>
<li><i>migration stage by stage.</i></li>
<li><i>urban rural difference.</i></li>
<li><i>migration and technology.</i></li>
<li><i>economic condition.</i></li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lee">Lee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Lee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lee's laws divide factors causing migrations into two groups of factors: push and pull factors. Push factors are things that are unfavourable about the area that one lives in, and pull factors are things that attract one to another area.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Push factors</b>
</p>
<ul><li>Not enough jobs</li>
<li>Few opportunities</li>
<li>Inadequate conditions</li>
<li>Desertification</li>
<li>Famine or drought</li>
<li>Political fear or persecution</li>
<li>Slavery or forced labor</li>
<li>Poor medical care</li>
<li>Loss of wealth</li>
<li>Natural disasters</li>
<li>Death threats</li>
<li>Desire for more political or religious freedom</li>
<li>Pollution</li>
<li>Poor housing</li>
<li>Landlord/tenant issues</li>
<li>Bullying</li>
<li>Mentallity</li>
<li>Discrimination</li>
<li>Poor chances of marrying</li>
<li>Condemned housing (radon gas, etc.)</li>
<li>War</li></ul>
<p><b>Pull factors</b>
</p>
<ul><li>Job opportunities</li>
<li>Better living conditions</li>
<li>The feeling of having more political or religious freedom</li>
<li>Enjoyment</li>
<li>Education</li>
<li>Better medical care</li>
<li>Attractive climates</li>
<li>Security</li>
<li>Family links</li>
<li>Industry</li>
<li>Better chances of marrying</li></ul>
<p>See also article by Gürkan Çelik, in <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20111023113817/http://www.turkishreview.org/tr/newsDetail_getNewsById.action?newsId=223107">Turkish Review: Turkey Pulls, The Netherlands Pushes?</a> An increasing number of Turks, the Netherlands’ largest ethnic minority, are beginning to return to Turkey, taking with them the education and skills they have acquired abroad, as the Netherlands faces challenges from economic difficulties, social tension and increasingly powerful far-right parties. At the same time Turkey’s political, social and economic conditions have been improving, making returning home all the more appealing for Turks at large. (pp.&#160;94–99)
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_cycles">Climate cycles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Climate cycles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The modern field of climate history suggests that the successive waves of Eurasian nomadic movement throughout history have had their origins in <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climatic cycles</a>, which have expanded or contracted pastureland in Central Asia, especially <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> and to its west the <a href="/wiki/Altai_mountains" class="mw-redirect" title="Altai mountains">Altai</a>. People were displaced from their home ground by other tribes trying to find land that could be grazed by essential flocks, each group pushing the next further to the south and west, into the highlands of <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pannonian_Plain" class="mw-redirect" title="Pannonian Plain">Pannonian Plain</a>, into <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">Mesopotamia</a>, or southwards, into the rich pastures of China. Bogumil Terminski uses the term "migratory domino effect" to describe this process in the context of <a href="/wiki/Sea_People" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea People">Sea People</a> invasion.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_models">Other models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Human_migration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Other models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><i>Migration occurs because individuals search for food, sex and security outside their usual habitation.</i><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>  Idyorough is of the view that towns and cities are a creation of the human struggle to obtain food, sex and security. To produce food, security and reproduction, human beings must, out of necessity, move out of their usual habitation and enter into indispensable social relationships that are cooperative or antagonistic.  Human beings also develop the tools and equipment to enable them to interact with nature to produce the desired food and security. The improved relationship (cooperative relationships) among human beings and improved technology further conditioned by the push and pull factors all interact together to cause or bring about migration and higher concentration of individuals into towns and cities.  The higher the technology of production of food and security and the higher the cooperative relationship among human beings in the production of food and security and in the reproduction of the human species, the higher would be the push and pull factors in the migration and concentration of human beings in towns and cities.  Countryside, towns and cities do not just exist but they do so to meet the human basic needs of food, security and the reproduction of the human species.  Therefore, migration occurs because individuals search for food, sex and security outside their usual habitation. Social services in the towns and cities are provided to meet these basic needs for human survival and pleasure.</li>
