<p><b>Manganese(VII) oxide</b> (manganese heptoxide) is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> Mn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>. This volatile liquid is highly reactive and more often discussed than intentionally prepared. It is a dangerous oxidizer and was first described in 1860.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_acid_anhydride" class="mw-redirect" title="Inorganic acid anhydride">acid anhydride</a> of <a href="/wiki/Permanganic_acid" title="Permanganic acid">permanganic acid</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese_heptoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Crystalline" class="mw-redirect" title="Crystalline">crystalline</a> form of this chemical compound is dark green. The liquid is green by reflected light and red by transmitted light. It is soluble in <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">carbon tetrachloride</a>, and decomposes when in contact with water. It melts at only 5.9&#160;°C, and sublimes at −10&#160;°C.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> These properties indicate a <a href="/wiki/Polarity_(chemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Polarity (chemistry)">nonpolar</a> molecular species, which is confirmed by its structure. The molecules consist of a pair of <a href="/wiki/Tetrahedra" class="mw-redirect" title="Tetrahedra">tetrahedra</a> that share a common vertex. The vertices are occupied by oxygen atoms and at the centers of the tetrahedra are the Mn(VII) centers. The connectivity is indicated by the formula O<sub>3</sub>Mn-O-MnO<sub>3</sub>. The terminal Mn−O distances are 1.585 Å and the bridging oxygen is 1.77&#160;Å distant from the two Mn atoms. The Mn−O−Mn angle is 120.7°.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It contains manganese in its highest oxidation state. This oxidation state, +7, is shared by <a href="/wiki/Permanganate" title="Permanganate">permanganates</a>, which are more stable compounds.
</p><p>Pyrosulfate, pyrophosphate, and dichromate adopt structures similar to that of Mn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>. Probably the most similar main group species is <a href="/wiki/Dichlorine_heptoxide" title="Dichlorine heptoxide">Cl<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub></a>. Focusing on comparisons within the transition metal series, <a href="/wiki/Technetium(VII)_oxide" title="Technetium(VII) oxide">Tc<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub></a> and Mn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> are structurally similar but the Tc−O−Tc angle is 180°. Solid <a href="/wiki/Rhenium(VII)_oxide" title="Rhenium(VII) oxide">Re<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub></a> is not molecular but consists of crosslinked Re centers with both tetrahedral and octahedral sites;<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> in the vapour phase it is molecular with a similar structure to Tc<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis_and_reactions">Synthesis and reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manganese_heptoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Synthesis and reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> arises as a dark green oil by the addition of concentrated <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub></a> to <a href="/wiki/Potassium_permanganate" title="Potassium permanganate">KMnO<sub>4</sub></a>. The reaction initially produces permanganic acid, HMnO<sub>4</sub> (structurally, HOMnO<sub>3</sub>), which is dehydrated by cold sulfuric acid to form its <a href="/wiki/Anhydride" class="mw-redirect" title="Anhydride">anhydride</a>, Mn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>.
</p>
<dl><dd>2 KMnO<sub>4</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> → Mn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O + 2 KHSO<sub>4</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Mn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> can react further with sulfuric acid to give the remarkable manganyl(VII) cation <span class="chemf nowrap">MnO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>, which is <a href="/wiki/Isoelectronicity" title="Isoelectronicity">isoelectronic</a> with <a href="/wiki/Chromium_trioxide" title="Chromium trioxide">CrO<sub>3</sub></a>
</p>
<dl><dd>Mn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> → 2 <span class="chemf nowrap">[MnO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span>]<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span>[HSO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>]<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:0.8em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sub></span></span> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Mn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> decomposes near room temperature, explosively so above 55&#160;°C. The explosion can be initiated by striking the sample or by its exposure to oxidizable organic compounds. The products are <a href="/wiki/Manganese_dioxide" title="Manganese dioxide">MnO<sub>2</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O<sub>2</sub></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">Ozone</a> is also produced, giving a strong smell to the substance. The ozone can spontaneously ignite a piece of paper impregnated with an alcohol solution. The production of manganese heptoxide is an undesirable byproduct in many situations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
