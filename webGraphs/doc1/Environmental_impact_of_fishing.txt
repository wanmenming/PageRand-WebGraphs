<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Environmental impact of fishing</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg/350px-Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg" width="350" height="237" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg/525px-Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg/700px-Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5433" data-file-height="3675" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fishing_down_the_food_web.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><div class="center" style="width:auto; margin-left:auto; margin-right:auto;"><a href="/wiki/Fishing_down_the_foodweb" class="mw-redirect" title="Fishing down the foodweb">Fishing down the foodweb</a></div></div></div></div>
<p>The <b>environmental impact of fishing</b> includes issues such as the availability of <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fisheries" class="mw-redirect" title="Fisheries">fisheries</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_management" title="Fisheries management">fisheries management</a>; as well as the impact of fishing on other elements of the environment, such as <a href="/wiki/By-catch" class="mw-redirect" title="By-catch">by-catch</a>. These issues are part of <a href="/wiki/Marine_conservation" title="Marine conservation">marine conservation</a>, and are addressed in <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_science" title="Fisheries science">fisheries science</a> programs. There is a growing gap between the supply of fish and demand, due in part to <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">world population</a> growth.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The journal <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i> published a four-year study in November 2006, which  predicted that, at prevailing trends, the world would run out of wild-caught <a href="/wiki/Seafood" title="Seafood">seafood</a> in 2048.   The scientists stated that the decline was a result of <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> and other environmental factors that were reducing the population of fisheries at the same time as their ecosystems were being annihilated. Yet again the analysis has met criticism  as being fundamentally flawed, and many fishery management officials, industry representatives and scientists challenge the findings, although the debate continues. Many countries, such as <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bahamas" class="mw-redirect" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, and international management bodies have taken steps to appropriately manage marine resources.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Reefs are also being destroyed by <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a> because of the huge nets that are dragged along the ocean floor while <a href="/wiki/Trawling" title="Trawling">trawling</a>. Many corals are being destroyed and as a consequence, the <a href="/wiki/Ecological_niche" title="Ecological niche">ecological niche</a> of many species is at stake.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_Marine_habitat">Effects on Marine habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Effects on Marine habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Blast_fishing" title="Blast fishing">Blast fishing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyanide_fishing" title="Cyanide fishing">Cyanide fishing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bottom_trawling" title="Bottom trawling">Bottom trawling</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ghost_net" title="Ghost net">Ghost net</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Turtlekill1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Turtlekill1.jpg/220px-Turtlekill1.jpg" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Turtlekill1.jpg/330px-Turtlekill1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Turtlekill1.jpg/440px-Turtlekill1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3587" data-file-height="2218" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Turtlekill1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A sea turtle killed by a boat propeller</div></div></div>
<p>Some fishing techniques cause habitat destruction.<sup id="cite_ref-:11_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Blast_fishing" title="Blast fishing">Blast fishing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyanide_fishing" title="Cyanide fishing">cyanide fishing</a>, which are illegal in many places, harm surrounding habitat.<sup id="cite_ref-:11_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Blast fishing refers to the practice of using explosives to capture fish.  Cyanide fishing refers to the practice of using cyanide to stun fish for collection.  These two practices are commonly used for the aquarium trade and the live fish food trade.<sup id="cite_ref-:11_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:11-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> These practices are destructive because they impact the habitat that the reef fish live on after the fish have been removed.  <a href="/wiki/Bottom_trawling" title="Bottom trawling">Bottom trawling</a>, the practice of pulling a <a href="/wiki/Fishing_net" title="Fishing net">fishing net</a> along the sea bottom behind <a href="/wiki/Commercial_trawler" class="mw-redirect" title="Commercial trawler">trawlers</a>, removes around 5 to 25% of an area's seabed life on a single run.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Most of the impacts are due to commercial fishing practices.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> A 2005 report of the <a href="/wiki/UN_Millennium_Project" class="mw-redirect" title="UN Millennium Project">UN Millennium Project</a>, commissioned by UN Secretary-General <a href="/wiki/Kofi_Annan" title="Kofi Annan">Kofi Annan</a>, recommended the elimination of bottom trawling on the high seas by 2006 to protect seamounts and other ecologically sensitive habitats. This was not done.
</p><p>In mid-October 2006, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> joined other world leaders calling for a moratorium on deep-sea <a href="/wiki/Trawling" title="Trawling">trawling</a>, a practice shown to often have harmful effects on sea habitat and, hence, on fish populations.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> No further action was taken (Divek). the sea animals aquatic ecosystem may also collapse due to the destruction in the food chain.
</p><p>Additionally, ghost fishing is a major threat due to capture fisheries.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ghost_net" title="Ghost net">Ghost fishing</a> occurs when a net, such as a gill net or trawl, is lost or discarded at sea and drifts within the oceans and can still act to capture marine organisms.  According to the <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">FAO</a> Code of Conduct for Responsible Fisheries, States should act to minimize the amount of lost and abandoned gear, and work to minimize ghost fishing.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overfishing">Overfishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overfishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">Overfishing</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">Overfishing</a> has also been widely reported due to increases in the volume of fishing hauls to feed a quickly growing number of consumers. This has led to the breakdown of some sea ecosystems and several fishing industries whose catch has been greatly diminished.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The extinction of many species has also been reported.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> According to a <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> estimate, over 70% of the world’s fish species are either fully exploited or depleted.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  According to the Secretary General of the 2002 World Summit on Sustainable Development, "Overfishing cannot continue, the depletion of fisheries poses a major threat to the food supply of millions of people."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The cover story of the May 15, 2003 issue of the science journal <a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)"><i>Nature</i></a> – with Dr. <a href="/wiki/Ransom_A._Myers" title="Ransom A. Myers">Ransom A. Myers</a>, an internationally prominent fisheries biologist (Dalhousie University, Halifax, Canada) as the lead author – was devoted to a summary of the scientific information.  The story asserted that, as compared with 1950 levels, only a remnant (in some instances, as little as 10%) of all large ocean-fish stocks are left in the seas.  These large ocean fish are the species at the top of the food chains (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tuna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cod" title="Cod">cod</a>, among others). This article was subsequently criticized as being fundamentally flawed, although much debate still exists (Walters 2003; Hampton et al. 2005; Maunder et al. 2006; Polacheck 2006;Sibert et al. 2006) and the majority of fisheries scientists now consider the results irrelevant with respect to large <a href="/wiki/Pelagic" class="mw-redirect" title="Pelagic">pelagics</a> (the open seas).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The fishing down the food web is something that occurs when overfishing arises. Once all larger fish are caught, the fisherman will start to fish the smaller individuals, which would lead to more fish needing to be caught to keep up with demand.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  This decreases fish populations, as well as genetic diversity of the species, making them more susceptible to disease, and less likely to adapt to their stressors and the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, catching smaller fish leads to breeding of smaller offspring, which can be problematic for fish. In many species, the smaller the female, the less fecund it is, impacting the fish population.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_disruption">Ecological disruption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ecological disruption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Over-fishing can result in the over-exploitation of marine ecosystem services.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Fishing can cause several negative physiological and psychological effects for fish populations including: increased stress levels and bodily injuries resulting from lodged fish hooks.<sup id="cite_ref-:52_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:52-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Often times, when this threshold is crossed, <a href="/wiki/Hysteresis" title="Hysteresis">hysteresis</a> may occur within the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> More specifically, some ecological disturbances observed within the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a> marine ecosystem resulted from a combination of over-fishing and various other related human activities which adversely affected the marine environment and ecosystem.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Ecological disruption can also occur due to the over-fishing of critical fish species such as the tilefish and grouper fish who can be referred to as <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_engineer" title="Ecosystem engineer">ecosystem-engineers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Fishing may disrupt <a href="/wiki/Food_web" title="Food web">food webs</a> by targeting specific, in-demand species. There might be too much fishing of prey species such as <a href="/wiki/Sardine" title="Sardine">sardines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anchovies" class="mw-redirect" title="Anchovies">anchovies</a>, thus reducing the food supply for the predators. Disrupting these types of wasp-waist species may have effects throughout the ecosystem.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> It may also cause the increase of prey species when the target fishes are predator species, such as <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">tuna</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bycatch">Bycatch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bycatch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bycatch" title="Bycatch">Bycatch</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bycatch" title="Bycatch">Bycatch</a> is the portion of the catch that is not the target species. These are either kept to be sold or discarded.  In some instances the discarded portion is known as <a href="/wiki/Discards" title="Discards">discards</a>. Even sports fisherman discard a lot of non-target and target fish on the bank while fishing.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marine_debris">Marine debris</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Marine debris">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Marine_debris" title="Marine debris">Marine debris</a></div>
<p>Recent research has shown that fishing debris such as nets, buoys, and lines, accounts for a majority of plastic debris found in the oceans,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> such as in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Pacific_garbage_patch" title="Great Pacific garbage patch">Great Pacific garbage patch</a>.  Similarly, fishing debris has been shown to be a major source of plastic debris found on the shores of Korea.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Marine life interacts with debris in two ways; either through <a href="/wiki/Plastic_pollution#Entanglement" title="Plastic pollution">entanglement</a> (where debris entangles or entraps animals), or ingestion of the debris (either intentionally or accidentally). Both are harmful to the animal.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Tracking garbage and monitoring the logistics of human waste disposal, especially waste materials primarily associated with fishing, is one method to reduce marine debris.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Using technological or mechanical innovations such as marine debris-clearing drones can further serve to reduce the amount of debris within oceans.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Marine debris consisting of old fishing nets or trawls can often be linked to phenomena such as ghost fishing; wherein the netting debris continues to entangle and capture fish and is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Ghost_net" title="Ghost net">ghost nets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> A study performed in southern Japan on octopus noted that there was an estimated mortality rate of 212,000 - 505,000 octopus killed per year within the area's fishing grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance_of_Recreational_Fishing_Impacts">Importance of Recreational Fishing Impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Importance of Recreational Fishing Impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Recreational_fishing" title="Recreational fishing">Recreational fishing</a></div>
<p>Recreational fishing is fishing done for sport or competition, whereas commercial fishing is catching seafood, often in mass quantities, for profit. Both can have different environmental impacts when it comes to fishing.
</p><p>Though many assume recreational fishing does not have a large impact on fish, it actually accounts for almost a quarter of the fish caught in the United States, many of those being commercially valuable fish.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Since recreational fishing is not nearly as regulated or monitored as commercial fishing, this is large section of environmental impact that needs to be taken into account.
</p><p>Recreational fishing has its biggest impact on marine debris, overfishing, and fish mortality. Release mortality in recreational fisheries is the same as the impacts of bycatch in commercial fisheries.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Countermeasures">Countermeasures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Countermeasures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fisheries_management_and_fish_farming">Fisheries management and fish farming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Fisheries management and fish farming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_management" title="Fisheries management">Fisheries management</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish_farming" title="Fish farming">Fish farming</a></div>
<p>One method to increase fish population numbers and reduce the severity of adverse environmental impacts and ecological disturbances is the utilization of traditional <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_management" title="Fisheries management">fisheries management</a> systems within fisheries.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Essentially, traditional fisheries management incorporates the aspects of fisheries management; however, the conservation efforts take into account concepts that place restrictions on the type of gear used and the allotment of permitted angling.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Traditional fisheries management also incorporates communities within its conservation efforts which often result in management scenarios where there is co-management conservation efforts led by communities.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ecosystem-based_management" title="Ecosystem-based management">Ecosystem-based management</a> of fisheries is another method used for fish conservation and impact remediation.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_32-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:9_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Instead of solely focusing conservation efforts on a single species of marine life, ecosystem-based management is used across various species of fish within an environment.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_32-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:9_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> To improve the adoption of these types of fisheries management, it is important to reduce barriers to entry for management scenarios in order to make these methods more accessible to fisheries globally.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_32-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many governments and intergovernmental bodies have implemented <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_management" title="Fisheries management">fisheries management</a> policies designed to curb the environmental impact of fishing.  Fishing conservation aims to control the human activities that may completely decrease a fish stock or washout an entire aquatic environment. These laws include the quotas on the total catch of particular species in a fishery, effort quotas (e.g., number of days at sea), the limits on the number of vessels allowed in specific areas, and the imposition of seasonal restrictions on fishing.
</p><p>In 2008 a large scale study of fisheries that used <a href="/wiki/Individual_transferable_quota" class="mw-redirect" title="Individual transferable quota">individual transferable quotas</a> and ones that didn't provided strong evidence that individual transferable quotas can help to prevent collapses and restore fisheries that appear to be in decline.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fish_farming" title="Fish farming">Fish farming</a> has been proposed as a more sustainable alternative to traditional capture of <a href="/wiki/Wild_fisheries" title="Wild fisheries">wild fish</a>. However, fish farming has been found to have negative impacts on nearby wild fish<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> and farming of predatory fish <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture_of_salmon#Impact_on_forage_fish" class="mw-redirect" title="Aquaculture of salmon">like salmon</a> can rely on fish feed that is based on <a href="/wiki/Fish_meal" title="Fish meal">fish meal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fish_oil" title="Fish oil">oil</a> from <a href="/wiki/Forage_fish" title="Forage fish">wild fish</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Alliance_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alliance-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marine_reserves">Marine reserves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Marine reserves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Marine_reserve" title="Marine reserve">Marine reserves</a> serve to foster both environmental protection and marine wildlife safety.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The reserves themselves are established via environmental protection plans or policies which designate a specific marine environment as protected.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Coral reefs are one of the many examples which involve the application of marine reserves in establishing marine protected areas.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> There have also been marine reserve initiatives located in the United States, <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_40-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> To mitigate the negative environmental impacts of fishing within marine environments, marine reserves are intended to create, enhance, and re-introduce biodiversity within the area.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_40-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:8_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> As a result, the primary benefits arising from the implementation of this type of management effort include positive impacts towards habitat protection and species conservation.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_40-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catch-and-release">Catch-and-release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_fishing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Catch-and-release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Based upon <a href="/wiki/Catch_and_release" title="Catch and release">catch-and-release</a> research, this method of mitigation involves several practices to reduce the negative environmental impacts of fishing that include: the duration, timing, and type of hook used during angling.<sup id="cite_ref-:52_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:52-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> To increase the effectiveness of catch and release fishing and mitigate its negative impacts, species-specific guidelines are required.<sup id="cite_ref-:52_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:52-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> These guidelines help tailor specific rules and regulations to specific species of fish in relation to their locations and mating and migration cycles.<sup id="cite_ref-:52_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:52-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> While catch-and-release fishing has been wildly used in recreational fishing, it is also beneficial for maintaining fish populations at a stable level for commercial fisheries to receive social and economic benefits.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Combining catch-and-release fishing with <a href="/wiki/Biotelemetry" title="Biotelemetry">biotelementry</a> data collection methods allows for researchers to study the biological effects of catch-and-release fishing on fish in order to better suit future conservation efforts and remedies.<sup id="cite_ref-:10_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The environmental impact of recreational fishing may be alleviated to some extent by <a href="/wiki/Catch_and_release" title="Catch and release">catch and release</a> fishing.
</p>
