<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Respiratory acidosis</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Respiratory acidosis</b> is a medical emergency in which decreased ventilation (<a href="/wiki/Hypoventilation" title="Hypoventilation">hypoventilation</a>) increases the concentration of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in the blood and decreases the blood's <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> (a condition generally called <a href="/wiki/Acidosis" title="Acidosis">acidosis</a>).
</p><p>Carbon dioxide is produced continuously as the body's cells respire, and this CO<sub>2</sub> will accumulate rapidly if the lungs do not adequately expel it through <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_alveolus" title="Pulmonary alveolus">alveolar</a> ventilation. Alveolar hypoventilation thus leads to an increased <a href="/wiki/PaCO2" class="mw-redirect" title="PaCO2"><i>Pa</i>CO<sub>2</sub></a> (a condition called <a href="/wiki/Hypercapnia" title="Hypercapnia">hypercapnia</a>). The increase in <i>Pa</i>CO<sub>2</sub> in turn decreases the HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>&#8722;</sup>/<i>Pa</i>CO<sub>2</sub> ratio and decreases pH.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_respiratory_acidosis">Types of respiratory acidosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Respiratory_acidosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of respiratory acidosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Respiratory acidosis can be acute or chronic. 
</p>
<ul><li>In <i>acute respiratory acidosis</i>, the <i>Pa</i>CO<sub>2</sub> is elevated above the upper limit of the reference range (over 6.3 kPa or 45&#160;mm Hg) with an accompanying acidemia (pH &lt;7.36).</li>
<li>In <i>chronic respiratory acidosis</i>, the <i>Pa</i>CO<sub>2</sub> is elevated above the upper limit of the reference range, with a normal blood pH (7.35 to 7.45) or near-normal pH secondary to <a href="/wiki/Renal_compensation" title="Renal compensation">renal compensation</a> and an elevated serum <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> (HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>&#8722;</sup> &gt;30&#160;mm Hg).</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Respiratory_acidosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acute">Acute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Respiratory_acidosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Acute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Acute respiratory acidosis occurs when an abrupt failure of ventilation occurs. This failure in ventilation may be caused by depression of the <a href="/wiki/Central_respiratory_center" class="mw-redirect" title="Central respiratory center">central respiratory center</a> by cerebral disease or drugs, inability to ventilate adequately due to <a href="/wiki/Neuromuscular_disease" title="Neuromuscular disease">neuromuscular disease</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Myasthenia_gravis" title="Myasthenia gravis">myasthenia gravis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amyotrophic_lateral_sclerosis" title="Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis">amyotrophic lateral sclerosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guillain%E2%80%93Barr%C3%A9_syndrome" title="Guillain–Barré syndrome">Guillain–Barré syndrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muscular_dystrophy" title="Muscular dystrophy">muscular dystrophy</a>), or airway obstruction related to asthma or chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) exacerbation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chronic">Chronic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Respiratory_acidosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chronic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Chronic respiratory acidosis may be secondary to many disorders, including <a href="/wiki/Chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disease" title="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease">COPD</a>. Hypoventilation in COPD involves multiple mechanisms, including decreased responsiveness to <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">hypoxia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hypercapnia" title="Hypercapnia">hypercapnia</a>, increased <a href="/wiki/Ventilation-perfusion_mismatch" class="mw-redirect" title="Ventilation-perfusion mismatch">ventilation-perfusion mismatch</a> leading to increased <a href="/wiki/Dead_space_ventilation" class="mw-redirect" title="Dead space ventilation">dead space ventilation</a>, and decreased <a href="/wiki/Diaphragm_(anatomy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Diaphragm (anatomy)">diaphragm</a> function secondary to fatigue and hyperinflation.
</p><p>Chronic respiratory acidosis also may be secondary to <a href="/wiki/Obesity_hypoventilation_syndrome" title="Obesity hypoventilation syndrome">obesity hypoventilation syndrome</a> (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Pickwickian_syndrome" class="mw-redirect" title="Pickwickian syndrome">Pickwickian syndrome</a>), neuromuscular disorders such as <a href="/wiki/Amyotrophic_lateral_sclerosis" title="Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis">amyotrophic lateral sclerosis</a>, and severe restrictive ventilatory defects as observed in <a href="/wiki/Interstitial_lung_disease" title="Interstitial lung disease">interstitial lung disease</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thoracic" class="mw-redirect" title="Thoracic">thoracic</a> deformities.
</p><p>Lung diseases that primarily cause abnormality in <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_gas_exchange" class="mw-redirect" title="Alveolar gas exchange">alveolar gas exchange</a> usually do not cause hypoventilation but tend to cause stimulation of ventilation and hypocapnia secondary to hypoxia. Hypercapnia only occurs if severe disease or respiratory muscle fatigue occurs.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiological_response">Physiological response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Respiratory_acidosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Physiological response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Respiratory_acidosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Metabolism rapidly generates a large quantity of <a href="/wiki/Volatile_acid" class="mw-redirect" title="Volatile acid">volatile acid</a> (H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Nonvolatile_acid" title="Nonvolatile acid">nonvolatile acid</a>. The metabolism of fats and carbohydrates leads to the formation of a large amount of CO<sub>2</sub>. The CO<sub>2</sub> combines with H<sub>2</sub>O to form <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> (H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>). The lungs normally excrete the volatile fraction through ventilation, and acid accumulation does not occur. A significant alteration in ventilation that affects elimination of CO<sub>2</sub> can cause a respiratory acid-base disorder. The <i>Pa</i>CO<sub>2</sub> is maintained within a range of 35–45&#160;mm Hg in normal states.
</p><p>Alveolar ventilation is under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_center" title="Respiratory center">respiratory center</a>, which is located in the <a href="/wiki/Pons" title="Pons">pons</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Medulla_oblongata" title="Medulla oblongata">medulla</a>. Ventilation is influenced and regulated by <a href="/wiki/Chemoreceptors" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemoreceptors">chemoreceptors</a> for <i>Pa</i>CO<sub>2</sub>, PaO<sub>2</sub>, and pH located in the brainstem, and in the <a href="/wiki/Aortic_and_carotid_bodies" class="mw-redirect" title="Aortic and carotid bodies">aortic and carotid bodies</a> as well as by neural impulses from lung <a href="/wiki/Mechanoreceptors" class="mw-redirect" title="Mechanoreceptors">stretch receptors</a> and impulses from the <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">cerebral cortex</a>. Failure of ventilation quickly increases the <i>Pa</i>CO<sub>2</sub>.
</p><p>In acute respiratory acidosis, compensation occurs in 2 steps. 
</p>
<ul><li>The initial response is cellular buffering (plasma protein buffers) that occurs over minutes to hours. Cellular buffering elevates plasma bicarbonate (HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>&#8722;</sup>) only slightly, approximately 1 mEq/L for each 10-mm Hg increase in <i>Pa</i>CO<sub>2</sub>.</li>
<li>The second step is <a href="/wiki/Renal" class="mw-redirect" title="Renal">renal</a> compensation that occurs over 3–5 days. With renal compensation, renal excretion of carbonic acid is increased and bicarbonate reabsorption is increased. For instance, <a href="/wiki/PEPCK" class="mw-redirect" title="PEPCK">PEPCK</a> is upregulated in <a href="/wiki/Renal_proximal_tubule_brush_border_cell" class="mw-redirect" title="Renal proximal tubule brush border cell">renal proximal tubule brush border cells</a>, in order to secrete more <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">NH<sub>3</sub></a> and thus to produce more <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>&#8722;</sup></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-boron858_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boron858-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Estimated_changes">Estimated changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Respiratory_acidosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Estimated changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In renal compensation, plasma bicarbonate rises 3.5 mEq/L for each increase of 10&#160;mm Hg in <a href="/wiki/PaCO2" class="mw-redirect" title="PaCO2"><i>Pa</i>CO<sub>2</sub></a>. The expected change in serum bicarbonate concentration in respiratory acidosis can be estimated as follows:
</p>
<ul><li>Acute respiratory acidosis: HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>&#8722;</sup> increases 1 mEq/L for each 10&#160;mm Hg rise in <i>Pa</i>CO<sub>2</sub>.</li>
<li>Chronic respiratory acidosis: HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>&#8722;</sup> rises 3.5 mEq/L for each 10&#160;mm Hg rise in <i>Pa</i>CO<sub>2</sub>.</li></ul>
<p>The expected change in pH with respiratory acidosis can be estimated with the following equations:
</p>
<ul><li>Acute respiratory acidosis: Change in pH = 0.08 X (40 &#8722; <a href="/wiki/PaCO2" class="mw-redirect" title="PaCO2"><i>Pa</i>CO<sub>2</sub></a>)</li>
<li>Chronic respiratory acidosis: Change in pH = 0.03 X (40 &#8722; <i>Pa</i>CO<sub>2</sub>)</li></ul>
<p>Respiratory acidosis does not have a great effect on <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">electrolyte</a> levels. Some small effects occur on calcium and potassium levels. Acidosis decreases binding of calcium to albumin and tends to increase serum ionized calcium levels. In addition, acidemia causes an extracellular shift of potassium, but respiratory acidosis rarely causes clinically significant <a href="/wiki/Hyperkalemia" title="Hyperkalemia">hyperkalemia</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Respiratory_acidosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Diagnoses can be done by doing an ABGA.
</p>
