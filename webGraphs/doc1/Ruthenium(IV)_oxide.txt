<p><b>Ruthenium(IV) oxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the formula <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">Ru</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O<sub>2</sub></a>.  This black solid is the most common oxide of <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium" title="Ruthenium">ruthenium</a>.  It is widely used as an <a href="/wiki/Electrocatalyst" title="Electrocatalyst">electrocatalyst</a> for producing chlorine, chlorine oxides, and O<sub>2</sub> catalyst is ruthenium(IV) oxide.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Like many dioxides, RuO<sub>2</sub> adopts the <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a> structure.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is usually prepared by oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Ruthenium_trichloride" class="mw-redirect" title="Ruthenium trichloride">ruthenium trichloride</a>.  Nearly stoichiometric single crystals of RuO<sub>2</sub> can be obtained by <a href="/wiki/Chemical_vapor_transport" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical vapor transport">chemical vapor transport</a>, using O<sub>2</sub> as the transport agent:<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>RuO<sub>2</sub>  +  O<sub>2</sub> ⇌  RuO<sub>4</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Films of RuO<sub>2</sub> can be prepared by <a href="/wiki/Chemical_vapor_deposition" title="Chemical vapor deposition">chemical vapor deposition</a> (CVD) from volatile ruthenium compounds.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  RuO<sub>2</sub> can also be prepared through <a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">electroplating</a> from a solution of ruthenium trichloride.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ruthenium (IV) oxide is being used as the main component in the catalyst of the Sumitomo-<a href="/wiki/Deacon_process" title="Deacon process">Deacon process</a> which produces <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> by the oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">hydrogen chloride</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>RuO<sub>2</sub> can be used as catalyst in many other situations.  Noteworthy reactions are the <a href="/wiki/Fischer-Tropsch_process" class="mw-redirect" title="Fischer-Tropsch process">Fischer-Tropsch process</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haber-Bosch_process" class="mw-redirect" title="Haber-Bosch process">Haber-Bosch process</a>, and various manifestations of <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cells" class="mw-redirect" title="Fuel cells">fuel cells</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_and_niche_applications">Potential and niche applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruthenium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Potential and niche applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>RuO<sub>2</sub> is extensively used for the coating of titanium anodes for the electrolytic production of chlorine and for the preparation of <a href="/wiki/Resistors" class="mw-redirect" title="Resistors">resistors</a> or <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuits" class="mw-redirect" title="Integrated circuits">integrated circuits</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Ruthenium oxide resistors can be used as sensitive thermometers in the temperature range .02 &lt; T &lt; 4 K. It can be also used as active material in supercapacitor because has very high charge transfer capability.  Ruthenium oxide has great capacity to store charge when used in aqueous solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Average capacities of ruthenium(IV) oxide have reached 650&#160;F/g when in H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> solution and annealed at temperatures lower than 200&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In attempts to optimise its capacitive properties, prior work has looked at the hydration of ruthenium oxide, its crystallinity and particle size.
</p>
