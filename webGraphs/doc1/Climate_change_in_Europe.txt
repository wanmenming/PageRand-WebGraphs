<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Climate change in Europe</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_in_the_European_Union" title="Climate change in the European Union">Climate change in the European Union</a></div>
<p><b>Climate change in Europe</b> describes the <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> related issues in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. This includes the climate politics, contribution in the <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> and the influence of the global warming in Europe. 
According to international climate experts global temperature rise should not exceed 2 <a href="/wiki/%C2%B0C" class="mw-redirect" title="°C">°C</a> to prevent the most dangerous consequences of the climate change.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It was also made essential to halve greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide by 2050 compared to the shocking 1990 levels. Other countries such as Brazil and China will also reduce emissions by between 80% and 95% by 2050. It is estimated that from the now living persons alive are 70% in the year 2050. Emission reduction means development and implementation of new energy technology solutions. Some people consider that the technology revolution has already started in Europe since the markets of the renewable technology have annually grown.<sup id="cite_ref-yle2008_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yle2008-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In only the last hundred years there has been a recorded increase in temperature of 1 degrees in Europe. Although this seems minuscule, it has had huge repercussions on the global economy and in agriculture. Furthermore, “the IPCC report expects an increase of the mean global temperature by 1.8◦C to 4.0◦C”
<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>European Union climate chief is <a href="/wiki/Connie_Hedegaard" title="Connie Hedegaard">Connie Hedegaard</a> since 10 February 2010.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. </p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Climate change affects both people and the environment in the world as well as in Europe. Human-induced climate change has the potential to alter the prevalence and severity of <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> like storms, floods, droughts, heat waves and cold waves. These extreme weather changes may increase the severity of diseases in animals as well as humans. The heat waves will increase the forest fires. Experts have warned that the climate change may increase the number of global climate refugees from 150 million in 2008 to 800 million in future. <a href="/wiki/Convention_Relating_to_the_Status_of_Refugees" title="Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees">International agreement of refugees</a> does not recognize the climate change refugees.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/2003_European_heat_wave" title="2003 European heat wave">The summer of 2003</a> was probably the <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">hottest</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> since at latest ad 1500, and unusually large numbers of heat-related deaths were reported in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>. According to <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> it is very likely that the heat wave was human induced by greenhouse gases.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/European_Environment_Agency" title="European Environment Agency">European Environment Agency</a> (2012) the average temperature over land in Europe in the last decade was 1.3 °C warmer than the pre-industrial level, which makes it the warmest decade on record. Exceptional melting in the Greenland ice sheet was recorded in the summer of 2012. Arctic sea ice extent and volume have been decreasing much faster than projected.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A study of future changes in flood, heat-waves, and drought impacts for 571 European cities, using climate model runs from the Coupled Model Inter-comparison Project Phase 5 (CMIP5) found that heat-wave days increase across all cities, but especially in southern Europe, whilst the greatest heatwave temperature increases are expected in central European cities. For the low impact scenario drought conditions intensify in southern European cities while river flooding worsens in northern European cities. However, the high impact scenario projects that most European cities will see increases in both drought and river flood risks. Over 100 cities are particularly vulnerable to two or more climate impacts.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In the absence of climate change, extreme <a href="/wiki/Heat_waves" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat waves">heat waves</a> in Europe would be expected to occur only once every several hundred years. In addition to hydrological changes, grain crops mature earlier at higher temperature, which may reduce the critical growth period and lead to lower grain yields The Russian heat wave in 2010 caused grain harvest down by 25 percent, government ban wheat exports, and losses were 1% of GDP. Russian heat wave 2010 estimate for deaths is 55,000.<sup id="cite_ref-WB2012_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WB2012-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice" class="mw-redirect" title="Arctic sea ice">Arctic sea ice</a> reached a record minimum in September 2012. It halved the area of ice covering the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> in summers over the last 30 years.<sup id="cite_ref-WB2012_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WB2012-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Emissions">Emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coal">Coal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Coal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Coal_in_Europe" title="Coal in Europe">Coal in Europe</a></div>
<p>The annual CO<sub>2</sub> emissions from coal were in the <a href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a> Europe during 2005-2008 on average equal to the year 2000. There are however country specific differences. During 1990-2008 emissions from coal were the highest in Europe in Russia, Germany, Poland, Ukraine and United Kingdom. Among the top 20 coal emission countries only four countries have increased their annual average emissions from coal during 2005-2008 compared to the year 1990, namely <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Turkey" title="Energy in Turkey">Turkey</a> (181%), <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Finland" title="Energy in Finland">Finland</a> ( 121%), <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Italy" title="Energy in Italy">Italy</a> (115%) and <a href="/wiki/Energy_in_Greece" title="Energy in Greece">Greece</a> (108%).
</p><p>Many Eastern European countries, including  East Germany, Russia, Poland, Ukraine, Slovakia, Estonia and Hungary, and also United Kingdom, have significantly declined their coal dependency from 1990 to 2000. However, the statistics of IEA gives no evidence of decline of coal dependency during 2000-2008 in Europe. Belgium is the only European country among the top 20 countries that have clearly declined their climate change emissions from coal during 2000-2008.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation">Legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is in place national legislation, international agreements and EU directives. The EU directive 2001/77/EU promotes renewable energy in electricity production. The climate sub programme will provide €864 million in co-financing for climate projects between 2014 and 2020.  Its main objectives are to contribute to the shift towards a low carbon and climate resilient economy and improve the development, implementation and enforcement of EU climate change policies and laws.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stern_report_2006">Stern report 2006</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Stern report 2006">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>British government and economist <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Stern" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicholas Stern">Nicholas Stern</a> published the <a href="/wiki/Stern_report" class="mw-redirect" title="Stern report">Stern report</a> in 2006. The Review states that climate change is the greatest and widest-ranging <a href="/wiki/Market_failure" title="Market failure">market failure</a> ever seen, presenting a unique challenge for economics. The Review provides prescriptions including <a href="/wiki/Ecotax" title="Ecotax">environmental taxes</a> to minimize the economic and social disruptions. The Stern Review's main conclusion is that the benefits of strong, early action on climate change far outweigh the costs of not acting.<sup id="cite_ref-shortes_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shortes-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The Review points to the potential impacts of climate change on water resources, food production, health, and the environment. According to the Review, without action, the overall costs of climate change will be equivalent to losing at least 5% of global <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> (GDP) each year, now and forever. Including a wider range of risks and impacts could increase this to 20% of GDP or more.
</p><p>No-one can predict the consequences of climate change with complete certainty; but we now know enough to understand the risks. The review leads to a simple conclusion: the benefits of strong, early action considerably outweigh the costs.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="EU_energy_plan_2008">EU energy plan 2008</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: EU energy plan 2008">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the end of 2008, the parliament of the EU approved the <a href="/wiki/Climate_and_energy" title="Climate and energy">climate and energy</a> plan including:<sup id="cite_ref-yle2008_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yle2008-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><br />
</p><p>- 20% emission cut of climate gases from 1990 to 2020<br />
</p><p>- 20% increase in the share of renewable energy from 1990 to 2020 <br />
</p><p>- 20% increase of the energy efficiency from 1990 to 2020.<br />
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paris_Climate_Agreement_2016"><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Climate_Agreement_2016&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Paris Climate Agreement 2016 (page does not exist)">Paris Climate Agreement 2016</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Paris Climate Agreement 2016">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On April 22, 2016 the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Agreement" title="Paris Agreement">Paris Climate Accords</a> were signed by all but three countries around the world.  The conference to talk about this document was held in Paris, France. This put Europe in the epicenter of talks about the environment and climate change.  The EU was the first major economy that decided to submit its intended contribution to the new agreement in March of 2015. The EU ratified the Paris Agreement on October 5th, 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  
</p><p>In these talks the countries agreed that they all had a long-term goal of keeping global warming to well below 2 degrees Celsius. They agreed that global emissions need to peak as soon as possible, and recognize that this will take longer for developing countries. On the subject of transparency the countries agreed that they would meet every five years to set ambitious goals, report their progress to the public and each other, and track progress for their long-term goals throughout a transparent and accountable system.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The countries recognized the importance of non-party stakeholders to be involved in this process. Cities, regions, and local authorities are encouraged to uphold and promote regional and international cooperation.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critics">Critics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_change_in_Europe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Critics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The critics include that European companies, like in other OECD countries, have <a href="/wiki/Low-cost_country_sourcing" title="Low-cost country sourcing">moved</a> the energy intensive, polluting and  <a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_gas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Climate gas (page does not exist)">climate gas</a> emitting industry to Asia and South America. In respect to climate change there are no harmless areas. Carbon emissions from all countries are equal. The agreements exclude significant factors like <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_aviation" title="Environmental impact of aviation">aviation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_tourism" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental impact of tourism">tourism</a>, the actual end consumption of energy and the history of emissions. Negotiations are country oriented but the economical interests are in conflict between the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_producer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Energy producer (page does not exist)">energy producers</a>, consumers and the environment.
</p>
