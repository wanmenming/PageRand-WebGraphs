<p><b>Niobium pentoxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> <a href="/wiki/Niobium" title="Niobium">Nb</a><sub>2</sub><a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">O</a><sub>5</sub>. It is a colourless insoluble solid that is fairly unreactive. It is the main precursor to all materials made of niobium, the dominant application being alloys, but other specialized applications include <a href="/wiki/Capacitor" title="Capacitor">capacitors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lithium_niobate" title="Lithium niobate">lithium niobate</a>, and optical glasses.<sup id="cite_ref-Cardarelli_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cardarelli-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It has many polymorphic forms all based largely on octahedrally coordinated niobium atoms.<sup id="cite_ref-Nico_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nico-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wells_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wells-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The polymorphs are identified with a variety of prefixes.<sup id="cite_ref-Nico_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nico-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wells_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wells-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The form most commonly encountered is monoclinic H-<a href="/wiki/Niobium" title="Niobium">Nb</a><sub>2</sub><a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">O</a><sub>5</sub> which has a complex structure, with a unit cell containing 28 niobium atoms and 70 oxygen,  where 27 of the niobium atoms are octahedrally coordinated and one tetrahedrally.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> There is an uncharacterised solid hydrate, Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>.<i>n</i>H<sub>2</sub>O, the so-called <b>niobic acid</b> (previously called <b>columbic acid</b>), which can be prepared by hydrolysis of a basic solution of niobium pentachloride or Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> dissolved in HF.<sup id="cite_ref-Bayot_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bayot-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is prepared by hydrolysis of alkali-metal niobates and alkoxides and the fluorides using base. Such ostensibly simple procedures afford hydrated oxides that are calcined.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_routes">Other routes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other routes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Given that Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is the most common and robust compound of niobium, many methods, both practical and esoteric, exist for its formation.  The oxide for example, arises when niobium metal is oxidised in air.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  The oxidation of niobium dioxide, NbO<sub>2</sub> in air forms the polymorph, L-Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Pure Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> can be prepared by hydrolysis of <a href="/wiki/Niobium_pentachloride" class="mw-redirect" title="Niobium pentachloride">NbCl<sub>5</sub></a>:<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 NbCl<sub>5</sub> + 5 H<sub>2</sub>O → Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> + 10 HCl</dd></dl>
<p>A method of production via <a href="/wiki/Sol-gel" class="mw-redirect" title="Sol-gel">sol-gel</a> techniques has been reported hydrolysing niobium alkoxides in the presence of acetic acid, followed by calcination of the gels to produce the polymorphic form, T-Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Nano-sized niobium pentoxide particles have been synthesised by LiH reduction of NbCl<sub>5</sub>, followed by aerial oxidation as part of a synthesis of nano structured niobates.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is attacked by HF and dissolves in fused alkali.<sup id="cite_ref-Bayot_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bayot-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduction_to_the_metal">Reduction to the metal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reduction to the metal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The conversion of Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is the main route for the industrial production of niobium metal.  In the 1980s, about 15,000,000&#160;kg of Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> were consumed annually for reduction to the metal.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The main method is reduction of this oxide with <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>:
</p>
<dl><dd>3 Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> + 10 Al   →   6 Nb + 5 Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>An alternative but less practiced route involves carbothermal reduction, which proceeds via reduction with carbon and forms the basis of the two stage Balke process:<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> + 7 C → 2 NbC + 5 CO (heated under vacuum at 1800&#160;°C)</dd>
<dd>5 NbC + Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>  → 7 Nb + 5 CO</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conversion_to_halides">Conversion to halides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Conversion to halides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many methods are known for conversion of Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> to the halides.  The main problem is incomplete reaction to give the oxyhalides.  In the laboratory, the conversion can be effected with thionyl chloride:<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<dl><dd>Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> + 5 SOCl<sub>2</sub>   →   2 NbCl<sub>5</sub> + 5 SO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> reacts with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">CCl<sub>4</sub></a> to give <a href="/wiki/Niobium_oxychloride" title="Niobium oxychloride">niobium oxychloride</a> NbOCl<sub>3</sub>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conversion_to_niobates">Conversion to niobates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conversion to niobates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Treating Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> with aqueous <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">NaOH</a> at 200&#160;°C can give crystalline sodium niobate, NaNbO<sub>3</sub> whereas the reaction with <a href="/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide" title="Potassium hydroxide">KOH</a> may yield soluble Lindqvist-type hexaniobates, <span class="chemf nowrap">Nb<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">6</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">8−</sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">19</sub></span></span>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lithium_niobate" title="Lithium niobate">Lithium niobates</a> such as LiNbO<sub>3</sub> and Li<sub>3</sub>NbO<sub>4</sub> can be prepared by reaction <a href="/wiki/Lithium_carbonate" title="Lithium carbonate">lithium carbonate</a> and Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Li3NbO4_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Li3NbO4-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conversion_to_reduced_niobium_oxides">Conversion to reduced niobium oxides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Conversion to reduced niobium oxides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>High temperature reduction with H<sub>2</sub> gives NbO<sub>2</sub>:<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> + H<sub>2</sub> → 2 NbO<sub>2</sub> +  H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Niobium monooxide arises from a comproportionation using an arc-furnace:<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> + 3Nb →  5 NbO</dd></dl>
<p>The burgundy-coloured niobium(III) oxide, one of the first superconducting oxides, can be prepared again by an comproportionation:<sup id="cite_ref-Li3NbO4_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Li3NbO4-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Li<sub>3</sub>NbO<sub>4</sub> + 2 NbO →  3 LiNbO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Niobium_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Niobium pentoxide is used mainly in the production of niobium metal,<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> but specialized applications exist for <a href="/wiki/Lithium_niobate" title="Lithium niobate">lithium niobate</a> and as a component of optical glass.<sup id="cite_ref-Cardarelli_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cardarelli-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
