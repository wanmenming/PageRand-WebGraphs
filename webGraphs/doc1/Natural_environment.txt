<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Natural environment</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">All living and non-living things occurring naturally, generally on Earth</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For the biology term, see <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Environment (biophysical)">Environment (biophysical)</a>. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Environment_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Environment (disambiguation)">Environment</a>.</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Natural force" redirects here. It is not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Natural_Force" title="Natural Force">Natural Force</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hopetoun_falls.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Hopetoun_falls.jpg/220px-Hopetoun_falls.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Hopetoun_falls.jpg/330px-Hopetoun_falls.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Hopetoun_falls.jpg/440px-Hopetoun_falls.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hopetoun_falls.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Land_management" title="Land management">Land management</a> has preserved the natural characteristics of <a href="/wiki/Hopetoun_Falls" title="Hopetoun Falls">Hopetoun Falls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> while allowing ample access for visitors.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sahara_satellite_hires.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Sahara_satellite_hires.jpg/220px-Sahara_satellite_hires.jpg" width="220" height="123" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Sahara_satellite_hires.jpg/330px-Sahara_satellite_hires.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Sahara_satellite_hires.jpg/440px-Sahara_satellite_hires.jpg 2x" data-file-width="7432" data-file-height="4168" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sahara_satellite_hires.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Satellite image of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara desert</a>; the world's largest hot desert and third-largest desert after the <a href="/wiki/Polar_desert" title="Polar desert">polar deserts</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>natural environment</b> encompasses all <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">living</a> and non-living things occurring <a href="/wiki/Nature" title="Nature">naturally</a>, meaning in this case not <a href="/wiki/Artificiality" title="Artificiality">artificial</a>. The term is most often applied to the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> or some parts of Earth. This environment encompasses the interaction of all living <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>, weather and natural resources that affect human survival and economic activity.
<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
The concept of the <i>natural environment</i> can be distinguished as components:
</p>
<ul><li>Complete <a href="/wiki/Ecological_units" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological units">ecological units</a> that function as natural systems without massive civilized human intervention, including all vegetation, <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Natural_phenomenon" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural phenomenon">natural phenomena</a> that occur within their boundaries and their nature.</li>
<li>Universal <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_phenomena" class="mw-redirect" title="Physical phenomena">physical phenomena</a> that lack clear-cut boundaries, such as air, water, and climate, as well as <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">electric charge</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Magnetism" title="Magnetism">magnetism</a>, not originating from civilized human actions</li></ul>
<p>In contrast to the natural environment is the <a href="/wiki/Built_environment" title="Built environment">built environment</a>. In such areas where man has fundamentally transformed landscapes such as urban settings and agricultural <a href="/wiki/Land_development" title="Land development">land conversion</a>, the natural environment is greatly modified into a simplified human environment.  Even acts which seem less extreme, such as building a mud <a href="/wiki/Hut" title="Hut">hut</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_system" title="Photovoltaic system">photovoltaic system</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a>, the modified environment becomes an artificial one.  Though many animals build things to provide a better environment for themselves, they are not human, hence <a href="/wiki/Beaver_dam" title="Beaver dam">beaver dams,</a> and the works of <a href="/wiki/Mound-building_termites" title="Mound-building termites">Mound-building termites</a>, are thought of as natural.
</p><p>People seldom find <i>absolutely natural</i> environments on Earth, and naturalness usually varies in a continuum, from  100% natural in one extreme to 0% natural in the other. More precisely, we can consider the different aspects or components of an environment, and see that their degree of naturalness is not uniform.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> If, for instance, in an agricultural field, the <a href="/wiki/Mineralogy" title="Mineralogy">mineralogic composition</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Soil_structure" title="Soil structure">structure</a> of its soil are similar to those of an undisturbed forest soil, but the structure is quite different.
</p><p><i>Natural environment</i> is often used as a synonym for <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitat</a>. For instance, when we say that the natural environment of giraffes is the <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Volcano_q.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Volcano_q.jpg/220px-Volcano_q.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Volcano_q.jpg/330px-Volcano_q.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Volcano_q.jpg/440px-Volcano_q.jpg 2x" data-file-width="678" data-file-height="449" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Volcano_q.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> fissure and <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">lava</a> channel.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">Earth science</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jordens_inre-numbers.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Jordens_inre-numbers.svg/220px-Jordens_inre-numbers.svg.png" width="220" height="209" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Jordens_inre-numbers.svg/330px-Jordens_inre-numbers.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Jordens_inre-numbers.svg/440px-Jordens_inre-numbers.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="875" data-file-height="833" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jordens_inre-numbers.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s layered structure. (1) inner core; (2) outer core; (3) lower mantle; (4) upper mantle; (5) lithosphere; (6) crust</div></div></div>
<p>Earth science generally recognizes 4 spheres, the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a><sup id="cite_ref-wheeling_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wheeling-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> as correspondent to <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Air" class="mw-redirect" title="Air">air</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a> respectively. Some scientists include, as part of the spheres of the Earth, the <a href="/wiki/Cryosphere" title="Cryosphere">cryosphere</a> (corresponding to <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>) as a distinct portion of the hydrosphere, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Pedosphere" title="Pedosphere">pedosphere</a> (corresponding to <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>) as an active and intermixed sphere. <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">Earth science</a> (also known as geoscience, the geosciences or the Earth Sciences), is an all-embracing term for the <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">sciences</a> related to the planet <a href="/wiki/Earth_(planet)" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth (planet)">Earth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> There are four major <a href="/wiki/Discipline" title="Discipline">disciplines</a> in earth sciences, namely <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">geophysics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">geodesy</a>. These major disciplines use <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chronology" title="Chronology">chronology</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> to build a qualitative and quantitative understanding of the principal areas or <i><a href="/wiki/Outline_of_Earth_sciences#Earth&#39;s_spheres" title="Outline of Earth sciences">spheres</a></i> of Earth.
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geological_activity">Geological activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geological activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">Geology</a></div>
<p>The Earth's <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">crust</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>, is the outermost solid surface of the planet and is chemically and mechanically different from underlying <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle</a>. It has been generated greatly by <a href="/wiki/Igneous" class="mw-redirect" title="Igneous">igneous</a> processes in which <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">magma</a>  cools and solidifies to form solid rock. Beneath the lithosphere lies the mantle which is heated by the <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">decay</a> of <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_element" class="mw-redirect" title="Radioactive element">radioactive elements</a>. The mantle though solid is in a state of <a href="/wiki/Rheid" title="Rheid">rheic</a> <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a>. This convection process causes the lithospheric plates to move, albeit slowly. The resulting process is known as <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">plate tectonics</a>. <a href="/wiki/Volcanoes" class="mw-redirect" title="Volcanoes">Volcanoes</a> result primarily from the melting of <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">subducted</a> crust material or of rising mantle at <a href="/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge" title="Mid-ocean ridge">mid-ocean ridges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mantle_plume" title="Mantle plume">mantle plumes</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_on_Earth">Water on Earth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Water on Earth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Blue_Linckia_Starfish.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Blue_Linckia_Starfish.JPG/120px-Blue_Linckia_Starfish.JPG" width="120" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Blue_Linckia_Starfish.JPG/180px-Blue_Linckia_Starfish.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Blue_Linckia_Starfish.JPG/240px-Blue_Linckia_Starfish.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="1600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Blue_Linckia_Starfish.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center><a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">Coral reefs</a> have significant marine <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>.</center></div></div></div>
<p>Most water is found in one or another natural kind of <a href="/wiki/Body_of_water" title="Body of water">body of water</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceans">Oceans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oceans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">Ocean</a></div>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a> is a major body of <a href="/wiki/Seawater" title="Seawater">saline water</a>, and a component of the hydrosphere. Approximately 71% of the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Surface_of_the_Earth" class="mw-redirect" title="Surface of the Earth">surface</a> (an area of some 362 million square kilometers) is covered by ocean, a <a href="/wiki/World_Ocean" title="World Ocean">continuous body of water</a> that is customarily divided into several principal oceans and smaller <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">seas</a>. More than half of this area is over 3,000 meters (9,800&#160;ft) deep. Average oceanic <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> is around 35 <a href="/wiki/Parts_per_thousand" class="mw-redirect" title="Parts per thousand">parts per thousand</a> (ppt) (3.5%), and nearly all seawater has a salinity in the range of 30 to 38 ppt. Though generally recognized as several 'separate' oceans, these waters comprise one global, interconnected body of salt water often referred to as the <a href="/wiki/World_Ocean" title="World Ocean">World Ocean</a> or global ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-UNAoO_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNAoO-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  The deep <a href="/wiki/Seabed" title="Seabed">seabeds</a> are more than half the Earth's surface, and are among the least-modified natural environments.  The major oceanic divisions are defined in part by the <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">continents</a>, various <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelagos</a>, and other criteria: these divisions are (in descending order of size) the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a>.
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rivers">Rivers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rivers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ColumbiarivergorgeJRH.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/ColumbiarivergorgeJRH.jpg/220px-ColumbiarivergorgeJRH.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/ColumbiarivergorgeJRH.jpg/330px-ColumbiarivergorgeJRH.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/ColumbiarivergorgeJRH.jpg/440px-ColumbiarivergorgeJRH.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ColumbiarivergorgeJRH.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center>The <a href="/wiki/Columbia_River" title="Columbia River">Columbia River</a>, along the border of the U.S. states of <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>.</center></div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">River</a></div>
<p>A river is a natural <a href="/wiki/Watercourse" title="Watercourse">watercourse</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> usually <a href="/wiki/Freshwater" class="mw-redirect" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a>, flowing toward an <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">sea</a> or another river.  A few rivers simply flow into the ground and dry up completely before reaching another body of water. 
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hawaii_Creek.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e4/Hawaii_Creek.jpg/220px-Hawaii_Creek.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e4/Hawaii_Creek.jpg/330px-Hawaii_Creek.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e4/Hawaii_Creek.jpg/440px-Hawaii_Creek.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hawaii_Creek.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rocky stream in the U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a></div></div></div>
<p>The water in a river is usually in a <a href="/wiki/Channel_(geography)" title="Channel (geography)">channel</a>, made up of a <a href="/wiki/Stream_bed" title="Stream bed">stream bed</a> between <a href="/wiki/Stream_bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Stream bank">banks</a>. In larger rivers there is also a wider <a href="/wiki/Floodplain" title="Floodplain">floodplain</a> shaped by waters over-topping the channel. Flood plains may be very wide in relation to the size of the river channel. Rivers are a part of the <a href="/wiki/Hydrological_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrological cycle">hydrological cycle</a>. Water within a river is generally collected from <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> through <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">surface runoff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Groundwater_recharge" title="Groundwater recharge">groundwater recharge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spring_(hydrosphere)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spring (hydrosphere)">springs</a>, and the release of water stored in <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a> and snowpacks.
</p><p>Small rivers may also be termed by several other names, including <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">stream</a>, creek and brook.  Their <a href="/wiki/Current_(stream)" title="Current (stream)">current</a> is confined within a <a href="/wiki/Stream_bed" title="Stream bed">bed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stream_bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Stream bank">stream banks</a>.  Streams play an important <a href="/wiki/Wildlife_corridor" title="Wildlife corridor">corridor</a> role in connecting <a href="/wiki/Habitat_fragmentation" title="Habitat fragmentation">fragmented habitats</a> and thus in conserving <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>. The study of streams and waterways in general is known as <i>surface <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a>.</i><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">Stream</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lakes">Lakes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lakes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:LAGO_LACAR.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/LAGO_LACAR.JPG/220px-LAGO_LACAR.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/LAGO_LACAR.JPG/330px-LAGO_LACAR.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/LAGO_LACAR.JPG/440px-LAGO_LACAR.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:LAGO_LACAR.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/L%C3%A1car_Lake" title="Lácar Lake">Lácar Lake</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacial</a> origin, in the <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Argentina" title="Provinces of Argentina">province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Neuqu%C3%A9n_Province" title="Neuquén Province">Neuquén</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">Lake</a></div>
<p>A lake (from Latin <i>lacus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Terrain_feature" class="mw-redirect" title="Terrain feature">terrain feature</a>, a body of water that is localized to the bottom of <a href="/wiki/Depression_(geology)" title="Depression (geology)">basin</a>.  A body of water is considered a lake when it is inland, is not part of an <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>, and is larger and deeper than a <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">pond</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Everglades_Park_swamp.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Everglades_Park_swamp.JPG/220px-Everglades_Park_swamp.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Everglades_Park_swamp.JPG/330px-Everglades_Park_swamp.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Everglades_Park_swamp.JPG/440px-Everglades_Park_swamp.JPG 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Everglades_Park_swamp.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Swamp" title="Swamp">swamp</a> area in <a href="/wiki/Everglades_National_Park" title="Everglades National Park">Everglades National Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, <a href="/wiki/US" class="mw-redirect" title="US">US</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Natural lakes on Earth are generally found in <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountainous</a> areas, <a href="/wiki/Rift_zone" title="Rift zone">rift zones</a>, and areas with ongoing or recent <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciation</a>. Other lakes are found in <a href="/wiki/Endorheic_basin" title="Endorheic basin">endorheic basins</a> or along the courses of mature rivers. In some parts of the world, there are many lakes because of chaotic drainage patterns left over from the last <a href="/wiki/Ice_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Ice Age">Ice Age</a>. All lakes are temporary over geologic time scales, as they will slowly fill in with sediments or spill out of the basin containing them.
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ponds">Ponds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ponds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pond" title="Pond">Pond</a></div>
<p>A pond is a <a href="/wiki/Body_of_water" title="Body of water">body</a> of <a href="/wiki/Water_stagnation" title="Water stagnation">standing water</a>, either natural or man-made, that is usually smaller than a <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a>. A wide variety of man-made bodies of water are classified as ponds, including <a href="/wiki/Water_garden" title="Water garden">water gardens</a> designed for aesthetic ornamentation, <a href="/wiki/Fish_pond" title="Fish pond">fish ponds</a> designed for commercial fish breeding, and <a href="/wiki/Solar_pond" title="Solar pond">solar ponds</a> designed to store thermal energy. Ponds and lakes are distinguished from streams by their <a href="/wiki/Flow_velocity" title="Flow velocity">current speed</a>. While currents in streams are easily observed, ponds and lakes possess thermally driven micro-currents and moderate wind driven currents. These features distinguish a pond from many other aquatic terrain features, such as <a href="/wiki/Stream_pool" title="Stream pool">stream pools</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tide_pool" title="Tide pool">tide pools</a>.
</p>
<h2><span id="Atmosphere.2C_climate_and_weather"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmosphere,_climate_and_weather">Atmosphere, climate and weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Atmosphere, climate and weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Top_of_Atmosphere.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Top_of_Atmosphere.jpg/220px-Top_of_Atmosphere.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Top_of_Atmosphere.jpg/330px-Top_of_Atmosphere.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Top_of_Atmosphere.jpg/440px-Top_of_Atmosphere.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3027" data-file-height="2010" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Top_of_Atmosphere.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Atmospheric gases <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">scatter blue light more than other wavelengths</a>, creating a blue <a href="/wiki/Halo_(optical_phenomenon)" title="Halo (optical phenomenon)">halo</a> when seen from space.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Troposphere_CIMG1853.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Troposphere_CIMG1853.JPG/220px-Troposphere_CIMG1853.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Troposphere_CIMG1853.JPG/330px-Troposphere_CIMG1853.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Troposphere_CIMG1853.JPG/440px-Troposphere_CIMG1853.JPG 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Troposphere_CIMG1853.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A view of Earth's <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> from an <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">airplane</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lightnings_sequence_2_animation-wcag.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/67/Lightnings_sequence_2_animation-wcag.gif/220px-Lightnings_sequence_2_animation-wcag.gif" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/6/67/Lightnings_sequence_2_animation-wcag.gif 1.5x" data-file-width="317" data-file-height="239" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lightnings_sequence_2_animation-wcag.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">Lightning</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth&#39;s atmosphere">atmospheric</a> discharge of <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Thunder" title="Thunder">thunder</a>, which occurs during <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> and certain other natural conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-noaa1_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-noaa1-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The atmosphere of the Earth serves as a key factor in sustaining the planetary ecosystem. The thin layer of <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a> that envelops the Earth is held in place by the planet's gravity. Dry <a href="/wiki/Air" class="mw-redirect" title="Air">air</a> consists of 78% <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a>, 21% <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, 1% <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Inert_gases" class="mw-redirect" title="Inert gases">inert gases</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. The remaining gases are often referred to as trace gases,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> among which are the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> such as water vapor, carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, and ozone. Filtered air includes trace amounts of many other <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compounds</a>. Air also contains a variable amount of <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(chemistry)" title="Suspension (chemistry)">suspensions</a> of water droplets and <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> crystals seen as <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a>. Many natural substances may be present in tiny amounts in an unfiltered air sample, including <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spores" class="mw-redirect" title="Spores">spores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_spray" title="Sea spray">sea spray</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">volcanic ash</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Meteoroid" title="Meteoroid">meteoroids</a>. Various industrial <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">pollutants</a> also may be present, such as <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> (elementary or in compounds), <a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">fluorine</a> compounds, elemental <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sulphur" class="mw-redirect" title="Sulphur">sulphur</a> compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Sulphur_dioxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Sulphur dioxide">sulphur dioxide</a> [SO<sub>2</sub>].
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a> of the Earth's atmosphere plays an important role in depleting the amount of <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> (UV) radiation that reaches the surface. As <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> is readily damaged by UV light, this serves to protect life at the surface. The atmosphere also retains heat during the night, thereby reducing the daily temperature extremes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Layers_of_the_Atmosphere">Layers of the Atmosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Layers of the Atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth&#39;s atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Principal_layers">Principal layers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Principal layers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Earth's atmosphere can be divided into five main layers. These layers are mainly determined by whether temperature increases or decreases with altitude. From highest to lowest, these layers are:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">Exosphere</a>: The outermost layer of Earth's atmosphere extends from the exobase upward, mainly composed of <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">helium</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">Thermosphere</a>: The top of the thermosphere is the bottom of the exosphere, called the <a href="/wiki/Exobase" class="mw-redirect" title="Exobase">exobase</a>. Its height varies with solar activity and ranges from about 350–800&#160;km (220–500&#160;mi; 1,150,000–2,620,000&#160;ft). The <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> orbits in this layer, between 320 and 380&#160;km (200 and 240&#160;mi).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mesosphere" title="Mesosphere">Mesosphere</a>: The mesosphere extends from the stratopause to 80–85&#160;km (50–53&#160;mi; 262,000–279,000&#160;ft). It is the layer where most <a href="/wiki/Meteor" class="mw-redirect" title="Meteor">meteors</a> burn up upon entering the atmosphere.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">Stratosphere</a>: The stratosphere extends from the tropopause to about 51&#160;km (32&#160;mi; 167,000&#160;ft). The <a href="/wiki/Stratopause" title="Stratopause">stratopause</a>, which is the boundary between the stratosphere and mesosphere, typically is at 50 to 55&#160;km (31 to 34&#160;mi; 164,000 to 180,000&#160;ft).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">Troposphere</a>: The troposphere begins at the surface and extends to between 7&#160;km (23,000&#160;ft) at the poles and 17&#160;km (56,000&#160;ft) at the equator, with some variation due to weather. The troposphere is mostly heated by transfer of energy from the surface, so on average the lowest part of the troposphere is warmest and temperature decreases with altitude. The <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a> is the boundary between the troposphere and stratosphere.</li></ul>
<dl><dt>Other layers</dt></dl>
<p>Within the five principal layers determined by temperature are several layers determined by other properties.
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a> is contained within the stratosphere. It is mainly located in the lower portion of the stratosphere from about 15–35&#160;km (9.3–21.7&#160;mi; 49,000–115,000&#160;ft), though the thickness varies seasonally and geographically. About 90% of the ozone in our atmosphere is contained in the stratosphere.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">ionosphere</a>, the part of the atmosphere that is ionized by solar radiation, stretches from 50 to 1,000&#160;km (31 to 621&#160;mi; 160,000 to 3,280,000&#160;ft) and typically overlaps both the exosphere and the thermosphere. It forms the inner edge of the magnetosphere.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Homosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Homosphere">homosphere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heterosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Heterosphere">heterosphere</a>: The homosphere includes the troposphere, stratosphere, and mesosphere. The upper part of the heterosphere is composed almost completely of hydrogen, the lightest element.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Planetary_boundary_layer" title="Planetary boundary layer">planetary boundary layer</a> is the part of the troposphere that is nearest the Earth's surface and is directly affected by it, mainly through <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulent diffusion</a>.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_global_warming">Effects of global warming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Effects of global warming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gletscherschmelze.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Gletscherschmelze.jpg/220px-Gletscherschmelze.jpg" width="220" height="112" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Gletscherschmelze.jpg/330px-Gletscherschmelze.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Gletscherschmelze.jpg/440px-Gletscherschmelze.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="519" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gletscherschmelze.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Retreat of glaciers since 1850 of <a href="/wiki/Aletsch_Glacier" title="Aletsch Glacier">Aletsch Glacier</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Alps" title="Swiss Alps">Swiss Alps</a> (situation in 1979, 1991 and 2002), due to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">Effects of global warming</a></div>
<p>The potential dangers of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> are being increasingly studied by a wide global consortium of scientists. These scientists are increasingly concerned about the potential long-term effects of global warming on our natural environment and on the planet. Of particular concern is how <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> and global warming caused by <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a>, or human-made releases of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>, most notably <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, can act interactively, and have adverse effects upon the planet, its natural environment and humans' existence. It is clear the planet is warming, and warming rapidly.–This warming is also responsible for the extinction of natural habitats, which in turn leads to a reduction in wildlife population.The most recent report from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (the group of the leading climate scientists in the world) concluded that the earth will warm anywhere from 2.7 to almost 11 degrees Fahrenheit (1.5 to 6 degrees Celsius) between 1990 and 2100.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
Efforts have been increasingly focused on the <a href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitigation of global warming">mitigation</a> of greenhouse gases that are causing climatic changes, on <a href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Adaptation to global warming">developing adaptative strategies</a> to global warming, to assist humans, other animal, and plant species, ecosystems, regions and <a href="/wiki/Nation" title="Nation">nations</a> in adjusting to the <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">effects of global warming</a>. Some examples of recent collaboration to address climate change and global warming include:
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aletschgletsjer_Zwitserland.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Aletschgletsjer_Zwitserland.JPG/220px-Aletschgletsjer_Zwitserland.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Aletschgletsjer_Zwitserland.JPG/330px-Aletschgletsjer_Zwitserland.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Aletschgletsjer_Zwitserland.JPG/440px-Aletschgletsjer_Zwitserland.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aletschgletsjer_Zwitserland.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Another view of the <a href="/wiki/Aletsch_Glacier" title="Aletsch Glacier">Aletsch Glacier</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Alps" title="Swiss Alps">Swiss Alps</a> and because of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> it has been decreasing</div></div></div>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Framework_Convention_on_Climate_Change" title="United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change">United Nations Framework Convention Treaty</a> and convention on Climate Change, to stabilize greenhouse gas concentrations in the atmosphere at a level that would <a href="/wiki/Avoiding_dangerous_climate_change" class="mw-redirect" title="Avoiding dangerous climate change">prevent dangerous anthropogenic interference with the climate system</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>, which is the protocol to the international Framework Convention on Climate Change treaty, again with the objective of reducing greenhouse gases in an effort to prevent anthropogenic climate change.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Western_Climate_Initiative" title="Western Climate Initiative">Western Climate Initiative</a>, to identify, evaluate, and implement collective and cooperative ways to reduce greenhouse gases in the region, focusing on a market-based cap-and-trade system.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>A significantly profound challenge is to identify the natural environmental dynamics in contrast to environmental changes not within natural variances. A common solution is to adapt a static view neglecting natural variances to exist. Methodologically, this view could be defended when looking at processes which change slowly and short time series, while the problem arrives when fast processes turns essential in the object of the study.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ClimateMap_World.png" class="image"><img alt="Map of world dividing climate zones, largely influenced by latitude. The zones, going from the equator upward (and downward) are Tropical, Dry, Moderate, Continental and Polar. There are subzones within these zones." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/ClimateMap_World.png/220px-ClimateMap_World.png" width="220" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/ClimateMap_World.png/330px-ClimateMap_World.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/ClimateMap_World.png/440px-ClimateMap_World.png 2x" data-file-width="727" data-file-height="470" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ClimateMap_World.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center>Worldwide <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> classifications map</center></div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">Climate</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">Climate</a> looks at the statistics of <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rainfall" class="mw-redirect" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a>, atmospheric particle count and  other <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological</a> elements in a given region over long periods of time.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">Weather</a>, on the other hand, is the present condition of these same elements over periods up to two weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Climates can be <a href="/wiki/Climate_classification" title="Climate classification">classified</a> according to the average and typical ranges of different variables, most commonly temperature and precipitation. The most commonly used classification scheme is the one originally developed by <a href="/wiki/Wladimir_K%C3%B6ppen" title="Wladimir Köppen">Wladimir Köppen</a>. The Thornthwaite system,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> in use since 1948, uses <a href="/wiki/Evapotranspiration" title="Evapotranspiration">evapotranspiration</a> as well as temperature and precipitation information to study animal species diversity and the potential impacts of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate changes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weather">Weather</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Weather">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rainbow_over_Bristol.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Rainbow_over_Bristol.jpg/220px-Rainbow_over_Bristol.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Rainbow_over_Bristol.jpg/330px-Rainbow_over_Bristol.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Rainbow_over_Bristol.jpg/440px-Rainbow_over_Bristol.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1229" data-file-height="922" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rainbow_over_Bristol.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Rainbow" title="Rainbow">Rainbows</a> are <a href="/wiki/Optics" title="Optics">optical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological</a> phenomenon that causes a <a href="/wiki/Optical_spectrum" class="mw-redirect" title="Optical spectrum">spectrum</a> of <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">light</a> to appear in the sky when the Sun shines onto droplets of moisture in the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth&#39;s atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a>.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">Weather</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">Weather</a> is a set of all the <a href="/wiki/Phenomenon" title="Phenomenon">phenomena</a> occurring in a given <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric</a> area at a given <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Most weather phenomena occur in the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-trop_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trop-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> just below the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>. Weather refers, generally, to day-to-day temperature and precipitation activity, whereas <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> is the term for the average atmospheric conditions over longer periods of time.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> When used without qualification, "weather" is understood to be the weather of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.
</p><p>Weather occurs due to density (temperature and moisture) differences between one place and another. These differences can occur due to the sun angle at any particular spot, which varies by latitude from the tropics. The strong temperature contrast between polar and tropical air gives rise to the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a>. Weather systems in the <a href="/wiki/Mid-latitudes" class="mw-redirect" title="Mid-latitudes">mid-latitudes</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a>, are caused by instabilities of the jet stream flow. Because the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth&#39;s rotation">axis</a> is tilted relative to its orbital plane, <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> is incident at different angles at different times of the year. On the Earth's surface, temperatures usually range ±40&#160;°C (100&#160;°F to −40&#160;°F) annually. Over thousands of years, changes in the Earth's orbit have affected the amount and distribution of solar energy received by the Earth and influence long-term climate
</p><p>Surface <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> differences in turn cause pressure differences. Higher altitudes are cooler than lower altitudes due to differences in compressional heating. Weather forecasting is the application of science and technology to predict the state of the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth&#39;s atmosphere">atmosphere</a> for a future time and a given location. The <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth&#39;s atmosphere">atmosphere</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic system</a>, and small changes to one part of the system can grow to have large effects on the system as a whole. Human attempts to <a href="/wiki/Weather_control" class="mw-redirect" title="Weather control">control the weather</a> have occurred throughout human history, and there is evidence that civilized human activity such as <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industry</a> has inadvertently modified weather patterns.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Diversity_of_plants_image_version_5.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Diversity_of_plants_image_version_5.png/220px-Diversity_of_plants_image_version_5.png" width="220" height="327" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Diversity_of_plants_image_version_5.png/330px-Diversity_of_plants_image_version_5.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Diversity_of_plants_image_version_5.png/440px-Diversity_of_plants_image_version_5.png 2x" data-file-width="896" data-file-height="1331" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Diversity_of_plants_image_version_5.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center>There are many <a href="/wiki/Plant_species" class="mw-redirect" title="Plant species">plant species</a> on the planet.</center></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Animal_diversity.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Animal_diversity.png/220px-Animal_diversity.png" width="220" height="322" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Animal_diversity.png/330px-Animal_diversity.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/14/Animal_diversity.png 2x" data-file-width="354" data-file-height="518" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Animal_diversity.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center>An example of the many <a href="/wiki/Animal_species" class="mw-redirect" title="Animal species">animal species</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.</center></div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">Life</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">Biology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">Biosphere</a></div>
<p>Evidence suggests that <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life on Earth</a> has existed for about 3.7 <a href="/wiki/1000000000_(number)" class="mw-redirect" title="1000000000 (number)">billion</a> years.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> All known life forms share fundamental molecular mechanisms, and based on these observations, theories on the origin of life attempt to find a mechanism explaining the formation of a primordial single cell organism from which all life originates. There are many different hypotheses regarding the path that might have been taken from simple <a href="/wiki/Organic_molecule" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic molecule">organic molecules</a> via pre-cellular life to protocells and metabolism.
</p><p>Although there is no universal agreement on the definition of life, scientists generally accept that the biological manifestation of life is characterized by <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cell_growth" title="Cell growth">growth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adaptation" title="Adaptation">adaptation</a>, response to <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">stimuli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">reproduction</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Life may also be said to be simply the characteristic state of <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, the science of living organisms, "life" is the condition which distinguishes active <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">organisms</a> from <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic matter</a>, including the capacity for growth, <a href="/wiki/Physiology" title="Physiology">functional activity</a> and the continual change preceding death.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Merriam-Webster_Dictionary_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merriam-Webster_Dictionary-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A diverse variety of living organisms (life forms) can be found in the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, and properties common to these organisms—<a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fungus" title="Fungus">fungi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Protist" title="Protist">protists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archaea" title="Archaea">archaea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>—are a <a href="/wiki/Carbon-based_life" title="Carbon-based life">carbon</a>- and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Water_and_life&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Water and life (page does not exist)">water</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cellular</a> form with complex <a href="/wiki/Organization" title="Organization">organization</a> and heritable <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genetic</a> information. Living organisms undergo <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a>, maintain <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostasis</a>, possess a capacity to <a href="/wiki/Cell_growth" title="Cell growth">grow</a>, respond to <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(physiology)" title="Stimulus (physiology)">stimuli</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reproduce" class="mw-redirect" title="Reproduce">reproduce</a> and, through <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">natural selection</a>, adapt to their environment in successive generations. More complex living organisms can communicate through various means.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystems">Ecosystems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Ecosystems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:River_gambia_Niokolokoba_National_Park.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/River_gambia_Niokolokoba_National_Park.gif/220px-River_gambia_Niokolokoba_National_Park.gif" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/River_gambia_Niokolokoba_National_Park.gif/330px-River_gambia_Niokolokoba_National_Park.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/River_gambia_Niokolokoba_National_Park.gif/440px-River_gambia_Niokolokoba_National_Park.gif 2x" data-file-width="691" data-file-height="443" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:River_gambia_Niokolokoba_National_Park.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">Rainforests</a> often have a great deal of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> with many plant and animal species. This is the <a href="/wiki/Gambia_River" title="Gambia River">Gambia River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Niokolo-Koba_National_Park" title="Niokolo-Koba National Park">Niokolo-Koba National Park</a>.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">Ecosystem</a></div>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> (also called as environment) is a natural unit consisting of all plants, animals and micro-organisms (<a href="/wiki/Biotic_component" title="Biotic component">biotic</a> factors) in an area functioning together with all of the non-living physical (<a href="/wiki/Abiotic" class="mw-redirect" title="Abiotic">abiotic</a>) factors of the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Central to the ecosystem concept is the idea that <a href="/wiki/Living_organism" class="mw-redirect" title="Living organism">living organisms</a> are continually engaged in a highly interrelated set of relationships with every other element constituting the <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a> in which they exist. <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Odum" title="Eugene Odum">Eugene Odum</a>, one of the founders of the science of <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, stated: "Any unit that includes all of the organisms (ie: the "community") in a given area interacting with the physical environment so that a flow of energy leads to clearly defined trophic structure, biotic diversity, and material cycles (i.e.: exchange of materials between living and nonliving parts) within the system is an ecosystem."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Creek_and_old-growth_forest-Larch_Mountain.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Creek_and_old-growth_forest-Larch_Mountain.jpg/220px-Creek_and_old-growth_forest-Larch_Mountain.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Creek_and_old-growth_forest-Larch_Mountain.jpg/330px-Creek_and_old-growth_forest-Larch_Mountain.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Creek_and_old-growth_forest-Larch_Mountain.jpg/440px-Creek_and_old-growth_forest-Larch_Mountain.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Creek_and_old-growth_forest-Larch_Mountain.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Old-growth_forest" title="Old-growth forest">Old-growth forest</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Creek_(tidal)" title="Creek (tidal)">creek</a> on <a href="/wiki/Larch_Mountain_(Multnomah_County,_Oregon)" title="Larch Mountain (Multnomah County, Oregon)">Larch Mountain</a>, in the U.S. state of <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>.</div></div></div><p> The human ecosystem concept is then grounded in the deconstruction of the human/nature <a href="/wiki/Dichotomy" title="Dichotomy">dichotomy</a>, and the emergent premise that all species are ecologically integrated with each other, as well as with the abiotic constituents of their <a href="/wiki/Biotope" title="Biotope">biotope</a>.
</p><p>A greater number or variety of species or <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biological diversity</a> of an ecosystem may contribute to greater resilience of an ecosystem, because there are more species present at a location to respond to change and thus "absorb" or reduce its effects. This reduces the effect before the ecosystem's structure is fundamentally changed to a different state. This is not universally the case and there is no proven relationship between the species diversity of an ecosystem and its ability to provide goods and services on a sustainable level.
</p><p>The term ecosystem can also pertain to human-made environments, such as <a href="/wiki/Human_ecosystem" title="Human ecosystem">human ecosystems</a> and human-influenced ecosystems, and can describe any situation where there is relationship between living organisms and their environment. Fewer areas on the surface of the earth today exist free from human contact, although some genuine <a href="/wiki/Wilderness" title="Wilderness">wilderness</a> areas continue to exist without any forms of human intervention.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biomes">Biomes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Biomes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vegetation-no-legend.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Vegetation-no-legend.PNG/220px-Vegetation-no-legend.PNG" width="220" height="99" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Vegetation-no-legend.PNG/330px-Vegetation-no-legend.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Vegetation-no-legend.PNG/440px-Vegetation-no-legend.PNG 2x" data-file-width="1385" data-file-height="622" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vegetation-no-legend.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of Terrestrial <a href="/wiki/Biomes" class="mw-redirect" title="Biomes">biomes</a> classified by vegetation.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biome" title="Biome">Biome</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Biomes" class="mw-redirect" title="Biomes">Biomes</a> are terminologically similar to the concept of ecosystems, and are <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climatically</a> and geographically defined areas of ecologically similar climatic conditions on the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Community_(ecology)" title="Community (ecology)">communities</a> of <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Soil_biology" title="Soil biology">soil organisms</a>, often referred to <i>as</i> ecosystems. Biomes are defined on the basis of factors such as plant structures (such as trees, shrubs, and grasses), leaf types (such as broadleaf and needleleaf), plant spacing (forest, woodland, savanna), and climate. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Ecozone" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecozone">ecozones</a>, biomes are not defined by genetic, taxonomic, or historical similarities. Biomes are often identified with particular patterns of <a href="/wiki/Ecological_succession" title="Ecological succession">ecological succession</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climax_vegetation" class="mw-redirect" title="Climax vegetation">climax vegetation</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biogeochemical_cycles">Biogeochemical cycles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Biogeochemical cycles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Plagiomnium_affine_laminazellen.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Plagiomnium_affine_laminazellen.jpeg/220px-Plagiomnium_affine_laminazellen.jpeg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Plagiomnium_affine_laminazellen.jpeg/330px-Plagiomnium_affine_laminazellen.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Plagiomnium_affine_laminazellen.jpeg/440px-Plagiomnium_affine_laminazellen.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Plagiomnium_affine_laminazellen.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Chloroplasts" class="mw-redirect" title="Chloroplasts">Chloroplasts</a> conduct <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a> and are found in <a href="/wiki/Plant_cell" title="Plant cell">plant cells</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotic</a> organisms. These are Chloroplasts visible in the cells of <i>Plagiomnium affine</i> — Many-fruited Thyme-moss.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycles" class="mw-redirect" title="Biogeochemical cycles">Biogeochemical cycles</a></div>
<p>Global <a href="/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycle" title="Biogeochemical cycle">biogeochemical cycles</a> are critical to life, most notably those of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle" title="Nitrogen cycle">nitrogen cycle</a> is the transformation of nitrogen and nitrogen-containing compounds in nature. It is a cycle which includes gaseous components.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a>, is the continuous movement of water on, above, and below the surface of the Earth. Water can change states among liquid, vapour, and ice at various places in the water cycle. Although the balance of water on Earth remains fairly constant over time, individual water molecules can come and go.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> is the biogeochemical cycle by which carbon is exchanged among the biosphere, pedosphere, geosphere, hydrosphere, and atmosphere of the Earth.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_cycle" title="Oxygen cycle">oxygen cycle</a> is the movement of oxygen within and between its three main reservoirs: the atmosphere, the biosphere, and the <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a>. The main driving factor of the oxygen cycle is <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, which is responsible for the modern Earth's atmospheric composition and life.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_cycle" title="Phosphorus cycle">phosphorus cycle</a> is the movement of phosphorus through the lithosphere, hydrosphere, and biosphere. The atmosphere does not play a significant role in the movements of phosphorus, because phosphorus and phosphorus compounds are usually solids at the typical ranges of temperature and pressure found on Earth.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wilderness">Wilderness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Wilderness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wilderness" title="Wilderness">Wilderness</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wilderness" title="Wilderness">Wilderness</a> is generally defined as a natural environment on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> that has not been significantly modified by <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">human</a> activity. The WILD Foundation goes into more detail, defining wilderness as: "The most intact, undisturbed wild natural areas left on our planet - those last truly wild places that humans do not control and have not developed with roads, pipelines or other industrial infrastructure."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Wilderness areas and protected <a href="/wiki/Parks" class="mw-redirect" title="Parks">parks</a> are considered important for the survival of certain <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, ecological studies, <a href="/wiki/Habitat_conservation" title="Habitat conservation">conservation</a>, solitude, and <a href="/wiki/Recreation" title="Recreation">recreation</a>. Wilderness is deeply valued for cultural, spiritual, <a href="/wiki/Moral" title="Moral">moral</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aesthetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Aesthetic">aesthetic</a> reasons. Some nature writers believe wilderness areas are vital for the human spirit and creativity.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The word, "wilderness", derives from the notion of <a href="/wiki/Wildness" title="Wildness">wildness</a>; in other words that which is not controllable by humans. The word's <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> is from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>wildeornes</i>, which in turn derives from <i>wildeor</i> meaning <i>wild beast</i> (wild + deor = beast, deer).<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> From this point of view, it is the wildness of a place that makes it a wilderness. The mere presence or activity of people does not disqualify an area from being "wilderness." Many ecosystems that are, or have been, inhabited or influenced by activities of people may still be considered "wild." This way of looking at wilderness includes areas within which natural processes operate without very noticeable human interference.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">Wildlife</a> includes all non-<a href="/wiki/Domesticated" class="mw-redirect" title="Domesticated">domesticated</a> plants, animals and other organisms. Domesticating wild plant and animal species for human benefit has occurred many times all over the planet, and has a major impact on the environment, both positive and negative. Wildlife can be found in all ecosystems. Deserts, rain forests, plains, and other areas—including the most developed <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban</a> sites—all have distinct forms of wildlife. While the term in popular culture usually refers to animals that are untouched by civilized human factors, most scientists agree that wildlife around the world is (now) impacted by human activities.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Challenges">Challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Air_pollution_by_industrial_chimneys.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Air_pollution_by_industrial_chimneys.jpg/220px-Air_pollution_by_industrial_chimneys.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Air_pollution_by_industrial_chimneys.jpg/330px-Air_pollution_by_industrial_chimneys.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Air_pollution_by_industrial_chimneys.jpg/440px-Air_pollution_by_industrial_chimneys.jpg 2x" data-file-width="750" data-file-height="500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Air_pollution_by_industrial_chimneys.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Before <a href="/wiki/Flue-gas_desulfurization" title="Flue-gas desulfurization">flue-gas desulfurization</a> was installed, the <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air-polluting</a> emissions from this power plant in <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a> contained excessive amounts of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a></div></div></div>

The tropical rainforests of South America contain the largest diversity of species on Earth, including some that have evolved within the past few hundred thousand years.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_environmental_issues" title="List of environmental issues">List of environmental issues</a></div>
<p>It is the common understanding of <i>natural environment</i> that underlies <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a> — a broad <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">political</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">social</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophical</a> movement that advocates various actions and policies in the interest of protecting what nature remains in the natural environment, or restoring or expanding the role of nature in this environment. While true wilderness is increasingly rare, <i>wild</i> nature (e.g., unmanaged <a href="/wiki/Forests" class="mw-redirect" title="Forests">forests</a>, uncultivated <a href="/wiki/Grasslands" class="mw-redirect" title="Grasslands">grasslands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wildlife" title="Wildlife">wildlife</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wildflower" title="Wildflower">wildflowers</a>) can be found in many locations previously inhabited by humans.
</p><p>Goals for the benefit of people and natural systems, commonly expressed by <a href="/wiki/Environmental_scientist" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental scientist">environmental scientists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Environmentalist" title="Environmentalist">environmentalists</a> include:
</p>
<ul><li>Elimination of <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toxicant" title="Toxicant">toxicants</a> in air, water, soil, buildings, manufactured goods, and food.</li>
<li>Preservation of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a> and protection of <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered species</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Conservation_movement" title="Conservation movement">Conservation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_use" class="mw-redirect" title="Sustainable use">sustainable use</a> of resources such as <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> land, air, energy, raw materials, and natural resources.</li>
<li>Halting human-induced <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>, which represents pollution, a threat to biodiversity, and a threat to human populations.</li>
<li>Shifting from <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuels">fossil fuels</a> to <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> in electricity, heating and cooling, and transportation, which addresses pollution, global warming, and sustainability.  This may include <a href="/wiki/Public_transportation" class="mw-redirect" title="Public transportation">public transportation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Distributed_generation" title="Distributed generation">distributed generation</a>, which have benefits for traffic congestion and electric reliability.</li>
<li>Establishment of <a href="/wiki/Nature_reserve" title="Nature reserve">nature reserves</a> for recreational purposes and ecosystem preservation.</li>
<li>Sustainable and less polluting <a href="/wiki/Waste_management" title="Waste management">waste management</a> including <a href="/wiki/Waste_reduction" class="mw-redirect" title="Waste reduction">waste reduction</a> (or even <a href="/wiki/Zero_waste" title="Zero waste">zero waste</a>), <a href="/wiki/Reuse" title="Reuse">reuse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Composting" class="mw-redirect" title="Composting">composting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waste-to-energy" title="Waste-to-energy">waste-to-energy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion" title="Anaerobic digestion">anaerobic digestion</a> of sewage sludge.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natural_environment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In some cultures the term environment is meaningless because there is no separation between people and what they view as the natural world, or their surroundings.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Specifically in the United States, many native cultures do not recognize the "environment", or see themselves as environmentalists.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
