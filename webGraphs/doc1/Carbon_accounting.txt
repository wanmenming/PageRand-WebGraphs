<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbon accounting</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Carbon accounting</b> refers generally to processes undertaken to "<a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">measure</a>" amounts of  <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_equivalent" title="Carbon dioxide equivalent">carbon dioxide equivalents</a> emitted by an entity. It is used by <a href="/wiki/Nation_states" class="mw-redirect" title="Nation states">nation states</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corporations" class="mw-redirect" title="Corporations">corporations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Individual" title="Individual">individuals</a> – to create the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_credit" title="Carbon credit">carbon credit</a> <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">commodity</a> traded on <a href="/wiki/Carbon_market" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon market">carbon markets</a> (or to establish the <a href="/wiki/Demand" title="Demand">demand</a> for <a href="/wiki/Carbon_credit" title="Carbon credit">carbon credits</a>). Correspondingly, examples for products based upon forms of carbon accounting can be found in <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_inventory" title="Greenhouse gas inventory">national inventories</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_reports" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental reports">corporate environmental reports</a> or <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">carbon footprint</a> calculators. Likening <a href="/wiki/Sustainability_measurement" title="Sustainability measurement">sustainability measurement</a>, as an instance of <a href="/wiki/Ecological_modernisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological modernisation">ecological modernisation</a> <a href="/wiki/Discourse" title="Discourse">discourses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Policy" title="Policy">policy</a>, carbon accounting is hoped to provide a <a href="/wiki/Fact" title="Fact">factual</a> ground for carbon-related <a href="/wiki/Decision-making" title="Decision-making">decision-making</a>. However, <a href="/wiki/Social_sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Social sciences">social scientific</a> studies of accounting challenge this hope,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> pointing to the <a href="/wiki/Social_constructionism" title="Social constructionism">socially constructed</a> character of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> <a href="/wiki/Conversion_factor" class="mw-redirect" title="Conversion factor">conversion factors</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or of the accountants' work practice<sup id="cite_ref-cognition_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cognition-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> which cannot <a href="/wiki/Implementation" title="Implementation">implement</a> abstract accounting schemes into reality.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While natural sciences claim to know and measure <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, for organisations it is usually easier to employ forms of carbon accounting to represent carbon.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The trustworthiness of accounts of carbon emissions can easily be contested.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Thus, how well carbon accounting represents carbon is difficult to exactly know. <a href="/wiki/Science_and_Technology_Studies" class="mw-redirect" title="Science and Technology Studies">Science and Technology Studies</a> scholar <a href="/wiki/Donna_Haraway" title="Donna Haraway">Donna Haraway</a>'s pluralised concept of <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a>, i.e. knowledges, can well be used to understand better the status of knowledge produced by carbon accounting: carbon accounting produced a version of understanding of carbon emissions. Other carbon accountants would produce other results.
</p><p>== Carbon accounting in corporations 
Carbon accounting can be used as part of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability_accounting" title="Sustainability accounting">sustainability accounting</a> by <a href="/wiki/For-profit" class="mw-redirect" title="For-profit">for-profit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Non-profit" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-profit">non-profit</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> organisations. A corporate or organisational "carbon" or greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions assessment <a href="/wiki/Promise" title="Promise">promises</a> to <a href="/wiki/Quantification_(science)" title="Quantification (science)">quantify</a> the  greenhouse gases produced directly and indirectly from a business or organisation's activities within a set of boundaries. Also known as a <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">carbon footprint</a>, it is a business tool that constructs information that may (or may not) be useful for understanding and managing climate change impacts.<sup id="cite_ref-cognition_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cognition-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The drivers for corporate carbon accounting include mandatory GHG reporting in directors' reports, investment due diligence, shareholder and stakeholder communication, staff engagement, green messaging, and tender requirements for business and government contracts.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Accounting for greenhouse gas emissions is increasingly framed as a standard requirement for business. As of June 2011, 60% of  UK <a href="/wiki/FTSE_100" class="mw-redirect" title="FTSE 100">FTSE 100</a> companies had published environmental targets, with 53% of these 240+ targets relating to carbon, greenhouse gas emissions or energy reductions (representing 59% of the FTSE 100).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In June 2012, the UK <a href="/wiki/Coalition_government" title="Coalition government">coalition government</a> announced the introduction of mandatory carbon reporting,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> requiring around 1,100 of the UK's largest listed companies to report their <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse gas emissions">greenhouse gas emissions</a> every year. <a href="/wiki/Deputy_Prime_Minister" class="mw-redirect" title="Deputy Prime Minister">Deputy Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Nick_Clegg" title="Nick Clegg">Nick Clegg</a> confirmed that emission reporting rules would come into effect from April 2013 in his piece for The Guardian.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Enterprise_carbon_accounting">Enterprise carbon accounting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Enterprise carbon accounting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Enterprise Carbon Accounting (ECA)</b> or <b>Corporate Carbon Footprint</b> aims to be a rapid and cost effective process for businesses to collect, summarise, and report enterprise and supply chain GHG inventories. ECA leverages financial accounting principles, whilst utilising a hybrid of input-output LCA (Life Cycle Analysis)and process methodologies as appropriate. The evolution to ECA is necessary to address the urgent need for a more comprehensive and scalable approach to carbon accounting. While an emerging area, a number of new companies offer ECA solutions.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> ECA is a critical part of broader Enterprise Sustainability Accounting.
</p><p>To be successful, an Enterprise Carbon Accounting system should have the following characteristics:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
<br /><b>Comprehensive:</b> Incorporates Scope 1,2 and 3 emissions
<br /><b>Periodic:</b> Enables updates at regular intervals and comparisons across reporting periods
<br /><b>Auditable:</b> Traces transactions and enables independent reviews for compliance
<br /><b>Flexible:</b> Incorporates data from multiple approaches to life cycle analysis
<br /><b>Standards-Based:</b> Accommodates existing generally accepted standards and emerging standards
<br /><b>Scalable:</b> Accommodates growing volume and complexity of business operations
<br /><b>Efficient:</b> Delivers data in the timeframe required for decision making
</p><p>Enterprises that realize reduced emissions and energy consumption utilize systems with the following capabilities:
<br />* Real-time historical energy data that is easily accessible
<br />* Role-based visibility into plant emissions data
<br />* Provides executives with real-time visibility into emissions data
<br />* Ability to benchmark emissions levels with goals and industry standards
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle_analysis_of_ECA">Life cycle analysis of ECA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Life cycle analysis of ECA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Process_LCA">Process LCA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Process LCA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Process LCA is the most popular method, currently, for conducting life-cycle assessment, and is often referred to as the SETAC-EPA method because of the role played by SETAC<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and EPA in this method’s development.<sup id="cite_ref-Hendrickson,_C._2006_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hendrickson,_C._2006-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The inputs and outputs of multiple stages of a product’s life are investigated in turn, and the results are aggregated into single metrics of impact such as eutrophication, toxicity, and greenhouse gas emissions. Three tools exist on the market to assist researchers in conducting process LCA (such as GaBi, Ecoinvent, and Umberto). These tools contain data from previous researchers on the environmental impact of materials and processes that are then strung together by the user to form a system.<sup id="cite_ref-Hendrickson,_C._2006_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hendrickson,_C._2006-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_Input-Output_LCA">Economic Input-Output LCA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Economic Input-Output LCA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Input-Output LCA utilizes economic input-output tables<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and industry-level environmental data to construct a database of environmental impacts per dollar sold by an industry. The boundary problem of process LCA is solved in this method because the economic input-output table captures the interrelations of all economic sectors;<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> however, aggregated industrial categories limit the specificity of the results.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Input–output analysis is a very powerful tool for the upfront screening of corporate carbon footprints, for informing streamlined supply-chain GHG accounting and for setting priorities for more detailed analyses.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hybrid_LCA">Hybrid LCA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hybrid LCA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many methods for hybrid life-cycle assessments have been discussed, which aim to combine the infinite boundary of EIO-LCA with the specificity of Process LCA.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Enterprise_carbon_accounting_.28ECA.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Enterprise_carbon_accounting_(ECA)">Enterprise carbon accounting (ECA)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Enterprise carbon accounting (ECA)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At its core, ECA is essentially a hybrid life-cycle assessment; however, rather than the traditional bottom-up approach of life-cycle assessment, ECA links financial data directly to LCA data to produce a snapshot of the companies' operations. Rather than probing at areas thought to be problematic, ECA quickly identifies problem areas in the supply chain so that rapid action can be taken. This fundamental shift in thinking enables decision makers to rapidly address critical areas within the enterprise and supply chain.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Socialised_supply_chain">Socialised supply chain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Socialised supply chain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Socialised supply chain accounting is the term generally applied to Enterprise Carbon Accounting Solutions that provide a collaborative mechanism for supply chain participants to engage, expose and determine supply chain emissions through the process of shared knowledge.  The term "Socialised Supply Chain" was coined by the CEO of Nootrol, Mark Kearns to describe a platform where supply chain participants exposed Process LCA and embedded emissions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_accounting_of_avoided_emissions">Carbon accounting of avoided emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_accounting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Carbon accounting of avoided emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A special case of carbon accounting is the <a href="/wiki/Accounting" title="Accounting">accounting</a> process undertaken to measure the amount of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_equivalent" title="Carbon dioxide equivalent">carbon dioxide equivalents</a> that will not be released into the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> as a result of <a href="/wiki/Flexible_mechanisms" class="mw-redirect" title="Flexible mechanisms">flexible mechanisms</a> projects under the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="please specify (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> These projects thus include (but are not limited to) <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> projects and <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, forage and tree <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantations</a>.
</p>
