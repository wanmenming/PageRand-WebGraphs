<p><i><b>The Christian Science Monitor</b></i> (<i><b>CSM</b></i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Nonprofit" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonprofit">nonprofit</a> news organization that publishes daily articles in <a href="/wiki/Electronic_publishing" title="Electronic publishing">electronic format</a> as well as a weekly print edition.<sup id="cite_ref-Barnett_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barnett-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kasuya_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kasuya-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It was founded in 1908 as a daily <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">newspaper</a> by <a href="/wiki/Mary_Baker_Eddy" title="Mary Baker Eddy">Mary Baker Eddy</a>, the founder of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Christ,_Scientist" title="Church of Christ, Scientist">Church of Christ, Scientist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Koestler-Grack_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Koestler-Grack-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> As of  2011<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_Christian_Science_Monitor&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, the print circulation was 75,052.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the organization's website, "the <i>Monitor's</i> global approach is reflected in how Mary Baker Eddy described its object as 'To injure no man, but to bless all mankind.'  The aim is to embrace the human family, shedding light with the conviction that understanding the world's problems and possibilities moves us towards solutions." The <i>Christian Science Monitor</i> has won seven Pulitzer Prizes and more than a dozen Overseas Press Club awards."<sup id="cite_ref-AboutCSM_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AboutCSM-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reporting">Reporting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Christian_Science_Monitor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Reporting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite its name, the <i>Monitor</i> is not a religious-themed paper, and does not promote the <a href="/wiki/Doctrine" title="Doctrine">doctrine</a> of its patron church. However, at its founder Eddy's request, a daily religious article has appeared in every issue of the <i>Monitor</i>.
</p><p>The paper has been known for avoiding <a href="/wiki/Sensationalism" title="Sensationalism">sensationalism</a>, producing a "distinctive brand of nonhysterical journalism".<sup id="cite_ref-beam_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beam-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-askt_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-askt-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In 1997, the <i><a href="/wiki/Washington_Report_on_Middle_East_Affairs" title="Washington Report on Middle East Affairs">Washington Report on Middle East Affairs</a></i>, a publication critical of United States policy in the Middle East, praised the <i>Monitor</i> for its objective and informative coverage of Islam and the Middle East.<sup id="cite_ref-curtiss_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-curtiss-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006, <a href="/wiki/Jill_Carroll" title="Jill Carroll">Jill Carroll</a>, a freelance reporter for the <i>Monitor</i>, was kidnapped in Baghdad, and released safely after 82 days.  Although Carroll was initially a freelancer, the paper worked tirelessly for her release, even hiring her as a staff writer shortly after her abduction to ensure that she had financial benefits, according to Bergenheim.<sup id="cite_ref-cnn_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Beginning in August 2006, the <i>Monitor</i> published an account<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> of Carroll's kidnapping and subsequent release, with first-person reporting from Carroll and others involved.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Circulation">Circulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Christian_Science_Monitor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Circulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The paper's overall circulation has ranged widely, from a peak of over 223,000 in 1970, to just under 56,000 shortly before the suspension of the daily print edition in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-Fine_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fine-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Partially in response to declining circulation and the struggle to earn a profit, the church's directors and the manager of the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Science_Publishing_Society" title="Christian Science Publishing Society">Christian Science Publishing Society</a> were purportedly forced to plan cutbacks and closures (later denied), which led in 1989 to the mass protest resignations by its chief editor <a href="/wiki/Kay_Fanning" title="Kay Fanning">Kay Fanning</a> (an <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Newspaper_Editors" class="mw-redirect" title="American Society of Newspaper Editors">ASNE</a> president and former editor of the <i><a href="/wiki/Anchorage_Daily_News" title="Anchorage Daily News">Anchorage Daily News</a></i>), managing editor David Anable, associate editor David Winder, and several other newsroom staff. These developments also presaged administrative moves to scale back the print newspaper in favor of expansions into radio, a magazine, shortwave broadcasting, and television. Expenses, however, rapidly outpaced revenues, contradicting predictions by church directors. On the brink of bankruptcy, the board was forced to close the broadcast programs in 1992.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Christian_Science_Monitor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding">Founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Christian_Science_Monitor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Founding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Monitor</i>'s inception was, in part, a response by its founder <a href="/wiki/Mary_Baker_Eddy" title="Mary Baker Eddy">Mary Baker Eddy</a> to the journalism of her day, which relentlessly covered the sensations and scandals surrounding her new religion with varying degrees of accuracy. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Pulitzer" title="Joseph Pulitzer">Joseph Pulitzer</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_World" title="New York World">New York World</a></i> was consistently critical of Eddy, and this, along with a derogatory article in <i><a href="/wiki/McClure%27s" title="McClure&#39;s">McClure's</a></i>, furthered Eddy's decision to found her own media outlet.<sup id="cite_ref-AboutCSM_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AboutCSM-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Eddy also required the inclusion of "<a href="/wiki/Christian_Science" title="Christian Science">Christian Science</a>" in the paper's name, over initial opposition by some of her advisors who thought the religious reference might repel a <a href="/wiki/Secularity_(non-religiosity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Secularity (non-religiosity)">secular</a> audience.<sup id="cite_ref-AboutCSM_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AboutCSM-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>
Eddy also saw a vital need to counteract the fear often spread by media reporting: </p><blockquote><p>Looking over the newspapers of the day, one naturally reflects that it is dangerous to live, so loaded with disease seems the very air. These descriptions carry fears to many minds, to be depicted in some future time upon the body. A periodical of our own will counteract to some extent this public nuisance; for through our paper, at the price at which we shall issue it, we shall be able to reach many homes with healing, purifying thought.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote><p>Eddy declared that the <i>Monitor</i>'s mission should be "to injure no man, but to bless all mankind".<sup id="cite_ref-AboutCSM_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AboutCSM-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_and_television">Radio and television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Christian_Science_Monitor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Radio and television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>MonitoRadio was a radio service produced by the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Christ,_Scientist" title="Church of Christ, Scientist">Church of Christ, Scientist</a> between 1984 and 1997. It featured several one-hour news broadcasts a day, as well as top of the hour news bulletins. The service was widely heard on <a href="/wiki/Public_radio" class="mw-redirect" title="Public radio">public radio</a> stations throughout the United States. The Monitor later launched an international broadcast over <a href="/wiki/Shortwave" class="mw-redirect" title="Shortwave">shortwave</a> radio, called the World Service of the Christian Science Monitor. Weekdays were news-led, but weekend schedules were exclusively dedicated to religious programming. That service ceased operations on June 28, 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-MtN_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MtN-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1986, the Monitor started producing a current affairs television series, <i>The Christian Science Monitor Reports,</i> which was distributed via syndication to television stations across the United States.  In 1988, the Christian Science Monitor Reports won a <a href="/wiki/Peabody_Award" title="Peabody Award">Peabody Award</a><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> for a series of reports on Islamic fundamentalism.  That same year, the program was canceled and the Monitor created a daily television program, <i>World Monitor</i>, anchored by former NBC correspondent John Hart, which was initially shown on the Discovery Channel. In 1991, World Monitor moved to the Monitor Channel, a 24-hour news and information channel.<sup id="cite_ref-MtN_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MtN-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The channel launched on May 1, 1991 with programming from its Boston TV station.<sup id="cite_ref-bc_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bc-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The only religious programming on the channel was a five-minute Christian Science program early each morning.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In 1992, after eleven months on the air, the service was shut down amid huge financial losses.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modernization">Modernization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Christian_Science_Monitor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Modernization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The print edition continued to struggle for readership, and, in 2004, faced a renewed mandate from the church to earn a profit. Subsequently, the <i>Monitor</i> began relying more on the Internet as an integral part of its business model. The <i>Monitor</i> was one of the first newspapers to put its text online in 1996, and was also one of the first to launch a <a href="/wiki/Portable_Document_Format" class="mw-redirect" title="Portable Document Format">PDF</a> edition in 2001. It was also an early pioneer of <a href="/wiki/RSS_(file_format)" class="mw-redirect" title="RSS (file format)">RSS</a> feeds.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bergenheim" title="Richard Bergenheim">Richard Bergenheim</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Christian_Science_practitioner" title="Christian Science practitioner">Christian Science practitioner</a>, was named the new editor. Shortly before his death in 2008, Bergenheim was replaced by a veteran <i><a href="/wiki/Boston_Globe" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Globe">Boston Globe</a></i> editor and former <i>Monitor</i> reporter <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Yemma&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John Yemma (page does not exist)">John Yemma</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-yemma_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yemma-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2008, citing net losses of $US18.9 million per year versus $US12.5 million in annual revenue, the <i>Monitor</i> announced that it would cease printing daily and instead print weekly editions starting in April 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-fine_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fine-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-clifford_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clifford-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>  The last daily print edition was published on March 27, 2009.
</p><p>The weekly magazine follows on from the <i>Monitor'</i>s London edition, also a weekly, launched in 1960 and the weekly World Edition which replaced the London edition in 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Mark Sappenfield became the editor in March 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Christian_Science_Monitor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Monitor</i> staff have been the recipients of seven <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prizes</a>:
</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/1967_Pulitzer_Prize" title="1967 Pulitzer Prize">1967</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize_for_International_Reporting" title="Pulitzer Prize for International Reporting">Pulitzer Prize for International Reporting</a>: <a href="/wiki/R._John_Hughes" class="mw-redirect" title="R. John Hughes">R. John Hughes</a>, For his thorough reporting of Indonesia's attempted <a href="/wiki/Transition_to_the_New_Order" title="Transition to the New Order">Transition to the New Order</a> in 1965 and the purge that followed in 1965–66.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/1968_Pulitzer_Prize" title="1968 Pulitzer Prize">1968</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize_for_National_Reporting" title="Pulitzer Prize for National Reporting">Pulitzer Prize for National Reporting</a>: Howard James, for his series of articles, <i>Crisis in the Courts</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/1969_Pulitzer_Prize" title="1969 Pulitzer Prize">1969</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize_for_National_Reporting" title="Pulitzer Prize for National Reporting">Pulitzer Prize for National Reporting</a>: <a href="/wiki/Robert_Cahn" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Cahn">Robert Cahn</a>, for his inquiry into the future of the United States' <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Park">national parks</a> and the methods that may help to preserve them.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/1978_Pulitzer_Prize" title="1978 Pulitzer Prize">1978</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize_Special_Citations_and_Awards" title="Pulitzer Prize Special Citations and Awards">Pulitzer Prize Special Citations and Awards</a>, Journalism: <a href="/wiki/Richard_Strout" title="Richard Strout">Richard Strout</a>, for distinguished commentary from Washington over many years as staff correspondent for The <i><a href="/wiki/Christian_Science_Monitor" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Science Monitor">Christian Science Monitor</a></i> and contributor to <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Republic" title="The New Republic">The New Republic</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/1996_Pulitzer_Prize" title="1996 Pulitzer Prize">1996</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize_for_International_Reporting" title="Pulitzer Prize for International Reporting">Pulitzer Prize for International Reporting</a>: <a href="/wiki/David_Rohde" class="mw-redirect" title="David Rohde">David Rohde</a>, for his persistent on-site reporting of <a href="/wiki/Srebrenica_massacre" title="Srebrenica massacre">the slaughter of thousands of Bosnian Muslims</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Srebrenica_genocide" class="mw-redirect" title="Srebrenica genocide">Srebrenica Genocide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></li>
