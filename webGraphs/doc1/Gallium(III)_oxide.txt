<p><b>Gallium(III) oxide</b>  is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the formula <a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">Ga<sub>2</sub></a><a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">O<sub>3</sub></a>.  It exists as several <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a>, all of which are white, water-insoluble solids.  Although no commercial applications exist, Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is an intermediate in the purification of gallium, which is consumed almost exclusively as <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">gallium arsenide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gallium oxide is precipitated in hydrated form upon <a href="/wiki/Neutralization_(chemistry)" title="Neutralization (chemistry)">neutralization</a> of acidic or basic solution of gallium salt.  Also, it is formed on heating gallium in air or by thermally decomposing gallium nitrate at 200–250 ˚C. It can occur in five different modifications, α, β, γ, δ, and ε. Of these modifications β-Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is the most stable form.<sup id="cite_ref-Bailar,_J_1973_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bailar,_J_1973-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>β-Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is prepared by heating nitrate, acetate, oxalate or other organic derivatives above 1000&#160;°C.</li>
<li>α-Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> can be obtained by heating β-Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> at 65 kbars and 1100&#160;°C. The hydrated form can be prepared by decomposing precipitated and "aged" gallium hydroxide at 500&#160;°C.</li>
<li>γ-Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is prepared by rapidly heating the hydroxide gel at 400–500&#160;°C. A more crystalline form of this polymorph can be prepared directly from gallium metal by a solvothermal synthesis.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>δ-Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is obtained by heating Ga(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub> at 250&#160;°C.</li>
<li>ε-Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is prepared by heating δ-Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> at 550&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-Bailar,_J_1973_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bailar,_J_1973-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>. Thin films of ε-Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> are deposited by means of <a href="/wiki/Metalorganic_vapour_phase_epitaxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Metalorganic vapour phase epitaxy">Metalorganic vapour phase epitaxy</a> using <a href="/wiki/Trimethylgallium" title="Trimethylgallium">Trimethylgallium</a> and water on sapphire substrates at temperatures between 550 and 650 °C <sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gallium(III) oxide is <a href="/wiki/Amphoteric" class="mw-redirect" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It reacts with <a href="/wiki/Alkali_metal" title="Alkali metal">alkali metal</a> oxides at high temperature to form e.g. NaGaO<sub>2</sub>, and with Mg, Zn, Co, Ni, Cu oxides to form <a href="/wiki/Spinel" title="Spinel">spinels</a>, e.g. MgGa<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
It dissolves in strong alkali to form a solution of the gallate ion, <span class="chemf nowrap">Ga(OH)<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">−</sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>.
</p><p>With HCl, it forms <a href="/wiki/Gallium_trichloride" title="Gallium trichloride">gallium trichloride</a> GaCl<sub>3</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 6 HCl → 2 GaCl<sub>3</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>It can be reduced to <a href="/wiki/Gallium_suboxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Gallium suboxide">gallium suboxide</a> (gallium(I) oxide) Ga<sub>2</sub>O by H<sub>2</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> or by reaction with gallium metal<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub> → Ga<sub>2</sub>O + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 4 Ga → 3 Ga<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>β-Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, with a melting point of 1900 ˚C, is the most stable crystalline modification. The oxide ions are in a distorted cubic closest packing arrangement, and the gallium (III) ions occupy  distorted tetrahedral and octahedral sites, with Ga-O bond distances of 1.83 and 2.00 Å respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>α-Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has the same structure (<a href="/wiki/Corundum" title="Corundum">corundum</a>) as <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">α-Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></a>, wherein Ga ions are 6-coordinate. γ-Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has a defect spinel structure similar to that of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">γ-Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>ε-Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> films deposited by <a href="/wiki/Metalorganic_vapour_phase_epitaxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Metalorganic vapour phase epitaxy">Metalorganic vapour phase epitaxy</a> observed by <a href="/wiki/Transmission_electron_microscopy" title="Transmission electron microscopy">Transmission electron microscopy</a> show a columnar structure with <a href="/wiki/Orthorhombic_crystal_system" title="Orthorhombic crystal system">Orthorhombic crystal system</a>. Macroscopically, this structure is seen by <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">X-ray crystallography</a> as <a href="/wiki/Hexagonal_crystal_family#Hexagonal_Close_Packed" title="Hexagonal crystal family">hexagonal close packed</a><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Potential_applications">Potential applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Potential applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gallium(III) oxide has been studied in the use of lasers, phosphors, and luminescent materials.<sup id="cite_ref-Bailar,_J_1973_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bailar,_J_1973-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It has also been used as an insulating barrier in tight junctions.<sup id="cite_ref-Dai,_Z_2002_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dai,_Z_2002-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Monoclinic β-Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is used in gas sensors and luminescent <a href="/wiki/Phosphors" class="mw-redirect" title="Phosphors">phosphors</a> and can be applied to dielectric coatings for <a href="/wiki/Solar_cells" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar cells">solar cells</a>. This stable oxide has also shown potential for deep-ultraviolet transparent conductive oxides,<sup id="cite_ref-Rebien,_M_2002_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rebien,_M_2002-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and transistor applications.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>ε-Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> thin films deposited on sapphire show potential applications as solar-blind UV <a href="/wiki/Photodetector" title="Photodetector">Photodetector</a> <sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Thin Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> films are of commercial interest as gas sensitive materials and Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.. <a href="/wiki/Ellipsometry" title="Ellipsometry">Ellipsometry</a> is a procedure that can be used to determine optical functions of the β-Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rebien,_M_2002_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rebien,_M_2002-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>β-Gallium(III) oxide is used in the production of Ga<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>-Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> catalyst.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
