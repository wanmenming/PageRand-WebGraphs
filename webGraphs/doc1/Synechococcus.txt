<p><i><b>Synechococcus</b></i> (from the Greek <i>synechos</i>, in succession, and the Greek <i>kokkos</i>, granule) is a unicellular <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacterium" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyanobacterium">cyanobacterium</a> that is very widespread in the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">marine environment</a>. Its size varies from 0.8 to 1.5&#160;<a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">µm</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Photosynthetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Photosynthetic">photosynthetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Coccus" title="Coccus">coccoid cells</a> are preferentially found in well–lit <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface waters</a> where it can be very abundant (generally 1,000 to 200,000 cells per ml). Many <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">freshwater</a> species of <i>Synechococcus</i> have also been described.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> of <i>S. elongatus</i> strain PCC7002 has a size of 3,008,047 <a href="/wiki/Base_pair" title="Base pair">bp</a>, whereas the oceanic strain WH8102 has a genome of size 2.4&#160;Mbp.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synechococcus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Synechococcus</i> is one of the most important components of the <a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">prokaryotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Autotroph" title="Autotroph">autotrophic</a> <a href="/wiki/Picoplankton" title="Picoplankton">picoplankton</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Temperate" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperate">temperate</a> to <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a> oceans. The genus was first described in 1979,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wat79_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wat79-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and was originally defined to include "small unicellular cyanobacteria with ovoid to cylindrical cells that reproduce by binary traverse fission in a single plane and lack sheaths".<sup id="cite_ref-Rip79_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rip79-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> This definition of the genus <i>Synechococcus</i> contained organisms of considerable genetic diversity and was later subdivided into subgroups based on the presence of the accessory pigment <a href="/wiki/Phycoerythrin" title="Phycoerythrin">phycoerythrin</a>. The marine forms of <i>Synechococcus</i> are coccoid cells between 0.6 and 1.6&#160;µm in size. They are <a href="/wiki/Gram-negative" class="mw-redirect" title="Gram-negative">Gram-negative</a> cells with highly structured cell walls that may contain projections on their surface.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Electron microscopy frequently reveals the presence of phosphate inclusions, <a href="/wiki/Glycogen" title="Glycogen">glycogen</a> granules, and more importantly, highly structured <a href="/wiki/Carboxysome" title="Carboxysome">carboxysomes</a>.
</p><p>Cells are known to be <a href="/wiki/Motile" class="mw-redirect" title="Motile">motile</a> by a gliding method<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and a novel uncharacterized, nonphototactic swimming method<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> that does not involve flagellar motion. While some cyanobacteria are capable of <a href="/wiki/Photoheterotroph" title="Photoheterotroph">photoheterotrophic</a> or even <a href="/wiki/Chemoheterotrophic" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemoheterotrophic">chemoheterotrophic</a> growth, all marine <i>Synechococcus</i> strains appear to be obligate photoautotrophs<sup id="cite_ref-Wat86b_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wat86b-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> that are capable of supporting their nitrogen requirements using nitrate, ammonia, or in some cases <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a> as a sole nitrogen source. Marine <i>Synechococcus</i> species are traditionally not thought to fix nitrogen.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pigments">Pigments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synechococcus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pigments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main photosynthetic pigment in <i>Synechococcus</i> is <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll_a" title="Chlorophyll a">chlorophyll a</a>, while its major accessory pigments are <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phycobilliprotein&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Phycobilliprotein (page does not exist)">phycobilliproteins</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wat79_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wat79-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The four commonly recognized <a href="/wiki/Phycobilin" title="Phycobilin">phycobilins</a> are <a href="/wiki/Phycocyanin" title="Phycocyanin">phycocyanin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allophycocyanin" title="Allophycocyanin">allophycocyanin</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Allophycocyanin_B&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Allophycocyanin B (page does not exist)">allophycocyanin B</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phycoerythrin" title="Phycoerythrin">phycoerythrin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In addition <i>Synechococcus</i> also contains <a href="/wiki/Zeaxanthin" title="Zeaxanthin">zeaxanthin</a> but no diagnostic pigment for this organism is known. Zeaxanthin is also found in <i><a href="/wiki/Prochlorococcus" title="Prochlorococcus">Prochlorococcus</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Red_algae" title="Red algae">red algae</a> and as a minor pigment in some <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyta" title="Chlorophyta">chlorophytes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eustigmatophyte" title="Eustigmatophyte">eustigmatophytes</a>. Similarly, phycoerythrin is also found in <a href="/wiki/Red_algae" title="Red algae">rhodophytes</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Cryptomonad" title="Cryptomonad">cryptomonads</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wat86b_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wat86b-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phylogeny">Phylogeny</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synechococcus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Phylogeny">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">Phylogenetic</a> description of <i>Synechococcus</i> is difficult. Isolates are <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">morphologically</a> very similar, yet exhibit a <a href="/wiki/GC-content" title="GC-content">G+C content</a> ranging from 39 to 71%,<sup id="cite_ref-Wat86b_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wat86b-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> illustrating the large genetic diversity of this provisional taxon. Initially, attempts were made to divide the group into three subclusters, each with a specific range of genomic G+C content.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The observation that open-ocean isolates alone nearly span the complete G+C spectrum, however, indicates that <i>Synechococcus</i> is composed of at least several species. <i>Bergey's Manual</i> (Herdman <i>et al.</i> 2001) now divides <i>Synechococcus</i> into five clusters (equivalent to genera) based on morphology, physiology, and genetic traits.
</p><p>Cluster 1 includes relatively large (1–1.5&#160;µm) nonmotile obligate photoautotrophs that exhibit low salt tolerance. Reference strains for this cluster are PCC6301 (formerly <i>Anacycstis nidulans</i>) and PCC6312, which were isolated from fresh water in <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> and <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-Rip79_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rip79-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Cluster 2 also is characterized by low salt tolerance. Cells are obligate photoautrotrophs, lack phycoerythrin, and are thermophilic. The reference strain PCC6715 was isolated from a hot spring in <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_National_Park" title="Yellowstone National Park">Yellowstone National Park</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Cluster 3 includes phycoerythrin-lacking marine <i>Synechococcus</i> species that are <a href="/wiki/Euryhaline" title="Euryhaline">euryhaline</a>, i.e. capable of growth in both marine and freshwater environments. Several strains, including the reference strain PCC7003, are facultative heterotrophs and require <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B12" title="Vitamin B12">vitamin B<sub>12</sub></a> for growth. Cluster 4 contains a single isolate, PCC7335. This strain is obligate marine.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> This strain contains phycoerthrin and was first isolated from the <a href="/wiki/Intertidal_zone" title="Intertidal zone">intertidal zone</a> in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Pe%C3%B1asco" title="Puerto Peñasco">Puerto Peñasco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rip79_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rip79-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The last cluster contains what had previously been referred to as ‘marine A and B clusters’ of <i>Synechococcus</i>. These cells are truly marine and have been isolated from both the coastal and the open ocean. All strains are obligate photoautrophs and are around 0.6–1.7&#160;µm in diameter. This cluster is, however, further divided into a population that either contains (cluster 5.1) or does not contain (cluster 5.2) phycoerythrin. The reference strains are WH8103 for the phycoerythrin-containing strains and WH5701 for those strains that lack this pigment.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  More recently, Badger <i>et al.</i> (2002) proposed the division of the cyanobacteria into a α- and a β-subcluster based on the type of <a href="/wiki/RuBisCO_large_subunit" class="mw-redirect" title="RuBisCO large subunit">rbcL</a> (large subunit of <a href="/wiki/RuBisCO" title="RuBisCO">ribulose 1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase</a>) found in these organisms.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> α-cyanobacteria were defined to contain a form IA, while β-cyanobacteria were defined to contain a form IB of this gene. In support for this division Badger <i>et al.</i> analyze the phylogeny of carboxysomal proteins, which appear to support this division. Also, two particular <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bicarbonate_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bicarbonate transport (page does not exist)">bicarbonate transport</a> systems appear to only be found in α-cyanobacteria, which lack carboxysomal carbonic anhydrases.
</p><p>The complete <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetic_tree" title="Phylogenetic tree">phylogenetic tree</a> of 16S rRNA sequences of <i>Synechococcus</i> revealed at least 12 groups, which morphologically correspond to <i>Synechococcus</i>, but they have not derived from the common ancestor. Moreover, it has been estimated based on molecular dating that the first <i>Synechococcus</i> lineage has appeared 3 billion years ago in thermal springs with subsequent radiation to marine and freshwater environments.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology_and_distribution">Ecology and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synechococcus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecology and distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Synechococcus</i> has been observed to occur at concentrations ranging between a few cells to 10<sup>6</sup> cells per ml in virtually all regions of oceanic <a href="/wiki/Euphotic_zone" class="mw-redirect" title="Euphotic zone">euphotic zone</a> except in samples from the <a href="/wiki/McMurdo_Sound" title="McMurdo Sound">McMurdo Sound</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ross_Ice_Shelf" title="Ross Ice Shelf">Ross Ice Shelf</a> in <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wat86b_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wat86b-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Cells are generally much more abundant in nutrient-rich environments than in the oligotrophic ocean and prefer the upper, well-lit portion of the euphotic zone.<sup id="cite_ref-Par99a_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Par99a-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <i>Synechococcus</i> has also been observed to occur at high abundances in environments with low salinities and/or low temperatures. It is usually far outnumbered by <i><a href="/wiki/Prochlorococcus" title="Prochlorococcus">Prochlorococcus</a></i> in all environments where they co-occur. Exceptions to this rule are areas of permanently enriched nutrients such as <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a> areas and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coastal_watershed&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Coastal watershed (page does not exist)">coastal watersheds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Par99a_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Par99a-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In the nutrient-depleted areas of the oceans, such as the central gyres, <i>Synechococcus</i> is apparently always present, although only at low concentrations, ranging from a few to 4×10³ cells per ml.<sup id="cite_ref-Li95_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Li95-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ols90b_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ols90b-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bla92_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bla92-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cam93_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cam93-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Vertically <i>Synechococcus</i> is usually relatively equitably distributed throughout the <a href="/wiki/Mixed_layer" title="Mixed layer">mixed layer</a> and exhibits an affinity for the higher-light areas. Below the mixed layer, cell concentrations rapidly decline. Vertical profiles are strongly influenced by hydrologic conditions and can be very variable both seasonally and spatially. Overall, <i>Synechococcus</i> abundance often parallels that of <i>Prochlorococcus</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Water_column" title="Water column">water column</a>. In the Pacific <a href="/w/index.php?title=High-nutrient,_low-chlorophyll&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="High-nutrient, low-chlorophyll (page does not exist)">high-nutrient, low-chlorophyll</a> zone and in temperate open seas where stratification was recently established both profiles parallel each other and exhibit abundance maxima just about the subsurface chlorophyll maximum.<sup id="cite_ref-Li95_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Li95-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ols90b_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ols90b-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The factors controlling the abundance of <i>Synechococcus</i> still remain poorly understood, especially considering that even in the most nutrient-depleted regions of the <a href="/wiki/Ocean_gyre" title="Ocean gyre">central gyres</a>, where cell abundances are often very low, population growth rates are often high and not drastically limited.<sup id="cite_ref-Par99a_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Par99a-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Factors such as grazing, viral mortality, genetic variability, light adaptation, and temperature, as well as nutrients are certainly involved, but remain to be investigated on a rigorous and global scale. Despite the uncertainties,  a relationship probably exists between ambient nitrogen concentrations and <i>Synechococcus</i> abundance,<sup id="cite_ref-Par99a_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Par99a-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bla92_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bla92-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> with an inverse relationship to <i>Prochlorococcus</i><sup id="cite_ref-Cam93_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cam93-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> in the upper <a href="/wiki/Euphotic_zone" class="mw-redirect" title="Euphotic zone">euphotic zone</a>, where light is not limiting. One environment where <i>Synechococcus</i> thrives particularly well is coastal plumes of major rivers.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Such <a href="/wiki/Plume_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Plume (fluid dynamics)">plumes</a> are coastally enriched with nutrients such as nitrate and phosphate, which drives large <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton_bloom" class="mw-redirect" title="Phytoplankton bloom">phytoplankton blooms</a>. High productivity in coastal river <a href="/wiki/Plume_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Plume (fluid dynamics)">plumes</a> is often associated with large populations of <i>Synechococcus</i> and elevated form IA (cyanobacterial) <i>rbcL</i> mRNA.
</p><p><i>Prochlorococcus</i> is thought to be at least 100 times more abundant than <i>Synechococcus</i> in warm oligotrophic waters.<sup id="cite_ref-Par99a_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Par99a-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Assuming average cellular carbon concentrations, it has thus been estimated that <i>Prochlorococcus</i> accounts for at least 22 times more carbon in these waters, thus may be of much greater significance to the global <a href="/wiki/Carbon_cycle" title="Carbon cycle">carbon cycle</a> than <i>Synechococcus</i>.
</p>
