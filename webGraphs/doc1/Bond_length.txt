<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Bond length</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Molecular_geometry" title="Molecular geometry">molecular geometry</a>, <b>bond length</b> or <b>bond distance</b> is the average distance between nuclei of two <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">bonded</a> <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">molecule</a>. It is a <a href="/wiki/Transferability_(chemistry)" title="Transferability (chemistry)">transferable property</a> of a bond between atoms of fixed types, relatively independent of the rest of the molecule.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Explanation">Explanation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bond_length&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Explanation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bond length is related to <a href="/wiki/Bond_order" title="Bond order">bond order</a>: when more <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electrons</a> participate in bond formation the bond is shorter. Bond length is also inversely related to <a href="/wiki/Bond_strength" class="mw-redirect" title="Bond strength">bond strength</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bond_dissociation_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Bond dissociation energy">bond dissociation energy</a>: all other factors being equal, a stronger bond will be shorter. In a bond between two identical atoms, half the bond distance is equal to the <a href="/wiki/Covalent_radius" title="Covalent radius">covalent radius</a>.
</p><p>Bond lengths are measured in the solid phase by means of <a href="/wiki/X-ray_diffraction" class="mw-redirect" title="X-ray diffraction">X-ray diffraction</a>, or approximated in the gas phase by <a href="/wiki/Microwave_spectroscopy" title="Microwave spectroscopy">microwave spectroscopy</a>. A bond between a given pair of atoms may vary between different molecules. For example, the carbon to hydrogen bonds in <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> are different from those in <a href="/wiki/Methyl_chloride" class="mw-redirect" title="Methyl chloride">methyl chloride</a>. It is however possible to make generalizations when the general structure is the same.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bond_lengths_of_carbon_with_other_elements">Bond lengths of carbon with other elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bond_length&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Bond lengths of carbon with other elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A table with experimental <a href="/wiki/Single_bond" title="Single bond">single bonds</a> for carbon to other elements<sup class="reference plainlinks nourlexpansion" id="ref_1"><a class="external autonumber" href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_length#endnote_1">[1]</a></sup> is given below. Bond lengths are given in <a href="/wiki/Picometer" class="mw-redirect" title="Picometer">picometers</a>. By approximation the bond distance between two different atoms is the sum of the individual <a href="/wiki/Covalent_radius" title="Covalent radius">covalent radii</a> (these are given in the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> articles for each element). As a general trend, bond distances <i>decrease</i> across the <i>row</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">periodic table</a> and <i>increase</i> down a <i>group</i>. This trend is identical to that of the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_radius" title="Atomic radius">atomic radius</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bond_lengths_in_organic_compounds">Bond lengths in organic compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bond_length&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bond lengths in organic compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The bond length between two atoms in a molecule depends not only on the atoms but also on such factors as the <a href="/wiki/Orbital_hybridization" class="mw-redirect" title="Orbital hybridization">orbital hybridization</a> and the electronic and <a href="/wiki/Steric_effects" title="Steric effects">steric</a> nature of the <a href="/wiki/Substituent" title="Substituent">substituents</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Carbon%E2%80%93carbon_bond" title="Carbon–carbon bond">carbon–carbon (C–C) bond</a> length in diamond is 154 pm, which is also the largest bond length that exists for ordinary carbon covalent bonds. Since one atomic unit of length(i.e., a Bohr radius) is 52.9177 pm, the C–C bond length is 2.91 atomic units, or approximately three  Bohr radii long.
</p><p>Unusually long bond lengths do exist. In one compound, <a href="/wiki/Tricyclobutabenzene" title="Tricyclobutabenzene">tricyclobutabenzene</a>, a bond length of 160 pm is reported. The current record holder is another cyclobutabenzene with length 174 pm based on <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">X-ray crystallography</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In this type of compound the cyclobutane ring would force 90° angles on the carbon atoms connected to the benzene ring where they ordinarily have angles of 120°.
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a href="/wiki/File:Unusualbondlength.png" class="image" title="Cyclobutabenzene with a bond length in red of 174 pm"><img alt="Cyclobutabenzene with a bond length in red of 174 pm" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Unusualbondlength.png/200px-Unusualbondlength.png" width="200" height="157" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6d/Unusualbondlength.png 1.5x" data-file-width="299" data-file-height="234" /></a></div></div>
<p>The existence of a very long C–C bond length of up to 290 pm is claimed in a <a href="/wiki/Dimer_(chemistry)" title="Dimer (chemistry)">dimer</a> of two <a href="/wiki/Tetracyanoethylene" title="Tetracyanoethylene">tetracyanoethylene</a> dianions, although this concerns a 2-electron-4-center bond.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This type of bonding has also been observed in neutral <a href="/wiki/Phenalene" title="Phenalene">phenalenyl</a> dimers. The bond lengths of these so-called "pancake bonds"<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> are up to 305 pm.
</p><p>Shorter than average C–C bond distances are also possible: <a href="/wiki/Alkene" title="Alkene">alkenes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alkyne" title="Alkyne">alkynes</a> have bond lengths of respectively 133 and 120 pm due to increased <a href="/wiki/Orbital_hybridization" class="mw-redirect" title="Orbital hybridization">s-character</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sigma_bond" title="Sigma bond">sigma bond</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> all bonds have the same length: 139 pm. Carbon–carbon single bonds increased s-character is also notable in the central bond of <a href="/wiki/Diacetylene" title="Diacetylene">diacetylene</a> (137 pm) and that of a certain <a href="/wiki/Tetrahedrane" title="Tetrahedrane">tetrahedrane dimer</a> (144 pm).
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Propionitrile" title="Propionitrile">propionitrile</a> the <a href="/wiki/Cyano" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyano">cyano</a> group withdraws electrons, also resulting in a reduced bond length (144 pm). Squeezing a C–C bond is also possible by application of <a href="/wiki/Steric_strain" class="mw-redirect" title="Steric strain">strain</a>. An unusual organic compound exists called <a href="/wiki/In-methylcyclophane" class="mw-redirect" title="In-methylcyclophane">In-methylcyclophane</a> with a very short bond distance of 147 pm for the methyl group being squeezed between a <a href="/wiki/Triptycene" title="Triptycene">triptycene</a> and a phenyl group. In an <a href="/wiki/In_silico" title="In silico">in silico</a> experiment a bond distance of 136 pm was estimated for <a href="/wiki/Neopentane" title="Neopentane">neopentane</a> locked up in <a href="/wiki/Fullerene" title="Fullerene">fullerene</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The smallest theoretical C–C single bond obtained in this study is 131 pm for a hypothetical tetrahedrane derivative.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The same study also estimated that stretching or squeezing the C–C bond in an <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">ethane</a> molecule by 5 pm required 2.8 or 3.5 <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">kJ</a>/<a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mol</a>, respectively. Stretching or squeezing the same bond by 15 pm required an estimated 21.9 or 37.7 kJ/mol.
</p>
