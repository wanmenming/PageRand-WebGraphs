<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Earth Charter</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Earth Charter</b> is an international declaration of fundamental values and principles considered useful by its supporters for building a just, sustainable, and peaceful global society in the 21st century. Created by a global consultation process, and endorsed by organizations representing millions of people, the Charter "seeks to inspire in all peoples a sense of global interdependence and shared responsibility for the well-being of the human family, the greater community of life, and future generations."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It calls upon humanity to help create a global partnership at a critical juncture in history. The Earth Charter's ethical vision proposes that <a href="/wiki/Environmental_protection" title="Environmental protection">environmental protection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a>, equitable <a href="/wiki/Human_development_(humanity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Human development (humanity)">human development</a>, and <a href="/wiki/World_peace" title="World peace">peace</a> are interdependent and indivisible. The Charter attempts to provide a new framework for thinking about and addressing these issues. The <a href="/wiki/Earth_Charter_Initiative" title="Earth Charter Initiative">Earth Charter Initiative</a> organization exists to promote the Charter.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_Charter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The idea of the Earth Charter originated in 1987, by <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Strong" title="Maurice Strong">Maurice Strong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a> as members of The <a href="/wiki/Club_of_Rome" title="Club of Rome">Club of Rome</a>, when the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Commission_on_Environment_and_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="World Commission on Environment and Development">World Commission on Environment and Development</a> called for a new charter to guide the transition to <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>. In 1992, the need for a charter was urged by then-<a href="/wiki/Secretary_General_of_the_United_Nations" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary General of the United Nations">Secretary General</a> <a href="/wiki/Boutros_Boutros-Ghali" title="Boutros Boutros-Ghali">Boutros Boutros-Ghali</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> <a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit" title="Earth Summit">Earth Summit</a>, but the time for such a declaration was not believed to be right. The <a href="/wiki/Rio_Declaration" class="mw-redirect" title="Rio Declaration">Rio Declaration</a> became the statement of the achievable consensus at that time. In 1994, <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Strong" title="Maurice Strong">Maurice Strong</a> (Chairman of the Earth Summit) and <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Gorbachev" title="Mikhail Gorbachev">Mikhail Gorbachev</a>, working through organizations they each founded (the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Earth Council (page does not exist)">Earth Council</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green_Cross_International" title="Green Cross International">Green Cross International</a> respectively), restarted the Earth Charter as a <a href="/wiki/Civil_society" title="Civil society">civil society</a> initiative, with the help of the government of the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mstrong_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mstrong-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Strong died in November 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-mstrong_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mstrong-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>"The Ark of Hope was created for a celebration of the Earth Charter held at Shelburne Farms, Vermont on September 9, 2001."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Drafting">Drafting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_Charter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Drafting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The drafting of the text was done during a six-year worldwide consultation process (1994–2000), overseen by the independent Earth Charter Commission, which was convened by Strong and Gorbachev with the purpose of developing a global consensus on values and principles for a sustainable future. The Commission continues to serve as the steward of the Earth Charter text.
</p><p>One of the members of the Earth Charter Commission and Steering Committee was <a href="/wiki/Steven_Clark_Rockefeller" title="Steven Clark Rockefeller">Steven Clark Rockefeller</a>, who, among other things is professor emeritus of Religion at <a href="/wiki/Middlebury_College" title="Middlebury College">Middlebury College</a> and an advisory trustee of the <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Brothers_Fund" title="Rockefeller Brothers Fund">Rockefeller Brothers Fund</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> According to a 2001 interview with Rockefeller,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> he "chaired the Earth Charter international drafting committee". Other members included <a href="/wiki/Amadou_Toumani_Tour%C3%A9" title="Amadou Toumani Touré">Amadou Toumani Touré</a> (Mali), <a href="/wiki/Princess_Basma_bint_Talal" title="Princess Basma bint Talal">Princess Basma bint Talal</a> (Jordan), <a href="/wiki/Mohamed_Sahnoun" title="Mohamed Sahnoun">Mohamed Sahnoun</a> (Algeria), <a href="/wiki/A._T._Ariyaratne" title="A. T. Ariyaratne">A. T. Ariyaratne</a> (Sri Lanka), <a href="/wiki/Wakako_Hironaka" title="Wakako Hironaka">Wakako Hironaka</a> (Japan), <a href="/wiki/Erna_Witoelar" title="Erna Witoelar">Erna Witoelar</a> (Indonesia), <a href="/wiki/Ruud_Lubbers" title="Ruud Lubbers">Ruud Lubbers</a> (The Netherlands), <a href="/wiki/Federico_Mayor" class="mw-redirect" title="Federico Mayor">Federico Mayor</a> (Spain), <a href="/wiki/Mercedes_Sosa" title="Mercedes Sosa">Mercedes Sosa</a> (Argentina), <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_Boff" title="Leonardo Boff">Leonardo Boff</a> (Brazil), <a href="/wiki/Yolanda_Kakabadse" title="Yolanda Kakabadse">Yolanda Kakabadse</a> (Ecuador), <a href="/wiki/Shridath_Ramphal" title="Shridath Ramphal">Shridath Ramphal</a> (Guyana), <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_May" title="Elizabeth May">Elizabeth May</a> (Canada), <a href="/wiki/Severn_Cullis-Suzuki" title="Severn Cullis-Suzuki">Severn Cullis-Suzuki</a> (Canada), and others.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The final text of the Earth Charter was approved at a meeting of the Earth Charter Commission at the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> headquarters in Paris in March 2000. The official launch was on 29 June 2000 in a ceremony at The <a href="/wiki/Peace_Palace" title="Peace Palace">Peace Palace</a> in <a href="/wiki/The_Hague" title="The Hague">The Hague</a>, Netherlands. <a href="/wiki/Queen_Beatrix" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Beatrix">Queen Beatrix</a> of the Netherlands attended the ceremony.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contents">Contents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_Charter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Contents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The approximately 2,400 word document is divided into sections (called pillars), which have sixteen main principles containing sixty-one supporting principles.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  The document opens with a preamble and ends with a conclusion entitled “The Way Forward”.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Principles">Principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_Charter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The four pillars and sixteen principles of the Earth Charter are:<sup id="cite_ref-ECdoc_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ECdoc-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>I. Respect and Care for the Community of Life</b>
</p>
<dl><dd>1. Respect Earth and life in all its diversity.</dd>
<dd>2. Care for the community of life with understanding, compassion and love.</dd>
<dd>3. Build democratic societies that are just, participatory, sustainable and peaceful.</dd>
<dd>4. Secure Earth's bounty and beauty for present and future generations.</dd></dl>
<p><b>II. Ecological Integrity</b>
</p>
<dl><dd>5. Protect and restore the integrity of Earth's ecological systems, with special concern for biological diversity and the natural processes that sustain life.</dd>
<dd>6. Prevent harm as the best method of environmental protection and, when knowledge is limited, apply a precautionary approach.</dd>
<dd>7. Adopt patterns of production, consumption and reproduction that safeguard Earth's regenerative capacities, human rights and community well-being.</dd>
<dd>8. Advance the study of ecological sustainability and promote the open exchange and wide application of the knowledge acquired.</dd></dl>
<p><b>III. Social and Economic Justice</b>
</p>
<dl><dd>9. Eradicate poverty as an ethical, social and environmental imperative.</dd>
<dd>10. Ensure that economic activities and institutions at all levels promote human development in an equitable and sustainable manner.</dd>
<dd>11. Affirm gender equality and equity as prerequisites to sustainable development and ensure universal access to education, health care and economic opportunity.</dd>
<dd>12. Uphold the right of all, without discrimination, to a natural and social environment supportive of human dignity, bodily health and spiritual well-being, with special attention to the rights of indigenous peoples and minorities.</dd></dl>
<p><b>IV. Democracy, Nonviolence, and Peace</b>
</p>
<dl><dd>13. Strengthen democratic institutions at all levels, and provide transparency and accountability in governance, inclusive participation in decision-making, and access to justice.</dd>
<dd>14. Integrate into formal education and lifelong learning the knowledge, values and skills needed for a sustainable way of life.</dd>
<dd>15. Treat all living beings with respect and consideration.</dd>
<dd>16. Promote a culture of tolerance, nonviolence and peace.</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction">Reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_Charter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Charter has been formally endorsed by organizations representing millions of people, including the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> over 250 universities around the world,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/World_Conservation_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="World Conservation Union">World Conservation Union</a> of IUCN, the Indian National Capital Territory of <a href="/wiki/Delhi" title="Delhi">Delhi</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> the 2001 <a href="/wiki/United_States_Conference_of_Mayors" title="United States Conference of Mayors">U.S. Conference of Mayors</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and dozens of youth organizations.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Various religious groups from a wide range of religions support the Earth Charter. The <a href="/wiki/Soka_Gakkai_International" title="Soka Gakkai International">Soka Gakkai International</a>, representing more than 12 million Buddhists worldwide, has supported the Earth Charter since its inception.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Unitarian_Universalist_Association" title="Unitarian Universalist Association">Unitarian Universalist Association of Congregations</a> representing over 1000 Unitarian Universalist congregations in the United States supports the measure.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  The official body of the <a href="/wiki/Baha%27i_Faith" class="mw-redirect" title="Baha&#39;i Faith">Baha'i Faith</a> religion reacted by saying "While not officially endorsing the Earth Charter, the Baha'i International Community considers the effort toward drafting it and activities in support of its essential objectives to be highly commendable, and it will continue to participate in related activities, such as conferences, forums and the like."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/World_Pantheist_Movement" title="World Pantheist Movement">World Pantheist Movement</a>, which supports a naturalistic view of religion, endorses the plan.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/Leadership_Conference_of_Women_Religious" title="Leadership Conference of Women Religious">Leadership Conference of Women Religious</a>, a Catholic organization in the United States approved the measure in 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_Diocese_of_Newark" title="Episcopal Diocese of Newark">Episcopal Diocese of Newark</a> (New Jersey), an Episcopalian Christian organization, endorsed the Earth Charter in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 1992, more than 650 representatives of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">indigenous peoples</a> adopted their own 109-point Indigenous Peoples Earth Charter.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Representatives of indigenous peoples also participated in the Earth Charter consultations in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In 2000, the Russian Association of the Indigenous Peoples of the North (RAIPON), representing 31 indigenous peoples living in Siberia and far eastern Russia, formally endorsed the Earth Charter.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mayor Hsu of <a href="/wiki/Tainan_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Tainan City">Tainan</a>, a city of 750,000 in <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, endorsed the charter in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The cities of <a href="/wiki/Corvallis,_Oregon" title="Corvallis, Oregon">Corvallis</a> (Oregon), Berkeley (California), Pickering (Canada) and 21 towns in Vermont have endorsed the measure.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Nine other towns in Vermont rejected measures endorsing the Earth Charter.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Engineers_Without_Borders" title="Engineers Without Borders">Engineers Without Borders</a>, an international association whose mission is to help its member groups assist poor communities in their respective countries and around the world, also endorses the Earth Charter.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>  The Green Party of <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a> supports the plan.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/African_Conservation_Foundation" title="African Conservation Foundation">African Conservation Foundation</a> describes the Earth Charter movement as a "partner".<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the UK, Bournemouth Borough Council endorsed the Charter in 2008.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The Charter has received opposition from several groups. For example, in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, members of religious groups, such as the <a href="/wiki/Christian_right" title="Christian right">Religious Right</a> have objected to the document on the grounds that it is <a href="/wiki/Secularism" title="Secularism">secular</a>, and espouses <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Please, provide a reliable source for this assertion. Nowhere on the Internet, I could find the mentioned objections. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In addition, some conservatives<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2009)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> cite an informal comment by Mikhail Gorbachev that the document is "a kind of <a href="/wiki/Ten_Commandments" title="Ten Commandments">Ten Commandments</a>" and point to the fact that at the 2002 <a href="/wiki/World_Summit_on_Sustainable_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="World Summit on Sustainable Development">World Summit on Sustainable Development</a> in <a href="/wiki/Johannesburg" title="Johannesburg">Johannesburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, a copy of the document was placed symbolically in an "Ark of Hope" — an independent project by the American artist Sally Linder.
</p><p>Earth Charter International, the organization responsible for promoting the Charter, states in its literature that the Earth Charter is respectful and inclusive of all religious traditions. They say that the Charter itself makes no statements to support claims of intent to supplant any of the world's religions or to create a world government. ECI asserts that the Charter is a statement of common ethical values towards sustainability, that recognizes humanity's shared responsibility to the Earth and to each other.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
