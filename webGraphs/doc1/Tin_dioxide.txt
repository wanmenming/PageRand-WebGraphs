<p><b>Tin(IV) Oxide</b>, also known as <b>stannic oxide</b>, is the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> <b>SnO<sub>2</sub></b>. The mineral form of SnO<sub>2</sub> is called <a href="/wiki/Cassiterite" title="Cassiterite">cassiterite</a>, and this is the main ore of <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> With many other names, this <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> of tin is an important material in tin chemistry. It is a colourless, <a href="/wiki/Diamagnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Diamagnetic">diamagnetic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amphoteric" class="mw-redirect" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a> solid.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tin(IV)_Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Diossido_di_stagno_7.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Diossido_di_stagno_7.jpg/200px-Diossido_di_stagno_7.jpg" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Diossido_di_stagno_7.jpg/300px-Diossido_di_stagno_7.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Diossido_di_stagno_7.jpg/400px-Diossido_di_stagno_7.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3488" data-file-height="2616" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Diossido_di_stagno_7.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tin (IV) oxide fibers (<a href="/wiki/Optical_microscope" title="Optical microscope">optical microscope</a>)</div></div></div>
<p>Tin(IV) oxide crystallises with the <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a> structure.  As such the tin atoms are six coordinate and the oxygen atoms three coordinate.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> SnO<sub>2</sub> is usually regarded as an oxygen-deficient <a href="/wiki/N-type_semiconductor" class="mw-redirect" title="N-type semiconductor">n-type semiconductor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Hydrous forms of SnO<sub>2</sub> have been described as <b>stannic acid</b>. Such materials appear to be hydrated particles of SnO<sub>2</sub> where the composition reflects the particle size.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tin(IV)_Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tin(IV) oxide occurs naturally. Synthetic tin(IV) oxide is produced by burning tin metal in air.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Annual production is in the range of 10 kilotons.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> SnO<sub>2</sub> is reduced industrially to the metal with carbon in a <a href="/wiki/Reverberatory_furnace" title="Reverberatory furnace">reverberatory furnace</a> at 1200–1300&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amphoterism">Amphoterism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tin(IV)_Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Amphoterism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although SnO<sub>2</sub> is insoluble in water, it is <a href="/wiki/Amphoteric" class="mw-redirect" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a>, dissolving in base and acid.<sup id="cite_ref-Sherwood_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sherwood-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> "Stannic acid" refers to hydrated tin (IV) oxide, SnO<sub>2</sub>, which is also called "stannic hydroxide."
</p><p>Tin oxides dissolve in acids. Halogen acids attack SnO<sub>2</sub> to give hexahalostannates,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> such as [SnI<sub>6</sub>]<sup>2−</sup>. One report describes reacting a sample in refluxing <a href="/wiki/Hydriodic_acid" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydriodic acid">HI</a> for many hours.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>SnO<sub>2</sub> + 6 HI → H<sub>2</sub>SnI<sub>6</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Similarly, SnO<sub>2</sub> dissolves in sulfuric acid to give the sulfate:<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>SnO<sub>2</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> → Sn(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>SnO<sub>2</sub> dissolves in strong base to give "<a href="/wiki/Stannate" title="Stannate">stannates</a>," with the nominal formula <a href="/wiki/Sodium_stannate" title="Sodium stannate">Na<sub>2</sub>SnO<sub>3</sub></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Dissolving the solidified SnO<sub>2</sub>/NaOH melt in water gives Na<sub>2</sub>[Sn(OH)<sub>6</sub>]<sub>2</sub>, "preparing salt," which is used in the dye industry.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tin(IV)_Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In conjunction with vanadium oxide, it is used as a catalyst for the oxidation of aromatic compounds in the synthesis of <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acids</a> and acid anhydrides.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ceramic_glazes">Ceramic glazes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tin(IV)_Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ceramic glazes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tin(IV) oxide has long been used as an <a href="/wiki/Opacifier" title="Opacifier">opacifier</a> and as a white colorant in <a href="/wiki/Ceramic_glaze" title="Ceramic glaze">ceramic glazes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> This has probably led to the discovery of the pigment <a href="/wiki/Lead-tin-yellow" title="Lead-tin-yellow">lead-tin-yellow</a>, which was produced using tin(IV) oxide as a compound.<sup id="cite_ref-Kühn1967_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kühn1967-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The use of tin(IV) oxide has been particularly common in glazes for <a href="/wiki/Earthenware" title="Earthenware">earthenware</a>, sanitaryware and wall tiles; see the articles <a href="/wiki/Tin-glazing" title="Tin-glazing">tin-glazing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tin-glazed_pottery" title="Tin-glazed pottery">Tin-glazed pottery</a>. Tin oxide remains in suspension in vitreous matrix of the fired glazes, and, with its high refractive index being sufficiently different from the matrix, light is scattered, and hence increases the <a href="/wiki/Opacity_(optics)" title="Opacity (optics)">opacity</a> of the glaze. The degree of dissolution increases with the firing temperature, and hence the extent of opacity diminishes.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Although dependent on the other constituents the solubility of tin oxide in glaze melts is generally low. Its solubility is increased by Na<sub>2</sub>O, K<sub>2</sub>O and B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, and reduced by CaO, BaO, ZnO, Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, and to a limited extent PbO.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>SnO<sub>2</sub> has been used as pigment in the manufacture of glasses, enamels and ceramic glazes. Pure SnO<sub>2</sub> gives a milky white colour; other colours are achieved when mixed with other metallic oxides e.g. <a href="/wiki/Vanadium(V)_oxide" title="Vanadium(V) oxide">V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub></a> yellow; <a href="/wiki/Chromium(III)_oxide" title="Chromium(III) oxide">Cr<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></a> pink; and <a href="/wiki/Antimony_pentoxide" title="Antimony pentoxide">Sb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub></a> grey blue.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polishing">Polishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tin(IV)_Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Polishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tin(IV) oxide can be used as a polishing powder,<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_11-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> sometimes in mixtures also with lead oxide, for polishing glass, jewelery, marble and silver.<sup id="cite_ref-mfa_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mfa-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Tin(IV) oxide for this use is sometimes called as "putty powder"<sup id="cite_ref-Sherwood_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sherwood-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> or "jeweler's putty".<sup id="cite_ref-mfa_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mfa-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Glass_coatings">Glass coatings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tin(IV)_Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Glass coatings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>SnO<sub>2</sub> coatings can be applied using <a href="/wiki/Chemical_vapor_deposition" title="Chemical vapor deposition">chemical vapor deposition</a>, vapour deposition techniques that employ <a href="/wiki/Tin(IV)_chloride" title="Tin(IV) chloride">SnCl<sub>4</sub></a><sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> or organotin trihalides<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> e.g. <a href="/wiki/Butyltin_trichloride" title="Butyltin trichloride">butyltin trichloride</a> as the volatile agent. This technique is used to coat glass bottles with a thin (&lt;0.1 μm) layer of SnO<sub>2</sub>, which helps to adhere a subsequent, protective polymer coating such as polyethylene to the glass.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Thicker layers doped with Sb or F ions are electrically conducting and used in electroluminescent devices.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_sensing">Gas sensing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tin(IV)_Oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Gas sensing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>SnO<sub>2</sub> is used in <a href="/wiki/Gas_detector" title="Gas detector">sensors of combustible gases</a> including <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_detector" title="Carbon monoxide detector">carbon monoxide detectors</a>. In these the sensor area is heated to a constant temperature (few hundred °C) and in the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Combustibility" class="mw-redirect" title="Combustibility">combustible</a> gas the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_resistivity" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical resistivity">electrical resistivity</a> drops.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Doping_(semiconductors)" class="mw-redirect" title="Doping (semiconductors)">Doping</a> with various compounds has been investigated (e.g. with <a href="/wiki/Copper(II)_oxide" title="Copper(II) oxide">CuO</a><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>). <a href="/wiki/Doping_(semiconductors)" class="mw-redirect" title="Doping (semiconductors)">Doping</a> with cobalt and manganese, gives a material that can be used in e.g. high voltage <a href="/wiki/Varistor" title="Varistor">varistors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Tin(IV) oxide can be doped with the oxides of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> or <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
