<p>The <b>carbonic anhydrases</b> (or <b>carbonate dehydratases</b>) form a family of <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> that <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalyst">catalyze</a> the interconversion between <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dissociation_(chemistry)" title="Dissociation (chemistry)">dissociated</a> ions of <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a> (i.e.  <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_ion" title="Hydrogen ion">hydrogen ions</a>). This interconversion is a <a href="/wiki/Reversible_reaction" title="Reversible reaction">reversible reaction</a> and the enzyme catalyzes both reactions, forward and reverse.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid9336012_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9336012-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Active_site" title="Active site">active site</a> of most carbonic anhydrases contains a <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> ion; they are therefore classified as <a href="/wiki/Metalloprotein" title="Metalloprotein">metalloenzymes</a>.
</p><p>The most important functions of this enzyme in <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> is to maintain <a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_homeostasis" title="Acid–base homeostasis">acid-base balance</a> in <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> and other tissues, and to help transport carbon dioxide out of tissues.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Reaction_rate" title="Reaction rate">reaction rate</a> of carbonic anhydrase is one of the fastest of all enzymes, and its rate is typically limited by the <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffusion</a> rate of its <a href="/wiki/Substrate_(biochemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Substrate (biochemistry)">substrates</a>.
Typical catalytic rates of the different forms of this enzyme ranging between 10<sup>4</sup> and 10<sup>6</sup> reactions per second.<sup id="cite_ref-Lindskog_1997_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lindskog_1997-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The reverse reaction is relatively slow (kinetics in the 15-second range) in the absence of a catalyst.  This is why a carbonated drink does not instantly degas when opening the container; however it will rapidly degas in the mouth when it comes in contact with carbonic anhydrase that is contained in saliva.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid9784398_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9784398-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An anhydrase is defined as an enzyme that catalyzes the removal of a water molecule from a compound, and so it is this "reverse" reaction that gives carbonic anhydrase its name, because it removes a water molecule from carbonic acid.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_anhydrase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carbonic_anhydrase_1CA2_active_site.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Carbonic_anhydrase_1CA2_active_site.png/200px-Carbonic_anhydrase_1CA2_active_site.png" width="200" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Carbonic_anhydrase_1CA2_active_site.png/300px-Carbonic_anhydrase_1CA2_active_site.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Carbonic_anhydrase_1CA2_active_site.png/400px-Carbonic_anhydrase_1CA2_active_site.png 2x" data-file-width="1240" data-file-height="1240" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Carbonic_anhydrase_1CA2_active_site.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Close-up rendering of active site of human carbonic anhydrase II, showing three <a href="/wiki/Histidine" title="Histidine">histidine</a> residues and a <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> group coordinating (dashed lines) the <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> ion at center. From <span class="plainlinks"><a href="/wiki/Protein_Data_Bank" title="Protein Data Bank">PDB</a>: <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.rcsb.org/structure/1CA2">1CA2</a></span>&#8203;.</div></div></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> <a href="/wiki/Prosthetic_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Prosthetic group">prosthetic group</a> in the enzyme is coordinated in three positions by <a href="/wiki/Histidine" title="Histidine">histidine</a> <a href="/wiki/Side_chain" title="Side chain">side-chains</a>. The fourth coordination position is occupied by water. This causes polarisation of the hydrogen-oxygen bond, making the oxygen slightly more positive, thereby weakening the bond.
</p><p>A fourth histidine is placed close to the substrate of water molecule and accepts a <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a>, in an example of  <a href="/w/index.php?title=General_acid&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="General acid (page does not exist)">general acid</a> – <a href="/w/index.php?title=General_base&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="General base (page does not exist)">general base</a> <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalysis</a> (see the article "<a href="/wiki/Acid_catalysis" title="Acid catalysis">Acid catalysis</a>"). This leaves a <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide" title="Hydroxide">hydroxide</a> attached to the zinc.
</p><p>The active site also contains a specificity pocket for carbon dioxide, bringing it close to the hydroxide group. This allows the electron-rich hydroxide to attack the carbon dioxide, forming bicarbonate.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Families">Families</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_anhydrase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Families">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carbonic_anhydrase_1CA2.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Carbonic_anhydrase_1CA2.png/200px-Carbonic_anhydrase_1CA2.png" width="200" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Carbonic_anhydrase_1CA2.png/300px-Carbonic_anhydrase_1CA2.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Carbonic_anhydrase_1CA2.png/400px-Carbonic_anhydrase_1CA2.png 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Carbonic_anhydrase_1CA2.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ribbon diagram of human carbonic anhydrase II. <a href="/wiki/Active_site" title="Active site">Active site</a> <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> ion visible at center. From <span class="plainlinks"><a href="/wiki/Protein_Data_Bank" title="Protein Data Bank">PDB</a>: <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.rcsb.org/structure/1CA2">1CA2</a></span>&#8203;.</div></div></div>
<p>There are at least five distinct CA families (α, β, γ, δ and ζ). These families have no significant <a href="/wiki/Protein_sequence" class="mw-redirect" title="Protein sequence">amino acid sequence</a> similarity and in most cases are thought to be an example of <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergent evolution</a>. The α-CAs are found in humans.
</p>
<h3><span id=".CE.B1-CA"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="α-CA">α-CA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_anhydrase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: α-CA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">Vertebrates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> have this family of CAs.
</p><p>The CA enzymes found in <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a> are divided into four broad subgroups,<sup id="cite_ref-pmid11875253_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid11875253-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> which, in turn consist of several isoforms:
</p>
<ul><li>the <a href="/wiki/Cytosol" title="Cytosol">cytosolic</a> CAs (CA-I, <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_anhydrase_II" title="Carbonic anhydrase II">CA-II</a>, CA-III, CA-VII and CA XIII) (<i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.genenames.org/data/hgnc_data.php?hgnc_id=1368">CA1</a></i>, <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.genenames.org/data/hgnc_data.php?hgnc_id=1373">CA2</a></i>, <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.genenames.org/data/hgnc_data.php?hgnc_id=1374">CA3</a></i>, <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.genenames.org/data/hgnc_data.php?hgnc_id=1381">CA7</a></i>, <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.genenames.org/data/hgnc_data.php?hgnc_id=14914">CA13</a></i>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mitochondrion" title="Mitochondrion">mitochondrial</a> CAs (CA-VA and CA-VB) (<i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.genenames.org/data/hgnc_data.php?match=CA5A">CA5A</a></i>, <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.genenames.org/data/hgnc_data.php?match=CA5B">CA5B</a></i>)</li>
<li>secreted CAs (CA-VI) (<i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.genenames.org/data/hgnc_data.php?hgnc_id=1380">CA6</a></i>)</li>
<li>membrane-associated CAs (CA-IV, CA-IX, CA-XII, CA-XIV and CA-XV) (<i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.genenames.org/data/hgnc_data.php?hgnc_id=1375">CA4</a></i>, <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.genenames.org/data/hgnc_data.php?hgnc_id=1383">CA9</a></i>, <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.genenames.org/data/hgnc_data.php?hgnc_id=1371">CA12</a></i>, <i><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.genenames.org/data/hgnc_data.php?hgnc_id=1372">CA14</a></i>)</li></ul>

<h3><span id=".CE.B2-CA"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="β-CA">β-CA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_anhydrase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: β-CA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most <a href="/wiki/Prokaryote" title="Prokaryote">prokaryotic</a> and plant <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplast</a> CAs belong to the beta family.
Two <a href="/wiki/Sequence_motif" title="Sequence motif">signature patterns</a> for this family have been identified:
</p>
<ul><li>C-[SA]-D-S-R-[LIVM]-x-[AP]</li>
<li>[EQ]-[YF]-A-[LIVM]-x(2)-[LIVM]-x(4)-[LIVMF](3)-x-G-H-x(2)-C-G</li></ul>
<h3><span id=".CE.B3-CA"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="γ-CA">γ-CA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_anhydrase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: γ-CA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The gamma class of CAs come from <a href="/wiki/Methanogen" title="Methanogen">methanogens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane-producing_bacteria" class="mw-redirect" title="Methane-producing bacteria">methane-producing bacteria</a> that grow in hot springs.
</p>
<h3><span id=".CE.B4-CA"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="δ-CA">δ-CA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_anhydrase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: δ-CA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The delta class of CAs has been described in <a href="/wiki/Diatom" title="Diatom">diatoms</a>. The distinction of this class of CA has recently<sup id="cite_ref-Sawaya_2006_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sawaya_2006-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> come into question, however.
</p>
<h3><span id=".CE.B6-CA"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="ζ-CA">ζ-CA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_anhydrase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: ζ-CA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The zeta class of CAs occurs exclusively in <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> in a few <a href="/wiki/Chemolithotroph" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemolithotroph">chemolithotrophs</a> and marine <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a> that contain cso-<a href="/wiki/Carboxysome" title="Carboxysome">carboxysomes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid14729686_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid14729686-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Recent 3-dimensional analyses<sup id="cite_ref-Sawaya_2006_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sawaya_2006-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> suggest that ζ-CA bears some structural resemblance to β-CA, particularly near the metal ion site. Thus, the two forms may be distantly related, even though the underlying <a href="/wiki/Protein_sequence" class="mw-redirect" title="Protein sequence">amino acid sequence</a> has since diverged considerably.
</p>
<h3><span id=".CE.B7-CA"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="η-CA">η-CA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_anhydrase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: η-CA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The eta family of CAs was recently found in organisms of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Plasmodium" title="Plasmodium">Plasmodium</a></i>. These are a group of enzymes previously thought to belong to the alpha family of CAs, however it has been demonstrated that η-CAs have unique features, such as their metal ion coordination pattern.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid25168745_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid25168745-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_function">Structure and function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_anhydrase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Structure and function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several forms of carbonic anhydrase occur in nature. In the best-studied <i>α-carbonic anhydrase</i> form present in animals, the zinc ion is coordinated by the <a href="/wiki/Imidazole" title="Imidazole">imidazole</a> rings of 3 <a href="/wiki/Histidine" title="Histidine">histidine</a> residues, His94, His96, and His119.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The primary function of the enzyme in animals is to interconvert carbon dioxide and bicarbonate to maintain <a href="/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_homeostasis" title="Acid–base homeostasis">acid-base balance</a> in blood and other tissues, and to help transport carbon dioxide out of tissues.
</p><p>There are at least 14 different isoforms in mammals. <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">Plants</a> contain a different form called <i>β-carbonic anhydrase</i>, which, from an evolutionary standpoint, is a distinct enzyme, but participates in the same reaction and also uses a zinc ion in its active site.  In plants, carbonic anhydrase helps raise the concentration of CO<sub>2</sub> within the <a href="/wiki/Chloroplast" title="Chloroplast">chloroplast</a> in order to increase the carboxylation rate of the enzyme <a href="/wiki/RuBisCO" title="RuBisCO">RuBisCO</a>. This is the reaction that integrates CO<sub>2</sub> into <a href="/wiki/Organic_carbon" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic carbon">organic carbon</a> sugars during <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>, and can use only the CO<sub>2</sub> form of carbon, not carbonic acid or bicarbonate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cadmium-containing_carbonic_anhydrase">Cadmium-containing carbonic anhydrase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_anhydrase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cadmium-containing carbonic anhydrase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Marine <a href="/wiki/Diatom" title="Diatom">diatoms</a> have been found to express a new form of ζ carbonic anhydrase. <a href="/wiki/Thalassiosira_weissflogii" title="Thalassiosira weissflogii"><i>T. weissflogii</i></a>, a species of <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> common to many marine ecosystems, was found to contain carbonic anhydrase with a <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a> ion in place of zinc.<sup id="cite_ref-Park_et_al_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Park_et_al-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Previously, it had been believed that cadmium was a toxic metal with no biological function whatsoever. However, this species of phytoplankton appears to have adapted to the low levels of zinc in the ocean by using cadmium when there is not enough zinc.<sup id="cite_ref-Lane_et_al_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lane_et_al-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Although the concentration of cadmium in sea water is also low (about 1x10<sup>−16</sup> <a href="/wiki/Molar_concentration" title="Molar concentration">molar</a>), there is an environmental advantage to being able to use either metal depending on which is more available at the time. This type of carbonic anhydrase is therefore cambialistic, meaning it can interchange the metal in its active site with other metals (namely, zinc and cadmium).<sup id="cite_ref-Bertini_et_al_corrected_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bertini_et_al_corrected-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Similarities_to_other_carbonic_anhydrases">Similarities to other carbonic anhydrases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_anhydrase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Similarities to other carbonic anhydrases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The mechanism of cadmium carbonic anhydrase (CDCA) is essentially the same as that of other carbonic anhydrases in its conversion of carbon dioxide and water into bicarbonate and a proton.<sup id="cite_ref-Book_on_Cadmium_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Book_on_Cadmium-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, like the other carbonic anhydrases, CDCA makes the reaction go almost as fast as the diffusion rate of its substrates, and it can be inhibited by <a href="/wiki/Sulfonamide" title="Sulfonamide">sulfonamide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfamate" class="mw-redirect" title="Sulfamate">sulfamate</a> derivatives.<sup id="cite_ref-Book_on_Cadmium_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Book_on_Cadmium-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_from_other_carbonic_anhydrases">Differences from other carbonic anhydrases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_anhydrase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Differences from other carbonic anhydrases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unlike most other carbonic anhydrases, the active site metal ion is not bound by three histidine residues and a hydroxide ion. Instead, it is bound by two <a href="/wiki/Cysteine" title="Cysteine">cysteine</a> residues, one histidine residue, and a hydroxide ion, which is characteristic of β-CA.<sup id="cite_ref-Book_on_Cadmium_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Book_on_Cadmium-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Xu_et_al_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Xu_et_al-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Due to the fact that cadmium is a <a href="/wiki/HSAB_theory" title="HSAB theory">soft acid</a>, it will be more tightly bound by <a href="/wiki/HSAB_theory" title="HSAB theory">soft base</a> ligands.<sup id="cite_ref-Bertini_et_al_corrected_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bertini_et_al_corrected-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> atoms on the cysteine residues are soft bases, thus binding the cadmium more tightly than the nitrogen on histidine residues would. CDCA also has a three-dimensional folding structure that is unlike any other carbonic anhydrase, and its amino acid sequence is dissimilar to the other carbonic anhydrases.<sup id="cite_ref-Book_on_Cadmium_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Book_on_Cadmium-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> It is a <a href="/wiki/Monomer" title="Monomer">monomer</a> with three domains, each one identical in amino acid sequence and each one containing an active site with a metal ion.<sup id="cite_ref-Xu_et_al_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Xu_et_al-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another key difference between CDCA and the other carbonic anhydrases is that CDCA has a mechanism for switching out its cadmium ion for a zinc ion in the event that zinc becomes more available to the phytoplankton than cadmium. The active site of CDCA is essentially "gated" by a chain of nine <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> with <a href="/wiki/Glycine" title="Glycine">glycine</a> residues at positions 1 and 9. Normally, this gate remains closed and the cadmium ion is trapped inside. However, due to the flexibility and position of the glycine residues, this gate can be opened in order to remove the cadmium ion. A zinc ion can then be put in its place and the gate will close behind it.<sup id="cite_ref-Book_on_Cadmium_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Book_on_Cadmium-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> As a <a href="/wiki/HSAB_theory" title="HSAB theory">borderline acid</a>, zinc will not bind as tightly to the cysteine ligands as cadmium would, but the enzyme will still be active and reasonably efficient. The metal in the active site can be switched between zinc and cadmium depending on which one is more abundant at the time. It is the ability of CDCA to utilize either cadmium or zinc that likely gives <i>T. weissflogii</i> a survival advantage.<sup id="cite_ref-Lane_et_al_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lane_et_al-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport_of_cadmium">Transport of cadmium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_anhydrase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Transport of cadmium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cadmium is still considered lethal to phytoplankton in high amounts. Studies have shown that <i>T. weissflogii</i> has an initial toxic response to cadmium when exposed to it. The toxicity of the metal is reduced by the transcription and translation of <a href="/wiki/Phytochelatin" title="Phytochelatin">phytochelatin</a>, which are proteins that can bind and transport cadmium. Once bound by phytochelatin, cadmium is no longer toxic, and it can be safely transported to the CDCA enzyme.<sup id="cite_ref-Park_et_al_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Park_et_al-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It's also been shown that the uptake of cadmium via phytochelatin leads to a significant increase in CDCA expression.<sup id="cite_ref-Park_et_al_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Park_et_al-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CDCA-like_proteins">CDCA-like proteins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_anhydrase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: CDCA-like proteins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Other phytoplankton from different water sources have been tested for the presence of CDCA. It was found that many of them contain proteins that are homologous to the CDCA found in <i>T. weissflogii</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Park_et_al_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Park_et_al-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This includes species from <a href="/wiki/Great_Bay_(New_Jersey)" title="Great Bay (New Jersey)">Great Bay, New Jersey</a> as well as in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>. In all species tested, CDCA-like proteins showed high levels of expression even in high concentrations of zinc and in the absence of cadmium.<sup id="cite_ref-Park_et_al_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Park_et_al-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The similarity between these proteins and the CDCA expressed by <i>T. weissflogii</i> varied, but they were always at least 67% similar.<sup id="cite_ref-Park_et_al_15-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Park_et_al-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_applications">Industrial applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_anhydrase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Industrial applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_capture_and_sequestration">Carbon capture and sequestration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonic_anhydrase&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Carbon capture and sequestration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">Carbon capture and storage</a></div>
<p>Carbonic anhydrase may prove to be a cost-effective means of capturing carbon dioxide from industrial emitters, such as coal power plants. The biggest challenge engineers face implementing carbonic anhydrase for this use is the harsh conditions of the flue (exhaust) streams. Researchers have used directed evolution to create a form of carbonic anhydrase that can withstand temperatures up to 107&#160;°C and extreme alkalinity (pH &gt; 10).<sup id="cite_ref-:0_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> A pilot run with the more stable CA on a flue stream that consisted of 12–13% mol composition CO₂ had a capture rate of 63.6% over a 60-hour period with no noticeable effects in enzyme performance. CA was placed in a N-methyldiethanolamine (MDEA) solution where it served to increase the concentration difference (driving force) of CO<sub>2</sub> between the flue stream of the power plant and liquid phase in a liquid-gas contactor.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> According to Codexis, a research firm involved with the directed evolution of carbonic anhydrase, CA added to liquid-gas contactors would decrease the absorbed column size and desorber volume by 90% and 80% respectively, so capex costs for installing a typical CCS unit would fall by $146 million, making carbon capture a much more viable option for energy firms that use fossil fuels.
</p>
