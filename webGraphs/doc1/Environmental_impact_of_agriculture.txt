<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Environmental impact of agriculture</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Water_pollution_in_the_Wairarapa.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Water_pollution_in_the_Wairarapa.JPG/220px-Water_pollution_in_the_Wairarapa.JPG" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Water_pollution_in_the_Wairarapa.JPG/330px-Water_pollution_in_the_Wairarapa.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Water_pollution_in_the_Wairarapa.JPG/440px-Water_pollution_in_the_Wairarapa.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1944" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Water_pollution_in_the_Wairarapa.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">Water pollution</a> in a rural <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">stream</a> due to runoff from <a href="/wiki/Agriculture_in_New_Zealand" title="Agriculture in New Zealand">farming activity in New Zealand</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <b>environmental impact of agriculture</b> is the effect that different farming practices have on the ecosystems around them, and how those effects can be traced back to those practices. The environmental impact of agriculture varies based on the wide variety of <a href="/wiki/Agricultural" class="mw-redirect" title="Agricultural">agricultural</a> practices employed around the world. Ultimately, the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental impact">environmental impact</a> depends on the production practices of the system used by farmers. The connection between emissions into the environment and the farming system is indirect, as it also depends on other climate variables such as <a href="/wiki/Rainfall" class="mw-redirect" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a> and temperature.
</p><p>There are two types of indicators of environmental impact: "means-based", which is based on the farmer's production methods, and "effect-based", which is the impact that farming methods have on the farming system or on emissions to the environment. An example of a means-based indicator would be the quality of groundwater, that is effected by the amount of nitrogen applied to the soil. An indicator reflecting the loss of nitrate to groundwater would be effect-based.<sup id="cite_ref-sciencedirect.com_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sciencedirect.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The means-based evaluation looks at farmers' practices of agriculture, and the effect-based evaluation considers the actual effects of the agricultural system. For example, means-based analysis might look at pesticides and fertilization methods that farmers are using, and effect-based analysis would consider how much CO2 is being emitted or what the Nitrogen content of the soil is.<sup id="cite_ref-sciencedirect.com_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sciencedirect.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The environmental impact of agriculture involves a variety of factors from the <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, to water, the air, animal and soil variety, people, plants, and the food itself. Some of the environmental issues that are related to agriculture are <a href="#Climate_change">climate change</a>, <a href="#Deforestation">deforestation</a>, <a href="#Genetic_engineering">genetic engineering</a>, <a href="#Irrigation">irrigation</a> problems, <a href="#Pollutants">pollutants</a>, <a href="#Soil_degradation">soil degradation</a>, and <a href="#Waste">waste</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Negatives">Negatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Negatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">Climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_agriculture" title="Climate change and agriculture">Climate change and agriculture</a></div>  
<p><a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a> and agriculture are interrelated processes, both of which take place on a worldwide scale. <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Global warming</a> is projected to have significant impacts on conditions affecting agriculture, including <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> and glacial run-off. These conditions determine the <a href="/wiki/Carrying_capacity" title="Carrying capacity">carrying capacity</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">biosphere</a> to produce enough <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Human_population" class="mw-redirect" title="Human population">human population</a> and domesticated animals. Rising <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> levels would also have effects, both detrimental and beneficial, on crop yields.  Assessment of the effects of global climate changes on agriculture might help to properly anticipate and adapt farming to maximize <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural production</a>. Although the net impact of climate change on agricultural production is uncertain it is likely that it will shift the suitable growing zones for individual crops. Adjustment to this geographical shift will involve considerable economic costs and social impacts..
</p><p>At the same time, agriculture has been shown to produce significant effects on climate change, primarily through the production and release of <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nitrous_oxide" title="Nitrous oxide">nitrous oxide</a>. In addition, agriculture that practices tillage, fertilization, and pesticide application also releases <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>, and many other pesticides that affect air, water, and soil quality, as well as <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-sciencedirect.com_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sciencedirect.com-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  Agriculture also alters the Earth's <a href="/wiki/Land_cover" title="Land cover">land cover</a>, which can change its ability to absorb or reflect heat and light, thus contributing to <a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">radiative forcing</a>. <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">Land use</a> change such as <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a>, together with use of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>, are the major <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> sources of carbon dioxide; agriculture itself is the major contributor to increasing methane and nitrous oxide concentrations in earth's <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Deforestation">Deforestation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Deforestation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">Deforestation</a></div>
<p>Deforestation is clearing the Earth's forests on a large scale worldwide and resulting in many land damages. One of the causes of deforestation is to clear land for pasture or crops. According to British environmentalist <a href="/wiki/Norman_Myers" title="Norman Myers">Norman Myers</a>, 5% of deforestation is due to <a href="/wiki/Cattle_ranching" class="mw-redirect" title="Cattle ranching">cattle ranching</a>, 19% due to over-heavy <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a>, 22% due to the growing sector of <a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">palm oil</a> plantations, and 54% due to <a href="/wiki/Slash-and-burn" title="Slash-and-burn">slash-and-burn</a> farming.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Deforestation causes the loss of habitat for millions of species, and is also a driver of climate change. Trees act as a carbon sink: that is, they absorb carbon dioxide, an unwanted greenhouse gas, out of the atmosphere. Removing trees releases carbon dioxide into the atmosphere and leaves behind fewer trees to absorb the increasing amount of carbon dioxide in the air. In this way, deforestation exacerbates climate change.  When trees are removed from forests, the soils tend to dry out because there is no longer shade, and there are not enough trees to assist in the <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">water cycle</a> by returning water vapor back to the environment. With no trees, landscapes that were once forests can potentially become barren deserts. The removal of trees also causes extreme fluctuations in temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2000 the United Nations <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization" title="Food and Agriculture Organization">Food and Agriculture Organization</a> (FAO) found that "the role of population dynamics in a local setting may vary from decisive to negligible," and that deforestation can result from "a combination of population pressure and stagnating economic, social and technological conditions."<sup id="cite_ref-population1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-population1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic_engineering">Genetic engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Genetic engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p><a href="/wiki/Genetically_engineered" class="mw-redirect" title="Genetically engineered">Genetically engineered</a> crops are <a href="/wiki/Herbicide" title="Herbicide">herbicide</a>-tolerant, and their overuse has created herbicide resistant "<a href="/wiki/Super_weed" class="mw-redirect" title="Super weed">super weeds</a>",<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> which may ultimately increase the use of herbicides. <a href="/wiki/Seed_contamination" class="mw-redirect" title="Seed contamination">Seed contamination</a> is another problem of genetic engineering; it can occur from wind or bee pollination that is blown from genetically-engineered crops to normal crops. About 50% of corn and soybean samples and more than 80% of canola samples were found to be contaminated by <a href="/wiki/Monsanto" title="Monsanto">Monsanto</a>'s (genetic engineering company) genes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This accidental contamination can cause <a href="/wiki/Organic_farmer" class="mw-redirect" title="Organic farmer">organic farmers</a> to lose a lot of money because they needed to recall their products. There are various cases of this such as in the corn and <a href="/wiki/Alfalfa" title="Alfalfa">alfalfa</a> industry.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Irrigation">Irrigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Irrigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_irrigation" title="Environmental impact of irrigation">Environmental impact of irrigation</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">Irrigation</a> can lead to a number of problems:<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  
</p><p>Among some of these problems is the depletion of underground <a href="/wiki/Aquifers" class="mw-redirect" title="Aquifers">aquifers</a> through <a href="/wiki/Overdrafting" title="Overdrafting">overdrafting</a>. Soil can be over-irrigated because of poor <a href="/wiki/Distribution_uniformity" title="Distribution uniformity">distribution uniformity</a> or <a href="/wiki/Irrigation_scheduling" title="Irrigation scheduling">management</a> <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">wastes</a> water, chemicals, and may lead to <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">water pollution</a>. Over-irrigation can cause deep drainage from rising water tables that can lead to problems of irrigation  <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity" title="Soil salinity">salinity</a> requiring <a href="/wiki/Watertable_control" title="Watertable control">watertable control</a> by some form of <a href="/wiki/Drainage_system_(agriculture)" title="Drainage system (agriculture)">subsurface land drainage</a>. However, if the soil is under irrigated, it gives poor <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity_control" title="Soil salinity control">soil salinity control</a> which leads to increased <a href="/wiki/Soil_salinity" title="Soil salinity">soil salinity</a> with consequent buildup of toxic <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salts</a> on soil surface in areas with high <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>. This requires either <a href="/wiki/Leaching_(agriculture)" title="Leaching (agriculture)">leaching</a> to remove these salts and a method of <a href="/wiki/Drainage" title="Drainage">drainage</a> to carry the salts away. Irrigation with <a href="/wiki/Saline_water" title="Saline water">saline</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sodium_adsorption_ratio" title="Sodium adsorption ratio">high-sodium</a> water may damage soil structure owing to the formation of <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_soil" class="mw-redirect" title="Alkaline soil">alkaline soil</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollutants">Pollutants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pollutants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_pollution" title="Agricultural pollution">Agricultural pollution</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_pesticides" title="Environmental impact of pesticides">Environmental impact of pesticides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Environmental_effects_of_fertilizers" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental effects of fertilizers">Environmental effects of fertilizers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_wastewater_treatment" title="Agricultural wastewater treatment">Agricultural wastewater treatment</a></div>
<p>Synthetic pesticides such as '<a href="/wiki/Malathion" title="Malathion">Malathion</a>', '<a href="/wiki/Rogor" class="mw-redirect" title="Rogor">Rogor</a>', '<a href="/wiki/Kelthane" class="mw-redirect" title="Kelthane">Kelthane</a>' and '<a href="/wiki/Confidor" class="mw-redirect" title="Confidor">confidor</a>' are the most widespread method of controlling pests in agriculture. Pesticides can leach through the soil and enter the <a href="/wiki/Groundwater_pollution" title="Groundwater pollution">groundwater</a>, as well as linger in food products and result in death in humans and non-targeted wildlife. <sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
A wide range of <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_chemicals" class="mw-redirect" title="Agricultural chemicals">agricultural chemicals</a> are used and some become <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">pollutants</a> through use, misuse, or ignorance. The erosion of topsoil, which can contain chemicals such as herbicides and pesticides, can be carried away from farms to other places.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Pesticides can be found in streams and ground water. Atrazine is a herbicide used to control weeds that grow among crops.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This herbicide can disrupt endocrine production which can cause reproductive problems in mammals, amphibians and fish that have been exposed.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> 
Pollutants from agriculture have a huge effect on <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a>. Agricultural <a href="/wiki/Nonpoint_source_pollution" title="Nonpoint source pollution">nonpoint source</a> (NPS) solution impacts lakes, rivers, <a href="/wiki/Wetland" title="Wetland">wetlands</a>, estuaries, and <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>. Agricultural NPS can be caused by poorly managed animal feeding operations, overgrazing, plowing, fertilizer, and improper, excessive, or badly timed use of <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">Pesticides</a>. Pollutants from farming include sediments, nutrients, pathogens, pesticides, metals, and salts.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Animal agriculture can also cause pollutants to enter the environment. Bacteria and pathogens in manure can make their way into streams and groundwater if grazing, storing manure in lagoons and applying manure to fields is not properly managed.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Listed below are additional and specific problems that may arise with the release of pollutants from agriculture.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soil_degradation">Soil degradation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Soil degradation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Soil_degradation" class="mw-redirect" title="Soil degradation">Soil degradation</a> is the decline in soil quality that can be a result of many factors, especially from agriculture. Soils hold the majority of the world's biodiversity, and healthy soils are essential for food production and an adequate water supply.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Common attributes of soil degradation can be salting, waterlogging, compaction, pesticide contamination, decline in soil structure quality, loss of fertility, changes in soil acidity, alkalinity, salinity, and <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>. Soil erosion is the wearing away of topsoil by water, wind, or farming activities.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Topsoil is very fertile, which makes it valuable to farmers growing crops.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Soil degradation also has a huge impact on biological degradation, which affects the microbial community of the soil and can alter nutrient cycling, pest and disease control, and chemical transformation properties of the soil.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Soil_contamination" title="Soil contamination">soil contamination</a>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Waste">Waste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Waste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Plasticulture" title="Plasticulture">Plasticulture</a> is the use of <a href="/wiki/Plastic_mulch" title="Plastic mulch">plastic mulch</a> in agriculture. Farmers use plastic sheets as mulch to cover 50-70% of the soil and allows them to use drip irrigation systems to have better control over <a href="/wiki/Soil_nutrient" class="mw-redirect" title="Soil nutrient">soil nutrients</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil_moisture" class="mw-redirect" title="Soil moisture">moisture</a>. Rain is not required in this system, and farms that use plasticulture are built to encourage the fastest runoff of rain. The use of <a href="/wiki/Pesticides" class="mw-redirect" title="Pesticides">pesticides</a> with plasticulture allows pesticides to be transported easier in the <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">surface runoff</a> towards wetlands or tidal creeks. The runoff from pesticides and chemicals in the plastic can cause serious deformations and death in shellfish as the runoff carries the chemicals towards the oceans.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to the increased runoff that results from plasticulture, there is also the problem of the increased amount of waste form the plastic mulch itself. The use of plastic mulch for vegetables, strawberries, and other row and orchard crops exceeds 110 million pounds annually in the United States. Most plastic ends up in the landfill, although there are other disposal options such as disking mulches into the soil, on-site burying, on-site storage, reuse, recycling, and incineration. The incineration and recycling options are complicated by the variety of the types of plastics that are used and by the geographic dispersal of the plastics. Plastics also contain stabilizers and dyes as well as heavy metals, which limits the amount of products that can be recycled. Research is continually being conducted on creating biodegradable or <a href="/wiki/Photodegradable" class="mw-redirect" title="Photodegradable">photodegradable</a> mulches. While there has been minor success with this, there is also the problem of how long the plastic takes to degrade, as many biodegradable products take a long time to break down.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_agriculture">Sustainable agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sustainable agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">Sustainable agriculture</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">Sustainable agriculture</a> is the idea that agriculture should occur in a way such that we can continue to produce what is necessary without infringing on the ability for future generations to do the same.
</p><p>The exponential population increase in recent decades has increased the practice of agricultural <a href="/wiki/Land_conversion" class="mw-redirect" title="Land conversion">land conversion</a> to meet demand for food which in turn has increased the effects on the environment. The global population is still increasing and will eventually stabilise, as some critics doubt that food production, due to lower yields from global warming, can support the global population.
Agriculture can have negative effects on biodiversity as well. <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">Organic farming</a> is a multifaceted <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainable agriculture</a> set of practices that can have a lower impact on the environment at the small scale. However, in most cases organic farming results in lower yields in terms of production per unit area.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Therefore, widespread adoption of organic agriculture will require additional land to be cleared and water resources extracted to meet the same level of production. A European meta-analysis found that organic farms tended to have higher <a href="/wiki/Soil_organic_matter" title="Soil organic matter">soil organic matter</a> content and lower nutrient losses (nitrogen leaching, nitrous oxide emissions and ammonia emissions) per unit of field area but higher ammonia emissions, nitrogen leaching and nitrous oxide emissions per product unit.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  It is believed by many that conventional farming systems cause less rich biodiversity than organic systems. <a href="/wiki/Organic_farming" title="Organic farming">Organic farming</a> has shown to have on average 30% higher species richness than conventional farming. Organic systems on average also have 50% more organisms. This data has some issues because there were several results that showed a negative effect on these things when in an organic farming system.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The opposition to organic agriculture believes that these negatives are an issue with the organic farming system. What began as a small scale, environmentally conscious has now become just as industrialized as conventional agriculture. This industrialization can lead to the issues shown above such as climate change, and deforestation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_tillage">Conservation tillage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_agriculture&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Conservation tillage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Conservation tillage is an alternative tillage method for farming which is more sustainable for the soil and surrounding ecosystem.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> This is done by allowing the residue of the previous harvest's crops to remain in the soil before tilling for the next crop. Conservation tillage has shown to improve many things such as soil moisture retention, and reduce erosion. Some disadvantages are the fact that more expensive equipment is needed for this process, more pesticides will need to be used, and the positive effects take a long time to be visible.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The barriers of instantiating a conservation tillage policy are that farmers are reluctant to change their methods, and would protest a more expensive, and time consuming method of tillage than the conventional one they are used to.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other specific methods include: <a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permaculture</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Biodynamic_agriculture" title="Biodynamic agriculture">biodynamic agriculture</a> which incorporates a spiritual element.
</p>
