<p><b>Vanadium(IV) oxide</b> (also called <b>vanadium dioxide</b><sup id="cite_ref-:2_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the formula VO<sub>2</sub>. It is a dark blue solid. <a href="/wiki/Vanadium" title="Vanadium">Vanadium</a>(IV) dioxide is <a href="/wiki/Amphoteric" class="mw-redirect" title="Amphoteric">amphoteric</a>, dissolving in non-oxidising acids to give the blue <a href="/wiki/Vanadyl_ion" title="Vanadyl ion">vanadyl ion</a>, [VO]<sup>2+</sup> and in alkali to give the brown [V<sub>4</sub>O<sub>9</sub>]<sup>2−</sup> ion, or at high pH [VO<sub>4</sub>]<sup>4−</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> VO<sub>2</sub> has a phase transition very close to room temperature(~66&#160;°C). Electrical resistivity, opacity, etc, can change up several orders. Due to these properties, it has been widely used in surface coating,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> sensors,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and imaging.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Potential applications include use in memory devices, <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> phase-change switches, <sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>aerospace communication systems and <a href="/wiki/Neuromorphic_computing" class="mw-redirect" title="Neuromorphic computing">neuromorphic computing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At temperatures below T<sub>c</sub>=340 K (67 °C), <span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> has a <a href="/wiki/Monoclinic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monoclinic">monoclinic</a> (<a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> P2<sub>1</sub>/c) crystal structure. Above T<sub>c</sub>, the structure is <a href="/wiki/Tetragonal" class="mw-redirect" title="Tetragonal">tetragonal</a>, like <a href="/wiki/Rutile" title="Rutile">rutile</a> <span class="chemf nowrap">TiO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>. In the monoclinic phase, the V<sup>4+</sup> ions form pairs along the c axis, leading to alternate short and long V-V distances of 2.65 Å and 3.12 Å. In comparison, in the rutile phase the V<sup>4+</sup> ions are separated by a fixed distance of 2.96 Å. As a result, the number of V<sup>4+</sup> ions in the crystallographic unit cell doubles from the rutile to the monoclinic phase.<sup id="cite_ref-Morin1959_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morin1959-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The equilibrium morphology of rutile <span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> particles is acicular, laterally confined by (110) surfaces, which are the most stable termination planes.<sup id="cite_ref-MellanGrau-Crespo2012_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MellanGrau-Crespo2012-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The surface tends to be oxidized with respect to the stoichiometric composition, with the oxygen adsorbed on the (110) surface forming vanadyl species.<sup id="cite_ref-MellanGrau-Crespo2012_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MellanGrau-Crespo2012-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The presence of V<sup>5+</sup> ions at the surface of <span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> films has been observed by x-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (<a href="/wiki/X-ray_photoelectron_spectroscopy" title="X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy">XPS</a>) measurements.<sup id="cite_ref-ManningParkin2004_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ManningParkin2004-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronic">Electronic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Electronic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the rutile to monoclinic transition temperature (67 °C), <span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> also exhibits a metal to <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">semiconductor</a> transition in its electronic structure: the rutile phase is metallic while the monoclinic phase is semiconducting.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Optical_band_gap" class="mw-redirect" title="Optical band gap">optical band gap</a> of VO<sub>2</sub> in the low-temperature monoclinic phase is about 0.7 eV.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal">Thermal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Thermal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Metallic VO<sub>2</sub> contradicts the <a href="/wiki/Wiedemann%E2%80%93Franz_law" title="Wiedemann–Franz law">Wiedemann-Franz Law</a> that holds that the ratio of the electronic contribution of the <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a> (<i>κ</i>) to the <a href="/wiki/Electrical_conductivity" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrical conductivity">electrical conductivity</a> (<i>σ</i>) of a <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a> is proportional to the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>. The thermal conductivity that could be attributed to electron movement was 10% of the amount predicted by the Wiedemann-Franz Law. The reason for this appears to be the fluidic way that the electrons move through the material, reducing the typical random electron motion.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Potential applications include converting waste heat from engines and appliances into electricity, or window coverings that keep buildings cool.  Thermal conductivity varied when VO<sub>2</sub> was mixed with other materials. At a low temperature it could act as an insulator, while conducting heat at a higher temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis_and_structure">Synthesis and structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Synthesis and structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the method described by <a href="/wiki/Berzelius" class="mw-redirect" title="Berzelius">Berzelius</a>, <span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> is prepared by <a href="/wiki/Comproportionation" title="Comproportionation">comproportionation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Vanadium(III)_oxide" title="Vanadium(III) oxide">vanadium(III) oxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vanadium(V)_oxide" title="Vanadium(V) oxide">vanadium(V) oxide</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">V<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span></span>  +  <span class="chemf nowrap">V<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span>   →    4 <span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span></dd></dl>
<p>At room temperature VO<sub>2</sub> has a distorted <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">rutile</a> structure with shorter distances between pairs of V atoms indicating metal-metal bonding. Above 68&#160;°C the structure changes to an undistorted rutile structure and the metal-metal bonds are broken causing an increase in electrical conductivity and magnetic susceptibility as the bonding electrons are "released".<sup id="cite_ref-Greenwood_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenwood-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The origin of this insulator to metal transition remains controversial and is of interest in <a href="/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics" title="Condensed matter physics">condensed matter physics</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrared_reflectance">Infrared reflectance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Infrared reflectance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> expresses temperature-dependent reflective properties. When heated from room temperature to 80&#160;°C, the material's thermal radiation rises normally until 74&#160;°C, before suddenly appearing to drop to around 20&#160;°C. At room temperature <span class="chemf nowrap">VO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> is almost transparent to infrared light. As its temperature rises it gradually changes to reflective. At intermediate temperatures it behaves as a highly absorbing dielectric.<sup id="cite_ref-phy1310_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phy1310-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A thin film of vanadium oxide on a highly reflecting substrate (for specific infrared wavelengths) such as sapphire is either absorbing or reflecting, dependent on temperature. Its emissivity varies considerably with temperature. When the vanadium oxide transitions with increased temperature, the structure undergoes a sudden decrease in emissivity – looking colder to infrared cameras than it really is.<sup id="cite_ref-phy1310_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phy1310-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Varying the substrate materials e.g., to indium tin oxide, and modifying the vanadium oxide coating using doping, straining and other processes, alter the wavelengths and temperature ranges at which the thermal effects are observed.<sup id="cite_ref-phy1310_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phy1310-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Nanoscale structures that appear naturally in the materials' transition region can suppress thermal radiation as the temperature rises. Doping the coating with <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> lowers the effect's thermal range to room temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-phy1310_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phy1310-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrared_radiation_management">Infrared radiation management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Infrared radiation management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>1.9% tungsten-doped material content has been investigated for use as a "spectrally-selective" window coating to block <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> transmission and reduce the loss of building interior heat through windows.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Varying the amount of tungsten allows regulating the phase transition temperature. The coating has a slight yellow-green color.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other potential applications of its thermal properties include passive camouflage, thermal beacons, communication, or to deliberately speed up or slow down cooling (which could be useful in a variety of structures from homes to satellites<sup id="cite_ref-phy1310_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phy1310-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>).
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nanostars-it1302.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Nanostars-it1302.jpg/220px-Nanostars-it1302.jpg" width="220" height="181" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Nanostars-it1302.jpg/330px-Nanostars-it1302.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Nanostars-it1302.jpg/440px-Nanostars-it1302.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1242" data-file-height="1020" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nanostars-it1302.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nanostars of Vanadium(IV) oxide</div></div></div>
<p>Vanadium dioxide can act as extremely fast <a href="/w/index.php?title=Optical_shutter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Optical shutter (page does not exist)">optical shutters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Optical_modulator" title="Optical modulator">optical modulators</a>, <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> modulators for <a href="/wiki/Missile_guidance" title="Missile guidance">missile guidance</a> systems, cameras, data storage, and other applications. The <a href="/wiki/Thermochromic" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermochromic">thermochromic</a> <a href="/wiki/Phase_transition" title="Phase transition">phase transition</a> between the transparent semiconductive and reflective conductive phase, occurring at 68&#160;°C, can happen in times as short as 100 femtoseconds.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phase_change_computing_and_memory">Phase change computing and memory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vanadium(IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Phase change computing and memory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The insulator-metal phase transition in VO<sub>2</sub> can be manipulated at the nanoscale using a biased conducting atomic force microscope tip,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> suggesting applications in computing and information storage.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
