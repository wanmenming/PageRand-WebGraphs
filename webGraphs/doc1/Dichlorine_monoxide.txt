<p><b>Dichlorine monoxide</b>,  is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Molecular_formula" class="mw-redirect" title="Molecular formula">molecular formula</a> Cl<sub>2</sub>O. It was first synthesised in 1834 by <a href="/wiki/Antoine_J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Balard" title="Antoine Jérôme Balard">Antoine Jérôme Balard</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> who along with <a href="/wiki/Gay-Lussac" class="mw-redirect" title="Gay-Lussac">Gay-Lussac</a> also determined its composition. In older literature it is often referred to as <b>chlorine monoxide</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-review_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-review-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> which can be a source of confusion as that name now refers to the neutral species <a href="/wiki/Chlorine_monoxide" title="Chlorine monoxide">ClO</a>.
</p><p>At room temperature it exists as a brownish-yellow gas which is soluble in both water and organic solvents. Chemically, it is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Chlorine_oxide" title="Chlorine oxide">chlorine oxide</a> family of compounds, as well as being the <a href="/wiki/Anhydride" class="mw-redirect" title="Anhydride">anhydride</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hypochlorous_acid" title="Hypochlorous acid">hypochlorous acid</a>. It is a strong oxidiser and chlorinating agent.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dichlorine_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest method of synthesis was to treat <a href="/wiki/Mercury(II)_oxide" title="Mercury(II) oxide">mercury(II) oxide</a> with <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> gas.<sup id="cite_ref-review_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-review-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> However this method is expensive, as well as highly dangerous due to the risk of <a href="/wiki/Mercury_poisoning" title="Mercury poisoning">mercury poisoning</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Cl<sub>2</sub> + HgO → <a href="/wiki/HgCl2" class="mw-redirect" title="HgCl2">HgCl<sub>2</sub></a> + Cl<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>A safer and more convenient method of production is the reaction of <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> gas with hydrated <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a>, at 20-30°C.<sup id="cite_ref-review_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-review-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Cl<sub>2</sub> + 2 Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O → Cl<sub>2</sub>O + 2 <a href="/wiki/NaHCO3" class="mw-redirect" title="NaHCO3">NaHCO<sub>3</sub></a> + 2 NaCl</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>2 Cl<sub>2</sub> + 2 <a href="/wiki/NaHCO3" class="mw-redirect" title="NaHCO3">NaHCO<sub>3</sub></a> → Cl<sub>2</sub>O + 2 CO<sub>2</sub> + 2 NaCl + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>This reaction can be performed in the absence of water but requires heating to 150-250°C; as dichlorine monoxide is unstable at these temperatures<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> it must therefore be continuously removed to prevent <a href="/wiki/Thermal_decomposition" title="Thermal decomposition">thermal decomposition</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Cl<sub>2</sub> + Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> → Cl<sub>2</sub>O + CO<sub>2</sub> + 2 NaCl</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dichlorine_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The structure of dichlorine monoxide is similar to that of water and <a href="/wiki/Hypochlorous_acid" title="Hypochlorous acid">hypochlorous acid</a>, with the molecule adopting a <a href="/wiki/Bent_molecular_geometry" title="Bent molecular geometry">bent molecular geometry</a> due to the <a href="/wiki/Lone_pair" title="Lone pair">lone pairs</a> on the oxygen; resulting in C<sub>2V</sub> <a href="/wiki/Molecular_symmetry" title="Molecular symmetry">molecular symmetry</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bond_angle" class="mw-redirect" title="Bond angle">bond angle</a> is slightly larger than normal, likely due to <a href="/wiki/Steric_repulsion" class="mw-redirect" title="Steric repulsion">steric repulsion</a> between the bulky chlorine atoms. 
</p>

<p>In the solid state, it crystallises in the tetrahedral <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">space group</a> I4<sub>1</sub>/amd, making it isostructural to the high pressure form of water, <a href="/wiki/Ice_VIII" title="Ice VIII">ice VIII</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dichlorine_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dichlorine monoxide is highly soluble in water,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> where it exists in an equilibrium with HOCl. The rate of hydrolysis is slow enough to allow the extraction of Cl<sub>2</sub>O with organic solvents such as <a href="/wiki/CCl4" class="mw-redirect" title="CCl4">CCl<sub>4</sub></a>,<sup id="cite_ref-review_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-review-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> but the <a href="/wiki/Equilibrium_constant" title="Equilibrium constant">equilibrium constant</a> ultimately favours the formation of hypochlorous acid.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<dl><dd>2 HOCl ⇌ Cl<sub>2</sub>O + H<sub>2</sub>O     K (0&#160;°C) = 3.55x10<sup>−3</sup> dm<sup>3</sup>/mol</dd></dl>
<p>Despite this, it has been suggested that dichlorine monoxide may be the active species in the reactions of <a href="/wiki/HOCl" class="mw-redirect" title="HOCl">HOCl</a> with <a href="/wiki/Olefin" class="mw-redirect" title="Olefin">olefins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aromatic_hydrocarbon" title="Aromatic hydrocarbon">aromatic compounds</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Swain_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Swain-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sivey_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sivey-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> as well as in the chlorination of drinking water.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="With_inorganic_compounds">With inorganic compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dichlorine_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: With inorganic compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dichlorine monoxide reacts with <a href="/wiki/Metal_halide" class="mw-redirect" title="Metal halide">metal halides</a>, with the loss of Cl<sub>2</sub>, to form unusual <a href="/wiki/Oxyhalide" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxyhalide">oxyhalides</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-review_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-review-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/VOCl3" class="mw-redirect" title="VOCl3">VOCl<sub>3</sub></a> + Cl<sub>2</sub>O → VO<sub>2</sub>Cl + 2 Cl<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd><a href="/wiki/TiCl4" class="mw-redirect" title="TiCl4">TiCl<sub>4</sub></a> + Cl<sub>2</sub>O → TiOCI<sub>2</sub> + 2 Cl<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd><a href="/wiki/SbCl5" class="mw-redirect" title="SbCl5">SbCI<sub>5</sub></a> + 2 CI<sub>2</sub>O → SbO<sub>2</sub>CI + 4 Cl<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Similar reactions have also been observed with certain inorganic halides.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><a href="/wiki/AsCl3" class="mw-redirect" title="AsCl3">AsCI<sub>3</sub></a> + 2 CI<sub>2</sub>O → AsO<sub>2</sub>CI + 3 Cl<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd><a href="/wiki/NOCl" class="mw-redirect" title="NOCl">NOCl</a> + Cl<sub>2</sub>O → NO<sub>2</sub>Cl + Cl<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="With_organic_compounds">With organic compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dichlorine_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: With organic compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dichlorine monoxide is an effective chlorinating agent. It can be used for either the side-chain or ring chlorination of deactivated <a href="/wiki/Aromatic" class="mw-redirect" title="Aromatic">aromatic</a> substrates.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> For activated aromatics such as <a href="/wiki/Phenol" title="Phenol">phenols</a> and aryl-ethers it primarily reacts to give ring halogenated products.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> It has been suggested that dichlorine monoxide may be the active species in the reactions of <a href="/wiki/HOCl" class="mw-redirect" title="HOCl">HOCl</a> with <a href="/wiki/Olefin" class="mw-redirect" title="Olefin">olefins</a> and aromatic compounds.<sup id="cite_ref-Swain_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Swain-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sivey_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sivey-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Photochemistry">Photochemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dichlorine_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Photochemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dichlorine monoxide undergoes <a href="/wiki/Photodissociation" title="Photodissociation">photodissociation</a>, eventually forming O<sub>2</sub> and Cl<sub>2</sub>. The process is primarily <a href="/wiki/Radical_(chemistry)" title="Radical (chemistry)">radical</a> based, with <a href="/wiki/Flash_photolysis" title="Flash photolysis">flash photolysis</a> showing <a href="/wiki/Chlorine_monoxide" title="Chlorine monoxide">radical hypochlorite</a> (ClO·) to be a key intermediate.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Cl<sub>2</sub>O → 2 Cl<sub>2</sub> + O<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Explosive_properties">Explosive properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dichlorine_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Explosive properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Dichlorine monoxide is explosive, although there is a lack of modern research into this behaviour. <a href="/wiki/Room_temperature" title="Room temperature">Room temperature</a> mixtures with oxygen could not be detonated by an electric spark until they contained at least 23.5% Cl<sub>2</sub>O.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> which is an exceedingly high <a href="/wiki/Explosive_limit#Lower_explosive_limit" class="mw-redirect" title="Explosive limit">minimum explosive limit</a>. There are conflicting reports of it exploding on exposure to strong light.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Heating above 120°C, or a rapid rate of heating at lower temperatures also apparently lead to explosions.<sup id="cite_ref-review_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-review-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
Liquid dichlorine monoxide has been reported to be shock-sensitive.<sup id="cite_ref-PilipovichLindahl1972_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PilipovichLindahl1972-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
