<p><b>Joseph Black</b> (16 April 1728 – 6 December 1799) was a Scottish physician and chemist, known for his discoveries of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Specific_heat" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific heat">specific heat</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>. He was Professor of Anatomy and Chemistry at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Glasgow" title="University of Glasgow">University of Glasgow</a> for 10 years from 1756, and then Professor of Medicine and Chemistry at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a> from 1766, teaching and lecturing there for more than 30 years.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> buildings at both the University of Edinburgh and the University of Glasgow are named after Black.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Black was born in <a href="/wiki/Bordeaux" title="Bordeaux">Bordeaux</a>, France, the sixth of the 12 children of Margaret Gordon (<i>d</i>. 1747) and John Black. His mother was from an <a href="/wiki/Aberdeenshire" title="Aberdeenshire">Aberdeenshire</a> family that had connections with the wine business and his father was from <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>, Ireland and worked as a factor in the <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine trade</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He was educated at home until the age of 12, after which he attended grammar school in Belfast. In 1746 at the age of 18 he entered the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Glasgow" title="University of Glasgow">University of Glasgow</a>, studying there for four years before spending another four at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a>, furthering his medical studies. During his studies he wrote a doctorate thesis on the treatment of <a href="/wiki/Kidney_stones" class="mw-redirect" title="Kidney stones">kidney stones</a> with the salt <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_carbonate" title="Magnesium carbonate">magnesium carbonate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Modinos2013_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Modinos2013-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_Principles">Chemical Principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical Principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like most 18th-century experimentalists, Black's conceptualisation of chemistry was based on five 'principles' of matter: Water, Salt, Earth, Fire and Metal.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He added the principle of 'Air' when his experiments definitely confirmed the presence of carbon dioxide, which he called 'fixed air'.  Black's research  was guided by questions relating to how the  'principles' combined with each other in various different forms and mixtures.  He used the term 'affinity' to describe the force that held such combinations together.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Throughout his career he used a variety of diagrams and formulas to teach his University of Edinburgh students how to manipulate 'affinity' through different kinds of experimentation.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Analytical_balance">Analytical balance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Analytical balance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BalanceMineralPachuca.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/BalanceMineralPachuca.JPG/220px-BalanceMineralPachuca.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/BalanceMineralPachuca.JPG/330px-BalanceMineralPachuca.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/BalanceMineralPachuca.JPG/440px-BalanceMineralPachuca.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BalanceMineralPachuca.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A precision analytical balance</div></div></div>
<p>In about 1750, while still a student, Black developed the <a href="/wiki/Analytical_balance" title="Analytical balance">analytical balance</a> based on a light-weight beam balanced on a wedge-shaped <a href="/wiki/Fulcrum_(mechanics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fulcrum (mechanics)">fulcrum</a>. Each arm carried a pan on which the sample or standard weights was placed. It far exceeded the accuracy of any other balance of the time and became an important scientific instrument in most chemistry laboratories.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latent_heat">Latent heat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Latent heat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ice-calorimeter.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Ice-calorimeter.jpg/220px-Ice-calorimeter.jpg" width="220" height="369" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Ice-calorimeter.jpg/330px-Ice-calorimeter.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/35/Ice-calorimeter.jpg 2x" data-file-width="358" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ice-calorimeter.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The world's first <b>ice-calorimeter</b>, used in the winter of 1782–83, by <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Lavoisier">Antoine Lavoisier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Simon_Laplace" title="Pierre-Simon Laplace">Pierre-Simon Laplace</a>, to determine the heat evolved in various <a href="/wiki/Chemical_change" title="Chemical change">chemical changes</a>, calculations which were based on Joseph Black's prior discovery of <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1757, Black was appointed <a href="/wiki/Regius_Professor_of_Medicine_and_Therapeutics,_Glasgow" class="mw-redirect" title="Regius Professor of Medicine and Therapeutics, Glasgow">Regius Professor of the Practice of Medicine</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Glasgow" title="University of Glasgow">University of Glasgow</a>.
</p><p>In 1761 he deduced that the application of heat to <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> at its melting point does not cause a rise in temperature of the ice/water mixture, but rather an increase in the amount of water in the mixture. Additionally, Black observed that the application of heat to boiling water does not result in a rise in temperature of a water/steam mixture, but rather an increase in the amount of steam. From these observations, he concluded that the heat applied must have combined with the ice particles and boiling water and become <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The theory of latent heat marks the beginning of <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Black's theory of latent heat was one of his more-important scientific contributions, and one on which his scientific fame chiefly rests. He also showed that different substances have different <a href="/wiki/Specific_heat" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific heat">specific heats</a>.
</p><p>The theory ultimately proved important not only in the development of abstract science but in the development of the <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The latent heat of water is large compared with many other liquids, so giving impetus to <a href="/wiki/James_Watt" title="James Watt">James Watt</a>'s attempts to improve the efficiency of the steam engine invented by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Newcomen" title="Thomas Newcomen">Thomas Newcomen</a>. Black and Watt became friends after meeting around 1757 while both were at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Glasgow" title="University of Glasgow">Glasgow</a>. Black provided significant financing and other support for Watt's early research in steam power.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_dioxide">Carbon dioxide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Carbon dioxide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Black also explored the properties of a gas produced in various reactions. He found that <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> (<a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a>) could be heated or treated with acids to yield a gas he called "fixed air." He observed that the fixed air was denser than air and did not support either flame or animal life. Black also found that when bubbled through an aqueous solution of <a href="/wiki/Lime_(material)" title="Lime (material)">lime</a> (<a href="/wiki/Calcium_hydroxide" title="Calcium hydroxide">calcium hydroxide</a>), it would precipitate calcium carbonate. He used this phenomenon to illustrate that <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> is produced by animal <a href="/wiki/Respiration_(physiology)" title="Respiration (physiology)">respiration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Microbial" class="mw-redirect" title="Microbial">microbial</a> <a href="/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fermentation (biochemistry)">fermentation</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professorship">Professorship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Professorship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1766, treading in the footsteps of his friend and former teacher at the University of Glasgow, Black succeeded <a href="/wiki/William_Cullen" title="William Cullen">William Cullen</a> as Professor of Medicine and Chemistry at the University of Edinburgh (Cullen had moved to Edinburgh in 1755). His position at Glasgow University was filled by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Stevenson_(physician)" title="Alexander Stevenson (physician)">Alexander Stevenson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At this point he gave up research and devoted himself exclusively to teaching. In this he was  successful with audience attendance at his lectures increasing from year to year for more than thirty years. His lectures had a powerful effect in popularising chemistry and attendance at them even came to be a fashionable amusement.
</p><p>Black was widely recognised as one of the most popular lecturers at the University. His chemistry course regularly attracted an exceptionally high number of students, with many attending two or three times. In addition to regularly introducing cutting-edge topics and meticulously selecting visually impressive experiments, Black employed a wide array of successful teaching tools that made chemistry accessible to his students (many of whom were as young as 14 years old).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> His students came from across the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, its <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">colonies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, and hundreds of them preserved his lectures in their notebooks and disseminated his ideas after they left university.
</p><p>On 17 November 1783 he became one of the founders of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Edinburgh" title="Royal Society of Edinburgh">Royal Society of Edinburgh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> From 1788 to 1790 he was President of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Physicians_of_Edinburgh" title="Royal College of Physicians of Edinburgh">Royal College of Physicians of Edinburgh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> He was a member of the revision committee for the editions of the college's <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Pharmacopoeia" title="Edinburgh Pharmacopoeia">Pharmacopoeia Edinburgensis</a> of 1774, 1783, and 1794. Black was appointed principal physician to <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George III of the United Kingdom">King George III</a> in Scotland.
</p><p>Black's research and teaching were reduced as a result of poor health. From 1793 his health declined further and he gradually withdrew from his teaching duties. In 1795, Charles Hope was appointed his <a href="/wiki/Coadjutor" title="Coadjutor">coadjutor</a> in his professorship, and in 1797 he lectured for the last time.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Black&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Joseph_Black%27s_grave_in_Greyfriars_Kirkyard.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Joseph_Black%27s_grave_in_Greyfriars_Kirkyard.jpg/220px-Joseph_Black%27s_grave_in_Greyfriars_Kirkyard.jpg" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Joseph_Black%27s_grave_in_Greyfriars_Kirkyard.jpg/330px-Joseph_Black%27s_grave_in_Greyfriars_Kirkyard.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Joseph_Black%27s_grave_in_Greyfriars_Kirkyard.jpg/440px-Joseph_Black%27s_grave_in_Greyfriars_Kirkyard.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2056" data-file-height="3088" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Joseph_Black%27s_grave_in_Greyfriars_Kirkyard.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Joseph Black's grave in Greyfriars Kirkyard</div></div></div>
<p>Black was a member of <a href="/wiki/The_Poker_Club" title="The Poker Club">The Poker Club</a> and associated with <a href="/wiki/David_Hume" title="David Hume">David Hume</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Smith" title="Adam Smith">Adam Smith</a>, and the literati of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Enlightenment" title="Scottish Enlightenment">Scottish Enlightenment</a>. He was also close to pioneering geologist <a href="/wiki/James_Hutton" title="James Hutton">James Hutton</a>.
</p><p>In 1773 he is listed as living on College Wynd on the south side of the <a href="/wiki/Old_Town,_Edinburgh" title="Old Town, Edinburgh">Old Town</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Black never married. He died peacefully at his home in Edinburgh in 1799 at the age of 71 and is buried in <a href="/wiki/Greyfriars_Kirkyard" title="Greyfriars Kirkyard">Greyfriars Kirkyard</a>. The large monument lies in the sealed section to the south-west known as the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Covenanter%27s_Prison&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Covenanter&#39;s Prison (page does not exist)">Covenanter's Prison</a>.
</p><p>In 2011, scientific equipment believed to belong to Black was discovered during an archaeological dig at the University of Edinburgh.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
