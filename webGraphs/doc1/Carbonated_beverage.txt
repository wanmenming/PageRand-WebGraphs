<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Soft drink</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">  (Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbonated_beverage&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbonated beverage">Carbonated beverage</a>)</span></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">sweetened non-alcoholic drink, often carbonated</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tumbler_of_cola_with_ice.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Tumbler_of_cola_with_ice.jpg/220px-Tumbler_of_cola_with_ice.jpg" width="220" height="301" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Tumbler_of_cola_with_ice.jpg/330px-Tumbler_of_cola_with_ice.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Tumbler_of_cola_with_ice.jpg/440px-Tumbler_of_cola_with_ice.jpg 2x" data-file-width="489" data-file-height="670" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tumbler_of_cola_with_ice.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A glass of <a href="/wiki/Cola" title="Cola">cola</a> served with <a href="/wiki/Ice_cube" title="Ice cube">ice cubes</a></div></div></div>
<p>A <b>soft drink</b> (see <a href="#Terminology"><i>Terminology</i></a> for other names) is a <a href="/wiki/Drink" title="Drink">drink</a> that typically contains <a href="/wiki/Carbonated_water" title="Carbonated water">carbonated water</a> (although some <a href="/wiki/Lemonade" title="Lemonade">lemonades</a> are not carbonated), a <a href="/wiki/Sweetness" title="Sweetness">sweetener</a>, and a natural or <a href="/wiki/Artificial_Flavoring" class="mw-redirect" title="Artificial Flavoring">artificial flavoring</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Sweetener" class="mw-redirect" title="Sweetener">sweetener</a> may be a <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>, <a href="/wiki/High-fructose_corn_syrup" title="High-fructose corn syrup">high-fructose corn syrup</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fruit_juice" class="mw-redirect" title="Fruit juice">fruit juice</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Sugar_substitute" title="Sugar substitute">sugar substitute</a> (in the case of <a href="/wiki/Diet_drink" title="Diet drink"><i>diet drinks</i></a>), or some combination of these. Soft drinks may also contain <a href="/wiki/Caffeine" title="Caffeine">caffeine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Food_coloring" title="Food coloring">colorings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Preservative" title="Preservative">preservatives</a>, and/or other ingredients.
</p><p>Soft drinks are called "soft" in contrast with "<a href="/wiki/Hard_drink" class="mw-redirect" title="Hard drink">hard</a>" <a href="/wiki/Alcoholic_drink" title="Alcoholic drink">alcoholic drinks</a>. Small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">alcohol</a> may be present in a soft drink, but the <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_by_volume" title="Alcohol by volume">alcohol content</a> must be less than 0.5% of the total volume<sup id="cite_ref-ECFE_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ECFE-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> if the drink is to be considered <a href="/wiki/Non-alcoholic_drink" title="Non-alcoholic drink">non-alcoholic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fruit_punch" class="mw-redirect" title="Fruit punch">Fruit punch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">tea</a> (even <a href="/wiki/Kombucha" title="Kombucha">kombucha</a>), and other such non-alcoholic drinks are technically soft drinks by this definition, but are not generally referred to as such.
</p><p>Soft drinks may be served chilled, over ice cubes, or at room temperature, especially soda.They are available in many container formats, including <a href="/wiki/Soda_can" class="mw-redirect" title="Soda can">cans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glass_bottles" class="mw-redirect" title="Glass bottles">glass bottles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plastic_bottles" class="mw-redirect" title="Plastic bottles">plastic bottles</a>. (Plastic bottles come in a variety of sizes, ranging from small bottles to large multi-liter containers.) Soft drinks are also widely available at <a href="/wiki/Fast_food" title="Fast food">fast food</a> restaurants, <a href="/wiki/Movie_theater" title="Movie theater">movie theaters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Convenience_store" title="Convenience store">convenience stores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Casual-dining_restaurants" class="mw-redirect" title="Casual-dining restaurants">casual-dining restaurants</a>, dedicated <a href="/wiki/Soda_store" class="mw-redirect" title="Soda store">soda stores</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bar" title="Bar">bars</a> from <a href="/wiki/Soda_fountain" title="Soda fountain">soda fountain</a> machines. Soda fountain drinks are typically served in paper or plastic <a href="/wiki/Disposable_cup" title="Disposable cup">disposable cups</a> in the first three venues. In casual dining restaurants and bars, soft drinks are often served in <a href="/wiki/List_of_glassware" title="List of glassware">glasses</a> made from glass or plastic. Soft drinks may be drunk with straws or sipped directly from the cups.
</p><p>Soft drinks are mixed with other ingredients in several contexts. In Western countries, in bars and other places where alcohol is served (e.g. airplanes, restaurants and nightclubs), many <a href="/wiki/Mixed_drink" title="Mixed drink">mixed drinks</a> are made by blending a soft drink with hard liquor and serving the drink over ice. One well-known example is the <a href="/wiki/Rum_and_coke" class="mw-redirect" title="Rum and coke">rum and coke</a>, which may also contain <a href="/wiki/Lime_juice" class="mw-redirect" title="Lime juice">lime juice</a>. Some homemade <a href="/wiki/Fruit_punch" class="mw-redirect" title="Fruit punch">fruit punch</a> recipes, which may or may not contain alcohol, contain a mixture of various <a href="/wiki/Fruit_juice" class="mw-redirect" title="Fruit juice">fruit juices</a> and a soft drink (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Ginger_ale" title="Ginger ale">ginger ale</a>). At <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream_parlour" class="mw-redirect" title="Ice cream parlour">ice cream parlours</a> and 1950s-themed <a href="/wiki/Diner" title="Diner">diners</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream_float" title="Ice cream float">ice cream floats</a>, and specifically <a href="/wiki/Root_beer_floats" class="mw-redirect" title="Root beer floats">root beer floats</a>, are often sold.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Names_for_soft_drinks_in_the_United_States" title="Names for soft drinks in the United States">Names for soft drinks in the United States</a></div>
<p>While the term "soft drink" is commonly used in product labeling and on restaurant menus, in many countries these drinks are more commonly referred to by regional names, including <b>carbonated drink</b>, <b>cool drink</b>, <b>cold drink</b>, <b>fizzy drink</b>, <b>fizzy juice</b>, <b>lolly water</b>, <b>pop</b>, <b>seltzer</b>, <b>soda</b>, <b>coke</b>, <b>soda pop</b>, <b>tonic</b>, and <b>mineral</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-dialect_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dialect-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-irish_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irish-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Due to the high sugar content in typical soft drinks, they may also be called <b>sugary drinks</b>.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the 2003 <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard</a> Dialect Survey tracked the usage of the nine most common names. Over half of the survey respondents preferred the term "soda", which was dominant in the <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_United_States" title="Northeastern United States">Northeastern United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, and the areas surrounding <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a> and <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis</a>. The term "pop", which was preferred by 25% of the respondents, was most popular in the <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwest</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a>, while the <a href="/wiki/Genericized_trademark" class="mw-redirect" title="Genericized trademark">genericized trademark</a> "coke", used by 12% of the respondents, was most popular in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">Southern United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dialect_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dialect-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The term "tonic" is <a href="/wiki/Hyperlocal" title="Hyperlocal">hyperlocal</a> to eastern <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, although usage is declining.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the English-speaking parts of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, the term "pop" is prevalent, but "soft drink" is the most common English term used in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, the terms "fizzy drink" and the <a href="/wiki/Genericized_trademark" class="mw-redirect" title="Genericized trademark">genericized trademark</a> "coke" are common. "Pop" and "fizzy pop" are used in <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">northern England</a>, while<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> "mineral"<sup id="cite_ref-irish_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irish-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> or "lemonade" (as a general term) are used in <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. In Scotland, "fizzy juice" or even simply "juice" is colloquially encountered. In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, "fizzy drink" or "soft drink" is typically used.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/South_African_English" title="South African English">South African English</a>, "cool drink" and "cold drink" are used,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> but in South African <a href="/wiki/Indian_English" title="Indian English">Indian English</a>, "cool drink" is most prevalent. Older people often use the term 'mineral'.
</p><p>In Spanish, speakers often use the word <i>refresco</i>, meaning 'refreshment'.  Given its carbonated content, it is also commonly called <i>gaseosa</i>, from <i>agua gaseosa</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origins of soft drinks lie in the development of fruit-flavored drinks. In the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Golden_Age" title="Islamic Golden Age">medieval Middle East</a>, a variety of fruit-flavoured soft drinks were widely drunk, such as <a href="/wiki/Sharbat" title="Sharbat">sharbat</a>, and were often sweetened with ingredients such as <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syrup" title="Syrup">syrup</a> and <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a>. Other common ingredients included <a href="/wiki/Lemon" title="Lemon">lemon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pomegranate" title="Pomegranate">pomegranate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamarind" title="Tamarind">tamarind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jujube" title="Jujube">jujube</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumac" title="Sumac">sumac</a>, <a href="/wiki/Musk" title="Musk">musk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mentha" title="Mentha">mint</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a>. Middle-Eastern drinks later became <a href="/wiki/Islamic_contributions_to_Medieval_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic contributions to Medieval Europe">popular in medieval Europe</a>, where the word "syrup" was derived from <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Tudor_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Tudor England">Tudor England</a>, 'water imperial' was widely drunk; it was a sweetened drink with lemon flavor and containing <a href="/wiki/Cream_of_tartar" class="mw-redirect" title="Cream of tartar">cream of tartar</a>. 'Manays Cryste' was a
sweetened cordial flavored with rosewater, violets or cinnamon.<sup id="cite_ref-Emmins_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emmins-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another early type of soft drink was lemonade, made of water and lemon juice sweetened with honey, but without carbonated water. The <i>Compagnie des Limonadiers</i> of Paris was granted a monopoly for the sale of lemonade soft drinks in 1676. Vendors carried tanks of lemonade on their backs and dispensed cups of the soft drink to Parisians.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbonated_drinks">Carbonated drinks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Carbonated drinks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Carbonated_drinks" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbonated drinks">Carbonated drinks</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Engraving of assorted scientific equipment, such as a pneumatic trough. A dead mouse rests under one glass canister." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg/350px-Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg" width="350" height="288" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg/525px-Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg/700px-Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1063" data-file-height="875" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Priestley_Joseph_pneumatic_trough.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Equipment used by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a> in his experiments on gases and the carbonation of water</div></div></div>
<p>In the late 18th century, scientists made important progress in replicating naturally carbonated <a href="/wiki/Mineral_water" title="Mineral water">mineral waters</a>. In 1767, Englishman <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a> first discovered a method of infusing water with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> to make <a href="/wiki/Carbonated_water" title="Carbonated water">carbonated water</a><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> when he suspended a bowl of distilled water above a beer vat at a local brewery in <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, England. His invention of carbonated water (also known as soda water) is the major and defining component of most soft drinks.<sup id="cite_ref-truetex.com_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-truetex.com-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Priestley found that water treated in this manner had a pleasant taste, and he offered it to his friends as a refreshing drink. In 1772, Priestley published a paper entitled <i>Impregnating Water with Fixed Air</i> in which he describes dripping <i>oil of vitriol</i> (or <a href="/wiki/Sulphuric_acid" class="mw-redirect" title="Sulphuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> as it is now called) onto <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a> to produce carbon dioxide gas, and encouraging the gas to dissolve into an agitated bowl of water.<sup id="cite_ref-truetex.com_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-truetex.com-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another Englishman, <a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Mervin_Nooth&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John Mervin Nooth (page does not exist)">John Mervin Nooth</a>, improved Priestley's design and sold his apparatus for commercial use in pharmacies. Swedish chemist <a href="/wiki/Torbern_Bergman" title="Torbern Bergman">Torbern Bergman</a> invented a generating apparatus that made carbonated water from chalk by the use of sulfuric acid. Bergman's apparatus allowed imitation mineral water to be produced in large amounts. Swedish chemist <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6ns_Jacob_Berzelius" title="Jöns Jacob Berzelius">Jöns Jacob Berzelius</a> started to add flavors (spices, juices, and wine) to carbonated water in the late eighteenth century.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Henry_(apothecary)" title="Thomas Henry (apothecary)">Thomas Henry</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Apothecary" title="Apothecary">apothecary</a> from Manchester, was the first to sell artificial mineral water to the general public for medicinal purposes, beginning in the 1770s. His recipe for 'Bewley's Mephitic Julep' consisted of 3 <a href="/wiki/Dram_(unit)" title="Dram (unit)">drachms</a> of fossil alkali to a <a href="/wiki/Quart" title="Quart">quart</a> of water, and the manufacture had to 'throw in streams of fixed air until all the alkaline taste is destroyed'.<sup id="cite_ref-Emmins_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emmins-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Johann_Jacob_Schweppe" title="Johann Jacob Schweppe">Johann Jacob Schweppe</a> developed a similar process to manufacture <a href="/wiki/Carbonated" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbonated">carbonated</a> <a href="/wiki/Mineral_water" title="Mineral water">mineral water</a> at the same time. He founded the Schweppes Company in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a> in 1783 to sell carbonated water,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and relocated his business to London in 1792. His drink soon gained in popularity; among his new found patrons was <a href="/wiki/Erasmus_Darwin" title="Erasmus Darwin">Erasmus Darwin</a>. In 1843, Schweppes commercialised <a href="/wiki/Malvern_Water" class="mw-redirect" title="Malvern Water">Malvern Water</a> at the Holywell Spring in the Malvern Hills, and received a royal warrant from King William IV.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was not long before flavoring was combined with carbonated water. The earliest reference to carbonated <a href="/wiki/Ginger_beer" title="Ginger beer">ginger beer</a> is in a <i>Practical Treatise on Brewing</i>. published in 1809. The drinking of either natural or artificial mineral <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> was considered at the time to be a healthy practice, and was promoted by advocates of <a href="/wiki/Temperance_movement" title="Temperance movement">temperance</a>. Pharmacists selling mineral waters began to add herbs and chemicals to unflavored mineral water. They used birch bark (see <a href="/wiki/Birch_beer" title="Birch beer">birch beer</a>), <a href="/wiki/Dandelion" class="mw-redirect" title="Dandelion">dandelion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smilax_regelii" class="mw-redirect" title="Smilax regelii">sarsaparilla</a>, fruit extracts, and other substances. Flavorings were also added to improve the taste.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mass_market_and_industrialization">Mass market and industrialization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mass market and industrialization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:(1883)_SCHWEPPES_MINERAL-WATERS.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/%281883%29_SCHWEPPES_MINERAL-WATERS.jpg/220px-%281883%29_SCHWEPPES_MINERAL-WATERS.jpg" width="220" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/%281883%29_SCHWEPPES_MINERAL-WATERS.jpg/330px-%281883%29_SCHWEPPES_MINERAL-WATERS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/%281883%29_SCHWEPPES_MINERAL-WATERS.jpg/440px-%281883%29_SCHWEPPES_MINERAL-WATERS.jpg 2x" data-file-width="932" data-file-height="935" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:(1883)_SCHWEPPES_MINERAL-WATERS.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An 1883 advertisement for <a href="/wiki/Schweppes" title="Schweppes">Schweppes</a> Mineral-Waters</div></div></div>
<p>Soft drinks soon outgrew their origins in the medical world and became a widely consumed product, available cheaply for the masses. By the 1840s there were more than fifty soft drink manufacturers – an increase from just ten in the previous decade. Carbonated <a href="/wiki/Lemonade" title="Lemonade">lemonade</a> was widely available in British refreshment stalls in 1833,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and in 1845 <a href="/wiki/R._White%27s_Lemonade" title="R. White&#39;s Lemonade">R. White's Lemonade</a> went on sale in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> For the <a href="/wiki/Great_Exhibition" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Exhibition">Great Exhibition</a> of 1851 in London, Schweppes was designated the official drink supplier and sold over a million bottles of lemonade, ginger beer, <a href="/wiki/Seltzer_water" class="mw-redirect" title="Seltzer water">Seltzer water</a> and soda-water. There was a Schweppes soda water fountain, situated directly at the entrance to the exhibition.<sup id="cite_ref-Emmins_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emmins-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mixer drinks became popular in the second half of the century. <a href="/wiki/Tonic_water" title="Tonic water">Tonic water</a> was originally <a href="/wiki/Quinine" title="Quinine">quinine</a> added to water as a <a href="/wiki/Prophylaxis" class="mw-redirect" title="Prophylaxis">prophylactic</a> against <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> and was consumed by British officials stationed in the tropical areas of <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. As the quinine powder was so bitter people began mixing the powder with soda and sugar, and a basic tonic water was created. The first commercial tonic water was produced in 1858.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Mixed_drink" title="Mixed drink">mixed drink</a> <a href="/wiki/Gin_and_tonic" title="Gin and tonic">gin and tonic</a> also originated in <a href="/wiki/British_India" class="mw-redirect" title="British India">British colonial India</a>, when the British population would mix their medicinal quinine tonic with <a href="/wiki/Gin" title="Gin">gin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Emmins_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emmins-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Image-Codd_bottle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Image-Codd_bottle.jpg/220px-Image-Codd_bottle.jpg" width="220" height="273" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Image-Codd_bottle.jpg/330px-Image-Codd_bottle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Image-Codd_bottle.jpg/440px-Image-Codd_bottle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="460" data-file-height="570" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Image-Codd_bottle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Codd-neck_bottle" title="Codd-neck bottle">Codd-neck bottle</a> provided an effective seal for soft drinks in the late 19th century</div></div></div>
<p>A persistent problem in the soft drinks industry was the lack of an effective sealing of the bottles. Carbonated drink <a href="/wiki/Bottle" title="Bottle">bottles</a> are under great pressure from the gas, so inventors tried to find the best way to prevent the carbon dioxide or bubbles from escaping. The bottles could also explode if the pressure was too great. <a href="/wiki/Hiram_Codd" title="Hiram Codd">Hiram Codd</a> devised a patented <a href="/wiki/Bottling" class="mw-redirect" title="Bottling">bottling</a> machine while working at a small mineral water works in the <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_Road_(London)" class="mw-redirect" title="Caledonian Road (London)">Caledonian Road</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islington" title="Islington">Islington</a>, in London in 1870. His <a href="/wiki/Codd-neck_bottle" title="Codd-neck bottle">Codd-neck bottle</a> was designed to enclose a <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Rubber" class="mw-redirect" title="Rubber">rubber</a> <a href="/wiki/Washer_(mechanical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Washer (mechanical)">washer</a> in the neck. The bottles were filled upside down, and pressure of the gas in the bottle forced the marble against the washer, sealing in the carbonation. The bottle was pinched into a special shape to provide a chamber into which the marble was pushed to open the bottle. This prevented the marble from blocking the neck as the drink was poured.<sup id="cite_ref-Emmins_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emmins-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By mid-1873 he had granted 20 licences and received a further 50 applications. This was boosted further by a <a href="/wiki/Trade_fair" title="Trade fair">Trade Show</a> held in London in the same year. By 1874 the licence was free to bottle manufacturers as long as they purchased the marbles, sealing rings and used his groove tool, and the mineral water firms they traded with had already bought a licence to use his bottle.
</p><p>In 1892, the "<a href="/wiki/Crown_cork" title="Crown cork">Crown Cork Bottle Seal</a>" was patented by <a href="/wiki/William_Painter_(inventor)" title="William Painter (inventor)">William Painter</a>, a Baltimore, Maryland machine shop operator. It was the first bottle top to successfully keep the bubbles in the bottle. In 1899, the first patent was issued for a <a href="/wiki/Glass-blowing" class="mw-redirect" title="Glass-blowing">glass-blowing</a> machine for the automatic production of glass bottles. Earlier glass bottles had all been hand-blown. Four years later, the new bottle-blowing machine was in operation. It was first operated by the inventor, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Joseph_Owens" title="Michael Joseph Owens">Michael Owens</a>, an employee of Libby Glass Company. Within a few years, glass bottle production increased from 1,400 bottles a day to about 58,000 bottles a day.
</p><p>In America, <a href="/wiki/Soda_fountain" title="Soda fountain">soda fountains</a> were initially more popular, and many Americans would frequent the soda fountain daily. Beginning in 1806, <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> chemistry professor <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Silliman" title="Benjamin Silliman">Benjamin Silliman</a> sold soda waters in <a href="/wiki/New_Haven,_Connecticut" title="New Haven, Connecticut">New Haven, Connecticut</a>. He used a Nooth apparatus to produce his waters. Businessmen in Philadelphia and New York City also began selling soda water in the early 19th century. In the 1830s, <a href="/wiki/John_Matthews_(soda_water_manufacturer)" title="John Matthews (soda water manufacturer)">John Matthews</a> of New York City and John Lippincott of Philadelphia began manufacturing soda fountains. Both men were successful and built large factories for fabricating fountains. Due to problems in the U.S. glass industry, bottled drinks remained a small portion of the market throughout much of the 19th century. (However, they were known in England. In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tenant_of_Wildfell_Hall" title="The Tenant of Wildfell Hall">The Tenant of Wildfell Hall</a></i>, published in 1848, the caddish Huntingdon, recovering from months of debauchery, wakes at noon and gulps a bottle of soda-water.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>)
</p><p>In the early 20th century, sales of bottled soda increased exponentially, and in the second half of the 20th century, canned soft drinks became an important share of the market.
</p><p>During the 1920s, "Home-Paks" were invented. "Home-Paks" are the familiar <a href="/wiki/Carton" title="Carton">six-pack cartons</a> made from cardboard. <a href="/wiki/Vending_machine" title="Vending machine">Vending machines</a> also began to appear in the 1920s. Since then, soft drink vending machines have become increasingly popular. Both hot and cold drinks are sold in these self-service machines throughout the world.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:7-Up_Bottlers_Flavor_jug.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A large green jug" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/7-Up_Bottlers_Flavor_jug.jpg/220px-7-Up_Bottlers_Flavor_jug.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/7-Up_Bottlers_Flavor_jug.jpg/330px-7-Up_Bottlers_Flavor_jug.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/7-Up_Bottlers_Flavor_jug.jpg/440px-7-Up_Bottlers_Flavor_jug.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="2048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:7-Up_Bottlers_Flavor_jug.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A jug of bottler's flavor for 7-Up. The syrup-like concentrate lacks sugar and is sold to franchisees to refill.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Soft_drink_in_Korea.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Soft_drink_in_Korea.jpg/220px-Soft_drink_in_Korea.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Soft_drink_in_Korea.jpg/330px-Soft_drink_in_Korea.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Soft_drink_in_Korea.jpg/440px-Soft_drink_in_Korea.jpg 2x" data-file-width="960" data-file-height="720" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Soft_drink_in_Korea.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Soft drink in Korea</div></div></div>
<p>Soft drinks are made by mixing dry or fresh ingredients with water. Production of soft drinks can be done at factories or at home. Soft drinks can be made at home by mixing a <a href="/wiki/Syrup" title="Syrup">syrup</a> or dry ingredients with carbonated water, or by lacto-fermentation. Syrups are commercially sold by companies such as <a href="/wiki/Soda-Club" class="mw-redirect" title="Soda-Club">Soda-Club</a>; dry ingredients are often sold in pouches, in a style of the popular U.S. drink mix <a href="/wiki/Kool-Aid" title="Kool-Aid">Kool-Aid</a>. Carbonated water is made using a <a href="/wiki/Soda_siphon" class="mw-redirect" title="Soda siphon">soda siphon</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Home_carbonation_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Home carbonation system">home carbonation system</a> or by dropping dry ice into water. Food-grade carbon dioxide, used for carbonating drinks, often comes from <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> plants.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Drinks like <a href="/wiki/Ginger_ale" title="Ginger ale">ginger ale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Root_beer" title="Root beer">root beer</a> are often brewed using <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a> to cause carbonation.
</p><p>Of most importance is that the ingredient meets the agreed specification on all major parameters. This is not only the functional parameter (in other words, the level of the major constituent), but the level of impurities, the microbiological status, and physical parameters such as color, particle size, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some soft drinks contain measurable amounts of alcohol. In some older preparations, this resulted from natural fermentation used to build the carbonation. In the United States, soft drinks (as well as other products such as <a href="/wiki/Low-alcohol_beer" title="Low-alcohol beer">non-alcoholic beer</a>) are allowed by law to contain up to 0.5% <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_by_volume" title="Alcohol by volume">alcohol by volume</a>. Modern drinks introduce carbon dioxide for carbonation, but there is some speculation that alcohol might result from fermentation of sugars in a non-sterile environment. A small amount of alcohol is introduced in some soft drinks where alcohol is used in the preparation of the flavoring extracts such as <a href="/wiki/Vanilla_extract" title="Vanilla extract">vanilla extract</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Producers">Producers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Producers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_soft_drink_producers" title="List of soft drink producers">List of soft drink producers</a></div>
<p>In every area of the world there are major soft drink producers. However, a few major North American companies are present in most of the countries of the world, such as <a href="/wiki/PepsiCo" title="PepsiCo">Pepsi</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Coca-Cola_Company" title="The Coca-Cola Company">Coca Cola</a>. Major North American producers other than the two previously-named companies include <a href="/wiki/Cott" title="Cott">Cott</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dr_Pepper_Snapple_Group" title="Dr Pepper Snapple Group">Dr Pepper Snapple Group</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jones_Soda" title="Jones Soda">Jones Soda</a>.
</p>

<p>The over-consumption of sugar-sweetened soft drinks is associated with <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Malik,_Schulze,_&amp;_Hu_(2006)_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Malik,_Schulze,_&amp;_Hu_(2006)-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Vartanian,_Schwartz,_&amp;_Brownell_(2007)_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vartanian,_Schwartz,_&amp;_Brownell_(2007)-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus_type_2" title="Diabetes mellitus type 2">type 2 diabetes</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-bmj_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bmj-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dental_caries" class="mw-redirect" title="Dental caries">dental caries</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">low nutrient levels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Vartanian,_Schwartz,_&amp;_Brownell_(2007)_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vartanian,_Schwartz,_&amp;_Brownell_(2007)-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Experimental studies tend to support a causal role for sugar-sweetened soft drinks in these ailments,<sup id="cite_ref-Malik,_Schulze,_&amp;_Hu_(2006)_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Malik,_Schulze,_&amp;_Hu_(2006)-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Vartanian,_Schwartz,_&amp;_Brownell_(2007)_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vartanian,_Schwartz,_&amp;_Brownell_(2007)-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> though this is challenged by other researchers.<sup id="cite_ref-Gibson_(2008)_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibson_(2008)-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> "Sugar-sweetened" includes drinks that use <a href="/wiki/High-fructose_corn_syrup" title="High-fructose corn syrup">high-fructose corn syrup</a>, as well as those using sucrose.
</p><p>Many soft drinks contain ingredients that are themselves sources of concern: <a href="/wiki/Caffeine" title="Caffeine">caffeine</a> is linked to anxiety and sleep disruption when consumed in excess,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and some critics<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (May 2018)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> question the health effects of added sugars and <a href="/wiki/Sugar_substitutes#Health_issues" class="mw-redirect" title="Sugar substitutes">artificial sweeteners</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sodium_benzoate" title="Sodium benzoate">Sodium benzoate</a> has been investigated by researchers at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Sheffield" title="University of Sheffield">University of Sheffield</a><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> as a possible cause of <a href="/wiki/DNA_damage" class="mw-redirect" title="DNA damage">DNA damage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hyperactivity" class="mw-redirect" title="Hyperactivity">hyperactivity</a>. Other substances have negative health effects, but are present in such small quantities that they are unlikely to pose any substantial health risk provided that the drinks are consumed only in moderation.
</p><p>In 1998, the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Science_in_the_Public_Interest" title="Center for Science in the Public Interest">Center for Science in the Public Interest</a> published a report titled <i><a href="/wiki/Liquid_Candy" title="Liquid Candy">Liquid Candy: How Soft Drinks are Harming Americans' Health</a></i>. The report examined statistics relating to the increase in soft drink consumption and claimed that consumption is "likely contributing to health problems". It also criticized marketing efforts by soft drink companies.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>  In 2005, the CSPI called for warning labels on soft drinks, similar to those on <a href="/wiki/Cigarettes" class="mw-redirect" title="Cigarettes">cigarettes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Obesity_and_weight-related_diseases">Obesity and weight-related diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Obesity and weight-related diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1977 to 2002, Americans doubled their consumption of <a href="/wiki/Sweetened_beverage" title="Sweetened beverage">sweetened beverages</a><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>—a trend that was paralleled by doubling the prevalence of obesity.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The consumption of sugar-sweetened beverages is associated with weight and obesity, and changes in consumption can help predict changes in weight.<sup id="cite_ref-Malik,_Schulze,_&amp;_Hu_(2006)_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Malik,_Schulze,_&amp;_Hu_(2006)-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Vartanian,_Schwartz,_&amp;_Brownell_(2007)_27-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vartanian,_Schwartz,_&amp;_Brownell_(2007)-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It remains possible that the correlation is due to a third factor: people who lead unhealthy lifestyles might consume more soft drinks. If so, then the association between soft drink consumption and weight gain could reflect the consequences of an unhealthy lifestyle rather than the consequences of consuming soft drinks. Experimental evidence is needed to definitively establish the causal role of soft drink consumption. Reviews of the experimental evidence suggest that soft drink consumption does cause weight gain,<sup id="cite_ref-Malik,_Schulze,_&amp;_Hu_(2006)_26-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Malik,_Schulze,_&amp;_Hu_(2006)-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Vartanian,_Schwartz,_&amp;_Brownell_(2007)_27-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vartanian,_Schwartz,_&amp;_Brownell_(2007)-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> but the effect is often small except for overweight individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-Gibson_(2008)_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibson_(2008)-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many of these experiments examined the influence of sugar-sweetened soft drinks on weight gain in children and adolescents. In one experiment, adolescents replaced sugar-sweetened soft drinks in their diet with artificially sweetened soft drinks that were sent to their homes over 25 weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Compared with children in a control group, children who received the artificially sweetened drinks saw a smaller increase in their BMI (by −0.14&#160;kg/m<sup>2</sup>), but this effect was only statistically significant among the heaviest children (who saw a benefit of −0.75&#160;kg/m<sup>2</sup>). In another study, an educational program encouraged schoolchildren to consume fewer soft drinks.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> During the school year, the prevalence of obesity decreased among children in the program by 0.2%, compared to a 7.5% increase among children in the control group. Another study, published in <a href="/wiki/Pediatrics_(journal)" title="Pediatrics (journal)">Pediatrics</a> in 2013, concluded that for children from the age of 2 to 5, their risk of obesity increased by 43% if they were regular soft drink consumers as opposed to those who rarely or never consumed them.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sugar-sweetened drinks have also been speculated<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> to cause weight gain in adults. In one study, overweight individuals consumed a daily supplement of sucrose-sweetened or artificially sweetened drinks or foods for a 10-week period.<sup id="cite_ref-Raben,_Vasilaras,_Moller,_&amp;_Astrup_(2002)_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raben,_Vasilaras,_Moller,_&amp;_Astrup_(2002)-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Most of the supplement was in the form of soft drinks. Individuals in the sucrose group gained 1.6&#160;kg, and individuals in the artificial-sweetener group lost 1.0&#160;kg. A two-week study had participants supplement their diet with sugar-sweetened soft drinks, artificially sweetened soft drinks, or neither.<sup id="cite_ref-Tordoff_&amp;_Alleva_(1990)_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tordoff_&amp;_Alleva_(1990)-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Although the participants gained the most weight when consuming the sugar-sweetened drinks, some of the differences were unreliable: the differences between men who consumed sugar-sweetened drinks or no drinks was not statistically significant.
</p><p>Other research suggests that soft drinks could play a special role in weight gain. One four-week experiment compared a 450 calorie/day supplement of sugar-sweetened soft drinks to a 450 calorie/day supplement of <a href="/wiki/Jelly_beans" class="mw-redirect" title="Jelly beans">jelly beans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DiMeglio_&amp;_Mattes_(2000)_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DiMeglio_&amp;_Mattes_(2000)-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> The jelly bean supplement did not lead to weight gain, but the soft drink supplement did. The likely reason for the difference in weight gain is that people who consumed the jelly beans lowered their caloric intake at subsequent meals, while people who consumed soft drinks did not. Thus, the low levels of satiety provided by sugar-sweetened soft drinks may explain their association with obesity. That is, people who consume calories in sugar-sweetened drinks may fail to adequately reduce their intake of calories from other sources. Indeed, people consume more total calories in meals and on days when they are given sugar-sweetened drinkss than when they are given artificially sweetened drinks<sup id="cite_ref-Tordoff_&amp;_Alleva_(1990)_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tordoff_&amp;_Alleva_(1990)-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rolls,_Kim,_&amp;_Fedoroff_(1990)_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rolls,_Kim,_&amp;_Fedoroff_(1990)-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> or water.<sup id="cite_ref-Rolls,_Kim,_&amp;_Fedoroff_(1990)_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rolls,_Kim,_&amp;_Fedoroff_(1990)-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> However, these results are contradicted by a study by Adam Drewnowski published in 2004, in which "32 subjects consumed a 300-calorie snack of fat-free raspberry cookies or regular cola on two occasions each – either two hours ("early") or 20 minutes ("late") before lunch." It found that "...the calories eaten at lunch were not affected by whether the snack was cookies or cola."<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The consumption of sugar-sweetened soft drinks can also be associated with many weight-related diseases, including diabetes,<sup id="cite_ref-bmj_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bmj-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> metabolic syndrome and cardiovascular risk factors,<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> and elevated blood pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-Raben,_Vasilaras,_Moller,_&amp;_Astrup_(2002)_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raben,_Vasilaras,_Moller,_&amp;_Astrup_(2002)-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to research presented at the <a href="/wiki/American_Heart_Association" title="American Heart Association">American Heart Association</a>'s Epidemiology and Prevention/Nutrition, Physical Activity and Metabolism 2013 Scientific Sessions by researchers at the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_School_of_Public_Health" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard School of Public Health">Harvard School of Public Health</a>, sugar-sweetened beverages may be responsible for 180,000 deaths every year worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dental_decay">Dental decay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Dental decay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Soft_drink_shelf_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Soft_drink_shelf_2.jpg/250px-Soft_drink_shelf_2.jpg" width="250" height="470" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Soft_drink_shelf_2.jpg/375px-Soft_drink_shelf_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Soft_drink_shelf_2.jpg/500px-Soft_drink_shelf_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="3008" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Soft_drink_shelf_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Soft drinks displayed on grocery store shelves.</div></div></div>
<p>Most soft drinks contain high concentrations of simple <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>: <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a> and other simple sugars. If oral bacteria ferment <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a> and produce acids that may dissolve tooth enamel and induce dental decay, then sweetened drinks may increase the risk of <a href="/wiki/Dental_caries" class="mw-redirect" title="Dental caries">dental caries</a>. The risk would be greater if the frequency of consumption is high.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A large number of soda pops are <a href="/wiki/Acid" title="Acid">acidic</a> as are many fruits, sauces and other foods. Drinking acidic drinks over a long period and continuous sipping may <a href="/wiki/Dental_erosion" class="mw-redirect" title="Dental erosion">erode the tooth enamel</a>. A 2007 study determined that some flavored sparkling waters are as erosive or more so than orange juice.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Using a <a href="/wiki/Drinking_straw" title="Drinking straw">drinking straw</a> is often advised by <a href="/wiki/Dentistry" title="Dentistry">dentists</a> as the drink does not come into as much contact with the teeth. It has also been suggested that <a href="/wiki/Oral_hygiene" title="Oral hygiene">brushing teeth</a> right after drinking soft drinks should be avoided as this can result in additional erosion to the teeth due to the presence of acid.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hypokalemia">Hypokalemia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Hypokalemia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There have been a handful of published reports describing individuals with severe <a href="/wiki/Hypokalemia" title="Hypokalemia">hypokalemia</a> (low potassium levels) related to extreme consumption of colas.<sup id="cite_ref-SharmaGuber2013_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SharmaGuber2013-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soft_drinks_related_to_bone_density_and_bone_loss">Soft drinks related to bone density and bone loss</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Soft drinks related to bone density and bone loss">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a meta-analysis of 88 studies, drinking soda correlates with a decrease in <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a> consumption along with the <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_D" title="Vitamin D">vitamin D</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B6" title="Vitamin B6">vitamin B6</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_B12" title="Vitamin B12">vitamin B12</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a>, protein and other <a href="/wiki/Micronutrient" title="Micronutrient">micronutrients</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Harvard_School_of_Public_Health_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvard_School_of_Public_Health-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">Phosphorus</a>, a micronutrient, can be found in cola-type drinks, but there may be a risk in consuming too much.<sup id="cite_ref-Harvard_School_of_Public_Health_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvard_School_of_Public_Health-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> Phosphorus and calcium are used in the body to create calcium-phosphate, which is the main component of bone. However, the combination of too much phosphorus with too little calcium in the body can lead to a degeneration of <a href="/wiki/Bone_mass" class="mw-redirect" title="Bone mass">bone mass</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Harvard_School_of_Public_Health_57-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harvard_School_of_Public_Health-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
Research suggests a statistically significant inverse relationship between consumption of carbonated drinks and bone mineral density in young girls, which places them at increased risk of fractures.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One hypothesis to explain this relationship is that the <a href="/wiki/Phosphoric_acid#Processed_food_use" title="Phosphoric acid">phosphoric acid</a> contained in some soft drinks (colas) displaces calcium from the bones, lowering bone density of the skeleton and leading to weakened bones, or <a href="/wiki/Osteoporosis" title="Osteoporosis">osteoporosis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Heaney2001_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heaney2001-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> However, 2001 calcium metabolism studies by Dr. Robert Heaney suggested that the net effect of carbonated soft drinks, (including colas, which use phosphoric acid as the acidulant) on calcium excretion in urine was negligible. Heaney concluded that carbonated soft drinks, which do not contain the nutrients needed for bone health, may displace other foods which do, and that the real issue is that people who drink a lot of soft drinks also tend to have an overall diet that is low in calcium.<sup id="cite_ref-Heaney2001_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heaney2001-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2006 study of several thousand men and women, found that women who regularly drank cola-based sodas (three or more a day) had significantly lower bone mineral density (BMD) of ~4&#160;% in the hip than those who didn't, even though researchers controlled for important factors like calcium and vitamin D intake. The study also found that women who drank non-cola soft drinks didn't appear to have lower BMD and that BMD of women drinking decaffeineted cola wasn't as low as women drinking caffeinated cola sodas. The study found that the effect of regular consumption of cola sodas was not significant on men's BMD.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1950s and 1960s there were attempts in France and Japan to ban the sale of Coca-Cola as dangerous since phosphates can block calcium absorption. However, these were unsuccessful as the amounts of phosphate were shown to be too small to have a significant effect.<sup id="cite_ref-Phosphatesin_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Phosphatesin-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sugar_content">Sugar content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sugar content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Diet_drink" title="Diet drink">Diet drink</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">USDA</a>'s recommended daily intake (<a href="/wiki/Reference_Daily_Intake" title="Reference Daily Intake">RDI</a>) of added sugars is less than 10 teaspoons per day for a 2,000-<a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">calorie</a> diet.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> High caloric intake contributes to <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a> if not balanced with exercise, with a large amount of exercise being required to offset even small but calorie-rich food and drinks.
</p><p>Until 1985, most of the calories in soft drinks came from <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a> or <a href="/wiki/Corn_syrup" title="Corn syrup">corn syrup</a>. As of 2010, in the United States <a href="/wiki/High-fructose_corn_syrup" title="High-fructose corn syrup">high-fructose corn syrup</a> (HFCS) is used nearly exclusively as a sweetener because of its lower cost,<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> while in Europe, <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">sucrose</a> dominates, because <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> agricultural policies favor production of <a href="/wiki/Sugar_beet" title="Sugar beet">sugar beets</a> in Europe proper and <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a> in the former colonies over the production of corn. HFCS has been criticized<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> as having a number of detrimental effects on human health, such as promoting diabetes, hyperactivity, hypertension, and a host of other problems.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Although <a href="/wiki/Anecdotal_evidence" title="Anecdotal evidence">anecdotal evidence</a> has been presented to support such claims, it is well known that the human body breaks sucrose down into <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fructose" title="Fructose">fructose</a> before it is absorbed by the intestines. Simple sugars such as fructose are converted into the same intermediates as in <a href="/wiki/Glucose_metabolism" class="mw-redirect" title="Glucose metabolism">glucose metabolism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> However, metabolism of fructose is extremely rapid and is initiated by <a href="/wiki/Fructokinase" title="Fructokinase">fructokinase</a>. Fructokinase activity is not regulated by metabolism or hormones and proceeds rapidly after intake of fructose. While the intermediates of fructose metabolism are similar to those of glucose, the rates of formation are excessive. This fact promotes <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid" title="Fatty acid">fatty acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Triglyceride" title="Triglyceride">triglyceride</a> synthesis in the liver, leading to accumulation of fat throughout the body and possibly <a href="/wiki/Non-alcoholic_fatty_liver_disease" title="Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease">non-alcoholic fatty liver disease</a>. Increased <a href="/wiki/Blood_lipid" class="mw-redirect" title="Blood lipid">blood lipid</a> levels also seem to follow fructose ingestion over time. A sugar drink or high-sugar drink may refer to any <a href="/wiki/Drink" title="Drink">drink</a> consisting primarily of <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">water</a> and sugar (often  <a href="/wiki/Cane_sugar" class="mw-redirect" title="Cane sugar">cane sugar</a> or high-fructose corn syrup), including some soft drinks, <a href="/wiki/Fruit_juice" class="mw-redirect" title="Fruit juice">some fruit juices</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Energy_drink" title="Energy drink">energy drinks</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Benzene">Benzene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Benzene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Benzene_in_soft_drinks" title="Benzene in soft drinks">Benzene in soft drinks</a></div>
<p>In 2006, the United Kingdom <a href="/wiki/Food_Standards_Agency" title="Food Standards Agency">Food Standards Agency</a> published the results of its survey of <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> levels in soft drinks,<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> which tested 150 products and found that four contained benzene levels above the <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a> (WHO) guidelines for drinking water.
</p><p>The United States <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> released its own test results of several soft drinks containing <a href="/wiki/Benzoates" title="Benzoates">benzoates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ascorbic_acid" class="mw-redirect" title="Ascorbic acid">ascorbic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Erythorbic_acid" title="Erythorbic acid">erythorbic acid</a>. Five tested drinks contained benzene levels above the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency's</a> recommended standard of 5 <a href="/wiki/Parts_per_billion" class="mw-redirect" title="Parts per billion">ppb</a>. The Environmental Working Group<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> has uncovered additional FDA test results that showed the following results: Of 24 samples of diet soda tested between 1995 and 2001 for the presence of benzene, 19 (79%) had amounts of benzene in excess of the federal tap water standard of 5 ppb. Average benzene levels were 19 ppb, about four times tap water standard. One sample contained 55 ppb of benzene, 11 fold tap water standards. Despite these findings, as of 2006, the FDA stated its belief that "the levels of benzene found in soft drinks and other beverages to date do not pose a safety concern for consumers".<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pesticides_in_India">Pesticides in India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Pesticides in India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2003, the Delhi non-profit <a href="/wiki/Centre_for_Science_and_Environment" title="Centre for Science and Environment">Centre for Science and Environment</a> published<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> a disputed report finding <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticide</a> levels in Coke and Pepsi soft drinks sold in India at levels 30 times that considered safe by the <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">European Economic Community</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> This was found in primarily 12 cold drink brands sold in and around <a href="/wiki/New_Delhi" title="New Delhi">New Delhi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> The Indian Health Minister said the CSE tests were inaccurate, and said that the government's tests found pesticide levels within India's standards but above EU standards.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A similar CSE report in August 2006 prompted many state governments to have issued a ban of the sale of soft drinks in schools. <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a> issued a complete ban on the sale or manufacture of soft drinks altogether. (These were later struck down in court.)  In return, the soft drink companies like Coca-Cola and Pepsi have issued ads in the media regarding the safety of consumption of the drinks.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The UK-based Central Science Laboratory, commissioned by Coke, found its products met EU standards in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> Coke and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a> commissioned an independent study of its bottling plants by The Energy and Resources Institute (TERI), which reported in 2008 no unsafe chemicals in the water supply used.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kidney_stones">Kidney stones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Kidney stones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A study published in the <i>Clinical Journal of the <a href="/wiki/American_Society_of_Nephrology" title="American Society of Nephrology">American Society of Nephrology</a></i> in 2013 concluded that consumption of soft drinks was associated with a 23% higher risk of developing <a href="/wiki/Kidney_stones" class="mw-redirect" title="Kidney stones">kidney stones</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_regulation">Government regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Government regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Schools">Schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since at least 2006, debate on whether high-calorie soft drink <a href="/wiki/Vending_machine" title="Vending machine">vending machines</a> should be allowed in schools has been on the rise. Opponents of the (soft drink) vending machines believe that soft drinks are a significant contributor to <a href="/wiki/Childhood_obesity" title="Childhood obesity">childhood obesity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tooth_decay" title="Tooth decay">tooth decay</a>, and that allowing soft drink sales in schools encourages children to believe they are safe to consume in moderate to large quantities.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> Opponents argue that schools have a responsibility to look after the health of the children in their care, and that allowing children easy access to soft drinks violates that responsibility.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> Vending machine proponents believe that obesity is a complex issue and soft drinks are not the only cause.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> A 2011 bill to tax soft drinks in California failed, with some opposing lawmakers arguing that parents—not the government—should be responsible for children's drink choices.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On May 3, 2006, the Alliance for a Healthier Generation,<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cadbury_Schweppes" class="mw-redirect" title="Cadbury Schweppes">Cadbury Schweppes</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Coca-Cola_Company" title="The Coca-Cola Company">The Coca-Cola Company</a>, <a href="/wiki/PepsiCo" title="PepsiCo">PepsiCo</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/American_Beverage_Association" title="American Beverage Association">American Beverage Association</a> announced new guidelines<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> that will voluntarily remove high-calorie soft drinks from all U.S. schools.
</p><p>On May 19, 2006, the British <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Education_and_Skills" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for Education and Skills">education secretary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Johnson" title="Alan Johnson">Alan Johnson</a>, announced new minimum nutrition standards for school food. Among a wide range of measures, from September 2006, school lunches will be free from carbonated drinks. Schools will also end the sale of junk food (including carbonated drinks) in vending machines and <a href="/wiki/Tuck_shop" title="Tuck shop">tuck shops</a>.
</p><p>In 2008, Samantha K Graff published an article in the <i>Annals of the American Academy of Political and Social Science</i> regarding the "First Amendment Implications of Restricting Food and Beverages Marketing in Schools". The article examines a school district's policy regarding limiting the sale and marketing of soda in public schools, and how certain policies can invoke a violation of the <a href="/wiki/First_Amendment" class="mw-redirect" title="First Amendment">First Amendment</a>. Due to district budget cuts and loss in state funding, many school districts allow commercial businesses to market and advertise their product (including junk food and soda) to public school students for additional revenue. Junk food and soda companies have acquired exclusive rights to vending machines throughout many public school campuses. Opponents of corporate marketing and advertising on school grounds urge school officials to restrict or limit a corporation's power to promote, market, and sell their product to school students. In the 1970s, the Supreme Court ruled that <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertising</a> was not a form of <a href="/wiki/Free_expression" class="mw-redirect" title="Free expression">free expression</a>, but a form of <a href="/wiki/Business_practices" class="mw-redirect" title="Business practices">business practices</a> which should be regulated by the government. In the 1976 case of <i>Virginia State Board of Pharmacy v. Virginia Citizens Consumer Council</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> the Supreme Court ruled that advertising, or "<a href="/wiki/Commercial_speech" title="Commercial speech">commercial speech</a>", to some degree is protected under the First Amendment. To avoid a First Amendment challenge by corporations, public schools could create contracts that restrict the sale of certain product and advertising. Public schools can also ban the selling of all food and drink products on campus, while not infringing on a corporation's right to free speech.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On December 13, 2010, President Obama signed the Healthy Hunger Free Kids Act of 2010<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> (effective in 2014) that mandates schools that receive federal funding must offer healthy snacks and drinks to students. The act bans the selling of soft drinks to students and requires schools to provide healthier options such as water, unflavored low-fat milk, 100% fruit and vegetable drinks or sugar-free carbonated drinks. The portion sizes available to students will be based on age: eight ounces for elementary schools, twelve ounces for middle and high schools. Proponents of the act predict the new mandate it will make it easier for students to make healthy drink choices while at school.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, Terry-McElarth and colleagues published a study in the <i>American Journal of Preventative Medicine</i> on regular soda policies and their effect on school drink availability and student consumption. The purpose of the study was to determine the effectiveness of a program beginning in the 2014–2015 school year that requires schools participating in federally reimbursable meal programs to remove all competitive venues (a la carte cafeteria sales, vending machines, and stores/snack bars/carts), on the availability of unhealthy drinks at schools and student consumption. The study analyzed state- and school district-level policies mandating soda bans and found that state bans were associated with significantly lower school soda availability but district bans showed no significant associations. In addition, no significant correlation was observed between state policies and student consumption. Among student populations, state policy was directly associated with significantly lower school soda availability and indirectly associated with lower student consumption. The same was not observed for other student populations.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxation">Taxation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Taxation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sugary_drinks_tax" class="mw-redirect" title="Sugary drinks tax">Sugary drinks tax</a></div>
<p>In the United States, legislators, health experts and consumer advocates are considering levying higher <a href="/wiki/Tax" title="Tax">taxes</a> on the sale of soft drinks and other sweetened products to help curb the epidemic of <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a> among Americans, and its harmful impact on overall health. Some speculate that higher taxes could help reduce soda consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-Duffey2010_92-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duffey2010-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> Others say that taxes should help fund education to increase consumer awareness of the unhealthy effects of excessive soft drink consumption, and also help cover costs of caring for conditions resulting from overconsumption.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Food_industry" title="Food industry">food</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drink_industry" title="Drink industry">drink industry</a> holds considerable clout in Washington, DC, as it has contributed more than $50 million to legislators since 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2013, a British <a href="/wiki/Lobby_group" class="mw-redirect" title="Lobby group">lobby group</a> called for the price of sugary fizzy drinks to be increased, with the money raised (an estimated £1 billion at 20p per litre) to be put towards a "Children's Future Fund", overseen by an independent body, which would encourage children to eat healthily in school.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2017, the United Arab Emirates imposed a 50% tax on soft drinks and energy drinks to curb excess consumption of the commodity and for additional revenue.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bans">Bans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Soft_drink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Bans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In March 2013, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>'s mayor <a href="/wiki/Michael_Bloomberg" title="Michael Bloomberg">Michael Bloomberg</a> proposed to ban the sale of non-diet soft drinks larger than 16 ounces, except in convenience stores and supermarkets. A lawsuit against the ban was upheld by a state judge, who voiced concerns that the ban was "fraught with arbitrary and capricious consequences". Bloomberg announced that he would be appealing the verdict.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
