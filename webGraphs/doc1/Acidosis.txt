<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Acidosis</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For acidosis referring to acidity of the urine, see <a href="/wiki/Renal_tubular_acidosis" title="Renal tubular acidosis">renal tubular acidosis</a>.</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Acidemia" redirects here. It is not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Academia" class="mw-redirect" title="Academia">Academia</a>.</div>
<table class="infobox" style="width:22em"><tbody><tr><th colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-size:125%;font-weight:bold;background:#ccc">Acidosis</th></tr><tr><th scope="row"><a href="/wiki/Specialty_(medicine)" title="Specialty (medicine)">Specialty</a></th><td>
<a href="/wiki/Critical_care_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Critical care medicine">Critical care medicine</a></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><b>Acidosis</b> is a process causing increased <a href="/wiki/Acidity" class="mw-redirect" title="Acidity">acidity</a> in the blood and other body tissues (i.e., an increased <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_ion" title="Hydrogen ion">hydrogen ion</a> <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentration</a>).  If not further qualified, it usually refers to acidity of the <a href="/wiki/Blood_plasma" title="Blood plasma">blood plasma</a>.
</p><p>The term <i><b>acidemia</b></i> describes the state of low blood pH, while <i>acidosis</i> is used to describe the processes leading to these states. Nevertheless, the terms are sometimes used interchangeably. The distinction may be relevant where a patient has factors causing both acidosis and alkalosis, wherein the relative severity of both determines whether the result is a high, low, or normal pH.
</p><p>Acidosis is said to occur when arterial <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> falls below 7.35 (except in the fetus – see below), while its counterpart (<a href="/wiki/Alkalosis" title="Alkalosis">alkalosis</a>) occurs at a pH over 7.45. <a href="/wiki/Arterial_blood_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Arterial blood gas">Arterial blood gas</a> analysis and other tests are required to separate the main causes.
</p><p>The rate of cellular metabolic activity affects and, at the same time, is affected by the pH of the body fluids. In mammals, the normal pH of arterial blood lies between 7.35 and 7.50 depending on the species (e.g., healthy human-arterial blood pH varies between 7.35 and 7.45). Blood pH values compatible with life in mammals are limited to a pH range between 6.8 and 7.8. Changes in the pH of arterial blood (and therefore the extracellular fluid) outside this range result in irreversible cell damage.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">Signs and symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acidosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Symptoms_of_acidosis.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Symptoms_of_acidosis.png/250px-Symptoms_of_acidosis.png" width="250" height="229" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Symptoms_of_acidosis.png/375px-Symptoms_of_acidosis.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Symptoms_of_acidosis.png/500px-Symptoms_of_acidosis.png 2x" data-file-width="2006" data-file-height="1837" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Symptoms_of_acidosis.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>General symptoms of acidosis.<sup id="cite_ref-Yee2010_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yee2010-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> These usually accompany symptoms of another primary defect (respiratory or metabolic).</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">Nervous system</a> involvement may be seen with acidosis and occurs more often with respiratory acidosis than with metabolic acidosis. Signs and symptoms that may be seen in acidosis include <a href="/wiki/Headache" title="Headache">headaches</a>, confusion, <a href="/wiki/Fatigue_(medical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fatigue (medical)">feeling tired</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tremor" title="Tremor">tremors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somnolence" title="Somnolence">sleepiness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asterixis" title="Asterixis">flapping tremor</a>, and dysfunction of the <a href="/wiki/Cerebrum" title="Cerebrum">cerebrum</a> of the brain which may progress to coma if there is no intervention.<sup id="cite_ref-Yee2010_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yee2010-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metabolic_acidosis">Metabolic acidosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acidosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Metabolic acidosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acidosis&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span>  <small><i>(October 2014)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_acidosis" title="Metabolic acidosis">Metabolic acidosis</a></div>
<p>Metabolic acidosis may result  from either increased production of metabolic acids, such as lactic acid, or disturbances in the ability to excrete acid via the <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidneys</a>, such as either renal tubular acidosis or the renal acidosis of renal failure, which is associated with an accumulation of <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Creatinine" title="Creatinine">creatinine</a> as well as metabolic acid residues of protein <a href="/wiki/Catabolism" title="Catabolism">catabolism</a>.
</p><p>An increase in the production of other acids may also produce metabolic acidosis.  For example, <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acidosis" title="Lactic acidosis">lactic acidosis</a> may occur from:
</p>
<ol><li>severe (PaO<sub>2</sub> &lt;36mm Hg) <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">hypoxemia</a> causing a fall in the rate of oxygen diffusion from arterial blood to tissues</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hypoperfusion" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypoperfusion">hypoperfusion</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Hypovolemic_shock" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypovolemic shock">hypovolemic shock</a>) causing an inadequate blood delivery of oxygen to tissues.</li></ol>
<p>A rise in lactate out of proportion to the level of <a href="/wiki/Pyruvate" class="mw-redirect" title="Pyruvate">pyruvate</a>, e.g., in mixed venous blood, is termed "excess lactate", and may also be an indicator of <a href="/wiki/Fermentation" title="Fermentation">fermentation</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_metabolism" class="mw-redirect" title="Anaerobic metabolism">anaerobic metabolism</a> occurring in muscle cells, as seen during strenuous exercise. Once oxygenation is restored, the acidosis clears quickly.  Another example of increased production of acids occurs in <a href="/wiki/Starvation" title="Starvation">starvation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diabetic_ketoacidosis" title="Diabetic ketoacidosis">diabetic ketoacidosis</a>.  It is due to the accumulation of ketoacids (via excessive <a href="/wiki/Ketosis" title="Ketosis">ketosis</a>) and reflects a severe shift from <a href="/wiki/Glycolysis" title="Glycolysis">glycolysis</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lipolysis" title="Lipolysis">lipolysis</a> for energy needs.
</p><p>Acid consumption from <a href="/wiki/Poison" title="Poison">poisoning</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> ingestion, elevated levels of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> in the blood, and chronically decreased production of <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> may also produce metabolic acidosis.
</p><p>Metabolic acidosis is compensated for in the lungs, as increased exhalation of carbon dioxide promptly shifts the buffering equation to reduce metabolic acid. This is a result of stimulation to <a href="/wiki/Chemoreceptor" title="Chemoreceptor">chemoreceptors</a>, which increases <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_ventilation" class="mw-redirect" title="Alveolar ventilation">alveolar ventilation</a>, leading to respiratory compensation, otherwise known as <a href="/wiki/Kussmaul_breathing" title="Kussmaul breathing">Kussmaul breathing</a> (a specific type of <a href="/wiki/Hyperventilation" title="Hyperventilation">hyperventilation</a>). Should this situation persist, the patient is at risk for exhaustion leading to <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_failure" title="Respiratory failure">respiratory failure</a>.
</p><p>Mutations to the <a href="/wiki/V-ATPase" title="V-ATPase">V-ATPase</a> 'a4' or 'B1' isoforms result in distal renal tubular acidosis, a condition that leads to metabolic acidosis, in some cases with sensorineural <a href="/wiki/Deafness" class="mw-redirect" title="Deafness">deafness</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Arterial_blood_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Arterial blood gas">Arterial blood gases</a> will indicate low <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a>, low blood <a href="/wiki/HCO3" class="mw-redirect" title="HCO3">HCO<sub>3</sub></a>, and normal or low PaCO<sub>2</sub>.
In addition to arterial blood gas, an <a href="/wiki/Anion_gap" title="Anion gap">anion gap</a> can also differentiate between possible causes.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Henderson-Hasselbalch_equation" class="mw-redirect" title="Henderson-Hasselbalch equation">Henderson-Hasselbalch equation</a> is useful for calculating blood pH, because blood is a <a href="/wiki/Buffer_solution" title="Buffer solution">buffer solution</a>. In the clinical setting, this equation is usually used to calculate <a href="/wiki/HCO3" class="mw-redirect" title="HCO3">HCO<sub>3</sub></a> from measurements of pH and PaCO2 in <a href="/wiki/Arterial_blood_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Arterial blood gas">arterial blood gases</a>. The amount of metabolic acid accumulating can also be quantitated by using buffer base deviation, a derivative estimate of the metabolic as opposed to the respiratory component. In hypovolemic shock for example, approximately 50% of the metabolic acid accumulation is <a href="/wiki/Lactic_acid" title="Lactic acid">lactic acid</a>, which disappears as blood flow and oxygen debt are corrected.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment">Treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acidosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Treatment of uncompensated metabolic acidosis is focused upon correcting the underlying problem. When metabolic acidosis is severe and can no longer be compensated for adequately by the lungs or kidneys ,neutralizing the acidosis with infusions of <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> may be required.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fetal_metabolic_acidemia">Fetal metabolic acidemia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acidosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fetal metabolic acidemia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a>, the normal range differs based on which umbilical vessel is sampled (<a href="/wiki/Umbilical_vein" title="Umbilical vein">umbilical vein</a> pH is normally 7.25 to 7.45; <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_artery" title="Umbilical artery">umbilical artery</a> pH is normally 7.18 to 7.38).<sup id="cite_ref-Yeomans_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yeomans-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  Fetal metabolic <a href="/wiki/Acidemia" class="mw-redirect" title="Acidemia">acidemia</a> is defined as an umbilical vessel pH of less than 7.20 and a <a href="/wiki/Base_excess" title="Base excess">base excess</a> of less than −8.<sup id="cite_ref-Pomerance_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pomerance-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Respiratory_acidosis">Respiratory acidosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acidosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Respiratory acidosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_acidosis" title="Respiratory acidosis">Respiratory acidosis</a></div>
<p>Respiratory acidosis results from a build-up of carbon dioxide in the blood (<a href="/wiki/Hypercapnia" title="Hypercapnia">hypercapnia</a>) due to <a href="/wiki/Hypoventilation" title="Hypoventilation">hypoventilation</a>. It is most often caused by <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">pulmonary</a> problems, although <a href="/wiki/Head_injury" title="Head injury">head injuries</a>, drugs (especially <a href="/wiki/Anaesthetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Anaesthetic">anaesthetics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sedative" title="Sedative">sedatives</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Brain_tumor" title="Brain tumor">brain tumors</a> can cause this acidemia. <a href="/wiki/Pneumothorax" title="Pneumothorax">Pneumothorax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emphysema" class="mw-redirect" title="Emphysema">emphysema</a>, chronic <a href="/wiki/Bronchitis" title="Bronchitis">bronchitis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>, severe <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aspiration_pneumonia" title="Aspiration pneumonia">aspiration</a> are among the most frequent causes. It can also occur as a compensatory response to chronic <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_alkalosis" title="Metabolic alkalosis">metabolic alkalosis</a>.
</p><p>One key to distinguish between respiratory and metabolic acidosis is that in respiratory acidosis, the CO<sub>2</sub> is increased while the <a href="/wiki/Bicarbonate" title="Bicarbonate">bicarbonate</a> is either normal (uncompensated) or increased (compensated). Compensation occurs if respiratory acidosis is present, and a chronic phase is entered with partial buffering of the acidosis through renal bicarbonate retention.
</p><p>However, in cases where chronic illnesses that compromise pulmonary function persist, such as late-stage emphysema and certain types of <a href="/wiki/Muscular_dystrophy" title="Muscular dystrophy">muscular dystrophy</a>, compensatory mechanisms will be unable to reverse this acidotic condition. As metabolic bicarbonate production becomes exhausted, and extraneous bicarbonate infusion can no longer reverse the extreme buildup of carbon dioxide associated with uncompensated respiratory acidosis, <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_ventilation" title="Mechanical ventilation">mechanical ventilation</a> will usually be applied.<sup id="cite_ref-urlMedlinePlus_Medical_Encyclopedia:_Respiratory_acidosis_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urlMedlinePlus_Medical_Encyclopedia:_Respiratory_acidosis-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-urleMedicine_-_Respiratory_Acidosis_:_Article_by_Jackie_A_Hayes_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urleMedicine_-_Respiratory_Acidosis_:_Article_by_Jackie_A_Hayes-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fetal_respiratory_acidemia">Fetal respiratory acidemia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Acidosis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Fetal respiratory acidemia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a>, the normal range differs based on which umbilical vessel is sampled (<a href="/wiki/Umbilical_vein" title="Umbilical vein">umbilical vein</a> pH is normally 7.25 to 7.45; <a href="/wiki/Umbilical_artery" title="Umbilical artery">umbilical artery</a> pH is normally 7.20 to 7.38).<sup id="cite_ref-Yeomans_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yeomans-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  In the fetus, the lungs are not used for ventilation.  Instead, the <a href="/wiki/Placenta" title="Placenta">placenta</a> performs ventilatory functions (<a href="/wiki/Gas_exchange" title="Gas exchange">gas exchange</a>).  Fetal respiratory acidemia is defined as an umbilical vessel pH of less than 7.20 and an umbilical artery <a href="/wiki/PCO2" title="PCO2">PCO<sub>2</sub></a> of 66 or higher or umbilical vein PCO<sub>2</sub> of 50 or higher.<sup id="cite_ref-Pomerance_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pomerance-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
