<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Arctic methane emissions</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Methanestorflaket.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Methanestorflaket.JPG/220px-Methanestorflaket.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Methanestorflaket.JPG/330px-Methanestorflaket.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Methanestorflaket.JPG/440px-Methanestorflaket.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2288" data-file-height="1712" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Methanestorflaket.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Poly(methyl_methacrylate)" title="Poly(methyl methacrylate)">PMMA</a> chambers used to measure methane and <a href="/wiki/CO2" class="mw-redirect" title="CO2">CO<sub>2</sub></a> emissions in <a href="/wiki/Storflaket" title="Storflaket">Storflaket</a> <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bog</a> near <a href="/wiki/Abisko" title="Abisko">Abisko</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CH4.BRW.Monthly.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/CH4.BRW.Monthly.png/300px-CH4.BRW.Monthly.png" width="300" height="231" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/CH4.BRW.Monthly.png/450px-CH4.BRW.Monthly.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/CH4.BRW.Monthly.png/600px-CH4.BRW.Monthly.png 2x" data-file-width="650" data-file-height="500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CH4.BRW.Monthly.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Arctic methane concentrations up to May 2018. A monthly peak of 1976.92 ppb was reached in February 2017.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Arctic methane release</b> is the release of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> from seas and soils in <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a> regions of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a>.  While a long-term natural process, it is exacerbated by <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. This results in a <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a> effect, as <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> is itself a powerful <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a>.
</p><p>The Arctic region is one of the many natural sources of the greenhouse gas methane.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Global warming accelerates its release, due to both release of methane from existing stores, and from <a href="/wiki/Methanogenesis" title="Methanogenesis">methanogenesis</a> in rotting <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Large quantities of methane are stored in the Arctic in natural gas <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(geology)" title="Deposition (geology)">deposits</a>, permafrost, and as undersea <a href="/wiki/Methane_clathrate" title="Methane clathrate">clathrates</a>. Permafrost and clathrates degrade on warming, thus large releases of methane from these sources may arise as a result of global warming.<sup id="cite_ref-zimov2006_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zimov2006-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-shakhova2005_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shakhova2005-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Other sources of methane include submarine <a href="/wiki/Talik" title="Talik">taliks</a>, river transport, ice complex retreat, submarine permafrost and decaying gas hydrate deposits.<sup id="cite_ref-SS07_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SS07-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Concentrations in the Arctic <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">atmosphere</a> are higher by 8–10% than that in the Antarctic atmosphere. During cold glacier epochs, this gradient decreases to practically insignificant levels.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Land ecosystems are considered the main sources of this asymmetry, although it has been suggested that "the role of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> is significantly underestimated."<sup id="cite_ref-shakhova_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shakhova-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  Soil temperature and moisture levels have been found to be significant variables in soil methane fluxes in <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a> environments.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contribution_to_climate_change">Contribution to climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_methane_emissions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Contribution to climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The release of methane from the Arctic is in itself a major contributor to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> as a result of <a href="/wiki/Polar_amplification" title="Polar amplification">polar amplification</a>. Recent observations in the <a href="/wiki/Siberian" class="mw-redirect" title="Siberian">Siberian</a> arctic show increased rates of methane release from the Arctic seabed.<sup id="cite_ref-shakhova2005_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shakhova2005-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Land-based permafrost, also in the Siberian arctic, was estimated in 2013 to release 17 million tonnes of methane per year – a significant increase on the 3.8 million tons estimated in 2006, and estimates before then of just 0.5 million tonnes.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> This compares to around 500 million tonnes released into the atmosphere annually from all sources.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Shakhova et al. (2008) estimate that not less than 1,400 <a href="/wiki/Gigatonne" class="mw-redirect" title="Gigatonne">gigatonnes</a> (Gt) of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> is presently locked up as methane and methane hydrates under the Arctic submarine permafrost, and 5–10% of that area is subject to puncturing by open <a href="/wiki/Talik" title="Talik">taliks</a>. They conclude that "release of up to 50 Gt of predicted amount of hydrate storage [is] highly possible for abrupt release at any time". That would increase the methane content of the planet's atmosphere by a factor of twelve.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008 the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy_National_Laboratories" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Department of Energy National Laboratories">United States Department of Energy National Laboratory</a> system<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> identified potential <a href="/wiki/Clathrate" class="mw-redirect" title="Clathrate">clathrate</a> destabilization in the Arctic as one of the most serious scenarios for <a href="/wiki/Abrupt_climate_change" title="Abrupt climate change">abrupt climate change</a>, which have been singled out for priority research. The US <a href="/wiki/Climate_Change_Science_Program" title="Climate Change Science Program">Climate Change Science Program</a> released a report in late December 2008 estimating the gravity of the risk of clathrate destabilization, alongside three other credible abrupt climate change scenarios.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Study findings based on NASA's CARVE mission concluded in 2015, that methane emissions in the Arctic during the cold season are higher than previously thought. The press release by JPL explained:<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The water trapped in the soil doesn't freeze completely even below 32 degrees Fahrenheit (0 degrees Celsius). The top layer of the ground, known as the active layer, thaws in the summer and refreezes in the winter, and it experiences a kind of sandwiching effect as it freezes. When temperatures are right around 32 degrees Fahrenheit – the so-called "zero curtain" – the top and bottom of the active layer begin to freeze, while the middle remains insulated. Microorganisms in this unfrozen middle layer continue to break down organic matter and emit methane many months into the Arctic's cold period each year.
</p></blockquote>
<p>Hong et al. (2017) studied the seepage from large mounds of hydrates in the shallow arctic seas at Storfjordrenna, in the Barents Sea close to Svalbard. They showed that though the temperature of the sea bed has fluctuated seasonally over the last century, between 1.8 and 4.8&#160;°C, it has only affected release of methane to a depth of about 1.6 meters. Hydrates can be stable through the top 60 meters of the sediments and the current rapid releases came from deeper below the sea floor. They concluded that the increase in flux started hundreds to thousands of years ago well before the onset of warming that others speculated as its cause, and that these seepages are not increasing due to momentary warming.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Summarizing his research, Hong stated:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"The results of our study indicate that the immense seeping found in this area is a result of natural state of the system. Understanding how methane interacts with other important geological, chemical and biological processes in the Earth system is essential and should be the emphasis of our scientific community,"<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Further research by Klaus Wallmann et al. (2018) found that the hydrate release is due to the rebound of the sea bed after the ice melted. The methane dissociation began around 8,000 years ago when the land began to rise faster than the sea level, and the water as a result started to get shallower with less hydrostatic pressure. This dissociation therefore was a result of the uplift of the sea bed rather than anthropogenic warming. The amount of methane released by the hydrate dissociation was small. They found that the methane seeps originate not from the hydrates but from deep geological gas reservoirs (seepage from these formed the hydrates originally). They concluded that the hydrates acted as a dynamic seal regulating the methane emissions from the deep geological gas reservoirs and when they were dissociated 8,000 years ago, weakening the seal, this led to the higher methane release still observed today.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arctic_sea_ice">Arctic sea ice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_methane_emissions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Arctic sea ice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice_decline" title="Arctic sea ice decline">Arctic sea ice decline</a></div>
<p>A 2015 study concluded that Arctic sea ice decline accelerates methane emissions from the Arctic tundra. One of the study researchers noted, "The expectation is that with further sea ice decline, temperatures in the Arctic will continue to rise, and so will <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_methane" title="Atmospheric methane">methane</a> emissions from northern wetlands."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ice_sheets">Ice sheets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_methane_emissions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ice sheets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 2014 study found evidence for methane cycling below the ice sheet of the <a href="/wiki/Russell_Glacier_(Greenland)" title="Russell Glacier (Greenland)">Russell Glacier</a>, based on subglacial drainage samples which were dominated by <a href="/wiki/Proteobacteria" title="Proteobacteria">Proteobacteria</a>. During the study, the most widespread surface melt on record for the past 120 years was observed in Greenland; on 12 July 2012, unfrozen water was present on almost the entire ice sheet surface (98.6%). The findings indicate that <a href="/wiki/Methanotrophs" class="mw-redirect" title="Methanotrophs">methanotrophs</a> could serve as a biological methane sink in the subglacial ecosystem, and the region was, at least during the sample time, a source of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_methane" title="Atmospheric methane">atmospheric methane</a>. Scaled dissolved methane flux during the 4 months of the summer melt season was estimated at 990 Mg CH4. Because the Russell-Leverett Glacier is representative of similar Greenland outlet glaciers, the researchers concluded that the Greenland Ice Sheet may represent a significant global methane source.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> A study in 2016 concluded that <a href="/wiki/Methane_clathrate" title="Methane clathrate">methane clathrates</a> may exist below Greenland's and Antarctica's ice sheets, based on past evidence.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Loss_of_permafrost">Loss of permafrost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_methane_emissions&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Loss of permafrost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Permafrost#Climate_change_effects" title="Permafrost">Permafrost §&#160;Climate change effects</a></div>
<p>Sea ice loss is correlated with warming of Northern latitudes.  This has melting effects on permafrost, both in the sea,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and on land.<sup id="cite_ref-ncar2008_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncar2008-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  Lawrence et al. suggest that current rapid melting of the sea ice may induce a rapid melting of arctic permafrost.<sup id="cite_ref-ncar2008_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncar2008-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>  This has consequential effects on methane release,<sup id="cite_ref-zimov2006_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zimov2006-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and wildlife.<sup id="cite_ref-ncar2008_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncar2008-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Some studies imply a direct link, as they predict cold air passing over ice is replaced by warm air passing over the sea.  This warm air carries heat to the permafrost around the Arctic, and melts it.<sup id="cite_ref-ncar2008_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncar2008-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> This permafrost then releases huge quantities of methane.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  Methane release can be gaseous, but is also transported in solution by rivers.<sup id="cite_ref-SS07_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SS07-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/New_Scientist" title="New Scientist">New Scientist</a></i> states that "Since existing models do not include feedback effects such as the heat generated by decomposition, the permafrost could melt far faster than generally thought."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is another possible mechanism for rapid methane release.  As the Arctic Ocean becomes more and more ice free, the ocean absorbs more of the incident energy from the sun. The Arctic Ocean becomes warmer than the former ice cover and much more water vapour enters the air.  At times when the adjacent land is colder than the sea, this causes rising air above the sea and an off-shore wind as air over the land comes in to replace the rising air over the sea.  As the air rises, the <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">dew point</a> is reached and clouds form, releasing latent heat and further reinforcing the buoyancy of the air over the ocean.  All this results in air being drawn from the south across the tundra rather than the present situation of cold air flowing toward the south from the cold sinking air over the Arctic Ocean.  The extra heat being drawn from the south further accelerates the warming of the permafrost and the Arctic Ocean with increased release of methane.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sinkhole" title="Sinkhole">Sinkholes</a> discovered in the <a href="/wiki/Yamal_Peninsula" title="Yamal Peninsula">Yamal Peninsula</a> in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>, Russia beginning in July 2014 are believed by Russian researchers to have been caused by methane released due to permafrost thawing. Near the bottom of the first sinkhole, air contained unusually high concentrations of methane, according to tests conducted by the researchers.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> This hypothesis points to the destabilization of gas hydrates containing huge amounts of methane gas.<sup id="cite_ref-CAGE_2014_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAGE_2014-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to researchers at Norway's Centre for Arctic Gas Hydrate (CAGE), through a process called geothermal heat flux, the Siberian <a href="/wiki/Permafrost" title="Permafrost">permafrost</a> which extends to the seabed of the <a href="/wiki/Kara_Sea" title="Kara Sea">Kara Sea</a>, a section of the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> between the Yamal Peninsula and <a href="/wiki/Novaya_Zemlya" title="Novaya Zemlya">Novaya Zemlya</a>, is thawing. According to a CAGE researcher, Aleksei Portnov,
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r856303468"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"The thawing of permafrost on the ocean floor is an ongoing process, likely to be exaggerated by the global warming of the world´s oceans."</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite>CAGE 2014</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Methane_hydrate" class="mw-redirect" title="Methane hydrate">Methane hydrate</a> is leaking in an area of at least 7500 m2. In some areas gas flares extend up to 25&#160;m (82&#160;ft). Prior to their research it was proposed that methane was tightly sealed into the permafrost by water depths up to 100&#160;m (330&#160;ft). Close to the shore however, where the permafrost seal tapers to as little as 20&#160;m (66&#160;ft), there are significant amounts of gas leakage.<sup id="cite_ref-CAGE_2014_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAGE_2014-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<div class="thumbcaption">The Permian–Triassic extinction event (the <a href="/wiki/Great_Dying" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Dying">Great Dying</a>) may have been caused by release of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> from <a href="/wiki/Clathrate" class="mw-redirect" title="Clathrate">clathrates</a>. An estimated 52% of marine <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> became extinct, representing 96% of all marine <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>.</div>
</div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">Sea ice</a>, and the cold conditions it sustains, serves to stabilise <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> deposits on and near the shoreline,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> preventing the <a href="/wiki/Clathrate" class="mw-redirect" title="Clathrate">clathrate</a> breaking down and outgassing <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> into the atmosphere, causing further warming.  Melting of this ice may release large quantities of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>, a powerful <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, causing further warming in a strong <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a> cycle.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Even with existing levels of warming and melting of the Arctic region, submarine methane releases linked to <a href="/wiki/Clathrate" class="mw-redirect" title="Clathrate">clathrate</a> breakdown have been discovered,<sup id="cite_ref-indep13DEC2011_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-indep13DEC2011-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> and demonstrated to be leaking into the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SS07_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SS07-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> A 2011 Russian survey off the East Siberian coast found plumes wider than one kilometer releasing methane directly into the atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-indep13DEC2011_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-indep13DEC2011-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to monitoring carried out in 2003/2004 by Shakhova et al., the surface layer of shelf water in the <a href="/wiki/East_Siberian_Sea" title="East Siberian Sea">East Siberian Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laptev_Sea" title="Laptev Sea">Laptev Sea</a>  was supersaturated up to 2500% relative to then present average <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_methane" title="Atmospheric methane">atmospheric methane</a> content of 1.85 ppm. Anomalously high concentrations (up to 154 nM or 4400% supersaturation) of dissolved methane in the bottom layer of shelf water suggest that the bottom layer is somehow affected by near-bottom sources. Considering the possible formation mechanisms of such plumes, their studies indicated thermoabrasion and the effects of shallow gas or gas hydrates release.<sup id="cite_ref-shakhova2005_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shakhova2005-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Research in 2008 in the Siberian Arctic has shown <a href="/wiki/Clathrate" class="mw-redirect" title="Clathrate">clathrate</a>-derived methane being released through perforations in the seabed permafrost.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The climatic effects of a potential release of methane from global ocean clathrates may be significant on timescales of 1–100 thousand years, depending on the water temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-AB05_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AB05-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
