<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Iris hypothesis</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>iris hypothesis</b> is a hypothesis proposed by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lindzen" title="Richard Lindzen">Richard Lindzen</a> et al. in 2001 that suggested increased <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperature</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> would result in reduced <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">cirrus clouds</a> and thus more <a href="/wiki/Infrared_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Infrared radiation">infrared radiation</a> leakage from <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>.  His study of observed changes in cloud coverage and modeled effects on infrared radiation released to space as a result supported the hypothesis.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  This suggested infrared radiation leakage was hypothesized to be a <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">negative feedback</a> in which an initial warming would result in an overall cooling of the surface. The consensus view is that increased sea surface temperature would result in increased cirrus clouds and reduced infrared radiation leakage and therefore a positive feedback.
</p><p>Other scientists subsequently tested the hypothesis.  Some concluded that there was no evidence supporting the hypothesis.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Others found evidence suggesting that increased sea surface temperature in the tropics did indeed reduce <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">cirrus clouds</a> but found that the effect was nonetheless a positive feedback rather than the negative feedback that Lindzen had hypothesized.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>A later 2007 study conducted by <a href="/wiki/Roy_Spencer_(scientist)" title="Roy Spencer (scientist)">Roy Spencer</a> et al. using updated satellite data potentially supported the iris hypothesis.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  In 2011, Lindzen published a rebuttal to the main criticisms.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  In 2015 a paper was published which again suggested the possibility of an "Iris Effect".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  It also proposed what it called a "plausible physical mechanism for an iris effect."  In 2017 a paper was published which found that "tropical anvil cirrus clouds exert a negative climate feedback in strong association with precipitation efficiency"<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>.  If confirmed then that finding would be highly supportive of the existence of an "Iris Effect".
</p>
