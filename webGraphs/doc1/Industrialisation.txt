<p><b>Industrialisation</b> or <b>industrialization</b> is the period of social and economic change that transforms a human group from an <a href="/wiki/Agrarian_society" title="Agrarian society">agrarian society</a> into an <a href="/wiki/Industrial_society" title="Industrial society">industrial society</a>, involving the extensive re-organisation of an <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a> for the purpose of <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacturing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As industrial workers' incomes rise, markets for consumer goods and services of all kinds tend to expand and provide a further stimulus to industrial <a href="/wiki/Investment" title="Investment">investment</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrialisation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first transformation to an industrial economy from an agricultural one, known as the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, took place from the mid-18th to early 19th century in certain areas in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and North America; starting in Great Britain, followed by Belgium, Germany, and France.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Characteristics of this early industrialisation were technological progress, a shift from rural work to industrial labor, financial investments in new industrial structure, and early developments in class consciousness and theories related to this.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Later commentators have called this the First Industrial Revolution.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The "<a href="/wiki/Second_Industrial_Revolution" title="Second Industrial Revolution">Second Industrial Revolution</a>" labels the later changes that came about in the mid-19th century after the refinement of the <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engine</a>, the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a>, the harnessing of <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electricity</a> and the construction of canals, railways and electric-power lines. The invention of the <a href="/wiki/Assembly_line" title="Assembly line">assembly line</a> gave this phase a boost.
Coal mines, steelworks, and textile factories replaced homes as the place of work.
<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the end of the 20th century, <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> had become one of the most recently industrialised regions of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The BRICS states (Brazil, Russia, India, China and South Africa) are undergoing the process of industrialisation.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is considerable literature on the factors facilitating industrial modernisation and enterprise development.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Urbanisation">Urbanisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrialisation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Urbanisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">Urbanisation</a></div>
<p>As the Industrial Revolution was a shift from the agrarian society, hence people migrated from villages in search of jobs to places where factories were set up. This shifting of rural people led to urbanisation and rise in the population of the towns. The concentration of labour into factories has increased urbanisation and the size of settlements, to serve and house the factory workers.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Exploitation">Exploitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrialisation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Exploitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Exploitation_of_labour" title="Exploitation of labour">Exploitation of labour</a> and <a href="/wiki/Exploitation_of_natural_resources" title="Exploitation of natural resources">Exploitation of natural resources</a></div>
<p>Workers have to either leave their families or bring them along in order to work in the towns and cities where these industries are found.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_in_family_structure">Changes in family structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Industrialisation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Changes in family structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The family structure changes with industrialisation. The sociologist <a href="/wiki/Talcott_Parsons" title="Talcott Parsons">Talcott Parsons</a> noted that in pre-industrial societies there is an <a href="/wiki/Extended_family" title="Extended family">extended family</a> structure spanning many generations who probably remained in the same location for generations. In industrialised societies the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_family" title="Nuclear family">nuclear family</a>, consisting of only parents and their growing children, predominates. Families and children reaching adulthood are more mobile and tend to relocate to where jobs exist. Extended family bonds become more tenuous.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

(March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> endorses development policies like water purification or <a href="/wiki/Primary_education" title="Primary education">primary education</a> and <a href="/wiki/South-South_Cooperation" class="mw-redirect" title="South-South Cooperation"> co-operation amongst third world communities</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Some members of the <a href="/wiki/Economic_communities" class="mw-redirect" title="Economic communities">economic communities</a> do not consider contemporary industrialisation policies as being adequate to the <a href="/wiki/Global_South" title="Global South">global south</a> (Third World countries) or beneficial in the longer term, with the perception that they may only create <a href="/wiki/Inefficiency" title="Inefficiency">inefficient</a> local industries unable to compete in the <a href="/wiki/Free_trade" title="Free trade">free-trade</a> dominated political order which industrialisation has fostered.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">Environmentalism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green_politics" title="Green politics">Green politics</a> may represent more visceral reactions to industrial growth. Nevertheless, repeated examples in history of apparently successful industrialisation (Britain, Soviet Union, South Korea, China, etc) may make conventional industrialisation seem like an attractive or even natural path forward, especially as populations grow, <a href="/wiki/Consumerism" title="Consumerism"> consumerist</a> expectations rise and agricultural opportunities diminish.
</p><p>The relationships among <a href="/wiki/International_Development#International_Economic_Inequality" class="mw-redirect" title="International Development">economic growth, employment, and poverty reduction</a> are complex. Higher <a href="/wiki/Productivity" title="Productivity">productivity</a>, it is argued<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (January 2018)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, may lead to lower <a href="/wiki/Employment_rate" class="mw-redirect" title="Employment rate"> employment</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Jobless_recovery" title="Jobless recovery">jobless recovery</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-ODI2_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI2-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
There are differences across <a href="/wiki/Economic_sector" title="Economic sector"> sectors</a>, whereby manufacturing is less able than the <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_sector" class="mw-redirect" title="Tertiary sector">tertiary sector</a> to accommodate both increased productivity and employment opportunities; more than 40% of the world's employees are "<a href="/wiki/Working_poor" title="Working poor">working poor</a>", whose incomes fail to keep themselves and their families above the $2-a-day <a href="/wiki/Poverty_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI2_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI2-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> There is also a phenomenon of <a href="/wiki/Deindustrialisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Deindustrialisation">deindustrialisation</a>, as in the former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">USSR</a> countries' transition to market economies, and the <a href="/wiki/Agriculture_sector" class="mw-redirect" title="Agriculture sector">agriculture sector</a> is often the key sector in absorbing the resultant unemployment.<sup id="cite_ref-ODI2_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODI2-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
