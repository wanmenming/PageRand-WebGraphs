<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Forest gardening</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Home gardens" redirects here. <div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Forestgarden2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Forestgarden2.jpg/240px-Forestgarden2.jpg" width="240" height="326" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Forestgarden2.jpg/360px-Forestgarden2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Forestgarden2.jpg/480px-Forestgarden2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="484" data-file-height="657" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Forestgarden2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Robert_Hart_(horticulturist)" title="Robert Hart (horticulturist)">Robert Hart</a>'s forest garden in <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Forest gardening</b> is a low-maintenance <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_gardening" title="Sustainable gardening">sustainable</a> plant-based food production and <a href="/wiki/Agroforestry" title="Agroforestry">agroforestry</a> system based on <a href="/wiki/Woodland" title="Woodland">woodland</a> <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, incorporating <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nut_(fruit)" title="Nut (fruit)">nut</a> <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shrubs" class="mw-redirect" title="Shrubs">shrubs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herb" title="Herb">herbs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">vines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Perennial_vegetable" title="Perennial vegetable">perennial vegetables</a> which have <a href="/wiki/Ecological_yield" title="Ecological yield">yields</a> directly useful to humans. Making use of <a href="/wiki/Companion_planting" title="Companion planting">companion planting</a>, these can be <a href="/wiki/Intercropping" title="Intercropping">intermixed</a> to grow in a succession of layers, to build a woodland habitat.
</p><p>Forest <a href="/wiki/Gardening" title="Gardening">gardening</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistoric</a> method of securing food in <a href="/wiki/Tropical_climate" title="Tropical climate">tropical areas</a>. In the 1980s, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hart_(horticulturist)" title="Robert Hart (horticulturist)">Robert Hart</a> coined the term "forest gardening" after adapting the principles and applying them to <a href="/wiki/Temperateness" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperateness">temperate climates</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Crawford_2010_18_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford_2010_18-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Forest <a href="/wiki/Garden" title="Garden">gardens</a> are probably the world's oldest form of <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a> and most resilient <a href="/wiki/Agroecosystem" title="Agroecosystem">agroecosystem</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> They originated in <a href="/wiki/Prehistory" title="Prehistory">prehistoric times</a> along <a href="/wiki/Jungle" title="Jungle">jungle</a>-clad river banks and in the wet foothills of <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> regions. In the gradual process of families improving their immediate environment, useful <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">tree</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vine" title="Vine">vine</a> species were identified, protected and improved whilst undesirable species were eliminated. Eventually superior foreign species were selected and incorporated into the gardens.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Forest gardens are still common in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> and known by various names such as: <i>home gardens</i> in <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">South India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>; <i>Kandyan forest gardens</i> in <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <i>huertos familiares</i>, the "family orchards" of <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>; and <i>pekarangan</i>, the gardens of "complete design", in <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> These are also called agroforests and, where the wood components are short-statured, the term <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrub</a> garden is employed. Forest gardens have been shown to be a significant source of income and food security for local populations.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Robert Hart adapted forest gardening for the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>'s temperate climate during the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-Crawford_2010_18_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crawford_2010_18-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> His theories were later developed by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Crawford" title="Martin Crawford">Martin Crawford</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Agroforestry_Research_Trust" title="Agroforestry Research Trust">Agroforestry Research Trust</a> and various permaculturalists such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Graham_Bell_(horticulturist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Graham Bell (horticulturist) (page does not exist)">Graham Bell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Whitefield" title="Patrick Whitefield">Patrick Whitefield</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Jacke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dave Jacke (page does not exist)">Dave Jacke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geoff_Lawton" title="Geoff Lawton">Geoff Lawton</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_tropical_climates">In tropical climates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In tropical climates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Forest gardens, or home gardens, are common in the tropics, using <a href="/wiki/Intercropping" title="Intercropping">intercropping</a> to cultivate <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">crops</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a> on the same land. In <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">south India</a> as well as in <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Northeastern India">northeastern India</a>, the home garden is the most common form of land use and is also found in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>. One example combines <a href="/wiki/Coconut" title="Coconut">coconut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_pepper" title="Black pepper">black pepper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cocoa_bean" title="Cocoa bean">cocoa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pineapple" title="Pineapple">pineapple</a>. These gardens exemplify <a href="/wiki/Polyculture" title="Polyculture">polyculture</a>, and conserve much crop <a href="/wiki/Genetic_diversity" title="Genetic diversity">genetic diversity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heirloom_plant" title="Heirloom plant">heirloom plants</a> that are not found in <a href="/wiki/Monocultures" class="mw-redirect" title="Monocultures">monocultures</a>. Forest gardens have been loosely compared to the religious concept of the <a href="/wiki/Garden_of_Eden" title="Garden of Eden">Garden of Eden</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Americas">Americas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Americas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The BBC's <i><a href="/wiki/Unnatural_Histories_(TV_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Unnatural Histories (TV series)">Unnatural Histories</a></i> claimed that the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_rainforest" title="Amazon rainforest">Amazon rainforest</a>, rather than being a pristine <a href="/wiki/Wilderness" title="Wilderness">wilderness</a>, has been shaped by humans for at least 11,000 years through practices such as forest gardening and <i><a href="/wiki/Terra_preta" title="Terra preta">terra preta</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> This was also explored in the bestselling book <i>1491</i> by author <a href="/wiki/Charles_C._Mann" title="Charles C. Mann">Charles C. Mann</a>. Since the 1970s, numerous <a href="/wiki/Geoglyph" title="Geoglyph">geoglyphs</a> have also been discovered on deforested land in the Amazon rainforest, furthering the evidence about <a href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian_era" title="Pre-Columbian era">Pre-Columbian</a> civilizations.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a>, much of the <a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">Maya</a> food supply was grown in "orchard-gardens", known as <i>pet kot</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The system takes its name from the low wall of stones (<i>pet</i> meaning circular and <i>kot</i> wall of loose stones) that characteristically surrounds the gardens.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The North American ecosystem was managed by the first nations use of fire to burn underbrush to encourage large game. Large Oak forests harvested for acorns disappeared as the Europeans arrived. Prairie and grasslands were often managed by the first nations.
<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In many <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a> countries, for example <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>, gardens are widespread in rural, periurban and urban areas and they play an essential role in establishing <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a>. Most well known are the <a href="/wiki/Chaga" class="mw-redirect" title="Chaga">Chaga</a> or Chagga gardens on the slopes of <a href="/wiki/Mt._Kilimanjaro" class="mw-redirect" title="Mt. Kilimanjaro">Mt. Kilimanjaro</a> in Tanzania. These are an excellent example of an <a href="/wiki/Agroforestry" title="Agroforestry">agroforestry</a> system. In many countries, women are the main actors in home gardening and food is mainly produced for <a href="/wiki/Subsistence" class="mw-redirect" title="Subsistence">subsistence</a>.
In North-Africa, oasis layered gardening with palm trees, fruit trees and vegetables is a traditional type of forest garden.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nepal">Nepal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nepal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, the <i>Ghar Bagaincha</i>, literally "home garden", refers to the traditional land use system around a homestead, where several species of plants are grown and maintained by household members and their products are primarily intended for the family consumption (Shrestha et al., 2002). The term “home garden” is often considered synonymous to the kitchen garden. However, they differ in terms of function, size, diversity, composition and features (Sthapit et al., 2006). In Nepal, 72% of households have home gardens of an area 2–11% of the total land holdings (Gautam et al., 2004). Because of their small size, the government has never identified home gardens as an important unit of food production and they thereby remain neglected from research and development. However, at the household level the system is very important as it is an important source of quality food and nutrition for the rural poor and, therefore, are important contributors to the household food security and livelihoods of farming communities in Nepal. The gardens are typically cultivated with a mixture of annual and perennial plants that can be harvested on a daily or seasonal basis. Biodiversity that has an immediate value is maintained in home gardens as women and children have easy access to preferred food. Home gardens, with their intensive and multiple uses, provide a safety net for households when food is scarce. These gardens are not only important sources of food, fodder, fuel, medicines, spices, herbs, flowers, construction materials and income in many countries, they are also important for the in situ conservation of a wide range of unique genetic resources for food and agriculture (Subedi et al., 2004). Many uncultivated, as well as neglected and underutilised species could make an important contribution to the dietary diversity of local communities (Gautam et al., 2004).
</p><p>In addition to supplementing diet in times of difficulty, home gardens promote whole-family and whole-community involvement in the process of providing food. Children, the elderly, and those caring for them can participate in this infield agriculture, incorporating it with other household tasks and scheduling. This tradition has existed in many cultures around the world for thousands of years.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Mediterranean_climates">In Mediterranean climates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In Mediterranean climates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">Mediterranean climate</a> has long, hot, rainless summers and relatively short, cool, rainy winters (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a> <i>Csa</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-loc_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-loc-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Its climate conditions are highly variable within an area and modified locally by altitude, latitude, and the proximity to the Mediterranean.<sup id="cite_ref-loc_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-loc-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> 
In the 1950s the Forest Research Department of the Ministry of Agriculture founded a botanical forest garden in the Sharon region in Israel, the Ilanot Forest.<sup id="cite_ref-KKL-Ilanot_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KKL-Ilanot-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> 
As the only one of its kind in Israel, it harbours more than 750 species of trees from locations all over the world, including the Japanese sago palm <a href="/wiki/Cycas_revoluta" title="Cycas revoluta">cycas revoluta</a>, fig trees (<a href="/wiki/Ficus_glomerata" class="mw-redirect" title="Ficus glomerata">ficus glomerata</a>), stone pine trees (<a href="/wiki/Pinus_pinea" class="mw-redirect" title="Pinus pinea">pinus pinea</a>) that produce tasty pine nuts and adds to the <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity_in_Israel,_the_West_Bank,_and_the_Gaza_Strip" title="Biodiversity in Israel, the West Bank, and the Gaza Strip">biodiversity of Israel</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_temperate_climates">In temperate climates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In temperate climates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Robert_Hart_(horticulturist).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Robert_Hart_%28horticulturist%29.jpg/220px-Robert_Hart_%28horticulturist%29.jpg" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Robert_Hart_%28horticulturist%29.jpg/330px-Robert_Hart_%28horticulturist%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Robert_Hart_%28horticulturist%29.jpg/440px-Robert_Hart_%28horticulturist%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="594" data-file-height="475" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Robert_Hart_(horticulturist).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Robert Hart, forest gardening pioneer</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Hart_(horticulturist)" title="Robert Hart (horticulturist)">Robert Hart</a> coined the term "forest gardening" during the 1980s. Hart began farming at <a href="/wiki/Wenlock_Edge" title="Wenlock Edge">Wenlock Edge</a> in <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a> with the intention of providing a healthy and therapeutic environment for himself and his brother Lacon.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Starting as relatively conventional <a href="/wiki/Smallholding" title="Smallholding">smallholders</a>, Hart soon discovered that maintaining large annual vegetable beds, rearing livestock and taking care of an <a href="/wiki/Orchard" title="Orchard">orchard</a> were tasks beyond their strength. However, a small bed of perennial vegetables and herbs he planted was looking after itself with little intervention.
</p><p>Following Hart's adoption of a <a href="/wiki/Raw_veganism" title="Raw veganism">raw vegan diet</a> for health and personal reasons, he replaced his farm animals with plants. The three main products from a forest garden are fruit, nuts and <a href="/wiki/Leaf_vegetable" title="Leaf vegetable">green leafy vegetables</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> He created a model forest garden from a 0.12 acre (500&#160;m²) orchard on his farm and intended naming his gardening method <i>ecological horticulture</i> or <i>ecocultivation</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Hart later dropped these terms once he became aware that <i>agroforestry</i> and <i>forest gardens</i> were already being used to describe similar systems in other parts of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> He was inspired by the <a href="/wiki/Forest_farming" title="Forest farming">forest farming</a> methods of <a href="/wiki/Toyohiko_Kagawa" title="Toyohiko Kagawa">Toyohiko Kagawa</a> and James Sholto Douglas, and the productivity of the Keralan home gardens as Hart explains:<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303468">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>From the agroforestry point of view, perhaps the world's most advanced country is the Indian state of Kerala, which boasts no fewer than three and a half million forest gardens...As an example of the extraordinary intensivity of cultivation of some forest gardens, one plot of only 0.12 hectares (0.30 acres) was found by a study group to have twenty-three young coconut palms, twelve cloves, fifty-six bananas, and forty-nine pineapples, with thirty pepper vines trained up its trees. In addition, the small holder grew fodder for his <a href="/wiki/House-cow" class="mw-redirect" title="House-cow">house-cow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seven-layer_system">Seven-layer system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Seven-layer system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Forgard2-003.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Forgard2-003.gif/330px-Forgard2-003.gif" width="330" height="207" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Forgard2-003.gif/495px-Forgard2-003.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Forgard2-003.gif/660px-Forgard2-003.gif 2x" data-file-width="1491" data-file-height="936" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Forgard2-003.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The seven layers of the forest garden</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Hart_(horticulturist)" title="Robert Hart (horticulturist)">Robert Hart</a> pioneered a system based on the observation that the natural forest can be divided into distinct levels. He used <a href="/wiki/Intercropping" title="Intercropping">intercropping</a> to develop an existing small orchard of apples and pears into an edible <a href="/wiki/Polyculture" title="Polyculture">polyculture</a> landscape consisting of the following layers:
</p>
<ol><li>‘<a href="/wiki/Canopy_layer" class="mw-redirect" title="Canopy layer">Canopy layer</a>’ consisting of the original mature fruit trees.</li>
<li>‘<a href="/wiki/Low-tree_layer" class="mw-redirect" title="Low-tree layer">Low-tree layer</a>’ of smaller nut and fruit trees on dwarfing root stocks.</li>
<li>‘<a href="/wiki/Shrub_layer" class="mw-redirect" title="Shrub layer">Shrub layer</a>’ of fruit bushes such as currants and berries.</li>
<li>‘<a href="/wiki/Herbaceous_layer" class="mw-redirect" title="Herbaceous layer">Herbaceous layer</a>’ of perennial vegetables and herbs.</li>
<li>‘<a href="/wiki/Rhizosphere" title="Rhizosphere">Rhizosphere</a>’ or ‘underground’ dimension of plants grown for their roots and tubers.</li>
<li>‘<a href="/wiki/Ground_cover_layer" class="mw-redirect" title="Ground cover layer">Ground cover layer</a>’ of edible plants that spread horizontally.</li>
<li>‘<a href="/w/index.php?title=Vertical_layer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Vertical layer (page does not exist)">Vertical layer</a>’ of vines and climbers.</li></ol>
<p>A key component of the seven-layer system was the plants he selected. Most of the traditional vegetable crops grown today, such as <a href="/wiki/Carrot" title="Carrot">carrots</a>, are sun loving plants not well selected for the more shady forest garden system. Hart favoured shade tolerant perennial vegetables.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_development">Further development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Further development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Agroforestry_Research_Trust" title="Agroforestry Research Trust">Agroforestry Research Trust</a> (ART), managed by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Crawford" title="Martin Crawford">Martin Crawford</a>, runs experimental forest gardening projects on a number of plots in <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Crawford describes a forest garden as a low-maintenance way of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture" title="Sustainable agriculture">sustainably producing food</a> and other household products.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ken Fern had the idea that for a successful temperate forest garden a wider range of edible shade tolerant plants would need to be used. To this end, Fern created the organisation <a href="/wiki/Plants_for_a_Future" title="Plants for a Future">Plants for a Future</a> (PFAF) which compiled a plant database suitable for such a system. Fern used the term <i>woodland gardening</i>, rather than forest gardening, in his book <i>Plants for a Future</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Movement_for_Compassionate_Living" title="Movement for Compassionate Living">Movement for Compassionate Living</a> (MCL) promote forest gardening and other types of <a href="/wiki/Vegan_organic_gardening" title="Vegan organic gardening">vegan organic gardening</a> to meet society's needs for food and natural resources. Kathleen Jannaway, the founder of MCL, wrote a book outlining a sustainable vegan future called <i>Abundant Living in the Coming Age of the Tree</i> in 1991. In 2009, the MCL provided a grant of £1,000 to the Bangor Forest Garden project in <a href="/wiki/Gwynedd" title="Gwynedd">Gwynedd</a>, North West Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kevin Bradley coined the phrase “Edible Forest” in the 1980s as the name of his nursery, garden, and orchard on 5 acres in the frigid zone 3 pine forests of northern Wisconsin. Among 3 options, he chose “Edible Forest” because it "evokes at once an ethereal, spiritual, and magical image", of Disney- like "Forest of No Return"; of the biblical "Garden of Eden". This image was perfectly in line with his ongoing experiment begun in 1985 in what he calls a closed loop human environment, combining multi- story tree and field crop "garden/orchards" for maximum beauty and use of space, someday to be very useful in an ever-shrinking world. "The name, at the same time, with its irrational first impression (of course we can’t eat a forest), forces the mind to think, if just a little bit, about its inference and thus sticks in our memories". It appeared from Bradley’s research that the two words had, prior to the 80's, never been put together before as a noun phrase but which by today, after more than two decades of Bradley's "Edible Forest Nursery" and  the 2005 text by Jacke and Toensmeirer's- "Edible Forest Gardens",  has grown into a movement and little "Edible Forests" all over the world.
</p><p>In 2005, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Jacke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dave Jacke (page does not exist)">Dave Jacke</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eric_Toensmeier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eric Toensmeier (page does not exist)">Eric Toensmeier</a>'s two-volume <i>Edible Forest Gardens</i> provided a deeply researched reference focused on North American forest gardening climates, habitats, and species.  The book attempts to ground forest gardening deeply in ecological science. The <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://apiosinstitute.org/">Apios Institute</a> wiki grew out of their work, and seeks to document and share the experience of people around the world working with the species in polycultures.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Permaculture">Permaculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Permaculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bill_Mollison" title="Bill Mollison">Bill Mollison</a>, who coined the term <i><a href="/wiki/Permaculture" title="Permaculture">permaculture</a></i>, visited Robert Hart at his forest garden in Wenlock Edge in October 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Hart's seven-layer system has since been adopted as a common permaculture design element.
</p><p>Numerous <a href="/wiki/Category:Permaculturalists" title="Category:Permaculturalists">permaculturalists</a> are proponents of forest gardens, or food forests, such as Graham Bell, <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Whitefield" title="Patrick Whitefield">Patrick Whitefield</a>, Dave Jacke, Eric Toensmeier and <a href="/wiki/Geoff_Lawton" title="Geoff Lawton">Geoff Lawton</a>. Bell started building his forest garden in 1991 and wrote the book <i>The Permaculture Garden</i> in 1995, Whitefield wrote the book <i>How to Make a Forest Garden</i> in 2002, Jacke and Toensmeier co-authored the two volume book set <i>Edible Forest Gardens</i> in 2005, and Lawton presented the film <i>Establishing a Food Forest</i> in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Austrian <a href="/wiki/Sepp_Holzer" title="Sepp Holzer">Sepp Holzer</a> practices "<a href="/wiki/Holzer_Permaculture" title="Holzer Permaculture">Holzer Permaculture</a>" on his <i>Krameterhof</i> farm, at varying altitudes ranging from 1,100 to 1,500 metres above sea level. His designs create micro-climates with rocks, ponds and living wind barriers, enabling the cultivation of a variety of fruit trees, vegetables and flowers in a region that averages 4&#160;°C, and with temperatures as low as -20&#160;°C in the winter.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Projects">Projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_gardening&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/El_Pilar" title="El Pilar">El Pilar</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>-<a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a> border features a forest garden to demonstrate <a href="/wiki/Agriculture_in_Mesoamerica" title="Agriculture in Mesoamerica">traditional Maya agricultural</a> practices.<sup id="cite_ref-Ford_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ford-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> A further 1-acre model forest garden, called Känan K’aax (meaning well-tended garden in Mayan), is being funded by the <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">National Geographic Society</a> and developed at Santa Familia Primary School in <a href="/wiki/Cayo_District" title="Cayo District">Cayo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States the largest known food forest on public land is believed to be the 7-acre <a href="/wiki/Beacon_Food_Forest" title="Beacon Food Forest">Beacon Food Forest</a> in Seattle, Washington.<sup id="cite_ref-Crosscut_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crosscut-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Other forest garden projects include those at the Central Rocky Mountain Permaculture Institute in <a href="/wiki/Basalt,_Colorado" title="Basalt, Colorado">Basalt, Colorado</a> and Montview Neighborhood farm in <a href="/wiki/Northampton,_Massachusetts" title="Northampton, Massachusetts">Northampton, Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://bostonfoodforest.org/">Boston Food Forest Coalition</a> offers an innovative new model of neighborhood forest gardens stewarded by local leaders linked in a web of mutual aid through a non-profit land trust.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Canada food forester Richard Walker has been developing and maintaining food forests in the province of British Columbia for over 30 years. He developed a 3-acre food forest that when at maturity provided raw materials for a nursery and herbalism business as well as food for his family.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The Living Centre have developed various forest garden projects in <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United Kingdom, other than those run by the Agroforestry Research Trust (ART), there are numerous forest garden projects such as the Bangor Forest Garden in <a href="/wiki/Gwynedd" title="Gwynedd">Gwynedd</a>, North West Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Martin Crawford from ART administers the Forest Garden Network, an informal network of people and organisations around the world who are cultivating their own forest gardens.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
