<p><b>Terbium(III,IV) oxide</b>, occasionally called <b> tetraterbium heptaoxide</b>, has the formula Tb<sub>4</sub>O<sub>7</sub>, though some texts refer to it as TbO<sub>1.75</sub>.  There is some debate as to whether it is a discrete compound, or simply one phase in an <a href="/wiki/Interstitial_compound" title="Interstitial compound">interstitial</a> oxide system.  Tb<sub>4</sub>O<sub>7</sub> is one of the main commercial <a href="/wiki/Terbium" title="Terbium">terbium</a> compounds, and the only such product containing at least some Tb(IV) (terbium in the +4 oxidation state), along with the more stable Tb(III). It is produced by heating the metal oxalate, and it is used in the preparation of other terbium compounds.  Terbium forms three other major <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxides</a>: <a href="/wiki/Terbium(III)_oxide" title="Terbium(III) oxide">Tb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></a>, TbO<sub>2</sub>, and Tb<sub>6</sub>O<sub>11</sub>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terbium(III,IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tb<sub>4</sub>O<sub>7</sub> is most often produced by ignition of the <a href="/wiki/Oxalate" title="Oxalate">oxalate</a> at or the <a href="/wiki/Sulfate" title="Sulfate">sulfate</a> in air.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The oxalate (at 1000 °C) is generally preferred, since the sulfate requires a higher temperature, and it produces an almost black product contaminated with  Tb<sub>6</sub>O<sub>11</sub> or other oxygen-rich oxides.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties">Chemical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Terbium(III,IV)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chemical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Terbium(III,IV) oxide loses <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O<sub>2</sub></a> when heated at high temperatures; at more moderate temperatures (ca. 350 °C) it reversibly loses oxygen, as shown by exchange with<sup>18</sup>O<sub>2</sub>.  This property, also seen in Pr<sub>6</sub>O<sub>11</sub> and <a href="/wiki/Vanadium(V)_oxide" title="Vanadium(V) oxide">V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub></a>, allows it to work like V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> as a <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">redox</a> <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> in reactions involving oxygen.  It was found as early as 1916 that hot Tb<sub>4</sub>O<sub>7</sub> catalyses the reaction of <a href="/wiki/Coal_gas" title="Coal gas">coal gas</a> (<a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">CO</a> + <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">H<sub>2</sub></a>) with air, leading to incandescence and often ignition.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Tb<sub>4</sub>O<sub>7</sub> reacts with atomic oxygen to produce TbO<sub>2</sub>, but a more convenient preparation of TbO<sub>2</sub> is by <a href="/wiki/Disproportionation" title="Disproportionation">disproportionation</a> of Tb<sub>4</sub>O<sub>7</sub>.  This is performed by refluxing with an excess of an equal mixture of concentrated <a href="/wiki/Acetic_acid" title="Acetic acid">acetic acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acids</a> for 30 minutes, producing <a href="/wiki/Terbium(III)_chloride" title="Terbium(III) chloride">terbium(III) chloride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_(molecule)" class="mw-redirect" title="Water (molecule)">water</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="chemf nowrap">Tb<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">7</sub></span></span> (s) + 6 HCl (aq) → 2 <span class="chemf nowrap">TbO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span> (s) + 2 <span class="chemf nowrap">TbCl<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">3</sub></span></span> (aq) + 3 <span class="chemf nowrap">H<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O</span> (l)</dd></dl>
<p>Tb<sub>4</sub>O<sub>7</sub> reacts with other hot concentrated acids to produce terbium(III) salts. For example, reaction with <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> gives <a href="/w/index.php?title=Terbium(III)_sulfate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Terbium(III) sulfate (page does not exist)">terbium(III) sulfate</a>. Terbium oxide reacts slowly with hydrochloric acid to form terbium(III) chloride solution, and elemental chlorine.  At ambient temperature, complete dissolution might require a month; in a hot water bath, about a week.
</p><p><br />
