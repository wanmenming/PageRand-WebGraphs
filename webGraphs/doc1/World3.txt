<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">World3</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>World3</b> <a href="/wiki/Model_(abstract)" class="mw-redirect" title="Model (abstract)">model</a> is a <a href="/wiki/System_dynamics" title="System dynamics">system dynamics</a> model for <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">computer simulation</a> of interactions between population, industrial growth, food production and limits in the <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a> of the earth. It was originally produced and used by a <a href="/wiki/Club_of_Rome" title="Club of Rome">Club of Rome</a> study that produced the model and the book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Limits_to_Growth" title="The Limits to Growth">The Limits to Growth</a></i> (1972). The creators of the model were <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Meadows" title="Dennis Meadows">Dennis Meadows</a>, project manager, and a team of 16 researchers.<sup id="cite_ref-Meadows-1972_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meadows-1972-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>8</span></sup>  
</p><p>The model was documented in the book <i>Dynamics of Growth in a Finite World</i>. It added new features to <a href="/wiki/Jay_W._Forrester" class="mw-redirect" title="Jay W. Forrester">Jay W. Forrester</a>'s World2 model. Since World3 was originally created, it has had minor tweaks to get to the World3/91 model used in the book <i><a href="/wiki/Beyond_the_Limits" title="Beyond the Limits">Beyond the Limits</a></i>, later improved to get the World3/2000 model distributed by the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Institute_for_Policy_and_Social_Science_Research&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Institute for Policy and Social Science Research (page does not exist)">Institute for Policy and Social Science Research</a> and finally the World3/2004 model used in the book <i>Limits to Growth: the 30 year update</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>World3 is one of several global models that have been generated throughout the world (Mesarovic/Pestel Model, Bariloche Model, MOIRA Model, SARU Model, FUGI Model) and is probably the model that generated the spark for all later models.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Model">Model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The model consisted of several interacting parts.  Each of these dealt with a different system of the model.  The main systems were 
</p>
<ul><li>the food system, dealing with agriculture and food production</li>
<li>the industrial system</li>
<li>the population system</li>
<li>the non-renewable resources system</li>
<li>the pollution system</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agricultural_system">Agricultural system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Agricultural system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The simplest useful view of this system is that land and <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilizer</a> are used for <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farming</a>, and more of either will produce more food. In the context of the model, since land is finite, and industrial output required to produce fertilizer and other <a href="/wiki/Agricultural" class="mw-redirect" title="Agricultural">agricultural</a> inputs can not keep up with demand, there necessarily will be a food collapse at some point in the future.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nonrenewable_resources_system">Nonrenewable resources system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nonrenewable resources system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/World3_nonrenewable_resource_sector" title="World3 nonrenewable resource sector">World3 nonrenewable resource sector</a></div>
<p>The nonrenewable resource system starts with the assumption that the total amount of resources available is finite (about 110 times the consumption at 1990s rates for the World3/91 model). These resources can be extracted and then used for various purposes in other systems in the model. An important assumption that was made is that as the nonrenewable resources are extracted, the remaining resources are increasingly difficult to extract, thus diverting more and more industrial output to resource extraction.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reference_run_predictions">Reference run predictions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reference run predictions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Dynamics of Growth in a Finite World</i> provides several different scenarios. The "reference run" is the one that "represent the most likely behavior mode of the system if the process of industrialization in the future proceeds in a way very similar to its progress in the past, and if technologies and value changes that have already been institutionalized continue to evolve."<sup id="cite_ref-Meadows_et_al.-1974_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meadows_et_al.-1974-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>502</span></sup> In this scenario, in 2000, the world population reaches six billion, and then goes on to peak at seven billion in 2030. After that population declines because of an increased death rate. In 2015, both industrial output per capita and food per capita peak at US$375 per person (1970s dollars) and 500 vegetable-equivalent kilograms/person. Persistent pollution peaks in the year 2035 at 11 times 1970s levels.<sup id="cite_ref-Meadows_et_al.-1974_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meadows_et_al.-1974-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>500</span></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_the_model">Criticism of the model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Criticism of the model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There has been quite a bit of criticism of the World3 model. Some has come from the model creators themselves, some has come from economists and some has come from other places.
</p><p>One of the major criticisms of the model is that it simply has not reflected the reality of the world since the 1970s when the model was first published.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> At least one study,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> however, claims that "30 years of historical data compare favorably with key features of a business-as-usual scenario called the 'standard run' scenario" produced by the World3 model.
</p><p>
In the book <i>Groping in the Dark: The First Decade of Global Modelling</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Meadows-1982_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meadows-1982-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>129</span></sup> <a href="/wiki/Donella_Meadows" title="Donella Meadows">Donella Meadows</a> states: </p><blockquote><p>We have great confidence in the basic qualitative assumptions and conclusions about the instability of the current global socioeconomic system and the general kinds of changes that will and will not lead to stability. We have relatively great confidence in the feedback-loop structure of the model, with some exceptions which I list below.  We have a mixed degree of confidence in the numerical parameters of the model; some are well-known physical or biological constants that are unlikely to change, some are statistically derived social indices quite likely to change, and some are pure guesses that are perhaps only of the right order of magnitude. The structural assumptions in World3 that I consider most dubious and also sensitive enough to be of concern are:
</p><ul><li>the constant capital-output ratio (which assumes no diminishing returns to capital)</li>
<li>the residual nature of the investment function</li>
<li>the generally ineffective labour contribution to output</li></ul></blockquote>
<p>A detailed criticism of the model is in the book <i>Models of Doom: A Critique of the Limits to Growth</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cole-1973_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cole-1973-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>905-908</span></sup>
</p><p>Both <a href="/wiki/Julian_Lincoln_Simon" class="mw-redirect" title="Julian Lincoln Simon">Julian Lincoln Simon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bj%C3%B8rn_Lomborg" title="Bjørn Lomborg">Bjørn Lomborg</a> have discussed the assumptions that the model makes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The first assumption that they criticize is the assumption of finite natural resources.  They also state that the limits on agriculture are invalid since they are based on the limit of the amount of land.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Vaclav_Smil" title="Vaclav Smil">Vaclav Smil</a> disagreed with the combination of physically different processes into simplified equations:
</p>
<dl><dd>But those of us who knew the <a href="/wiki/DYNAMO_(programming_language)" title="DYNAMO (programming language)">DYNAMO</a> language in which the simulation was written and those who took the model apart line-by-line quickly realized that we had to deal with an exercise in misinformation and obfustication rather than with a model delivering valuable insights.  I was particularly astonished by the variables labelled <i>Nonrenewable Resources</i> and <i>Pollution</i>. Lumping together (to cite just a few scores of possible examples) highly substitutable but relatively limited resources of liquid oil with unsubstitutable but immense deposits of sedimentary phosphate rocks, or short-lived atmospheric gases with long-lived radioactive wastes, struck me as extraordinarily meaningless.<sup id="cite_ref-Smil-2005_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smil-2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>168</span></sup></dd></dl>
<p>He does however consider continuous growth in world GDP a problem:
</p>
<dl><dd>Only the widespread scientific illiteracy and innumeracy—all you need to know in this case is how to execute the equation <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"  alttext="{\displaystyle y=x*e^{rt}}">

After all, even cancerous cells stop growing once they have destroyed the invaded tissues.<sup id="cite_ref-Smil-2005_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smil-2005-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>338-339</span></sup></dd></dl>
<p>Others have put forth criticisms, such as Henshaw, King, and Zarnikau who in a 2011 paper, <i>Systems Energy Assessment</i><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> point out that the methodology of such models may be valid empirically as a world model, but might not then also be useful for decision making. The impact data being used is generally collected according to where the impacts are recorded as occurring, following standard I/O material processes accounting methods. It is not reorganized according to who pays for or profits from the impacts, so who is actually responsible for economic impacts is never determined.  
</p>
<ul><li>The economic motives causing the impacts, that might also control them, would then not be reflected in the model.</li>
<li>As a seeming technicality, it could bring into question the use of many kinds of economic models for sustainability decision-making.</li></ul><p>
The authors of the book <i>Surviving 1,000 Centuries</i> consider some of the predictions too pessimistic, but some of the overall message correct.<sup id="cite_ref-Bonnet-2008_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bonnet-2008-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4-5</span></sup></p><blockquote><p>...[We] come to the well-known study, <i>Limits to Growth</i>, published under the sponsorship of the 'Club de Rome' - an influential body of private individuals.  A first attempt was made to make a complete systems analysis of the rapidly growing human-biological-resource-pollution system.  In this  analysis the manifold interactions between the different parts were explicitly taken into account.  The conclusion was that disaster was waiting around the corner in a few decades because of resource exhaustion, pollution and other factors.  Now, 35 years later, our world still exists, ... So the 'growth lobby' has laughed and proclaimed that <i>Limits to Growth</i> and, by extension, the environmental movements may be forgotten.  This entirely misses the point.  Certainly the timescale of the problems was underestimated in <i>Limits to Growth</i>, giving us a little more time than we thought.  Moreover, during the last three decades a variety of national or collaborative international measures have been taken that have forced reductions in pollution, as we shall discuss.  A shining example of this is the Montreal Protocol (1987) that limited the industrial production of fluorocarbons that damage the ozone layer and generated the 'ozone hole' over Antarctica. The publication of <i>Limits to Growth</i> has greatly contributed towards creating the general willingness of governments to consider such issues.  Technological developments have also lead to improvements in the efficiency of the use of energy and other resources, but, most importantly, the warnings from Malthus onward have finally had their effect as may be seen from the population-limiting policies followed by China and, more hesitantly, by India.  Without such policies all other efforts would be in vain.  However, the basic message of <i>Limits to Growth</i>, that exponential growth of our world civilization cannot continue very long and that a very careful management of the planet is needed, remain as valid as ever.</p></blockquote>
