<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Supercritical fluid</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>supercritical fluid</b> (<b>SCF</b><sup id="cite_ref-padrela_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-padrela-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) is any substance at a <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a> above its <a href="/wiki/Critical_point_(chemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Critical point (chemistry)">critical point</a>, where distinct liquid and gas phases do not exist. It can <a href="/wiki/Effusion" title="Effusion">effuse</a> through solids like a gas, and <a href="/wiki/Solvation" title="Solvation">dissolve</a> materials like a liquid. In addition, close to the critical point, small changes in pressure or temperature result in large changes in <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, allowing many properties of a supercritical fluid to be "fine-tuned". Supercritical fluids are suitable as a substitute for <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic</a> <a href="/wiki/Solvents" class="mw-redirect" title="Solvents">solvents</a> in a range of industrial and laboratory processes. <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_carbon_dioxide" title="Supercritical carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a> and water are the most commonly used supercritical fluids, being used for <a href="/wiki/Decaffeination" title="Decaffeination">decaffeination</a> and <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_water_reactor" title="Supercritical water reactor">power generation</a>, respectively.
</p>

<p>In addition, there is no <a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">surface tension</a> in a supercritical fluid, as there is no liquid/gas phase boundary. By changing the pressure and temperature of the fluid, the properties can be "tuned" to be more liquid-like or more gas-like. One of the most important properties is the solubility of material in the fluid. Solubility in a supercritical fluid tends to increase with density of the fluid (at constant temperature). Since density increases with pressure, solubility tends to increase with pressure. The relationship with temperature is a little more complicated. At constant density, solubility will increase with temperature. However, close to the critical point, the density can drop sharply with a slight increase in temperature. Therefore, close to the critical temperature, solubility often drops with increasing temperature, then rises again.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>All supercritical fluids are completely <a href="/wiki/Miscibility" title="Miscibility">miscible</a> with each other so for a mixture a single phase can be guaranteed if the critical point of the mixture is exceeded. The critical point of a binary mixture can be estimated as the <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic_mean" title="Arithmetic mean">arithmetic mean</a> of the critical temperatures and pressures of the two components,
</p>
<dl><dd><i>T</i><sub>c(mix)</sub> = (mole fraction <i>A</i>) × <i>T</i><sub>c</sub><i>A</i> + (mole fraction <i>B</i>) × <i>T</i><sub>c</sub><i>B</i>.</dd></dl>
<p>For greater accuracy, the critical point can be calculated using <a href="/wiki/Equations_of_state" class="mw-redirect" title="Equations of state">equations of state</a>, such as the Peng Robinson, or <a href="/w/index.php?title=Group_contribution&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Group contribution (page does not exist)">group contribution</a> methods. Other properties, such as density, can also be calculated using equations of state.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phase_diagram">Phase diagram</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phase diagram">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:291px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carbon_dioxide_density-pressure_phase_diagram.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/37/Carbon_dioxide_density-pressure_phase_diagram.jpg" width="289" height="256" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="289" data-file-height="256" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><b>Figure 2.</b> Carbon dioxide density-pressure phase diagram</div></div></div>
<p>Figures 1 and 2 show projections of a <a href="/wiki/Phase_diagram" title="Phase diagram">phase diagram</a>. In the pressure-temperature phase diagram (Fig. 1) the <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling</a> separates the <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> and liquid region and ends in the critical point, where the liquid and gas phases disappear to become a single supercritical phase. This can be observed in the density-pressure phase diagram for carbon dioxide, as shown in Figure 2. At well below the critical temperature, e.g., 280&#160;K, as the pressure increases, the gas compresses and eventually (at just over 40 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">bar</a>) condenses into a much denser liquid, resulting in the discontinuity in the line (vertical dotted line). The system consists of 2 phases in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium" title="Chemical equilibrium">equilibrium</a>, a dense liquid and a low density gas. As the critical temperature is approached (300&#160;K), the density of the gas at equilibrium becomes higher, and that of the liquid lower.  At the critical point, (304.1 K and 7.38 MPa (73.8 bar)), there is no difference in density, and the 2 phases become one fluid phase. Thus, above the critical temperature a gas cannot be liquefied by pressure. At slightly above the critical temperature (310&#160;K), in the vicinity of the critical pressure, the line is almost vertical. A small increase in pressure causes a large increase in the density of the supercritical phase.   Many other physical properties also show large gradients with pressure near the critical point, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Relative_permittivity" title="Relative permittivity">relative permittivity</a> and the solvent strength, which are all closely related to the density. At higher temperatures, the fluid starts to behave like a gas, as can be seen in Figure 2. For carbon dioxide at 400 K, the density increases almost linearly with pressure.
</p><p>Many pressurized gases are actually supercritical fluids. For example, nitrogen has a critical point of 126.2&#160;K (−147&#160;°C) and 3.4 MPa  (34 bar). Therefore, nitrogen (or compressed air) in a gas cylinder above this pressure is actually a supercritical fluid. These are more often known as permanent gases. At room temperature, they are well above their critical temperature, and therefore behave as a gas, similar to CO<sub>2</sub> at 400&#160;K above. However, they cannot be liquified by pressure unless cooled below their critical temperature.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermodynamics">Thermodynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Thermodynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In recent years, a significant effort has been devoted to investigation of various properties of supercritical fluids. This has been an exciting field with a long history since 1822 when Baron Charles Cagniard de la Tour discovered supercritical fluids while conducting experiments involving the discontinuities of the sound in a sealed cannon barrel filled with various fluids at high temperature. More recently, supercritical fluids have found application in a variety of fields, ranging from the extraction of floral fragrance from flowers to applications in food science such as creating decaffeinated coffee, functional food ingredients, pharmaceuticals, cosmetics, polymers, powders, bio- and functional materials, nano-systems, natural products, biotechnology, fossil and bio-fuels, microelectronics, energy and environment. Much of the excitement and interest of the past decade is due to the enormous progress made in increasing the power of relevant experimental tools. The development of new experimental methods and improvement of existing ones continues to play an important role in this field, with recent research focusing on dynamic properties of fluids.
</p><p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dima_Bolmatov&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dima Bolmatov (page does not exist)">Dima Bolmatov</a>, V. V. Brazhkin and K. Trachenko discovered that <a href="/wiki/Specific_heat" class="mw-redirect" title="Specific heat">specific heat</a> shows a crossover between two different dynamic regimes of the low-temperature rigid-liquid and high temperature non-rigid gas-like fluid.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Rigid liquids are rigid like a solid on short time scales, but flow like a liquid on long time scales;<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> while a supercritical gas-like fluid has the dynamic motions of a gas but is able to dissolve materials, like a liquid. The crossover challenges the currently held belief that no difference can be made between a gas and a liquid above the critical point and that the supercritical state is homogeneous in terms of physical properties. Bolmatov and colleagues formulated a theory of system thermodynamics and heat capacity above the crossover. In that theory, energy and heat capacity are governed by the minimal length of the longitudinal mode in the system only, and do not depend on system-specific structure and interactions. Bolmatov and colleagues predicted the relationship between supercritical exponents of heat capacity and viscosity and derived a <a href="/wiki/Power_law" title="Power law">power law</a> for the supercritical state.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Fisher-Widom_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Fisher-Widom line">Fisher-Widom line</a> allows to distinguish liquid-like and gas-like states within the supercritical fluid.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_occurrence">Natural occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Natural occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrothermal_circulation">Hydrothermal circulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hydrothermal circulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Blacksmoker_in_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Blacksmoker_in_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg/220px-Blacksmoker_in_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg" width="220" height="324" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Blacksmoker_in_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg/330px-Blacksmoker_in_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Blacksmoker_in_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg/440px-Blacksmoker_in_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1208" data-file-height="1780" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Blacksmoker_in_Atlantic_Ocean.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Black_smoker" class="mw-redirect" title="Black smoker">black smoker</a>, a type of hydrothermal vent</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_circulation" title="Hydrothermal circulation">Hydrothermal circulation</a></div>
<p>Hydrothermal circulation occurs within the Earth's crust wherever fluid becomes heated and begins to convect. These fluids are thought to reach supercritical conditions under a number of different settings, such as in the formation of porphyry copper deposits or high temperature circulation of seawater in the sea floor. At mid-ocean ridges, this circulation is most evident by the appearance of hydrothermal vents known as "black smokers". These are large (metres high) chimneys of sulfide and sulfate minerals which vent fluids up to 400&#160;°C. The fluids appear like great black billowing clouds of smoke due to the precipitation of dissolved metals in the fluid. It is likely that at depth many of these vent sites reach supercritical conditions, but most cool sufficiently by the time they reach the sea floor to be subcritical. One particular vent site, Turtle Pits, has displayed a brief period of supercriticality at the vent site. A further site, <a href="/wiki/Beebe_Hydrothermal_Vent_Field" title="Beebe Hydrothermal Vent Field">Beebe</a>, in the Cayman Trough, is thought to display sustained supercriticality at the vent orifice.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planetary_atmospheres">Planetary atmospheres</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Planetary atmospheres">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The atmosphere of <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> is 96.5% carbon dioxide and 3.5% nitrogen. The surface pressure is 9.3 MPa (93 bar) and the surface temperature is 735 K, above the critical points of both major constituents and making the surface atmosphere a supercritical fluid.
</p><p>The interior atmospheres of the solar system's <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">gas giant</a> planets are composed mainly of hydrogen and helium at temperatures well above their critical points. The gaseous outer atmospheres of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a> transition smoothly into the dense liquid interior, while the nature of the transition zones of <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a> is unknown. Theoretical models of <a href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" class="mw-redirect" title="Extrasolar planet">extrasolar planets</a> <a href="/wiki/55_Cancri_e" title="55 Cancri e">55 Cancri e</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gliese_876_d" title="Gliese 876 d">Gliese 876 d</a> have posited an ocean of pressurized, supercritical fluid water with a sheet of solid high pressure water ice at the bottom.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supercritical_fluid_extraction">Supercritical fluid extraction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Supercritical fluid extraction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The advantages of <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid_extraction" title="Supercritical fluid extraction">supercritical fluid extraction</a> (compared with liquid extraction) are that it is relatively rapid because of the low viscosities and high diffusivities associated with supercritical fluids. The extraction can be selective to some extent by controlling the density of the medium, and the extracted material is easily recovered by simply depressurizing, allowing the supercritical fluid to return to gas phase and evaporate leaving little or no solvent residues. Carbon dioxide is the most common supercritical solvent. It is used on a large scale for the <a href="/wiki/Decaffeination" title="Decaffeination">decaffeination</a> of green coffee beans, the extraction of <a href="/wiki/Hops" title="Hops">hops</a> for beer production,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and the production of <a href="/wiki/Essential_oils" class="mw-redirect" title="Essential oils">essential oils</a> and pharmaceutical products from plants.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> A few <a href="/wiki/Laboratory" title="Laboratory">laboratory</a> <a href="/wiki/Test_method" title="Test method">test methods</a> include the use of <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid_extraction" title="Supercritical fluid extraction">supercritical fluid extraction</a> as an extraction method instead of using traditional <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supercritical_fluid_decomposition">Supercritical fluid decomposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Supercritical fluid decomposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Supercritical water can be used to decompose biomass via supercritical water gasification of biomass.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> This type of biomass gasification can be used to produce hydrocarbon fuels for use in an efficient combustion device or to produce hydrogen for use in a fuel cell. In the latter case, hydrogen yield can be much higher than the hydrogen content of the biomass due to steam reforming where water is a hydrogen-providing participant in the overall reaction.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dry-cleaning">Dry-cleaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Dry-cleaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Supercritical carbon dioxide (SCD) can be used instead of PERC (<a href="/wiki/Perchloroethylene" class="mw-redirect" title="Perchloroethylene">perchloroethylene</a>) or other undesirable solvents for <a href="/wiki/Dry-cleaning" class="mw-redirect" title="Dry-cleaning">dry-cleaning</a>. Supercritical carbon dioxide sometimes <a href="/wiki/Intercalation_(chemistry)" title="Intercalation (chemistry)">intercalates</a> into buttons, and, when the SCD is depressurized, the buttons pop, or break apart. Detergents that are soluble in carbon dioxide improve the solvating power of the solvent.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supercritical_fluid_chromatography">Supercritical fluid chromatography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Supercritical fluid chromatography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Supercritical_fluid_chromatography" title="Supercritical fluid chromatography">Supercritical fluid chromatography</a> (SFC) can be used on an analytical scale, where it combines many of the advantages of <a href="/wiki/High_performance_liquid_chromatography" class="mw-redirect" title="High performance liquid chromatography">high performance liquid chromatography</a> (HPLC) and <a href="/wiki/Gas_chromatography" title="Gas chromatography">gas chromatography</a> (GC). It can be used with non-volatile and thermally labile analytes (unlike GC) and can be used with the universal <a href="/wiki/Flame_ionization_detector" title="Flame ionization detector">flame ionization detector</a> (unlike HPLC), as well as producing narrower peaks due to rapid diffusion. In practice, the advantages offered by SFC have not been sufficient to displace the widely used HPLC and GC, except in a few cases such as <a href="/wiki/Chiral" class="mw-redirect" title="Chiral">chiral</a> separations and analysis of high-molecular-weight hydrocarbons.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> For manufacturing, efficient preparative <a href="/wiki/Simulated_moving_bed" title="Simulated moving bed">simulated moving bed</a> units are available.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The purity of the final products is very high, but the cost makes it suitable only for very high-value materials such as pharmaceuticals.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_reactions">Chemical reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Chemical reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Changing the conditions of the reaction solvent can allow separation of phases for product removal, or single phase for reaction. Rapid diffusion accelerates diffusion controlled reactions. Temperature and pressure can tune the reaction down preferred pathways, e.g., to improve yield of a particular <a href="/wiki/Chiral" class="mw-redirect" title="Chiral">chiral</a> <a href="/wiki/Isomer" title="Isomer">isomer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> There are also significant environmental benefits over conventional organic solvents.
</p><p>An <a href="/wiki/Electrosynthesis#Cathodic_reductions" title="Electrosynthesis">electrochemical</a> <a href="/wiki/Carboxylation" title="Carboxylation">carboxylation</a> of a para-<a href="/wiki/Isobutyl" class="mw-redirect" title="Isobutyl">isobutyl</a><a href="/wiki/Benzyl_chloride" title="Benzyl chloride">benzyl chloride</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ibuprofen" title="Ibuprofen">Ibuprofen</a> is promoted under supercritical carbon dioxide.<sup id="cite_ref-SCY_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCY-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Impregnation_and_dyeing">Impregnation and dyeing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Impregnation and dyeing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Impregnation is, in essence, the converse of extraction. A substance is dissolved in the supercritical fluid, the solution flowed past a solid substrate, and is deposited on or dissolves in the substrate. Dyeing, which is readily carried out on polymer fibres such as polyester using disperse (non-ionic) <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">dyes</a>, is a special case of this. Carbon dioxide also dissolves in many polymers, considerably swelling and plasticising them and further accelerating the diffusion process.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nano_and_micro_particle_formation">Nano and micro particle formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Nano and micro particle formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Micronization" title="Micronization">micronization</a></div>
<p>The formation of small particles of a substance with a narrow size distribution is an important process in the pharmaceutical and other industries. Supercritical fluids provide a number of ways of achieving this by rapidly exceeding the <a href="/wiki/Saturation_point_(chemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Saturation point (chemistry)">saturation point</a> of a solute by dilution, depressurization or a combination of these. These processes occur faster in supercritical fluids than in liquids, promoting <a href="/wiki/Nucleation" title="Nucleation">nucleation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Spinodal_decomposition" title="Spinodal decomposition">spinodal decomposition</a> over <a href="/wiki/Crystal_growth" title="Crystal growth">crystal growth</a> and yielding very small and regularly sized particles. Recent supercritical fluids have shown the capability to reduce particles up to a range of 5-2000&#160;nm.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Generation_of_pharmaceutical_cocrystals">Generation of pharmaceutical cocrystals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Generation of pharmaceutical cocrystals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Supercritical fluids act as a new media for the generation of novel crystalline forms of APIs (Active Pharmaceutical Ingredients) named as pharmaceutical cocrystals. Supercritical fluid technology offers a new platform that allows a single-step generation of particles that are difficult or even impossible to obtain by traditional techniques. The generation of pure and dried new cocrystals (crystalline molecular complexes comprising the API and one or more conformers in the crystal lattice) can be achieved due to unique properties of SCFs by using different supercritical fluid properties: supercritical CO<sub>2</sub> solvent power, anti-solvent effect and its atomization enhancement.<sup id="cite_ref-padrela_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-padrela-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supercritical_drying">Supercritical drying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Supercritical drying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Critical_point_drying" class="mw-redirect" title="Critical point drying">Critical point drying</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Supercritical_drying" title="Supercritical drying">Supercritical drying</a> is a method of removing solvent without surface tension effects. As a liquid dries, the surface tension drags on small structures within a solid, causing distortion and shrinkage. Under supercritical conditions there is no surface tension, and the supercritical fluid can be removed without distortion. Supercritical drying is used for manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Aerogel" title="Aerogel">aerogels</a> and drying of delicate materials such as archeological samples and biological samples for <a href="/wiki/Electron_microscopy" class="mw-redirect" title="Electron microscopy">electron microscopy</a>. Carbon Dioxide is used as a supercritical solvent in some dry cleaning processes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supercritical_water_oxidation">Supercritical water oxidation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Supercritical water oxidation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Supercritical_water_oxidation" title="Supercritical water oxidation">Supercritical water oxidation</a> uses supercritical water as a medium in which to oxidize hazardous waste, eliminating production of toxic combustion products that burning can produce.
</p><p>The waste product to be oxidised is dissolved in the supercritical water along with molecular oxygen (or an oxidising agent that gives up oxygen upon decomposition, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide" title="Hydrogen peroxide">hydrogen peroxide</a>) at which point the oxidation reaction occurs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supercritical_water_hydrolysis">Supercritical water hydrolysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Supercritical water hydrolysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Supercritical_hydrolysis" title="Supercritical hydrolysis">Supercritical hydrolysis</a> is a method of converting all biomass polysaccharides as well the associated lignin into low molecular compounds by contacting with water alone under supercritical conditions. The supercritical water, acts as a solvent, a supplier of bond-breaking thermal energy, a heat transfer agent and as a source of hydrogen atoms. All polysaccharides are converted into simple sugars in near-quantitative yield in a second or less. The aliphatic inter-ring linkages of lignin are also readily cleaved into free radicals that are stabilized by hydrogen originating from the water. The aromatic rings of the lignin are unaffected under short reaction times so that the lignin-derived products are low molecular weight mixed phenols. To take advantage of the very short reaction times needed for cleavage a continuous reaction system must be devised. The amount of water heated to a supercritical state is thereby minimized.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supercritical_water_gasification">Supercritical water gasification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Supercritical water gasification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_water_gasification&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Supercritical water gasification (page does not exist)">Supercritical water gasification</a> is a process of exploiting the beneficial effect of supercritical water to convert aqueous biomass streams into clean water and gases like H<sub>2</sub>, CH<sub>4</sub>, CO<sub>2</sub>, CO etc.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supercritical_fluid_in_power_generation">Supercritical fluid in power generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Supercritical fluid in power generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">efficiency</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engine</a> is ultimately dependent on the temperature difference between heat source and sink (<a href="/wiki/Carnot_cycle" title="Carnot cycle">Carnot cycle</a>). To improve efficiency of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel_power_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Fossil fuel power plant">power stations</a> the <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">operating temperature</a> must be raised. Using water as the working fluid, this takes it into supercritical conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Efficiencies can be raised from about 39% for subcritical operation to about 45% using current technology.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_water_reactor" title="Supercritical water reactor">Supercritical water reactors</a> (SCWRs) are promising advanced nuclear systems that offer similar thermal efficiency gains. Carbon dioxide can also be used in supercritical cycle nuclear power plants, with similar efficiency gains.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Many coal-fired <a href="/wiki/Supercritical_steam_generator" title="Supercritical steam generator">supercritical steam generators</a> are operational all over the world, and have enhanced the efficiency of traditional steam-power  plants.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiesel_production">Biodiesel production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Biodiesel production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Conversion of vegetable oil to <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel_production" title="Biodiesel production">biodiesel</a> is via a <a href="/wiki/Transesterification" title="Transesterification">transesterification</a> reaction, where the <a href="/wiki/Triglyceride" title="Triglyceride">triglyceride</a> is converted to the methyl ester plus <a href="/wiki/Glycerol" title="Glycerol">glycerol</a>. This is usually done using <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Causticity" class="mw-redirect" title="Causticity">caustic</a> or acid catalysts, but can be achieved using supercritical methanol without a catalyst. The method of using supercritical methanol for biodiesel production was first studied by Saka and his coworkers. This has the advantage of allowing a greater range and water content of feedstocks (in particular, used cooking oil), the product does not need to be washed to remove catalyst, and is easier to design as a continuous process.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enhanced_oil_recovery_and_carbon_capture_and_storage">Enhanced oil recovery and carbon capture and storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Enhanced oil recovery and carbon capture and storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Supercritical carbon dioxide is used to <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_oil_recovery" title="Enhanced oil recovery">enhance oil</a> recovery in mature oil fields. At the same time, there is the possibility of using "<a href="/wiki/Clean_coal_technology" title="Clean coal technology">clean coal technology</a>" to combine enhanced recovery methods with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon sequestration</a>. The CO<sub>2</sub> is separated from other <a href="/wiki/Flue_gases" class="mw-redirect" title="Flue gases">flue gases</a>, compressed to the supercritical state, and injected into geological storage, possibly into existing oil fields to improve yields.
</p><p>At present, only schemes isolating fossil CO<sub>2</sub> from natural gas actually use carbon storage, (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Sleipner_gas_field" title="Sleipner gas field">Sleipner gas field</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  but there are many plans for future CCS schemes involving pre- or post- combustion CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> There is also the possibility to reduce the amount of CO<sub>2</sub> in the atmosphere by using <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> to generate power and sequestering the CO<sub>2</sub> produced.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Enhanced_geothermal_system">Enhanced geothermal system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Enhanced geothermal system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_geothermal_system#CO2_EGS" title="Enhanced geothermal system">Enhanced geothermal system §&#160;CO2 EGS</a></div>
<p>The use of supercritical carbon dioxide, instead of water, has been examined as a geothermal working fluid.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Refrigeration">Refrigeration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Refrigeration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg" class="image"><img alt="[icon]" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/44px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" width="44" height="31" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/66px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/88px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="44" data-file-height="31" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>&#32;with: an explanation what physical properties of SCF make it a good refrigerant, and the connection to the stated reduction in carbon footprint. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small>  <small><i>(July 2018)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Supercritical carbon dioxide is also an important emerging <a href="/wiki/Refrigeration" title="Refrigeration">refrigerant</a>, being used in new, low-carbon solutions for domestic heat pumps.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> These systems are undergoing continuous development with supercritical carbon dioxide heat pumps already being successfully marketed in Asia. The <a href="/wiki/EcoCute" title="EcoCute">EcoCute</a> systems from Japan, developed by consortium of companies including Mitsubishi, develop high-temperature domestic water with small inputs of electric power by moving heat into the system from their surroundings. Their success makes a future use in other world regions possible.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supercritical_fluid_deposition">Supercritical fluid deposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Supercritical fluid deposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Supercritical fluids can be used to deposit functional nanostructured films and nanometer-size particles of metals onto surfaces. The high diffusivities and concentrations of precursor in the fluid as compared to the vacuum systems used in <a href="/wiki/Chemical_vapour_deposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical vapour deposition">chemical vapour deposition</a> allow deposition to occur in a surface reaction rate limited regime, providing stable and uniform interfacial growth.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> This is crucial in developing more powerful electronic components, and metal particles deposited in this way are also powerful catalysts for chemical synthesis and electrochemical reactions. Additionally, due to the high rates of precursor transport in solution, it is possible to coat high surface area particles which under <a href="/wiki/Chemical_vapour_deposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical vapour deposition">chemical vapour deposition</a> would exhibit depletion near the outlet of the system and also be likely to result in unstable interfacial growth features such as <a href="/wiki/Dendrites" class="mw-redirect" title="Dendrites">dendrites</a>. The result is very thin and uniform films deposited at rates much faster than <a href="/wiki/Atomic_layer_deposition" title="Atomic layer deposition">atomic layer deposition</a>, the best other tool for particle coating at this size scale.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antimicrobial_properties">Antimicrobial properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Antimicrobial properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CO<sub>2</sub> at high pressures has <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial" title="Antimicrobial">antimicrobial</a> properties.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> While its effectiveness has been shown for various applications, the mechanisms of inactivation have not been fully understood although they have been investigated for more than 60 years.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Supercritical_fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1822, Baron <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cagniard_de_la_Tour" title="Charles Cagniard de la Tour">Charles Cagniard de la Tour</a> discovered the critical point of a substance in his famous <a href="/wiki/Cannon" title="Cannon">cannon</a> barrel experiments. Listening to discontinuities in the sound of a rolling <a href="/wiki/Flint" title="Flint">flint</a> ball in a sealed cannon filled with fluids at various temperatures, he observed the critical temperature. Above this temperature, the densities of the liquid and gas <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phases</a> become equal and the distinction between them disappears, resulting in a single supercritical fluid phase.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
