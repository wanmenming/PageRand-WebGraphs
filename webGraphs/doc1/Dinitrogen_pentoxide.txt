<p><b>Dinitrogen pentoxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>.  Also known as nitrogen pentoxide, N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is one of the binary <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a>, a family of compounds that only contain nitrogen and oxygen.  It is an unstable and potentially dangerous oxidizer that once was used as a <a href="/wiki/Reagent" title="Reagent">reagent</a> when dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a> for <a href="/wiki/Nitration" title="Nitration">nitrations</a> but has largely been superseded by NO<sub>2</sub>BF<sub>4</sub> (<a href="/wiki/Nitronium_tetrafluoroborate" title="Nitronium tetrafluoroborate">nitronium tetrafluoroborate</a>).
</p><p>N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is a rare example of a compound that adopts two structures depending on the conditions: most commonly it is a salt, but under some conditions it is a polar molecule:
</p>
<dl><dd>[NO<sub>2</sub><sup>+</sup>][NO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>] ⇌ N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub></dd></dl>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Syntheses_and_properties">Syntheses and properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dinitrogen_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Syntheses and properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> was first reported by <a href="/wiki/Henri_%C3%89tienne_Sainte-Claire_Deville" title="Henri Étienne Sainte-Claire Deville">Deville</a> in 1840, who prepared it by treating AgNO<sub>3</sub> with <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">Cl<sub>2</sub></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-b1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-b1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> A recommended laboratory synthesis entails dehydrating <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> (HNO<sub>3</sub>) with <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_pentoxide" title="Phosphorus pentoxide">phosphorus(V) oxide</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-Holl_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub>  +  12 HNO<sub>3</sub>  &#8594;  4 H<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>  +  6 N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub></dd></dl>
<p>In the reverse process, N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> reacts with water (<a href="/wiki/Hydrolysis" title="Hydrolysis">hydrolyses</a>) to produce nitric acid.  Thus, dinitrogen pentoxide is the <a href="/wiki/Anhydride" class="mw-redirect" title="Anhydride">anhydride</a> of nitric acid:
</p>
<dl><dd>N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>  +  H<sub>2</sub>O  &#8594;  2 HNO<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> exists as colourless crystals that sublime slightly above room temperature. The salt eventually decomposes at room temperature into <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">NO<sub>2</sub></a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O<sub>2</sub></a>.
<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dinitrogen_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:N2O5.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/N2O5.svg/220px-N2O5.svg.png" width="220" height="116" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/N2O5.svg/330px-N2O5.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/N2O5.svg/440px-N2O5.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="136" data-file-height="72" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:N2O5.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Lewis_structure" title="Lewis structure">Lewis structure</a> of gas-phase <span class="chemf nowrap">N<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span>O<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">5</sub></span></span></div></div></div>
<p>Solid N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is a <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a>, consisting of separated anions and cations.  The cation is the linear <a href="/wiki/Nitronium" class="mw-redirect" title="Nitronium">nitronium</a> ion NO<sub>2</sub><sup>+</sup> and the anion is the planar <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">nitrate</a> NO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> ion.  Thus, the solid could be called <i>nitronium nitrate</i>. Both <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> centers have oxidation state +5.
</p><p>The intact molecule O<sub>2</sub>N–O–NO<sub>2</sub> exists in the gas phase (obtained by subliming N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>) and when the solid is extracted into nonpolar <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride" title="Carbon tetrachloride">CCl<sub>4</sub></a>. In the gas phase, the O–N–O angle is 133° and the N–O–N angle is 114°. When gaseous N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is cooled rapidly ("quenched"), one can obtain the metastable molecular form, which exothermically converts to the ionic form above −70&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-Holl_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holl-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions_and_applications">Reactions and applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dinitrogen_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reactions and applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dinitrogen pentoxide, for example as a solution in <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a>, has been used as a reagent to introduce the NO<sub>2</sub> functionality.  This <a href="/wiki/Nitration" title="Nitration">nitration</a> reaction is represented as follows:
</p>
<dl><dd>N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>  +   Ar–H  →  HNO<sub>3</sub>  +  Ar–NO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>where Ar represents an <a href="/wiki/Arene" class="mw-redirect" title="Arene">arene</a> moiety.
</p><p>For this use, dinitrogen pentoxide has been largely replaced by <a href="/wiki/Nitronium_tetrafluoroborate" title="Nitronium tetrafluoroborate">nitronium tetrafluoroborate</a> [<span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2</sub></span></span>]<sup>+</sup>[<span class="chemf nowrap">BF<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"></sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span>]<sup>−</sup>.  This salt retains the high reactivity of NO<sub>2</sub><sup>+</sup>, but it is thermally stable, decomposing at about 180 °C  (into <a href="/wiki/Nitryl_fluoride" title="Nitryl fluoride">NO<sub>2</sub>F</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boron_trifluoride" title="Boron trifluoride">BF<sub>3</sub></a>).  The reactivity of the  NO<sub>2</sub><sup>+</sup> can be further enhanced with strong acids that generate the "super-electrophile" HNO<sub>2</sub><sup>2+</sup>.
</p><p>Dinitrogen pentoxide is relevant to the preparation of explosives.<sup id="cite_ref-b1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-b1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, dinitrogen pentoxide is an important reservoir of the NO<sub>x</sub> species that are responsible for <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a>: its formation provides a <a href="/wiki/Null_cycle" title="Null cycle">null cycle</a> with which NO and NO<sub>2</sub> are temporarily held in an unreactive state.  <a href="/wiki/Mixing_ratio" title="Mixing ratio">Mixing ratios</a> of several ppbv have been observed in polluted regions of the night-time troposphere <sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>.  Dinitrogen pentoxide has also been observed in the stratosphere<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> at similar levels, the reservoir formation having been postulated in considering the puzzling observations of a sudden drop in stratospheric NO<sub>2</sub> levels above 50 °N, the so-called 'Noxon cliff'.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards">Hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dinitrogen_pentoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>N<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is a strong oxidizer that forms explosive mixtures with organic compounds and <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a> salts.  The decomposition of dinitrogen pentoxide produces the highly toxic <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">nitrogen dioxide</a> gas.
</p>
