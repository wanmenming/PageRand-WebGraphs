<p><b>Magnesium oxide</b> (<b><a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">Mg</a><a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">O</a></b>), or <b>magnesia</b>, is a white <a href="/wiki/Hygroscopy" title="Hygroscopy">hygroscopic</a> solid <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> that occurs naturally as <a href="/wiki/Periclase" title="Periclase">periclase</a> and is a source of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">magnesium</a> (see also <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a>). It has an <a href="/wiki/Empirical_formula" title="Empirical formula">empirical formula</a> of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">Mg</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a> and consists of a lattice of Mg<sup>2+</sup> ions and O<sup>2−</sup> ions held together by <a href="/wiki/Ionic_bond" class="mw-redirect" title="Ionic bond">ionic bonding</a>. <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_hydroxide" title="Magnesium hydroxide">Magnesium hydroxide</a> forms in the presence of water (MgO + H<sub>2</sub>O → Mg(OH)<sub>2</sub>), but it can be reversed by heating it to separate moisture.
</p><p>Magnesium oxide was historically known as <b>magnesia alba</b> (literally, the white mineral from magnesia – other sources give magnesia alba as MgCO<sub>3</sub>), to differentiate it from <i><a href="/wiki/Magnesia_negra" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnesia negra">magnesia negra</a></i>, a black mineral containing what is now known as <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a>.
</p><p>While "magnesium oxide" normally refers to MgO, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_peroxide" title="Magnesium peroxide">magnesium peroxide</a> MgO<sub>2</sub> is also known as a compound. According to evolutionary crystal structure prediction,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> MgO<sub>2</sub> is thermodynamically stable at pressures above 116 GPa (gigapascals), and a semiconducting <a href="/wiki/Suboxide" title="Suboxide">suboxide</a> Mg<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> is thermodynamically stable above 500 GPa. Because of its stability, MgO is used as a model system for investigating vibrational properties of crystals.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnesium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Magnesium oxide is produced by the <a href="/wiki/Calcination" title="Calcination">calcination</a> of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_carbonate" title="Magnesium carbonate">magnesium carbonate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_hydroxide" title="Magnesium hydroxide">magnesium hydroxide</a>.  The latter is obtained by the treatment of <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_chloride" title="Magnesium chloride">magnesium chloride</a> solutions, typically seawater, with <a href="/wiki/Lime_(material)" title="Lime (material)">lime</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ullmann_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ullmann-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Mg<sup>2+</sup>  + Ca(OH)<sub>2</sub>  →  Mg(OH)<sub>2</sub>  +  Ca<sup>2+</sup></dd></dl>
<p>Calcining at different temperatures produces magnesium oxide of different reactivity. High temperatures 1500 – 2000&#160;°C diminish the available surface area and produces dead-burned (often called dead burnt) magnesia, an unreactive form used as a refractory. Calcining temperatures 1000 – 1500&#160;°C produce hard-burned magnesia, which has limited reactivity and calcining at lower temperature, (700–1000&#160;°C) produces light-burned magnesia, a reactive form, also known as caustic calcined magnesia.  Although some decomposition of the carbonate to oxide occurs at temperatures below 700&#160;°C, the resulting materials appears to reabsorb carbon dioxide from the air.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnesium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>MgO is prized as a <a href="/wiki/Refractory_material" class="mw-redirect" title="Refractory material">refractory material</a>, i.e. a solid that is physically and chemically stable at high temperatures. It has two useful attributes: high thermal conductivity and low electrical conductivity. "By far the largest consumer of magnesia worldwide is the refractory industry, which consumed about 56&#160;% of the magnesia in the United States in 2004, the remaining 44&#160;% being used in agricultural, chemical, construction, environmental, and other industrial applications."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> MgO is used as a basic <a href="/wiki/Refractory" title="Refractory">refractory</a> material for <a href="/wiki/Crucible" title="Crucible">crucibles</a>.
</p><p>It is a principal fireproofing ingredient in construction materials. As a construction material, <a href="/wiki/Magnesium_oxide_wallboard" title="Magnesium oxide wallboard">magnesium oxide wallboards</a> have several attractive characteristics: fire resistance, termite resistance, moisture resistance, mold and mildew resistance, and strength.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Niche_uses">Niche uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnesium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Niche uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>MgO is one of the components in <a href="/wiki/Portland_cement" title="Portland cement">Portland cement</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cement_kiln#The_wet_process_and_the_dry_process" title="Cement kiln">dry process plants</a>. 
</p><p>Magnesium oxide is used extensively in the soil and groundwater remediation, wastewater treatment, drinking water treatment, air emissions treatment, and waste treatment industries for its acid buffering capacity and related effectiveness in stabilizing dissolved heavy metal species.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (December 2016)">according to whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Many heavy metals species, such as lead and cadmium are most soluble in water at acidic pH (below 6) as well as high pH (above 11).  Solubility of metals affects bioavailability of the species and mobility soil and groundwater systems.  Most metal species are toxic to humans at certain concentrations, therefore it is imperative to minimize metal bioavailability and mobility.
</p><p>Granular MgO is often blended into metals-contaminated soil or waste material, which is also commonly of a low (acidic) pH, in order to drive the pH into the 8–10 range where most metals are at their lowest solubilities.  Metal-hydroxide complexes have a tendency to precipitate out of aqueous solution in the pH range of 8–10. MgO is widely regarded as the most effective metals stabilization compound when compared to Portland cement, lime, kiln dust products, power generation waste products, and various proprietary products due to MgO's superior buffering capacity, cost effectiveness, and ease/safety of handling.
</p><p>Most, if not all products that are marketed as metals stabilization technologies create very high pH conditions in aquifers whereas MgO creates an ideal aquifer condition with a pH of 8–10.   Additionally, magnesium, an essential element to most biological systems, is provided to soil and groundwater microbial populations during MgO-assisted metals remediation as an added benefit.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical">Medical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnesium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Medical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Magnesium oxide has poor solubility in water and is poorly absorbed from the gut. For this reason, magnesium oxide is relatively ineffective for correcting magnesium deficiency. Magnesium oxide is used for relief of heartburn and sour stomach, as an antacid, magnesium supplement, and as a short-term <a href="/wiki/Laxative" title="Laxative">laxative</a>. It is also used to improve symptoms of <a href="/wiki/Indigestion" title="Indigestion">indigestion</a>. Side effects of magnesium oxide may include nausea and cramping.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In quantities sufficient to obtain a laxative effect, side effects of long-term use include <a href="/wiki/Enterolith" title="Enterolith">enteroliths</a> resulting in <a href="/wiki/Bowel_obstruction" title="Bowel obstruction">bowel obstruction</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnesium_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>It is used as a reference white color in <a href="/wiki/Colorimetry" title="Colorimetry">colorimetry</a>, owing to its good <a href="/wiki/Diffuser_(optics)" title="Diffuser (optics)">diffusing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reflectivity" class="mw-redirect" title="Reflectivity">reflectivity</a> properties.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It may be smoked onto the surface of an opaque material to form an <a href="/wiki/Integrating_sphere" title="Integrating sphere">integrating sphere</a>.</li>
<li>It is used extensively as an electrical insulator in tubular construction <a href="/wiki/Heating_element" title="Heating element">heating elements</a>. There are several <a href="/wiki/Mesh_(scale)" title="Mesh (scale)">mesh</a> sizes available and most commonly used ones are 40 and 80&#160;mesh per the <a href="/wiki/American_Foundry_Society" title="American Foundry Society">American Foundry Society</a>. The extensive use is due to its high dielectric strength and average thermal conductivity. MgO is usually crushed and compacted with minimal airgaps or voids. The electrical heating industry also experimented with <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">aluminium oxide</a>, but it is not used anymore.</li>
<li>It is also used as an insulator in <a href="/wiki/Mineral-insulated_copper-clad_cable" title="Mineral-insulated copper-clad cable">heat-resistant electrical cable</a>.</li>
<li>MgO <a href="/wiki/Doping_(semiconductor)" title="Doping (semiconductor)">doping</a> has been shown to effectively inhibit <a href="/wiki/Grain_growth" title="Grain growth">grain growth</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ceramic_materials" class="mw-redirect" title="Ceramic materials">ceramics</a> and improve their <a href="/wiki/Fracture_toughness" title="Fracture toughness">fracture toughness</a> by transforming the mechanism of crack growth at nanoscale.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MgOcrystal.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/35/MgOcrystal.JPG/140px-MgOcrystal.JPG" width="140" height="129" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/35/MgOcrystal.JPG/210px-MgOcrystal.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/35/MgOcrystal.JPG/280px-MgOcrystal.JPG 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="462" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MgOcrystal.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Unpolished MgO crystal</div></div></div>
<ul><li>Pressed MgO is used as an optical material. It is transparent from 0.3 to 7&#160;µm. The <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a> is 1.72 at 1&#160;µm and the <a href="/wiki/Abbe_number" title="Abbe number">Abbe number</a> is 53.58. It is sometimes known by the <a href="/wiki/Eastman_Kodak" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastman Kodak">Eastman Kodak</a> trademarked name <b>Irtran-5</b>, although this designation is obsolete. Crystalline pure MgO is available commercially and has a small use in infrared optics.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>MgO is packed around <a href="/wiki/Transuranic_waste" title="Transuranic waste">transuranic waste</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Waste_Isolation_Pilot_Plant" title="Waste Isolation Pilot Plant">Waste Isolation Pilot Plant</a>, to control the solubility of radionuclides.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>MgO has an important place as a commercial plant fertilizer <sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and as animal feed.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>An aerosolized solution of MgO is used in library science and collections management for the <a href="/wiki/Deacidification" class="mw-redirect" title="Deacidification">deacidification</a> of at-risk paper items. In this process, the alkalinity of MgO (and similar compounds) neutralizes the relatively high acidity characteristic of low-quality paper, thus slowing the rate of deterioration.<sup id="cite_ref-Deacidification_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Deacidification-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>MgO is also used as a protective coating in <a href="/wiki/Plasma_displays" class="mw-redirect" title="Plasma displays">plasma displays</a>.</li>
<li>Magnesium oxide is used as an oxide barrier in spin-tunneling devices. Owing to the crystalline structure of its thin films, which can be deposited by <a href="/wiki/Magnetron_sputtering" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnetron sputtering">magnetron sputtering</a>, for example, it shows characteristics superior to those of the commonly used amorphous Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. In particular, <a href="/wiki/Spin_polarization" title="Spin polarization">spin polarization</a> of about 85% has been achieved with MgO<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> versus 40–60&#160;% with aluminium oxide.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The value of <a href="/wiki/Tunnel_magnetoresistance" title="Tunnel magnetoresistance">tunnel magnetoresistance</a> is also significantly higher for MgO (600% at room temperature and 1,100&#160;% at 4.2 K<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>) than Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> (ca. 70% at room temperature<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>). MgO is thermally stable up to about 700&#160;K, vs. 600&#160;K for Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.</li></ul>
