<p><b>Lanthanum oxide</b>, chemical formula  La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> containing the <a href="/wiki/Rare_earth_element" class="mw-redirect" title="Rare earth element">rare earth element</a> <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">lanthanum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>. It is used in some ferroelectric materials, as a component of optical materials, and is a feedstock for certain catalysts, among other uses.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a href="/wiki/File:La2O3powder.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/La2O3powder.jpg/160px-La2O3powder.jpg" width="160" height="130" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/La2O3powder.jpg/240px-La2O3powder.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/La2O3powder.jpg/320px-La2O3powder.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="405" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:La2O3powder.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> powder</div></div></div>
<p>La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has a band gap of approximately 5.8 eV.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It has the lowest lattice energy, with very high <a href="/wiki/Dielectric_constant" class="mw-redirect" title="Dielectric constant">dielectric constant</a>, ε = 27. La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is widely used in industry as well as in the research laboratory. Lanthanum oxide is an odorless, white solid that is insoluble in water, but soluble in dilute acid. Depending on the pH of the compound, different crystal structures can be obtained.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="It may not be the pH of the compound itself, rather the pH of the solution used to obtain the solid (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is hygroscopic; under atmosphere, lanthanum oxide absorbs moisture over time and converts to lanthanum hydroxide. Lanthanum oxide has p-type semiconducting properties. Its resistivity decreases with an increase in temperature. Its average room temperature resistivity is 10 kΩ·cm.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At low temperatures, La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has an A-M<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> hexagonal crystal structure. The La<sup>3+</sup> metal atoms are surrounded by a 7 coordinate group of O<sup>2−</sup>atoms, the oxygen ions are in an octahedral shape around the metal atom and there is one oxygen ion above one of the octahedral faces.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  On the other hand, at high temperatures the Lanthanum oxide converts to a C-M<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> cubic crystal structure.  The La<sup>3+</sup> ion is surrounded by a 6 coordinate group of O<sup>2−</sup> ions.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Elements_obtained_from_Lanthana">Elements obtained from Lanthana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Elements obtained from Lanthana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several elements were discovered as a consequence of lengthy analysis and decomposition of the ore <a href="/wiki/Gadolinite" title="Gadolinite">Gadolinite</a>.  As the ore was progressively analysed, the residue was first given the label <a href="/wiki/Ceria" class="mw-redirect" title="Ceria">ceria</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Lanthana" class="mw-redirect" title="Lanthana">lanthana</a> and then subsequently <a href="/wiki/Yttria" class="mw-redirect" title="Yttria">yttria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erbia" class="mw-redirect" title="Erbia">erbia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Terbia" class="mw-redirect" title="Terbia">terbia</a>. In order of date discovered, the list of elements includes <a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">Cerium</a> 58, <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">Lanthanum</a> 57, <a href="/wiki/Erbium" title="Erbium">Erbium</a> 68, <a href="/wiki/Terbium" title="Terbium">Terbium</a> 65, <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">Yttrium</a> 39, <a href="/wiki/Ytterbium" title="Ytterbium">Ytterbium</a> 70, <a href="/wiki/Holmium" title="Holmium">Holmium</a> 67, <a href="/wiki/Thulium" title="Thulium">Thulium</a> 69, <a href="/wiki/Scandium" title="Scandium">Scandium</a> 21, <a href="/wiki/Praseodymium" title="Praseodymium">Praseodymium</a> 59, <a href="/wiki/Neodymium" title="Neodymium">Neodymium</a> 60 and <a href="/wiki/Dysprosium" title="Dysprosium">Dysprosium</a> 66.  Several of these new elements were either discovered or isolated by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Gustaf_Mosander" title="Carl Gustaf Mosander">Carl Gustaf Mosander</a> in the 1830s and 1840s.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lanthanum oxide crystallizes as several <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a>.
</p><p>To produce hexagonal La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, a 0.1 M solution of LaCl<sub>3</sub> is sprayed onto a preheated substrate, usually made of metal chalcogenides.<sup id="cite_ref-kale_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kale-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The process can be viewed as occurring in two steps – hydrolysis followed by dehydration:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 LaCl<sub>3</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O   →   La(OH)<sub>3</sub> + 3 HCl</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>2 La(OH)<sub>3</sub>  →  La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 3 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>An alternative route to hexagonal La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> involves precipitation of nominal La(OH)<sub>3</sub> from aqueous solution using a combination of 2.5% NH<sub>3</sub> and the surfactant <a href="/wiki/Sodium_dodecyl_sulfate" title="Sodium dodecyl sulfate">sodium dodecyl sulfate</a> followed by heating and stirring for 24 hours at 80&#160;°C:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 LaCl<sub>3</sub>+ 3 H<sub>2</sub>O + 3 NH<sub>3</sub> → La(OH)<sub>3</sub> + 3 NH<sub>4</sub>Cl</dd>
<dd>LaCl<sub>3</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O → La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Other routes  include:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 La<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> + 3 CO<sub>2</sub>  → 2 La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 3 CS<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd>2 La<sub>2</sub>(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> + heat → 2 La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 6 SO<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lanthanum oxide is used as an additive to develop certain ferroelectric materials, such as La-doped Bi<sub>4</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>12</sub> (BLT).
Lanthanum oxide is used in optical materials, often the optical glasses are doped with La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> to improve the glass' refractive index, chemical durability, and mechanical strength.
</p>
<dl><dd>3 B<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> → 2 La(BO<sub>2</sub>)<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>When this 1:3 reaction is mixed into a glass composite, the high molecular weight of the lanthanum causes an increase of the homogeneous mixture of the melt which leads to a lower melting point.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  The addition of the La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> to the glass melt leads to a higher glass transition temperature from 658&#160;°C to 679&#160;°C.  The addition also leads to a higher density, microhardness, and refractive index of the glass.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_and_applications">Uses and applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lanthanum_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Uses and applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is used to make optical glasses, to which this oxide confers increased density, refractive index, and hardness. Together with oxides of <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tantalum" title="Tantalum">tantalum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a>, La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> improves the resistance of the glass to attack by alkali. La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is an ingredient for the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Piezoelectric" class="mw-redirect" title="Piezoelectric">piezoelectric</a> and thermoelectric materials. Automobile exhaust-gas converters contain La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is also used in X-ray imaging intensifying screens, phosphors as well as dielectric and conductive ceramics. Gives off bright glow.
</p><p>La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has been examined for the <a href="/wiki/Oxidative_coupling_of_methane" title="Oxidative coupling of methane">oxidative coupling of methane</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> films can be <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(chemistry)" title="Deposition (chemistry)">deposited</a> by many different methods, including: <a href="/wiki/Chemical_vapor_disposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical vapor disposition">chemical vapor disposition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atomic_layer_deposition" title="Atomic layer deposition">atomic layer deposition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermal_oxidation" title="Thermal oxidation">thermal oxidation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sputtering" title="Sputtering">sputtering</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Spray_pyrolysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Spray pyrolysis">spray pyrolysis</a>. Depositions of these films occur in a temperature range of 250–450&#160;°C. <a href="/wiki/Polycrystalline" class="mw-redirect" title="Polycrystalline">Polycrystalline</a> films are formed at 350&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-kale_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kale-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>La<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> tungsten electrodes are replacing thorianated tungsten electrodes in <a href="/wiki/Gas_tungsten_arc_welding" title="Gas tungsten arc welding">Gas tungsten arc welding (TIG)</a> due to safety concerns with thorium's radioactivity.
</p>
