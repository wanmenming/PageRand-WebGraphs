<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Carbanion</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carbanion_Structural_Formulae_V.1.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Carbanion_Structural_Formulae_V.1.svg/320px-Carbanion_Structural_Formulae_V.1.svg.png" width="320" height="95" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Carbanion_Structural_Formulae_V.1.svg/480px-Carbanion_Structural_Formulae_V.1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Carbanion_Structural_Formulae_V.1.svg/640px-Carbanion_Structural_Formulae_V.1.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="651" data-file-height="193" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Carbanion_Structural_Formulae_V.1.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Carbanion</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>carbanion</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Anion" class="mw-redirect" title="Anion">anion</a> in which <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> is trivalent (forms three bonds) and bears a formal negative charge in at least one significant mesomeric contributor (resonance form).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Absent π delocalization, carbanions assume a trigonal pyramidal, bent, or linear geometry when the carbanionic carbon is bound to three (e.g., methyl anion), two (e.g., phenyl anion), or one (e.g., acetylide anion) substituents, respectively. Formally, a carbanion is the <a href="/wiki/Conjugate_base" class="mw-redirect" title="Conjugate base">conjugate base</a> of a <b>carbon acid</b>:
</p>
<dl><dd>R<sub>3</sub>CH + B<sup>−</sup> → <b>R<sub>3</sub>C<sup>−</sup></b> + HB</dd></dl>
<p>where B stands for the base. A carbanion is one of several <a href="/wiki/Reactive_intermediate" title="Reactive intermediate">reactive intermediates</a> in <a href="/wiki/Organic_chemistry" title="Organic chemistry">organic chemistry</a>. In organic synthesis, <a href="/wiki/Organolithium_reagent" title="Organolithium reagent">organolithium reagents</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grignard_reagents" class="mw-redirect" title="Grignard reagents">Grignard reagents</a> are commonly regarded as carbanions. This is a convenient approximation, although these species are almost always multinuclear clusters containing polar covalent bonds rather than true carbanions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trends_and_occurrence">Trends and occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbanion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Trends and occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbanions are typically <a href="/wiki/Nucleophilic" class="mw-redirect" title="Nucleophilic">nucleophilic</a> and basic. The basicity and nucleophilicity of carbanions are determined by the substituents on carbon. These include
</p>
<ol><li>The <a href="/wiki/Inductive_effect" title="Inductive effect">inductive effect</a>. Electronegative atoms adjacent to the charge will stabilize the charge;</li>
<li>The extent of <a href="/wiki/Conjugated_system" title="Conjugated system">conjugation</a> of the anion. <a href="/wiki/Resonance_(chemistry)" title="Resonance (chemistry)">Resonance effects</a> can stabilize the anion. This is especially true when the anion is stabilized as a result of <a href="/wiki/Aromaticity" title="Aromaticity">aromaticity</a>.</li></ol>
<p>Geometry also affects the <a href="/wiki/Orbital_hybridization" class="mw-redirect" title="Orbital hybridization">orbital hybridization</a> of the charge-bearing carbanion. The greater the s-character of the charge-bearing atom, the more stable the anion.
</p><p>Organometallic reagents like <a href="/wiki/Butyllithium" title="Butyllithium">butyllithium</a> (hexameric cluster, [BuLi]<sub>6</sub>) or <a href="/wiki/Methylmagnesium_bromide" class="mw-redirect" title="Methylmagnesium bromide">methylmagnesium bromide</a> (ether complex, MeMgBr(OEt)<sub>2</sub>) are often referred to as "carbanions," at least in a <a href="/wiki/Retrosynthetic_analysis" title="Retrosynthetic analysis">retrosynthetic</a> sense. However, they are really clusters or complexes containing a polar covalent bond, though with electron density heavily polarized toward the carbon atom. In fact, true carbanions without stabilizing substituents are not available in the condensed phase, and these species must be studied in the gas phase.
</p><p>For some time, it was not known whether simple alkyl anions could exist as free species; many theoretical studies predicted that even methanide anion CH<sub>3</sub><sup>–</sup> should be an unbound species (i.e., the <a href="/wiki/Electron_affinity" title="Electron affinity">electron affinity</a> of CH<sub>3</sub>• was predicted to be negative). Such a species would decompose immediately by spontaneous ejection of an electron and are generally too fleeting to observe directly by mass spectrometry.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> However, in 1978, methyl anion was unambiguously synthesized by subjecting ketene to electric discharge, and the electron affinity (EA) of CH<sub>3</sub>• was determined by photoelectron spectroscopy to be +1.8 kcal mol<sup>−1</sup>.  The structure of CH<sub>3</sub><sup>–</sup> was found to be pyramidal with a 1.3 kcal mol<sup>−1</sup> inversion barrier, while CH<sub>3</sub>• was determined to be planar.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  Most simple primary, secondary and tertiary sp<sup>3</sup> carbanions (e.g., CH<sub>3</sub>CH<sub>2</sub><sup>–</sup>, (CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>CH<sup>–</sup>, and (CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>C<sup>–</sup>) were subsequently determined to be unbound species (EA of CH<sub>3</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>•, (CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>CH•, (CH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>C• = −6, –7.4, –3.6 kcal mol<sup>−1</sup>, respectively) indicating that α substitution is destabilizing.  However, relatively modest stabilizing effects can render them bound.  For example, cyclopropyl and cubyl anions are bound due to increased s character of the lone pair orbital, while neopentyl and phenethyl anion are also bound, as a result of negative hyperconjugation of the lone pair with the β-substituent (n<sub>C</sub> → σ*<sub>C-C</sub>).  The same holds true for anions with benzylic and allylic stabilization. Gas-phase carbanions that are sp<sup>2</sup> and sp hybridized are much more strongly stabilized and are often prepared directly by gas-phase deprotonation.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the condensed phase only carbanions that are sufficiently stabilized by delocalization have been isolated as truly ionic species. In 1984, Olmstead and <a href="/wiki/Philip_Power" title="Philip Power">Power</a> presented the lithium <a href="/wiki/Crown_ether" title="Crown ether">crown ether</a> <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salt</a> of the triphenylmethanide carbanion from triphenylmethane, <a href="/wiki/N-butyllithium" class="mw-redirect" title="N-butyllithium">n-butyllithium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crown_ether" title="Crown ether">12-crown-4</a> (which forms a stable complex with lithium cations) at low temperatures:<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a href="/wiki/File:TriphenylmethaneAnion.png" class="image" title="Formation of the triphenylmethane anion"><img alt="Formation of the triphenylmethane anion" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/TriphenylmethaneAnion.png/500px-TriphenylmethaneAnion.png" width="500" height="179" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/TriphenylmethaneAnion.png/750px-TriphenylmethaneAnion.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/TriphenylmethaneAnion.png/1000px-TriphenylmethaneAnion.png 2x" data-file-width="1019" data-file-height="364" /></a></div></div>
<p>Adding <a href="/wiki/N-Butyllithium" title="N-Butyllithium"><i>n</i>-butyllithium</a> to <a href="/wiki/Triphenylmethane" title="Triphenylmethane">triphenylmethane</a> (p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> in DMSO of CHPh<sub>3</sub> = 30.6) in <a href="/wiki/THF" class="mw-redirect" title="THF">THF</a> at low temperatures followed by <a href="/wiki/Crown_ether" title="Crown ether">12-crown-4</a> results in a red solution and the salt complex [Li(12-crown-4)]<sup>+</sup>[CPh<sub>3</sub>]<sup>–</sup> precipitates at −20&#160;°C. The central C–C <a href="/wiki/Bond_length" title="Bond length">bond lengths</a> are 145&#160;pm with the phenyl ring propellered at an average angle of 31.2°. This propeller shape is less pronounced with a tetramethylammonium counterion. A crystal structure for the analogous diphenylmethanide anion ([Li(12-crown-4)]<sup>+</sup>[CHPh<sub>2</sub>]<sup>–</sup>), prepared form diphenylmethane (p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> in DMSO of CH<sub>2</sub>Ph<sub>2</sub> = 32.3), was also obtained.  However, the attempted isolation of a complex of the benzyl anion [CH<sub>2</sub>Ph]<sup>–</sup> from toluene (p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> in DMSO of CH<sub>3</sub>Ph ≈ 43) was unsuccessful, due to rapid reaction of the formed anion with the THF solvent.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Early in 1904<sup id="cite_ref-:1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and 1917,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Schlenk" title="Wilhelm Schlenk">Schlenk</a> prepared two red-colored salts, formulated as [NMe<sub>4</sub>]<sup>+</sup>[CPh<sub>3</sub>]<sup>–</sup> and [NMe<sub>4</sub>]<sup>+</sup>[CH<sub>2</sub>Ph]<sup>–</sup>, respectively, by metathesis of the corresponding organosodium reagent with tetramethylammonium chloride.  Since tetramethylammonium cations cannot form a chemical bond to the carbanionic center, these species are believed to contain free carbanions.  While the structure of the former was verified by X-ray crystallography almost a century later,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> the instability of the latter has so far precluded structural verification. The reaction of the putative "[NMe<sub>4</sub>]<sup>+</sup>[CH<sub>2</sub>Ph]<sup>–</sup>" with water was reported to liberate toluene and tetramethylammonium hydroxide and provides indirect evidence for the claimed formulation.
</p><p>One tool for the detection of carbanions in solution is <a href="/wiki/Proton_NMR" class="mw-redirect" title="Proton NMR">proton NMR</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> A spectrum of <a href="/wiki/Cyclopentadiene" title="Cyclopentadiene">cyclopentadiene</a> in DMSO shows four vinylic protons at 6.5 ppm and two <a href="/wiki/Methylene_bridge" title="Methylene bridge">methylene bridge</a> protons at 3 ppm whereas the <a href="/wiki/Cyclopentadienyl" title="Cyclopentadienyl">cyclopentadienyl</a> anion has a single resonance at 5.50 ppm.  The use of <sup>6</sup>Li and <sup>7</sup>Li NMR has provided structural and reactivity data for a variety of organolithium species.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_acids">Carbon acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbanion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Carbon acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Any compound containing hydrogen can, in principle, undergo deprotonation to form its conjugate base. A compound is a <i><b>carbon acid</b></i> if deprotonation results in loss of a proton from a carbon atom. Compared to compounds typically considered to be acids (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Mineral_acid" title="Mineral acid">mineral acids</a> like nitric acid, or <a href="/wiki/Carboxylic_acid" title="Carboxylic acid">carboxylic acids</a> like acetic acid), carbon acids are typically many orders of magnitude weaker, although exceptions exist (see below).  For example, <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a> is not an acid in the classical <a href="/wiki/Arrhenius_acid" class="mw-redirect" title="Arrhenius acid">Arrhenius</a> sense, since its aqueous solutions are neutral.  Nevertheless, it is very weak <a href="/wiki/Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid%E2%80%93base_theory" title="Brønsted–Lowry acid–base theory">Brønsted acid</a> with an estimated <a href="/wiki/Acid_dissociation_constant" title="Acid dissociation constant">p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub></a> of 49 which may undergo deprotonation in the presence of a superbase like the <a href="/wiki/Schlosser%27s_base" title="Schlosser&#39;s base">Lochmann–Schlosser base</a> (<i>n</i>-BuLi:KO<i>t</i>-Bu).  As conjugate acid-base pairs, the factors that determine the relative stability of carbanions also determine the ordering of the p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> values of the corresponding carbon acids.  Furthermore, p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> values allow the prediction of whether a proton transfer process will be thermodynamically favorable: In order for the deprotonation of an acidic species HA with base B<sup>−</sup> to be thermodynamically favorable (<i>K</i> &gt; 1), the relationship p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub>(BH) &gt; p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub>(AH) must hold.
</p><p>These values below are p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> values determined in DMSO, which has a broader useful range (~0 to ~35) than values determined in water (~0 to ~15) and better reflect the basicity of the carbanions in typical organic solvents.  Values below less than 0 or greater than 35 are indirectly estimated; hence, the numerical accuracy of these values is limited.  Aqueous p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> values are also commonly encountered in the literature, particularly in the context of biochemistry and enzymology. Moreover, aqueous values are often given in introductory organic chemistry textbooks for pedagogical reasons, although the issue of solvent dependence is often glossed over.  In general, p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> values in water and organic solvent diverge significantly when the anion is capable of hydrogen bonding.  For instance, in the case of water, the values differ dramatically:  p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> in water of water = 15.7, while p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> in DMSO of water = 31.4,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> reflecting the differing ability of water and DMSO to stabilize hydroxide anion.  On the other hand, for cyclopentadiene, the numerical values are comparable: p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub><sup>aq</sup>(Cp-H) = 15, while p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub><sup>DMSO</sup>(Cp-H) = 18.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>

<p><i><b><small>Note that acetic acid, ammonia, aniline, ethanol, and hydrogen chloride are not carbon acids, but are common acids shown for comparison.</small></b></i>
</p><p>As indicated by the examples above, acidity increases (p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> decreases) when the negative charge is delocalized. This effect occurs when the substituents on the carbanion are unsaturated and/or electronegative.  Although carbon acids are generally thought of as acids that are much weaker than "classical" Brønsted acids like acetic acid or phenol, the cumulative (additive) effect of several electron accepting substituents can lead to acids that are as strong or stronger than the inorganic mineral acids.  For example, trinitromethane (HC(NO<sub>2</sub>)<sub>3</sub>), tricyanomethane (HC(CN)<sub>3</sub>), pentacyanocyclopentadiene (HC<sub>5</sub>(CN)<sub>5</sub>), and fulminic acid (HCNO) are all strong acids with aqueous p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> values that indicate complete or nearly complete proton transfer to water.  Triflidic acid, with three strongly electron withdrawing triflyl groups, has an estimated p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> well below –10.  On the other end of the scale, hydrocarbons bearing only alkyl groups are thought to have p<i>K</i><sub>a</sub> values in the range of 55 to 65.  The range of acid dissociation constants for carbon acids thus spans over 70 orders of magnitude.
</p><p>The acidity of the α-hydrogen in carbonyl compounds crucially these compounds to participate in synthetically important C–C bond-forming reactions including the <a href="/wiki/Aldol_reaction" title="Aldol reaction">aldol reaction</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_reaction" title="Michael reaction">Michael addition</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chiral_carbanions">Chiral carbanions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbanion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chiral carbanions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With the <a href="/wiki/Molecular_geometry" title="Molecular geometry">molecular geometry</a> for a carbanion described as a <a href="/wiki/Trigonal_pyramid_(chemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Trigonal pyramid (chemistry)">trigonal pyramid</a> the question is whether or not carbanions can display <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">chirality</a>, because if the activation barrier for inversion of this geometry is too low any attempt at introducing chirality will end in <a href="/wiki/Racemization" title="Racemization">racemization</a>, similar to the <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_inversion" title="Nitrogen inversion">nitrogen inversion</a>. However, solid evidence exists that carbanions can indeed be chiral for example in research carried out with certain <a href="/wiki/Organolithium" class="mw-redirect" title="Organolithium">organolithium</a> compounds.
</p><p>The first ever evidence for the existence of chiral organolithium compounds was obtained in 1950. Reaction of chiral 2-iodooctane with sec-butyllithium in <a href="/wiki/Petroleum_ether" title="Petroleum ether">petroleum ether</a> at −70&#160;°C followed by reaction with <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a> yielded mostly racemic <a href="/wiki/Butyric_acid" title="Butyric acid">2-methylbutyric acid</a> but also an amount of <a href="/wiki/Optically_active" class="mw-redirect" title="Optically active">optically active</a> 2-methyloctanoic acid which could only have formed from likewise optical active 2-methylheptyllithium with the carbon atom linked to lithium the carbanion:<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a href="/wiki/File:ChiralcarbanionsI.png" class="image" title="optically active organolithium"><img alt="optically active organolithium" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/ChiralcarbanionsI.png/400px-ChiralcarbanionsI.png" width="400" height="166" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/ChiralcarbanionsI.png/600px-ChiralcarbanionsI.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/ChiralcarbanionsI.png/800px-ChiralcarbanionsI.png 2x" data-file-width="1032" data-file-height="428" /></a></div></div>
<p>On heating the reaction to 0&#160;°C the optical activity is lost. More evidence followed in the 1960s. A reaction of the <a href="/wiki/Cis_isomer" class="mw-redirect" title="Cis isomer">cis isomer</a> of 2-methylcyclopropyl bromide with sec-butyllithium again followed by <a href="/wiki/Carboxylation" title="Carboxylation">carboxylation</a> with dry ice yielded cis-2-methylcyclopropylcarboxylic acid. The formation of the trans isomer would have indicated that the intermediate carbanion was unstable.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a href="/wiki/File:ChiralcarbanionsII.png" class="image" title="stereochemistry of organolithiums"><img alt="stereochemistry of organolithiums" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/ChiralcarbanionsII.png/500px-ChiralcarbanionsII.png" width="500" height="163" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/ChiralcarbanionsII.png/750px-ChiralcarbanionsII.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/ChiralcarbanionsII.png/1000px-ChiralcarbanionsII.png 2x" data-file-width="1027" data-file-height="334" /></a></div></div>
<p>In the same manner the reaction of (+)-(<i>S</i>)-<i>l</i>-bromo-<i>l</i>-methyl-2,2-diphenylcyclopropane with <i>n</i>-butyllithium followed by quench with <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a> resulted in product with <a href="/wiki/Retention_of_configuration" class="mw-redirect" title="Retention of configuration">retention of configuration</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a href="/wiki/File:ChiralcarbanionsIII.png" class="image" title="Optical Stability of 1-Methyl-2,2-diphenylcyclopropyllithium"><img alt="Optical Stability of 1-Methyl-2,2-diphenylcyclopropyllithium" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/ChiralcarbanionsIII.png/500px-ChiralcarbanionsIII.png" width="500" height="168" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/ChiralcarbanionsIII.png/750px-ChiralcarbanionsIII.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/ChiralcarbanionsIII.png/1000px-ChiralcarbanionsIII.png 2x" data-file-width="1032" data-file-height="347" /></a></div></div>
<p>Of recent date are chiral methyllithium compounds:<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="center"><div class="floatnone"><a href="/wiki/File:PhosphatePhosphonateRearrangement.png" class="image" title="Chiral Oxy[2H1]methyllithiums, Bu stands for butyl, i-Pr stands for isopropyl"><img alt="Chiral Oxy[2H1]methyllithiums, Bu stands for butyl, i-Pr stands for isopropyl" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/PhosphatePhosphonateRearrangement.png/500px-PhosphatePhosphonateRearrangement.png" width="500" height="270" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/PhosphatePhosphonateRearrangement.png/750px-PhosphatePhosphonateRearrangement.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/PhosphatePhosphonateRearrangement.png/1000px-PhosphatePhosphonateRearrangement.png 2x" data-file-width="1054" data-file-height="570" /></a></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Organophosphorus_chemistry" class="mw-redirect" title="Organophosphorus chemistry">phosphate</a> <b>1</b> contains a chiral group with a hydrogen and a <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuterium</a> substituent. The <a href="/wiki/Stannyl" class="mw-redirect" title="Stannyl">stannyl</a> group is replaced by lithium to intermediate <b>2</b> which undergoes a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phosphate-phosphorane_rearrangement&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Phosphate-phosphorane rearrangement (page does not exist)">phosphate-phosphorane rearrangement</a> to <a href="/wiki/Phosphorane" title="Phosphorane">phosphorane</a> <b>3</b> which on reaction with acetic acid gives <a href="/wiki/Alcohol" title="Alcohol">alcohol</a> <b>4</b>. Once again in the range of −78&#160;°C to 0&#160;°C the chirality is preserved in this reaction sequence.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbanion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A carbanionic structure first made an appearance in the reaction mechanism for the <a href="/wiki/Benzoin_condensation" title="Benzoin condensation">benzoin condensation</a> as correctly proposed by Clarke and <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Lapworth" title="Arthur Lapworth">Arthur Lapworth</a> in 1907.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In 1904 <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Schlenk" title="Wilhelm Schlenk">Wilhelm Schlenk</a> prepared Ph<sub>3</sub>C<sup>−</sup>N<span class="chemf nowrap">Me<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">+</sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span></span> in a quest for pentavalent nitrogen (from <a href="/wiki/Tetramethylammonium_chloride" title="Tetramethylammonium chloride">tetramethylammonium chloride</a> and 
<a href="/wiki/Organosodium_chemistry" title="Organosodium chemistry">Ph<sub>3</sub>CNa</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-:1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and in 1914 he demonstrated how triarylmethyl radicals could be reduced to carbanions by alkali metals <sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The phrase carbanion was introduced by Wallis and Adams in 1933 as the negatively charged counterpart of the <a href="/wiki/Carbonium_ion" title="Carbonium ion">carbonium ion</a><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
