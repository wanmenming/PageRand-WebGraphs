<p><b>Iron(III) oxide</b> or <b>ferric oxide</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with the formula Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. It is one of the three main <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxides</a> of <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a>, the other two being <a href="/wiki/Iron(II)_oxide" title="Iron(II) oxide">iron(II) oxide</a> (FeO), which is rare, and <a href="/wiki/Iron(II,III)_oxide" title="Iron(II,III) oxide">iron(II,III) oxide</a> (Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>), which also occurs naturally as the mineral <a href="/wiki/Magnetite" title="Magnetite">magnetite</a>. As the mineral known as <a href="/wiki/Hematite" title="Hematite">hematite</a>, Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is the main source of iron for the steel industry. Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetism" title="Ferromagnetism">ferromagnetic</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> dark red, and readily attacked by acids. Iron(III) oxide is often called <a href="/wiki/Rust" title="Rust">rust</a>, and is acidic in nature and to some extent this label is useful, because rust shares several properties and has a similar composition. To a chemist, rust is considered an ill-defined material, described as <i>hydrated</i> ferric oxide.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> can be obtained in various <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorphs</a>. In the main ones, α and γ, iron adopts octahedral coordination geometry. That is, each Fe center is bound to six oxygen <a href="/wiki/Ligand" title="Ligand">ligands</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alpha_phase">Alpha phase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Alpha phase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>α-Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has the <a href="/wiki/Rhombohedral" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhombohedral">rhombohedral</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">corundum</a> (α-Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) structure and is the most common form. It occurs naturally as the mineral <a href="/wiki/Hematite" title="Hematite">hematite</a> which is mined as the main <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ore</a> of iron. It is <a href="/wiki/Antiferromagnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Antiferromagnetic">antiferromagnetic</a> below ~260 K (<a href="/wiki/Morin_transition" title="Morin transition">Morin transition</a> temperature), and exhibits weak <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetism" title="Ferromagnetism">ferromagnetism</a> between 260 K and the <a href="/wiki/N%C3%A9el_temperature" title="Néel temperature">Néel temperature</a>, 950 K.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> It is easy to prepare using both <a href="/wiki/Thermal_decomposition" title="Thermal decomposition">thermal decomposition</a> and precipitation in the liquid phase. Its magnetic properties are dependent on many factors, e.g. pressure, particle size, and magnetic field intensity.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gamma_phase">Gamma phase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Gamma phase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>γ-Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has a <a href="/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system" title="Cubic crystal system">cubic</a> structure. It is metastable and converted from the alpha phase at high temperatures. It occurs naturally as the mineral maghemite. It is <a href="/wiki/Ferromagnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferromagnetic">ferromagnetic</a> and finds application in recording tapes,<sup id="cite_ref-InorgChem_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-InorgChem-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> although <a href="/wiki/Ultrafine_particles" class="mw-redirect" title="Ultrafine particles">ultrafine particles</a> smaller than 10 nanometers are <a href="/wiki/Superparamagnetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Superparamagnetic">superparamagnetic</a>. It can be prepared by thermal dehydratation of gamma <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide-hydroxide" title="Iron(III) oxide-hydroxide">iron(III) oxide-hydroxide</a>. Another method involves the careful oxidation of <a href="/wiki/Iron(II,III)_oxide" title="Iron(II,III) oxide">iron(II,III) oxide</a> (Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>).<sup id="cite_ref-InorgChem_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-InorgChem-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The ultrafine particles can be prepared by thermal decomposition of <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_oxalate" class="mw-redirect" title="Iron(III) oxalate">iron(III) oxalate</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_phases">Other phases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other phases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several other phases have been identified or claimed. The β-phase is cubic body-centered (space group Ia3), <a href="/wiki/Metastability_in_molecules" class="mw-redirect" title="Metastability in molecules">metastable</a>, and at temperatures above 500&#160;°C (930&#160;°F) converts to alpha phase. It can be prepared by reduction of hematite by carbon, <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">pyrolysis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_chloride" title="Iron(III) chloride">iron(III) chloride</a> solution, or thermal decomposition of <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_sulfate" title="Iron(III) sulfate">iron(III) sulfate</a>. The epsilon phase is rhombic, and shows properties intermediate between alpha and gamma, and may have useful magnetic properties. Preparation of the pure epsilon phase has proven very challenging due to contamination with alpha and gamma phases. Material with a high proportion of epsilon phase can be prepared by thermal transformation of the gamma phase. This phase is also metastable, transforming to the alpha phase at between 500 and 750&#160;°C (930 and 1,380&#160;°F). Can also be prepared by oxidation of iron in an <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc" title="Electric arc">electric arc</a> or by <a href="/wiki/Sol-gel" class="mw-redirect" title="Sol-gel">sol-gel</a> precipitation from <a href="/wiki/Iron(III)_nitrate" title="Iron(III) nitrate">iron(III) nitrate</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Additionally at high pressure an <a href="/wiki/Amorphous" class="mw-redirect" title="Amorphous">amorphous</a> form is claimed.<sup id="cite_ref-atmilab_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atmilab-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Recent research has revealed epsilon iron(III) oxide in ancient Chinese <a href="/wiki/Chinese_ceramics#Jian_tea_wares" title="Chinese ceramics">Jian ceramic</a> glazes, which may provide insight into ways to produce that form in the lab.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Hydrated_iron.28III.29_oxides"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrated_iron(III)_oxides">Hydrated iron(III) oxides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hydrated iron(III) oxides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several hydrates of Iron(III) oxide exists.
When alkali is added to solutions of soluble Fe(III) salts, a red-brown gelatinous precipitate forms. This is <i>not</i> Fe(OH)<sub>3</sub>, but Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>·H<sub>2</sub>O (also written as Fe(O)OH).
Several forms of the hydrated oxide of Fe(III) exist as well. The red <i>lepidocrocite</i> γ-Fe(O)OH, occurs on the outside of <a href="/wiki/Rusticle" title="Rusticle">rusticles</a>, and the orange <i>goethite</i>, which occurs internally in rusticles.
When Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>·H<sub>2</sub>O is heated, it loses its water of hydration. Further heating at 1670 K converts Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> to black Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (Fe<sup>II</sup>Fe<sup>III</sup><sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>), which is known as the mineral <i>magnetite</i>.
Fe(O)OH is soluble in acids, giving [Fe(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3+</sup>. In concentrated aqueous alkali, Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> gives [Fe(OH)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3−</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-InorgChem_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-InorgChem-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactions">Reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most important reaction is its <a href="/wiki/Carbothermal_reduction" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbothermal reduction">carbothermal reduction</a>, which gives iron used in steel-making:
</p>
<dl><dd>Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 3 CO → 2 Fe + 3 CO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>Another redox reaction is the extremely <a href="/wiki/Exothermic" class="mw-redirect" title="Exothermic">exothermic</a> <a href="/wiki/Thermite" title="Thermite">thermite</a> reaction with <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Al + Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> → 2 Fe + Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></dd></dl>
<p>This process is used to weld thick metals such as rails of train tracks by using a ceramic container to funnel the molten iron in between two sections of rail. Thermite is also used in weapons and making small-scale cast-iron sculptures and tools.
</p><p>Partial reduction with hydrogen at about 400&#160;°C produces magnetite, a black magnetic material that contains both Fe(III) and Fe(II):<sup id="cite_ref-Brauer_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brauer-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>3 Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub> → 2 Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>Iron(III) oxide is insoluble in water but dissolves readily in strong acid, e.g. hydrochloric and <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acids</a>. It also dissolves well in solutions of chelating agents such as <a href="/wiki/EDTA" class="mw-redirect" title="EDTA">EDTA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxalic_acid" title="Oxalic acid">oxalic acid</a>.
</p><p>Heating iron(III) oxides with other metal oxides or carbonates yields materials known as <a href="/wiki/Ferrate" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferrate">ferrates</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-Brauer_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brauer-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>ZnO + Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> → Zn(FeO<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation">Preparation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Preparation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Iron(III) oxide is a product of the oxidation of iron. It can be prepared in the laboratory by electrolyzing a solution of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate" title="Sodium bicarbonate">sodium bicarbonate</a>, an inert electrolyte, with an iron anode:
</p>
<dl><dd>4 Fe + 3 O<sub>2</sub> + 2 H<sub>2</sub>O → 4 FeO(OH)</dd></dl>
<p>The resulting hydrated iron(III) oxide, written here as Fe(O)OH, dehydrates around 200&#160;°C.<sup id="cite_ref-Brauer_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brauer-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 FeO(OH) → Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iron_industry">Iron industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Iron industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The overwhelming application of iron(III) oxide is as the feedstock of the steel and iron industries, e.g. the production of iron, steel, and many alloys.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polishing">Polishing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Polishing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A very fine powder of ferric oxide is known as "jeweler's rouge", "red rouge", or simply rouge. It is used to put the final polish on metallic <a href="/wiki/Jewellery" title="Jewellery">jewelry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">lenses</a>, and historically as a <a href="/wiki/Rouge_(cosmetics)" title="Rouge (cosmetics)">cosmetic</a>. Rouge cuts more slowly than some modern polishes, such as <a href="/wiki/Cerium(IV)_oxide" title="Cerium(IV) oxide">cerium(IV) oxide</a>, but is still used in optics fabrication and by jewelers for the superior finish it can produce. When polishing gold, the rouge slightly stains the gold, which contributes to the appearance of the finished piece. Rouge is sold as a powder, paste, laced on polishing cloths, or solid bar (with a <a href="/wiki/Wax" title="Wax">wax</a> or <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">grease</a> binder). Other polishing compounds are also often called "rouge", even when they do not contain iron oxide. Jewelers remove the residual rouge on jewelry by use of <a href="/wiki/Ultrasonic_cleaning" title="Ultrasonic cleaning">ultrasonic cleaning</a>. Products sold as "<a href="/wiki/Stropping_(blade)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stropping (blade)">stropping</a> compound" are often applied to a <a href="/wiki/Razor_strop" title="Razor strop">leather strop</a> to assist in getting a razor edge on knives, straight razors, or any other edged tool.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pigment">Pigment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Pigment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r856303569">@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .tmulti>.thumbinner{width:100%!important;max-width:none!important}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .tsingle{float:none!important;max-width:none!important;width:100%!important;text-align:center}}</style><div class="thumb tmulti tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:406px;max-width:406px"><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:222px;max-width:222px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:Iron_oxide_red_y.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Iron_oxide_red_y.jpg/220px-Iron_oxide_red_y.jpg" width="220" height="133" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Iron_oxide_red_y.jpg/330px-Iron_oxide_red_y.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Iron_oxide_red_y.jpg/440px-Iron_oxide_red_y.jpg 2x" data-file-width="536" data-file-height="325" /></a></div></div><div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:180px;max-width:180px"><div class="thumbimage"><a href="/wiki/File:Iron_oxide_yellow.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Iron_oxide_yellow.jpg/178px-Iron_oxide_yellow.jpg" width="178" height="133" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Iron_oxide_yellow.jpg/267px-Iron_oxide_yellow.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Iron_oxide_yellow.jpg/356px-Iron_oxide_yellow.jpg 2x" data-file-width="528" data-file-height="394" /></a></div></div><div style="clear:left"></div><div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left;text-align:left;background-color:transparent">Two different colors at different hydrate phase (α = red, β = yellow) of iron(III) oxide hydrate;<sup id="cite_ref-doc00_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-doc00-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> they are useful as pigments.</div></div></div>
<p>Iron(III) oxide is also used as a <a href="/wiki/Pigment" title="Pigment">pigment</a>, under names "Pigment Brown 6", "Pigment Brown 7", and "Pigment Red 101".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Some of them, e.g. Pigment Red 101 and Pigment Brown 6, are approved by the US <a href="/wiki/Food_and_Drug_Administration" title="Food and Drug Administration">Food and Drug Administration</a> (FDA) for use in cosmetics. <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxides" class="mw-redirect" title="Iron oxides">Iron oxides</a> are used as pigments in dental composites alongside titanium oxides.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hematite is the characteristic component of the Swedish paint color <a href="/wiki/Falu_red" title="Falu red">Falu red</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_recording">Magnetic recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Magnetic recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Iron(III) oxide was the most common <a href="/wiki/Magnet" title="Magnet">magnetic</a> particle used in all types of <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_storage" title="Magnetic storage">magnetic storage and recording</a> media, including magnetic disks (for data storage) and <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_tape" title="Magnetic tape">magnetic tape</a> (used in audio and video recording as well as data storage). Its use in computer disks was superseded by cobalt alloy, enabling thinner magnetic films with higher storage density.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Photocatalysis">Photocatalysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Photocatalysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>α-Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> has been studied as a <a href="/wiki/Photoanode" class="mw-redirect" title="Photoanode">photoanode</a> for solar water oxidation.<sup id="cite_ref-Kay2006_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kay2006-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> However, its efficacy is limited by a short diffusion length (2-4&#160;nm) of photo-excited charge carriers<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and subsequent fast <a href="/wiki/Electron-hole_recombination" class="mw-redirect" title="Electron-hole recombination">recombination</a>, requiring a large <a href="/wiki/Overpotential" title="Overpotential">overpotential</a> to drive the reaction.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Research has been focused on improving the water oxidation performance of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> using nanostructuring,<sup id="cite_ref-Kay2006_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kay2006-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> surface functionalization,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> or by employing alternate crystal phases such as β-Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicine">Medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iron(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A mixture of zinc oxide with about 0.5% iron(III) oxide is called <a href="/wiki/Calamine" title="Calamine">calamine</a>, which is the active ingredient of calamine lotion.
</p>
