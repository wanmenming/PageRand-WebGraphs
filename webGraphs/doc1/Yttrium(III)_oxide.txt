<p><b>Yttrium oxide</b>, also known as <b>yttria</b>, is <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">Y</a><sub>2</sub><a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">O</a><sub>3</sub>. It is an air-stable, white solid substance. <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">Yttrium</a> oxide is used as a common starting material for both <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">materials science</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compounds</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a> of yttrium oxide is 27 W/(m·K).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yttrium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phosphors">Phosphors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yttrium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phosphors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Yttria is widely used to make <a href="/wiki/Yttrium_orthovanadate" title="Yttrium orthovanadate">Eu:YVO<sub>4</sub></a> and Eu:Y<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> <a href="/wiki/Phosphors" class="mw-redirect" title="Phosphors">phosphors</a> that give the red color in color TV picture tubes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yttria_lasers">Yttria lasers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yttrium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Yttria lasers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Y<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is a prospective <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_laser" title="Solid-state laser">solid-state laser</a> material. In particular, lasers with <a href="/wiki/Ytterbium" title="Ytterbium">ytterbium</a> as <a href="/wiki/Dopant" title="Dopant">dopant</a> allow the efficient operation both in <a href="/wiki/Continuous_wave" title="Continuous wave">continuous operation</a><sup id="cite_ref-cw_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cw-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
and in pulsed regimes.<sup id="cite_ref-pulsed_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pulsed-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
At high concentration of excitations (of order of 1%) and poor cooling, the quenching of emission at laser frequency and avalanche broadband emission takes place.<sup id="cite_ref-bi_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bi-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> (Yttria-based lasers are not to be confused with YAG lasers using <a href="/wiki/Yttrium_aluminum_garnet" class="mw-redirect" title="Yttrium aluminum garnet">yttrium aluminum garnet</a>, a widely used crystal host for rare earth laser dopants).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gas_Lighting">Gas Lighting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yttrium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gas Lighting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The original use of the mineral yttria and the purpose of its extraction from mineral sources was as part of the process of making gas mantles and other products for turning the flames of artificially-produced gases (initially hydrogen, later coal gas, paraffin, or other products) into human-visible light. This use is almost obsolete - thorium and cerium oxides are larger components of such products these days.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dental_ceramics">Dental ceramics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yttrium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dental ceramics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Yttrium oxide is used to stabilize the <a href="/wiki/Yttria-stabilized_zirconia" title="Yttria-stabilized zirconia">Zirconia</a> in late-generation porcelain-free metal-free dental ceramics. This is a very hard ceramic used as a strong base material in some full ceramic restorations.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  The zirconia used in dentistry is <a href="/wiki/Zirconium_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Zirconium oxide">zirconium oxide</a> which has been stabilized with the addition of <a href="/wiki/Yttrium_oxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Yttrium oxide">yttrium oxide</a>.  The full name of zirconia used in dentistry is "yttria-stabilized zirconia" or YSZ.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Microwave_filters">Microwave filters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yttrium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Microwave filters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Yttrium oxide is also used to make <a href="/wiki/Yttrium_iron_garnet" title="Yttrium iron garnet">yttrium iron garnets</a>, which are very effective <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">microwave</a> filters.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Superconductors">Superconductors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yttrium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Superconductors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Y<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is used to make the <a href="/wiki/High_temperature_superconductor" class="mw-redirect" title="High temperature superconductor">high temperature superconductor</a> YBa<sub>2</sub>Cu<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>, known as "1-2-3" to indicate the ratio of the metal constituents:
</p>
<dl><dd>2 Y<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> + 8 BaO + 12 CuO + O<sub>2</sub> → 4 YBa<sub>2</sub>Cu<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub></dd></dl>
<p>This synthesis is typically conducted at 800&#160;°C.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inorganic_synthesis">Inorganic synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yttrium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Inorganic synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Yttrium oxide is an important starting point for inorganic compounds. For organometallic chemistry it is converted to <a href="/wiki/Yttrium(III)_chloride" title="Yttrium(III) chloride">YCl<sub>3</sub></a> in a reaction with concentrated <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">hydrochloric acid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_chloride" title="Ammonium chloride">ammonium chloride</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_occurrence">Natural occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yttrium(III)_oxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Natural occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Yttriaite-(Y)" title="Yttriaite-(Y)">Yttriaite-(Y)</a>, approved as a new mineral species in 2010, is the natural form of yttria. It is exceedingly rare, occurring as inclusions in native <a href="/wiki/Tungsten" title="Tungsten">tungsten</a> particles in a <a href="/wiki/Placer_deposit" title="Placer deposit">placer deposit</a> of the Bol’shaja Pol’ja (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">Большая Полья</span>) river, Prepolar <a href="/wiki/Ural_(region)" title="Ural (region)">Ural</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a>. As a chemical component of other minerals, the oxide yttria was first isolated in 1789 by <a href="/wiki/Johan_Gadolin" title="Johan Gadolin">Johan Gadolin</a>, from rare-earth minerals in a mine at the Swedish town of <a href="/wiki/Ytterby" title="Ytterby">Ytterby</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
