<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Spring (hydrology)</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Big_Spring_Missouri_1-02Aug08.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Big_Spring_Missouri_1-02Aug08.jpg/220px-Big_Spring_Missouri_1-02Aug08.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Big_Spring_Missouri_1-02Aug08.jpg/330px-Big_Spring_Missouri_1-02Aug08.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Big_Spring_Missouri_1-02Aug08.jpg/440px-Big_Spring_Missouri_1-02Aug08.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1552" data-file-height="1034" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Big_Spring_Missouri_1-02Aug08.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>On an average day nearly 303&#160;million US gallons (1,150,000&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of water flow from <a href="/wiki/Big_Spring_(Missouri)" title="Big Spring (Missouri)">Big Spring</a> in <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a> at a rate of 469 cubic feet per second (13.3&#160;m<sup>3</sup>/s).</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>spring </b> is a point at which water flows from an <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a> to the Earth's surface. It is a component of the <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">hydrosphere</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_(hydrology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nacentemackinac.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Nacentemackinac.jpg/170px-Nacentemackinac.jpg" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Nacentemackinac.jpg/255px-Nacentemackinac.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Nacentemackinac.jpg/340px-Nacentemackinac.jpg 2x" data-file-width="594" data-file-height="792" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nacentemackinac.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A natural spring on <a href="/wiki/Mackinac_Island" title="Mackinac Island">Mackinac Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a></div></div></div>
<p>A spring may be the result of <a href="/wiki/Karst_topography" class="mw-redirect" title="Karst topography">karst topography</a> where surface water has infiltrated the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s surface (recharge area), becoming part of the area <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>. The groundwater then travels through a network of cracks and fissure—openings ranging from intergranular spaces to large <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">caves</a>. The water eventually emerges from below the surface, in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Karst_spring" title="Karst spring">karst spring</a>.
</p><p>The forcing of the spring to the surface can be the result of a confined <a href="/wiki/Aquifer" title="Aquifer">aquifer</a> in which the recharge area of the spring water table rests at a higher elevation than that of the outlet. Spring water forced to the surface by elevated sources are <a href="/wiki/Artesian_aquifer" title="Artesian aquifer">artesian wells</a>. This is possible even if the outlet is in the form of a 300-foot-deep (91&#160;m) cave. In this case the cave is used like a hose by the higher elevated recharge area of groundwater to exit through the lower elevation opening.
</p><p>Non-artesian springs may simply flow from a higher elevation through the earth to a lower elevation and exit in the form of a spring, using the ground like a drainage pipe.
</p><p>Still other springs are the result of pressure from an underground source in the earth, in the form of <a href="/wiki/Volcanic" class="mw-redirect" title="Volcanic">volcanic</a> activity. The result can be water at elevated temperature such as a <a href="/wiki/Hot_spring" title="Hot spring">hot spring</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MiddleSpring.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/MiddleSpring.JPG/220px-MiddleSpring.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/MiddleSpring.JPG/330px-MiddleSpring.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/MiddleSpring.JPG/440px-MiddleSpring.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MiddleSpring.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sunrise at Middle Spring, <a href="/wiki/Fish_Springs_National_Wildlife_Refuge" title="Fish Springs National Wildlife Refuge">Fish Springs National Wildlife Refuge</a>, Utah</div></div></div>
<p>The action of the groundwater continually dissolves permeable bedrock such as <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dolomite" title="Dolomite">dolomite</a>, creating vast cave systems.<sup id="cite_ref-USGS_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_(hydrology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Seep_(hydrology)" title="Seep (hydrology)">Seepage</a> or filtration spring. The term seep refers to springs with small flow rates in which the source water has filtered through permeable earth.</li>
<li>Fracture springs, discharge from faults, joints, or fissures in the earth, in which springs have followed a natural course of voids or weaknesses in the bedrock.</li>
<li>Tubular springs, in which the water flows from underground caverns.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flow">Flow</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_(hydrology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Flow">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Spring discharge, or <b>resurgence</b>, is determined by the spring's recharge basin. Factors that affect the recharge include the size of the area in which groundwater is captured, the amount of precipitation, the size of capture points, and the size of the spring outlet.  Water may leak into the underground system from many sources including permeable earth, sinkholes, and <a href="/wiki/Losing_stream" title="Losing stream">losing streams</a>.  In some cases entire <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">creeks</a> seemingly disappear as the water sinks into the ground via the stream bed. <a href="/wiki/Grand_Gulf_State_Park_(Missouri)" title="Grand Gulf State Park (Missouri)">Grand Gulf State Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a> is an example of an entire creek vanishing into the groundwater system.  The water emerges 9 miles (14&#160;km) away, forming some of the discharge of <a href="/wiki/Mammoth_Spring" title="Mammoth Spring">Mammoth Spring</a> in <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a>. Human activity may also affect a spring's discharge—withdrawal of groundwater reduces the water pressure in an aquifer, decreasing the volume of flow.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_(hydrology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:La_Sorgue,_Fontaine-de-Vaucluse.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/La_Sorgue%2C_Fontaine-de-Vaucluse.JPG/220px-La_Sorgue%2C_Fontaine-de-Vaucluse.JPG" width="220" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/La_Sorgue%2C_Fontaine-de-Vaucluse.JPG/330px-La_Sorgue%2C_Fontaine-de-Vaucluse.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/La_Sorgue%2C_Fontaine-de-Vaucluse.JPG/440px-La_Sorgue%2C_Fontaine-de-Vaucluse.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2576" data-file-height="1952" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:La_Sorgue,_Fontaine-de-Vaucluse.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Fontaine_de_Vaucluse_(spring)" title="Fontaine de Vaucluse (spring)">Fontaine de Vaucluse</a> or Spring of Vaucluse in France discharges about 470&#160;million US gallons (1,800,000&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of water per day at a rate of 727 cubic feet (21 m<sup>3</sup>) per second.</div></div></div>
<p>Springs are often classified by the volume of the water they discharge. The largest springs are called "first-magnitude", defined as springs that discharge water at a rate of at least 2800 liters or 100 cubic feet (2.8&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of water per second.  Some locations contain many first-magnitude springs, such as <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> where there are at least 27 known to be that size; the <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a> <a href="/wiki/Ozarks" title="Ozarks">Ozarks</a>, which contain 10<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  known of first-magnitude; and 11<sup id="cite_ref-ui_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ui-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> more in the <a href="/wiki/Thousand_Springs_State_Park" title="Thousand Springs State Park">Thousand Springs</a> area along the <a href="/wiki/Snake_River" title="Snake River">Snake River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a>. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Water_content">Water content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_(hydrology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Water content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mineral_spring" title="Mineral spring">Mineral spring</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PruessLake.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/PruessLake.JPG/220px-PruessLake.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/PruessLake.JPG/330px-PruessLake.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/PruessLake.JPG/440px-PruessLake.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PruessLake.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Pruess_Lake" title="Pruess Lake">Pruess Lake</a> is spring-fed in the arid <a href="/wiki/Snake_Valley_(Great_Basin)" title="Snake Valley (Great Basin)">Snake Valley</a> of <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">Minerals</a> become dissolved in the water as it moves through the underground <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rocks</a>. This may give the water flavor and even <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> bubbles, depending on the nature of the <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geology</a> through which it passes. This is why spring water is often bottled and sold as <a href="/wiki/Mineral_water" title="Mineral water">mineral water</a>, although the term is often the subject of <a href="/wiki/Deception" title="Deception">deceptive</a> <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertising</a>. Springs that contain significant amounts of minerals are sometimes called '<a href="/wiki/Mineral_springs" class="mw-redirect" title="Mineral springs">mineral springs</a>'. (Springs without such mineral content, meanwhile, are sometimes distinguished as 'sweet springs'.) Springs that contain large amounts of dissolved <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> <a href="/wiki/Salts" class="mw-redirect" title="Salts">salts</a>, mostly <a href="/wiki/Sodium_carbonate" title="Sodium carbonate">sodium carbonate</a>, are called 'soda springs'.  Many resorts have developed around mineral springs and are known as <a href="/wiki/Spa_towns" class="mw-redirect" title="Spa towns">spa towns</a>.
</p><p>Water from springs is usually clear. However some springs may be colored by the minerals that are dissolved in the water. For instance, water heavy with iron or <a href="/wiki/Tannins" class="mw-redirect" title="Tannins">tannins</a> will have an orange color.<sup id="cite_ref-USGS_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>In parts of the United States a <a href="/wiki/Stream" title="Stream">stream</a> carrying the outflow of a spring to a nearby primary stream may be called a <b>spring branch</b> or run.  Groundwater tends to maintain a relatively long-term average temperature of its aquifer; so flow from a spring may be cooler than a summer day, but remain unfrozen in the winter.  The cool water of a spring and its branch may harbor species such as certain <a href="/wiki/Trout" title="Trout">trout</a> that are otherwise ill-suited to a warmer local <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_(hydrology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Springs have been used for a variety of human needs including drinking water, domestic water supply, irrigation, <a href="/wiki/Watermill" title="Watermill">mills</a>, navigation, and <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">electricity generation</a>.  Other modern uses include recreational activities such as fishing, swimming, and floating; <a href="/wiki/Hot_springs" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot springs">therapy</a>; water for livestock; fish hatcheries; and supply for bottled <a href="/wiki/Mineral_water" title="Mineral water">mineral water</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sacred_springs">Sacred springs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spring_(hydrology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sacred springs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Holy_well" title="Holy well">Holy well</a></div>
<p>A sacred spring, or holy well, is a small body of water emerging from underground and revered either in a <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">Christian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">pagan</a> or other religious context, sometimes both.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The lore and mythology of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a> was replete with sacred and storied springs—notably, the <a href="/wiki/Corycian_nymphs" class="mw-redirect" title="Corycian nymphs">Corycian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierian_Spring" title="Pierian Spring">Pierian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Castalian_Spring" title="Castalian Spring">Castalian</a>. In medieval Europe, holy wells were frequently pagan sacred sites that later became <a href="/wiki/Christianization" title="Christianization">Christianized</a>. The term "holy well" is commonly employed to refer to any water source of limited size (i.e. not a lake or river, but including pools and natural springs and seeps), which has some significance in local <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a>. This can take the form of a particular name, an associated <a href="/wiki/Legend" title="Legend">legend</a>, the attribution of healing qualities to the water through the <a href="/wiki/Numen" title="Numen">numinous</a> presence of its guardian spirit or Christian saint, or a ceremony or ritual centred on the well site. In Christian legend, the spring water is often said to have been made to flow by the action of a saint, a familiar theme especially in the <a href="/wiki/Hagiography" title="Hagiography">hagiography</a> of Celtic saints.
</p>
