<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Population momentum</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Population momentum</b> is a typical consequence of the demographic transition.  Even if a high-fertility, high-growth population experiences an immediate drop in fertility to <a href="/wiki/Replacement_rate" class="mw-redirect" title="Replacement rate">replacement rate</a>, that population will continue to grow for several decades.  Eventually, because the new fertility rate is replacement, the population achieves equilibrium at some new level.  Population momentum is defined as the ratio of the size of the population at that new equilibrium level to the size of the initial population.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Population momentum usually occurs in populations that are growing.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Example">Example</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_momentum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Example">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Assume that a population has three generations: First (oldest), Second (child bearing), and Third (children).  Further assume that this population has a fertility rate equal to four (4).  That is, each generation is twice the size of the previous.  If the population of the first generation is arbitrarily set at 100, the second is then 200, and the third is 400.  The spreadsheet below shows the initial population in the first row.

<p>First note that the second and third generation of the initial population are each twice the size of the previous.  The total of the initial population is 700 = 100 + 200 + 400.
</p><p>Then assume that at the end of the third generation, fertility falls to replacement (for simplicity assume that to be two). Now take the population forward in time to the next generation, line two of the spreadsheet. The first generation dies, and the new generation, the fourth, is equal to the third (because now fertility is replacement).  Repeat the process again to reach the fifth generation (line 3 in the spreadsheet). The fifth generation is again equal to the fourth and now the population’s three generations are equal, and the population has reached equilibrium.
</p><p>The initial population has grown from 700 to 1,200 even though fertility dropped from four to replacement (two) at the end of the third generation.  Population momentum carried the population to higher levels over the next two generations.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_steps_to_zero_population_growth">Further steps to zero population growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_momentum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Further steps to zero population growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Population momentum impacts the immediate birth and <a href="/wiki/Death_rates" class="mw-redirect" title="Death rates">death rates</a> in the population that determine the natural rate of growth. However, for the a population to have an absolute zero amount of natural growth, the US National Library of Medicine National Institutes of Health suggests that three things must occur.
</p><p>1. Fertility rates must level off to the <a href="/wiki/Replacement_rate" class="mw-redirect" title="Replacement rate">replacement rate</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Net_reproduction_rate" title="Net reproduction rate">net reproduction rate</a> should be 1). If the fertility rate remains higher than the replacement rate, the population would continue to grow.
</p><p>2. <a href="/wiki/Mortality_rate" title="Mortality rate">Mortality rate</a> must stop declining, that is, it must remain constant.
</p><p>3. Lastly, the age structure must adjust to the new rates of fertility and mortality. This last step takes the longest to complete.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implications_of_Population_Momentum">Implications of Population Momentum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_momentum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Implications of Population Momentum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Population momentum has implications for population policy for a number of reasons.
</p><p>1. With respect to high-fertility countries (for example in the <a href="/wiki/Developing_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing world">developing world</a>), a positive population momentum, meaning that the population is increasing, states that these countries will continue to grow despite large and rapid declines in fertility.
</p><p>2. With respect to <a href="/wiki/Total_fertility_rate" title="Total fertility rate">lowest-low fertility</a> countries (for example in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>), a negative population momentum implies that these countries may experience population decline even if they try to increase their rate of fertility to the replacement rate of 2.1. For example, some <a href="/wiki/Eastern_European" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern European">Eastern European</a> countries show a population shrinkage even if their birth rates recovered to replacement level. Population momentum can become negative if the fertility rate is under replacement level for a long period of time.
</p><p>3. Population momentum shows that replacement level fertility is a long-term concept rather than an indication of current population growth rates. Depending on the extant age structure, a fertility rate of two children per woman may correspond to short-term growth or decline.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>4. Another implication of population momentum is its effect on the labor force; more specifically the distribution of the labor force[1]. We established earlier that population momentum occurs when a population continues to grow despite the replacement fertility level being met. Meaning that there are more children being born. When there are more children being birthed for that time being until the child is able to work they become dependents meaning that they don't do much to "contribute" to their society. Which leads to an unfavorabale dependency ratio. When the children reach the age to work that countries dependency ratio will change and become favorable.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Population_momentum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Population momentum is typically caused by a shift in the country's <a href="/wiki/Demographic_transition" title="Demographic transition">demographic transition</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> When <a href="/wiki/Mortality_rate" title="Mortality rate">mortality rates</a> drop, the young survive childhood and the aging population live longer. <a href="/wiki/Total_fertility_rate" title="Total fertility rate">Fertility rates</a> remain high, causing the overall population size to grow.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> According to population momentum, even if high fertility rates were immediately replaced with replacement level fertility rates, the population would continue to grow due to the pre-childbearing population entering childbearing years.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
