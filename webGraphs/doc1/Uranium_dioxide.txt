<p><b>Uranium dioxide</b> or <b>uranium(IV) oxide (<a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">U</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>2</sub>)</b>, also known as <b>urania</b> or <b>uranous oxide</b>, is an <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> of <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>, and is a black, <a href="/wiki/Radioactivity" class="mw-redirect" title="Radioactivity">radioactive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystalline</a> powder that naturally occurs in the mineral <a href="/wiki/Uraninite" title="Uraninite">uraninite</a>. It is used in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuel</a> rods in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactors" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear reactors">nuclear reactors</a>. A mixture of uranium and <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a> dioxides is used as <a href="/wiki/MOX_fuel" title="MOX fuel">MOX fuel</a>. Prior to 1960, it was used as yellow and black color in <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a> <a href="/wiki/Ceramic_glaze" title="Ceramic glaze">glazes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glass" title="Glass">glass</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Uranium dioxide is produced by <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reducing</a> <a href="/wiki/Uranium_trioxide" title="Uranium trioxide">uranium trioxide</a> with <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd>UO<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub> → UO<sub>2</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O at 700&#160;°C (973&#160;K)</dd></dl>
<p>This reaction plays an important part in the creation of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuel</a> through <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reprocessing" title="Nuclear reprocessing">nuclear reprocessing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uranium_enrichment" class="mw-redirect" title="Uranium enrichment">uranium enrichment</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemistry">Chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The solid is <a href="/wiki/Isostructural" title="Isostructural">isostructural</a> with (has the same structure as) <a href="/wiki/Fluorite" title="Fluorite">fluorite</a> (<a href="/wiki/Calcium_fluoride" title="Calcium fluoride">calcium fluoride</a>), where each U is surrounded by eight O nearest neighbors in a cubic arrangement. In addition, the dioxides of <a href="/wiki/Cerium(IV)_oxide" title="Cerium(IV) oxide">cerium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neptunium" title="Neptunium">neptunium</a> have the same structures.  No other elemental dioxides have the fluorite structure. Upon melting, the measured average U-O coordination reduces from 8 in the crystalline solid (UO<sub>8</sub> cubes), down to 6.7±0.5 (at 3270 K) in the melt.<sup id="cite_ref-Skinner2014_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Skinner2014-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Models consistent with these measurements show the melt to consist mainly of UO<sub>6</sub> and UO<sub>7</sub> polyhedral units, where roughly &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>2</sup>&#8260;<sub>3</sub></span> of the connections between polyhedra are corner sharing and &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>3</sub></span> are edge sharing.<sup id="cite_ref-Skinner2014_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Skinner2014-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oxidation">Oxidation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oxidation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Uranium dioxide is <a href="/wiki/Oxidized" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxidized">oxidized</a> in contact with <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Triuranium_octaoxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Triuranium octaoxide">triuranium octaoxide</a>.
</p>
<dl><dd>3 UO<sub>2</sub> + O<sub>2</sub> → U<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub> at 700&#160;°C (970&#160;K)</dd></dl>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">electrochemistry</a> of uranium dioxide has been investigated in detail as the <a href="/wiki/Galvanic_corrosion" title="Galvanic corrosion">galvanic corrosion</a> of uranium dioxide controls the rate at which used <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuel</a> dissolves. See <a href="/wiki/Spent_nuclear_fuel" title="Spent nuclear fuel">spent nuclear fuel</a> for further details. <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">Water</a> increases the oxidation rate of <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> metals.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbonization">Carbonization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Carbonization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Uranium dioxide is <a href="/wiki/Carbonized" title="Carbonized">carbonized</a> in contact with <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, forming <a href="/wiki/Uranium_carbide" title="Uranium carbide">uranium carbide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>.
</p><p>UO<sub>2</sub> + 4 C → UC<sub>2</sub> + 2 CO
</p><p>This process must be done under an <a href="/wiki/Inert_gas" title="Inert gas">inert gas</a> as <a href="/wiki/Uranium_carbide" title="Uranium carbide">uranium carbide</a> is easily oxidized back into <a href="/wiki/Uranium_oxide" title="Uranium oxide">uranium oxide</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_fuel">Nuclear fuel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nuclear fuel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>UO<sub>2</sub> is used mainly as <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuel</a>, specifically as UO<sub>2</sub> or as a mixture of UO<sub>2</sub> and PuO<sub>2</sub> (<a href="/wiki/Plutonium_dioxide" class="mw-redirect" title="Plutonium dioxide">plutonium dioxide</a>) called a mixed oxide (<a href="/wiki/MOX_fuel" title="MOX fuel">MOX fuel</a>), in the form of <a href="/wiki/Fuel_rod" class="mw-redirect" title="Fuel rod">fuel rods</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactors</a>.
</p><p>Note that the <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conductivity" title="Thermal conductivity">thermal conductivity</a> of uranium dioxide is very low when compared with <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uranium_nitride" title="Uranium nitride">uranium nitride</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uranium_carbide" title="Uranium carbide">uranium carbide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zirconium" title="Zirconium">zirconium</a> cladding material. This low thermal conductivity can result in localised overheating in the centres of fuel pellets. The graph below shows the different temperature gradients in different fuel compounds. For these fuels the thermal power density is the same and the diameter of all the pellets are the same.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <p>Uranium oxide (urania) was used to color glass and ceramics prior to World War II, and until the applications of radioactivity were discovered this was its main use. In 1958 the military in both the USA and Europe allowed its commercial use again as depleted uranium, and its use began again on a more limited scale. Urania-based ceramic glazes are dark green or black when fired in a reduction or when  UO<sub>2</sub> is used; more commonly it is used in oxidation to produce bright yellow, orange and red glazes.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Orange-colored <a href="/wiki/Fiestaware" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiestaware">Fiestaware</a> is a well-known example of a product with a urania-colored glaze.  <a href="/wiki/Uranium_glass" title="Uranium glass">Uranium glass</a> is pale green to yellow, and often has strong fluorescent properties. Urania has also been used in formulations of <a href="/wiki/Vitreous_enamel" title="Vitreous enamel">enamel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Porcelain" title="Porcelain">porcelain</a>. It is possible to determine with a <a href="/wiki/Geiger_counter" title="Geiger counter">Geiger counter</a> if a glaze or glass produced before 1958 contains urania.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_use">Other use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Prior to the realisation of the harmfulness of radiation, uranium was included in false teeth and dentures, as its slight fluorescence made the dentures appear more like real teeth in a variety of lighting conditions.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Depleted_uranium" title="Depleted uranium">Depleted</a> UO<sub>2</sub> (DUO<sub>2</sub>) can be used as a material for <a href="/wiki/Radiation_shielding" class="mw-redirect" title="Radiation shielding">radiation shielding</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/DUCRETE" class="mw-redirect" title="DUCRETE">DUCRETE</a> is a "heavy <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a>" material where <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a> is replaced with uranium dioxide aggregate; this material is investigated for use for <a href="/wiki/Cask" class="mw-redirect" title="Cask">casks</a> for <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_waste" title="Radioactive waste">radioactive waste</a>. Casks can be also made of DUO<sub>2</sub>-<a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> <a href="/wiki/Cermet" title="Cermet">cermet</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite material</a> made of an <a href="/wiki/Aggregate_(composite)" title="Aggregate (composite)">aggregate</a> of uranium dioxide serving as radiation shielding, <a href="/wiki/Graphite" title="Graphite">graphite</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Silicon_carbide" title="Silicon carbide">silicon carbide</a> serving as <a href="/wiki/Neutron_radiation" title="Neutron radiation">neutron radiation</a> absorber and moderator, and steel as the matrix, whose high thermal conductivity allows easy removal of decay heat.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Depleted uranium dioxide can be also used as a <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a>, e.g. for degradation of <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">volatile organic compounds</a> in gaseous phase, <a href="/wiki/Oxidation" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> to <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>, and removal of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a> from <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a>. It has high efficiency and long-term stability when used to destroy VOCs when compared with some of the commercial <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">precious metals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">TiO<sub>2</sub></a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cobalt_oxide" title="Cobalt oxide">Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub></a> catalysts. Much research is being done in this area, DU being favoured for the uranium component due to its low radioactivity.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The use of uranium dioxide as a material for <a href="/wiki/Rechargeable_battery" title="Rechargeable battery">rechargeable batteries</a> is being investigated. The batteries could have high <a href="/wiki/Power_density" title="Power density">power density</a> and potential of 4.7 V per cell. Another investigated application is in <a href="/wiki/Photoelectrochemical_cell" title="Photoelectrochemical cell">photoelectrochemical cells</a> for solar-assisted hydrogen production where UO<sub>2</sub> is used as a <a href="/wiki/Photoanode" class="mw-redirect" title="Photoanode">photoanode</a>. In earlier times, uranium dioxide was also used as heat conductor for current limitation (URDOX-resistor), which was the first use of its semiconductor properties.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Uranium dioxide is also the strongest known piezomagnet in the antiferromagnetic state observed at cryogenic temperatures below 30 <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">kelvins</a>. UO<sub>2</sub> displays a linear magnetostriction that changes sign with the sign of the applied magnetic field, and magnetoelastic memory switching at magnetic fields near 180,000 Oe.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Semiconductor_properties">Semiconductor properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Semiconductor properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Band_gap" title="Band gap">band gap</a> of uranium dioxide is comparable to these of <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gallium_arsenide" title="Gallium arsenide">gallium arsenide</a>, near the optimum for efficiency vs band gap curve for absorption of solar radiation, suggesting its possible use for very efficient <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">solar cells</a> based on <a href="/wiki/Schottky_diode" title="Schottky diode">Schottky diode</a> structure; it also absorbs at five different wavelengths, including infrared, further enhancing its efficiency. Its intrinsic conductivity at room temperature is about the same as of <a href="/wiki/Single_crystal" title="Single crystal">single crystal</a> silicon.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Dielectric_constant" class="mw-redirect" title="Dielectric constant">dielectric constant</a> of uranium dioxide is about 22, which is almost twice as high as of silicon (11.2) and GaAs (14.1). This is an advantage over Si and GaAs in construction of <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a>, as it may allow higher density integration with higher <a href="/wiki/Breakdown_voltage" title="Breakdown voltage">breakdown voltages</a> and with lower susceptibility to the <a href="/wiki/CMOS" title="CMOS">CMOS</a> <a href="/wiki/Quantum_tunneling" class="mw-redirect" title="Quantum tunneling">tunneling</a> breakdown.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Seebeck_coefficient" title="Seebeck coefficient">Seebeck coefficient</a> of uranium dioxide at room temperature is about 750 µV/K, a value significantly higher than the 270 µV/K of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium_tin_telluride&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Thallium tin telluride (page does not exist)">thallium tin telluride</a> (Tl<sub>2</sub>SnTe<sub>5</sub>) and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thallium_germanium_telluride&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Thallium germanium telluride (page does not exist)">thallium germanium telluride</a> (Tl<sub>2</sub>GeTe<sub>5</sub>) and of <a href="/wiki/Bismuth" title="Bismuth">bismuth</a>-<a href="/wiki/Tellurium" title="Tellurium">tellurium</a> alloys, other materials promising for <a href="/wiki/Thermoelectric_generator" title="Thermoelectric generator">thermoelectric power generation</a> applications and <a href="/wiki/Peltier_element" class="mw-redirect" title="Peltier element">Peltier elements</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> impact of the <sup>235</sup>U and <sup>238</sup>U on its semiconducting properties was not measured as of  2005<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Uranium_dioxide&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>. Due to the slow decay rate of these isotopes, it should not meaningfully influence the properties of uranium dioxide solar cells and thermoelectric devices, but it may become an important factor for <a href="/wiki/Very-large-scale_integration" title="Very-large-scale integration">VLSI</a> chips. Use of <a href="/wiki/Depleted_uranium" title="Depleted uranium">depleted uranium</a> oxide is necessary for this reason. The capture of alpha particles emitted during radioactive decay as helium atoms in the crystal lattice may also cause gradual long-term changes in its properties.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Stoichiometry" title="Stoichiometry">stoichiometry</a> of the material dramatically influences its electrical properties. For example, the electrical conductivity of UO<sub>1.994</sub> is orders of magnitude lower at higher temperatures than the conductivity of UO<sub>2.001</sub>.
</p><p>Uranium dioxide, like U<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub>, is a <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramic</a> material capable of withstanding high temperatures (about 2300&#160;°C, in comparison with at most 200&#160;°C for silicon or GaAs), making it suitable for high-temperature applications like thermophotovoltaic devices.
</p><p>Uranium dioxide is also resistant to <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> damage, making it useful for <a href="/wiki/Radiation_hardening" title="Radiation hardening">rad-hard</a> devices for special military and <a href="/wiki/Aerospace" title="Aerospace">aerospace</a> applications.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Schottky_diode" title="Schottky diode">Schottky diode</a> of <a href="/wiki/Yellowcake" title="Yellowcake">U<sub>3</sub>O<sub>8</sub></a> and a <a href="/wiki/P-n-p_transistor" class="mw-redirect" title="P-n-p transistor">p-n-p transistor</a> of UO<sub>2</sub> were successfully manufactured in a laboratory.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toxicity">Toxicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Uranium_dioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Toxicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Uranium dioxide is known to be absorbed by <a href="/wiki/Phagocytosis" title="Phagocytosis">phagocytosis</a> in the lungs.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
