<p><b>Molybdenum trioxide</b> is <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">chemical compound</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> MoO<sub>3</sub>.  This compound is produced on the largest scale of any <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum" title="Molybdenum">molybdenum</a> compound.  It occurs as the rare <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">mineral</a> <a href="/wiki/Molybdite" title="Molybdite">molybdite</a>.  Its chief application is as an oxidation <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> and as a raw material for the production of molybdenum metal.<sup id="cite_ref-ullmann_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ullmann-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MoO3_chains.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/MoO3_chains.png/200px-MoO3_chains.png" width="200" height="92" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/MoO3_chains.png/300px-MoO3_chains.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/MoO3_chains.png/400px-MoO3_chains.png 2x" data-file-width="1178" data-file-height="539" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MoO3_chains.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A section of the chain comprising edge-sharing octahedra. Oxygen atoms in back and front of the chain link to other chains to build the layer.<sup id="cite_ref-webmineral_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webmineral-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>In the gas phase, three oxygen atoms are double bonded to the central molybdenum atom.  In the solid state, <a href="/wiki/Anhydrous" title="Anhydrous">anhydrous</a> MoO<sub>3</sub> is composed of layers of distorted MoO<sub>6</sub> octahedra in an orthorhombic crystal. The octahedra share edges and form chains which are cross-linked by oxygen atoms to form layers. The octahedra have one short molybdenum-oxygen bond to a non-bridging oxygen.<sup id="cite_ref-webmineral_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webmineral-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:left;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparation_and_principal_reactions">Preparation and principal reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparation and principal reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>MoO<sub>3</sub> is produced industrially by roasting <a href="/wiki/Molybdenum_disulfide" title="Molybdenum disulfide">molybdenum disulfide</a>, the chief ore of molybdenum:<sup id="cite_ref-ullmann_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ullmann-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 MoS<sub>2</sub>  +  7 O<sub>2</sub>   →   2 MoO<sub>3</sub>  +  4 SO<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The laboratory synthesis of the dihydrate entails acidification of aqueous solutions of <a href="/wiki/Sodium_molybdate" title="Sodium molybdate">sodium molybdate</a> with <a href="/wiki/Perchloric_acid" title="Perchloric acid">perchloric acid</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Na<sub>2</sub>MoO<sub>4</sub>  +  H<sub>2</sub>O  +  2 HClO<sub>4</sub>   →  MoO<sub>3</sub>(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>2</sub>  +  2 NaClO<sub>4</sub></dd></dl>
<p>The dihydrate loses water readily to give the monohydrate.  Both are bright yellow in color.
</p><p>Molybdenum trioxide dissolves slightly in water to give "<a href="/wiki/Molybdic_acid" title="Molybdic acid">molybdic acid</a>".  In base, it dissolves to afford the molybdate anion.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Molybdenum_trioxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Molybdenum trioxide is used to manufacture molybdenum metal, which serves as an additive to steel and corrosion-resistant alloys. The relevant conversion entails treatment of MoO<sub>3</sub> with hydrogen at elevated temperatures:
</p>
<dl><dd>MoO<sub>3</sub>  +  3 H<sub>2</sub>  →   Mo  +  3 H<sub>2</sub>O</dd></dl>
<p>It is also a component of the <a href="/wiki/Co-catalyst" class="mw-redirect" title="Co-catalyst">co-catalyst</a> used in the industrial production of <a href="/wiki/Acrylonitrile" title="Acrylonitrile">acrylonitrile</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Oxidation" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxidation">oxidation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Propene" title="Propene">propene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>.
</p><p>Because of its layered structure and the ease of the Mo(VI)/Mo(V) coupling, MoO<sub>3</sub> is of interest in electrochemical devices and displays.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Molybdenum trioxide has also been suggested as a potential anti-microbial agent, e.g., in polymers. In contact with water, it forms H<sup>+</sup> ions that can kill bacteria effectively.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  However, the issue of keeping the catalyst clean in an environment that would exploit such antimicrobial properties has not been explored.
</p>
