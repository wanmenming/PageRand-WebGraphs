<p><b>Carbon suboxide</b>, or <b>tricarbon dioxide</b>, is an <a href="/wiki/Oxide" title="Oxide">oxide</a> of <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> with <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">chemical formula</a> C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>  or O=C=C=C=O.  Its four cumulative double bonds make it a <a href="/wiki/Cumulene" title="Cumulene">cumulene</a>. It is one of the stable members of the series of linear <a href="/wiki/Oxocarbon" title="Oxocarbon">oxocarbons</a> O=C<sub><i>n</i></sub>=O, which also includes <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Pentacarbon_dioxide" title="Pentacarbon dioxide">pentacarbon dioxide</a> (C<sub>5</sub>O<sub>2</sub>). Although if carefully purified it can exist at room temperature in the dark without decomposing, it will polymerize under certain conditions.
</p><p>The substance was discovered in 1873 by <a href="/wiki/Sir_Benjamin_Collins_Brodie,_2nd_Baronet" title="Sir Benjamin Collins Brodie, 2nd Baronet">Benjamin Brodie</a> by subjecting <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> to an electric current. He claimed that the product was part of a series of "oxycarbons" with formulas C<sub><i>x</i>+1</sub>O<sub><i>x</i></sub>, namely C, C<sub>2</sub>O, C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, C<sub>4</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, C<sub>5</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, ..., and to have identified the last two;<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> however only C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> is known. In 1891 <a href="/wiki/Marcellin_Berthelot" title="Marcellin Berthelot">Marcellin Berthelot</a> observed that heating pure carbon monoxide at about 550&#160;°C created small amounts of carbon dioxide but no trace of carbon, and assumed that a carbon-rich oxide was created instead, which he named "sub-oxide".  He assumed it was the same product obtained by electric discharge and proposed the formula C<sub>2</sub>O.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Otto_Diels" title="Otto Diels">Otto Diels</a> later stated that the more organic names dicarbonylmethane and dioxallene were also correct.
</p><p>It is commonly described as an oily liquid or gas at room temperature with an extremely noxious odor.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_suboxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is synthesized by warming a dry mixture of <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus_pentoxide" title="Phosphorus pentoxide">phosphorus pentoxide</a> (P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>10</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Malonic_acid" title="Malonic acid">malonic acid</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a> of <a href="/wiki/Malonic_acid" title="Malonic acid">malonic acid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
Therefore, it can be also considered as the anhydride of <a href="/wiki/Malonic_anhydride" title="Malonic anhydride">malonic anhydride</a>, i.e. the "second anhydride" of <a href="/wiki/Malonic_acid" title="Malonic acid">malonic acid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several other ways for synthesis and reactions of carbon suboxide can be found in a review from 1930 by Reyerson.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polymerization">Polymerization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_suboxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Polymerization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon suboxide polymerizes spontaneously to a red, yellow, or black solid. The structure is postulated to be poly(α-pyronic), similar to the structure in <a href="/wiki/2-pyrone" class="mw-redirect" title="2-pyrone">2-pyrone</a> (α-pyrone).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The number of monomers in the polymers is variable (see <a href="/wiki/Oxocarbon#Polymeric_carbon_oxides" title="Oxocarbon">Oxocarbon#Polymeric carbon oxides</a>).
In 1969, it was hypothesized that the color of the Martian surface was caused by this compound; this was disproved by the <a href="/wiki/Viking_program" title="Viking program">Viking Mars probes</a> (the red color is instead due to <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxide</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_suboxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carbon suboxide is used in the preparation of <a href="/wiki/Malonate" title="Malonate">malonates</a>; and as an auxiliary to improve the dye affinity of furs.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_role">Biological role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_suboxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Biological role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carbon_suboxide_macrocyclic_polymers.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Carbon_suboxide_macrocyclic_polymers.gif/220px-Carbon_suboxide_macrocyclic_polymers.gif" width="220" height="100" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Carbon_suboxide_macrocyclic_polymers.gif/330px-Carbon_suboxide_macrocyclic_polymers.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d9/Carbon_suboxide_macrocyclic_polymers.gif 2x" data-file-width="364" data-file-height="166" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Carbon_suboxide_macrocyclic_polymers.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Those are 6- or 8-ring macrocyclic polymers of carbon suboxide that were found in living organisms. They are acting as an endogenous digoxin-like Na+/K+-ATP-ase and Ca-dependent ATP-ase inhibitors, endogenous natriuretics, antioxidants and antihypertensives</div></div></div>
<p>Carbon suboxide, C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, can be produced in small amounts in any biochemical process that normally produces <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, CO, for example, during heme oxidation by heme oxygenase-1. It can also be formed from malonic acid. It has been shown that carbon suboxide in an organism can quickly polymerize into macrocyclic polycarbon structures with the common formula (C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>)<sub>n</sub> (mostly (C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>)<sub>6</sub> and (C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>)<sub>8</sub>), and that those macrocyclic compounds are potent inhibitors of Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup>-ATP-ase and Ca-dependent ATP-ase, and have <a href="/wiki/Digoxin" title="Digoxin">digoxin</a>-like physiological properties and natriuretic and antihypertensive actions. Those macrocyclic carbon suboxide polymer compounds are thought to be endogenous digoxin-like regulators of Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup>-ATP-ases and Ca-dependent ATP-ases, and endogenous natriuretics and antihypertensives.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Other than that, some authors think also that those macrocyclic compounds of carbon suboxide can possibly diminish free radical formation and oxidative stress and play a role in endogenous anticancer protective mechanisms, for example in the <a href="/wiki/Retina" title="Retina">retina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_bonding">Structure and bonding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carbon_suboxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Structure and bonding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Studies general agreed that the molecule is highly non-rigid, with a very shallow barrier to bending (&lt; 0.5 kcal/mol for 140° ≤ <i>θ</i><sub>C<sub>2</sub></sub> ≤ 180°).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  The small energetic barrier to bending is around the same order of magnitude as the <a href="/wiki/Zero-point_energy" title="Zero-point energy">zero-point energy</a> of the vibrational ground state.  Therefore, the molecule is best described as <i>quasilinear.</i>  While infrared<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and electron diffraction<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> studies have indicated that C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> has a bent structure in the gas phase, the compound was found to possess at least an average linear geometry in the solid phase by X-ray crystallography, although the large thermal ellipsoids of the oxygen atoms and C<sub>2</sub> have been interpreted to be consistent with rapid bending (minimum <i>θ</i><sub>C<sub>2</sub></sub> ~ 170°), even in the solid state.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>  
</p><p>A heterocumulene resonance form of carbon suboxide based on minimization of formal charges does not readily explain the molecule's non-rigidity and deviation from linearity. To account for the quasilinear structure of carbon suboxide, Frenking has proposed that carbon suboxide be regarded as a "coordination complex" of carbon(0) bearing two carbonyl ligands and two lone pairs: OC→:C:←CO.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> However, the contribution of dative bonding in C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and similar species has been criticized as chemically unreasonable by others.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
