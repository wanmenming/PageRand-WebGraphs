<p><b>Calcite</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_minerals" title="Carbonate minerals">carbonate mineral</a> and the most stable <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">polymorph</a> of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> (CaCO<sub>3</sub>). The <a href="/wiki/Mohs_scale_of_mineral_hardness" title="Mohs scale of mineral hardness">Mohs scale of mineral hardness</a>, based on <a href="/wiki/Scratch_hardness" title="Scratch hardness">scratch</a> <a href="/wiki/Hardness_comparison" title="Hardness comparison">hardness comparison</a>, defines value 3 as "calcite".
</p><p>Other polymorphs of calcium carbonate are the minerals <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vaterite" title="Vaterite">vaterite</a>. Aragonite will change to calcite over timescales of days or less at temperatures exceeding 300°C,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and vaterite is even less stable.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Calcite is derived from the German <i>Calcit</i>, a term coined in the 19th century from the Latin word for <a href="/wiki/Lime_(material)" title="Lime (material)">lime</a>, <i>calx</i> (genitive calcis) with the suffix -ite used to name minerals. It is thus etymologically related to <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When applied by <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeologists</a> and stone trade professionals, the term <a href="/wiki/Alabaster" title="Alabaster">alabaster</a> is used not just as in geology and mineralogy, where it is reserved for a variety of <a href="/wiki/Gypsum" title="Gypsum">gypsum</a>; but also for a similar-looking, <a href="/wiki/Transparency_and_translucency" title="Transparency and translucency">translucent</a> variety of fine-grained banded deposit of calcite.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unit_cell_and_Miller_indices">Unit cell and Miller indices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Unit cell and Miller indices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In publications, two different sets of <a href="/wiki/Miller_indices" class="mw-redirect" title="Miller indices">Miller indices</a> are used to describe directions in calcite crystals - the hexagonal system with three indices <span class="texhtml"><i>h, k, l</i></span> and the rhombohedral system with four indices <span class="texhtml"><i>h, k, l, i</i></span>. To add to the complications, there are also two definitions of unit cell for calcite. One, an older "morphological" unit cell, was inferred by measuring angles between faces of crystals and looking for the smallest numbers that fit. Later, a "structural" unit cell was determined using <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">X-ray crystallography</a>. The morphological unit cell has approximate dimensions <span class="texhtml"><i>a</i> = 10 <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstrom" class="mw-redirect" title="Ångstrom">Å</a></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>c</i> = 8.5 <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstrom" class="mw-redirect" title="Ångstrom">Å</a></span>, while for the structural unit cell they are <span class="texhtml"><i>a</i> = 5 <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstrom" class="mw-redirect" title="Ångstrom">Å</a></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>c</i> = 17 <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstrom" class="mw-redirect" title="Ångstrom">Å</a></span>. For the same orientation, <span class="texhtml"><i>c</i></span> must be multiplied by 4 to convert from morphological to structural units. As an example, the cleavage is given as "perfect on {1 0 <span style="text-decoration:overline;">1</span> 1}" in morphological coordinates and "perfect on {1 0 <span style="text-decoration:overline;">1</span> 4}" in structural units. (In hexagonal indices, these are {1 0 1} and {1 0 4}.) Twinning, cleavage and crystal forms are always given in morphological units.<sup id="cite_ref-mindat_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mindat-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hazen_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hazen-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Form">Form</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Form">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Over 800 forms of calcite <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystals</a> have been identified. Most common are <a href="/wiki/Scalenohedron" class="mw-redirect" title="Scalenohedron">scalenohedra</a>, with faces in the hexagonal {2 1 1} directions (morphological unit cell) or {2 1 4} directions (structural unit cell); and rhombohedral, with faces in the {1 0 1} or {1 0 4} directions (the most common cleavage plane).<sup id="cite_ref-Hazen_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hazen-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Habits include acute to obtuse rhombohedra, tabular forms, <a href="/wiki/Prism_(geometry)" title="Prism (geometry)">prisms</a>, or various <a href="/wiki/Scalenohedron" class="mw-redirect" title="Scalenohedron">scalenohedra</a>. Calcite exhibits several <a href="/wiki/Crystal_twinning" title="Crystal twinning">twinning</a> types adding to the variety of observed forms. It may occur as fibrous, granular, lamellar, or compact. A fibrous, efflorescent form is known as <a href="/wiki/Lublinite" class="mw-redirect" title="Lublinite">lublinite</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Cleavage is usually in three directions parallel to the rhombohedron form. Its fracture is <a href="/wiki/Conchoidal_fracture" title="Conchoidal fracture">conchoidal</a>, but difficult to obtain.
</p><p>Scalenohedral faces are <a href="/wiki/Chiral" class="mw-redirect" title="Chiral">chiral</a> and come in pairs with mirror-image symmetry; their growth can be influenced by interaction with chiral biomolecules such as L- and D-<a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a>. Rhombohedral faces are achiral.<sup id="cite_ref-Hazen_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hazen-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hardness">Hardness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hardness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has a defining <a href="/wiki/Mohs_hardness" class="mw-redirect" title="Mohs hardness">Mohs hardness</a> of 3, a <a href="/wiki/Specific_gravity" title="Specific gravity">specific gravity</a> of 2.71, and its luster is vitreous in crystallized varieties. Color is white or none, though shades of gray, red, orange, yellow, green, blue, violet, brown, or even black can occur when the mineral is charged with impurities.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Optical">Optical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Optical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Calcite is transparent to opaque and may occasionally show <a href="/wiki/Phosphorescence" title="Phosphorescence">phosphorescence</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fluorescence" title="Fluorescence">fluorescence</a>. A transparent variety called <i><a href="/wiki/Iceland_spar" title="Iceland spar">Iceland spar</a></i> is used for optical purposes. Acute scalenohedral crystals are sometimes referred to as "dogtooth spar" while the rhombohedral form is sometimes referred to as "nailhead spar".
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Calcite-refraction-property.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A photograph of calcite laying over a text paragraph." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Calcite-refraction-property.jpg/220px-Calcite-refraction-property.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Calcite-refraction-property.jpg/330px-Calcite-refraction-property.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Calcite-refraction-property.jpg/440px-Calcite-refraction-property.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3008" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Calcite-refraction-property.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Photograph of calcite displaying the characteristic birefringence optical behaviour.</div></div></div>
<p>Single calcite crystals display an optical property called <a href="/wiki/Birefringence" title="Birefringence">birefringence</a> (double refraction). This strong birefringence causes objects viewed through a clear piece of calcite to appear doubled. The birefringent effect (using calcite) was first described by the <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> scientist <a href="/wiki/Rasmus_Bartholin" title="Rasmus Bartholin">Rasmus Bartholin</a> in 1669. At a wavelength of ~590&#160;nm calcite has ordinary and extraordinary <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive indices</a> of 1.658 and 1.486, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  Between 190 and 1700&#160;nm, the ordinary refractive index varies roughly between 1.9 and 1.5, while the extraordinary refractive index varies between 1.6 and 1.4.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical">Chemical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Chemical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Calcite, like most carbonates, will dissolve with most forms of acid. Calcite can be either <a href="/wiki/Solvation" title="Solvation">dissolved</a> by groundwater or <a href="/wiki/Precipitate" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitate">precipitated</a> by groundwater, depending on several factors including the water temperature, <a href="/wiki/Acidity" class="mw-redirect" title="Acidity">pH</a>, and dissolved <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">ion</a> concentrations. Although calcite is fairly insoluble in cold water, acidity can cause dissolution of calcite and release of carbon dioxide gas. Ambient carbon dioxide, due to its acidity, has a slight solubilizing effect on calcite. Calcite exhibits an unusual characteristic called retrograde solubility in which it becomes less soluble in water as the temperature increases.  When conditions are right for precipitation, calcite forms mineral coatings that cement the existing rock grains together or it can fill fractures.  When conditions are right for dissolution, the removal of calcite can dramatically increase the <a href="/wiki/Porosity" title="Porosity">porosity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Permeability_(fluid)" class="mw-redirect" title="Permeability (fluid)">permeability</a> of the rock, and if it continues for a long period of time may result in the formation of <a href="/wiki/Cave" title="Cave">caves</a>. On a landscape scale, continued dissolution of calcium carbonate-rich rocks can lead to the expansion and eventual collapse of cave systems, resulting in various forms of <a href="/wiki/Karst" title="Karst">karst topography</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_and_applications">Use and applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Use and applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tutankhamun%27s_Alabaster_Jar.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Tutankhamun%27s_Alabaster_Jar.jpg/220px-Tutankhamun%27s_Alabaster_Jar.jpg" width="220" height="325" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Tutankhamun%27s_Alabaster_Jar.jpg/330px-Tutankhamun%27s_Alabaster_Jar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Tutankhamun%27s_Alabaster_Jar.jpg/440px-Tutankhamun%27s_Alabaster_Jar.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1993" data-file-height="2942" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tutankhamun%27s_Alabaster_Jar.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>One of several calcite or alabaster perfume jars from the tomb of <a href="/wiki/Tutankhamun" title="Tutankhamun">Tutankhamun</a>, d. 1323 BC</div></div></div>
<p>Ancient Egyptians carved many items out of calcite, relating it to their goddess <a href="/wiki/Bastet" title="Bastet">Bast</a>, whose name contributed to the term <a href="/wiki/Alabaster" title="Alabaster">alabaster</a> because of the close association. Many other cultures have used the material for similar carved objects and applications.
</p><p>High-grade optical calcite was used in World War II for gun sights, specifically in bomb sights and anti-aircraft weaponry.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Also, experiments have been conducted to use calcite for a <a href="/wiki/Cloak_of_invisibility" title="Cloak of invisibility">cloak of invisibility</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Microbiologically_induced_calcite_precipitation" title="Microbiologically induced calcite precipitation">Microbiologically precipitated calcite</a> has a wide range of applications, such as soil remediation, soil stabilization and concrete repair.
</p><p>Calcite, obtained from an 80&#160;kg sample of <a href="/wiki/Carrara_marble" title="Carrara marble">Carrara marble</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-IAEA603RefSheet_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IAEA603RefSheet-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> is used as the <a href="/wiki/IAEA" class="mw-redirect" title="IAEA">IAEA</a>-603 isotopic standard in <a href="/wiki/Mass_spectrometry" title="Mass spectrometry">mass spectrometry</a> for the calibration of δ<sup>18</sup>O and δ<sup>13</sup>C.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_occurrence">Natural occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Natural occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Calcite is a common constituent of <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rocks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> in particular, much of which is formed from the shells of dead marine organisms.  Approximately 10% of sedimentary rock is limestone. It is the primary mineral in <a href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rock" title="Metamorphic rock">metamorphic</a> <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a>. It also occurs in deposits from <a href="/wiki/Hot_spring" title="Hot spring">hot springs</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Vein_(geology)" title="Vein (geology)">vein</a> mineral; in <a href="/wiki/Cavern" class="mw-redirect" title="Cavern">caverns</a> as <a href="/wiki/Stalactite" title="Stalactite">stalactites</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stalactite" title="Stalactite">stalagmites</a>; and in <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">mantle-derived</a> rocks such as <a href="/wiki/Carbonatite" title="Carbonatite">carbonatites</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kimberlite" title="Kimberlite">kimberlites</a>, or rarely in <a href="/wiki/Peridotite" title="Peridotite">peridotites</a>.
</p><p>Calcite is often the primary constituent of the <a href="/wiki/Seashell" title="Seashell">shells</a> of <a href="/wiki/Marine_biology" title="Marine biology">marine organisms</a>, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Coccolith" title="Coccolith">coccoliths</a> and planktic <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminifera</a>), the hard parts of red <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, some <a href="/wiki/Sea_sponge" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea sponge">sponges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brachiopod" title="Brachiopod">brachiopods</a>, <a href="/wiki/Echinoderm" title="Echinoderm">echinoderms</a>, some <a href="/wiki/Serpulidae" title="Serpulidae">serpulids</a>, most <a href="/wiki/Bryozoa" title="Bryozoa">bryozoa</a>, and parts of the shells of some <a href="/wiki/Bivalvia" title="Bivalvia">bivalves</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Oyster" title="Oyster">oysters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rudists" title="Rudists">rudists</a>). Calcite is found in spectacular form in the <a href="/wiki/Snowy_River_Cave" title="Snowy River Cave">Snowy River Cave</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a> as mentioned above, where microorganisms are credited with natural formations. <a href="/wiki/Trilobite" title="Trilobite">Trilobites</a>, which became <a href="/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event" title="Permian–Triassic extinction event">extinct a quarter billion years ago</a>, had unique compound eyes that used clear calcite crystals to form the lenses.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The largest documented single crystal of calcite originated from Iceland, measured 7×7×2 m and 6×6×3 m and weighed about 250 tons.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_processes">Formation processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Formation processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Calcite formation can proceed via several pathways, from the classical <a href="/wiki/Terrace_ledge_kink_model" title="Terrace ledge kink model">terrace ledge kink model</a><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> to the crystallization of poorly ordered precursor phases (amorphous calcium carbonate, ACC) via an <a href="/wiki/Ostwald_ripening" title="Ostwald ripening">Ostwald ripening</a> process, or via the agglomeration of nanocrystals.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The crystallization of ACC can occur in two stages: first, the ACC nanoparticles rapidly dehydrate and crystallize to form individual particles of vaterite. Secondly, the vaterite transforms to calcite via a dissolution and reprecipitation mechanism with the reaction rate controlled by the surface area of calcite.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The second stage of the reaction is approximately 10 times slower. However, the crystallization of calcite has been observed to be dependent on the starting pH and presence of Mg in solution.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> A neutral starting pH during mixing promotes the direct transformation of ACC into calcite. Conversely, when ACC forms in a solution that starts with a basic initial pH, the transformation to calcite occurs via metastable vaterite, which forms via a spherulitic growth mechanism.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In a second stage this vaterite transforms to calcite via a surface-controlled dissolution and recrystallization mechanism. Mg has a noteworthy effect on both the stability of ACC and its transformation to crystalline CaCO<sub>3</sub>, resulting in the formation of calcite directly from ACC, as this ion destabilizes the structure of vaterite.
</p><p>Calcite may form in the subsurface in response to activity of microorganisms, such as during sulfate-dependent <a href="/wiki/Anaerobic_oxidation_of_methane" title="Anaerobic oxidation of methane">anaerobic oxidation of methane</a>, where methane is oxidized and sulfate is reduced by a consortium of methane oxidizers and sulfate reducers, leading to precipitation of calcite and <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrite</a> from the produced bicarbonate and sulfide. These processes can be traced by the specific <a href="/wiki/Carbon_isotope" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon isotope">carbon isotope</a> composition of the calcites, which are extremely depleted in the <sup>13</sup>C isotope, by as much as −125 per mil <a href="/wiki/%CE%9413C#Reference_standard" title="Δ13C">PDB</a> (δ<sup>13</sup>C).<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Earth_history">In Earth history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calcite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In Earth history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Calcite_sea" title="Calcite sea">Calcite seas</a> existed in Earth history when the primary inorganic precipitate of calcium carbonate in marine waters was low-magnesium calcite (lmc), as opposed to the <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a> and high-magnesium calcite (hmc) precipitated today.  Calcite seas alternated with <a href="/wiki/Aragonite_sea" title="Aragonite sea">aragonite seas</a> over the Phanerozoic, being most prominent in the <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a>.  Lineages evolved to use whichever morph of calcium carbonate was favourable in the ocean at the time they became mineralised, and retained this mineralogy for the remainder of their evolutionary history.<sup id="cite_ref-Porter2007_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Porter2007-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Petrography" title="Petrography">Petrographic</a> evidence for these calcite sea conditions consists of calcitic <a href="/wiki/Ooid" title="Ooid">ooids</a>, lmc cements, <a href="/wiki/Hardgrounds" class="mw-redirect" title="Hardgrounds">hardgrounds</a>, and rapid early seafloor aragonite dissolution.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The evolution of marine organisms with calcium carbonate shells may have been affected by the calcite and <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a> sea cycle.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Calcite is one of the minerals that has been shown to <a href="/wiki/Catalysis" title="Catalysis">catalyze</a> an important biological reaction, the <a href="/wiki/Formose_reaction" title="Formose reaction">formose reaction</a>, and may have had a role in the origin of life.<sup id="cite_ref-Hazen_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hazen-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Interaction of its chiral surfaces (see <a href="#Form">Form</a>) with <a href="/wiki/Aspartic_acid" title="Aspartic acid">aspartic acid</a> molecules results in a slight bias in chirality; this is one possible mechanism for the origin of <a href="/wiki/Homochirality" title="Homochirality">homochirality</a> in living cells.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
