<p>In archaeology, the <b>classic Maya collapse</b> is the decline of <a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">Classic Maya civilization</a> and the abandonment of <a href="/wiki/Maya_city" title="Maya city">Maya cities</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Mesoamerica#Maya_Region" title="Geography of Mesoamerica">southern Maya lowlands</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</a> between the 8th and 9th&#160;centuries, at the end of the Classic Maya Period. <a href="/wiki/Preclassic_Maya" title="Preclassic Maya">Preclassic Maya</a> experienced a similar collapse in the 2nd century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The Classic Period of <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_chronology" title="Mesoamerican chronology">Mesoamerican chronology</a> is generally defined as the period from 250 to 900, the last century of which is referred to as the <b>Terminal Classic</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The Classic Maya collapse is one of the greatest unsolved mysteries in archaeology. Urban centers of the southern lowlands, among them <a href="/wiki/Palenque" title="Palenque">Palenque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cop%C3%A1n" title="Copán">Copán</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tikal" title="Tikal">Tikal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Calakmul" title="Calakmul">Calakmul</a>, went into decline during the 8th and 9th&#160;centuries and were abandoned shortly thereafter. Archaeologically, this decline is indicated by the cessation of monumental inscriptions<sup id="cite_ref-Knorozov_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knorozov-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and the reduction of large-scale <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_architecture" title="Mesoamerican architecture">architectural</a> construction at the primary urban centers of the Classic Period.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Although termed a <i>collapse</i>, it did not mark the end of the Maya civilization but rather a shift away from the Southern Lowlands as a power center; Northern Yucatán in particular prospered afterwards, although with very different artistic and architectural styles, and with much less use of monumental hieroglyphic writing. In the Post-Classic Period following the collapse, the state of <a href="/wiki/Chich%C3%A9n_Itz%C3%A1" class="mw-redirect" title="Chichén Itzá">Chichén Itzá</a> built an empire that briefly united much of the Maya region,<sup id="cite_ref-Knorozov_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knorozov-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and centers such as <a href="/wiki/Mayap%C3%A1n" class="mw-redirect" title="Mayapán">Mayapán</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uxmal" title="Uxmal">Uxmal</a> flourished, as did the Highland states of the <a href="/wiki/K%27iche%27_Kingdom_of_Q%27umarkaj" class="mw-redirect" title="K&#39;iche&#39; Kingdom of Q&#39;umarkaj">K'iche'</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kaqchikel_people" title="Kaqchikel people">Kaqchikel</a> Maya. Independent Maya civilization continued until 1697 when the Spanish conquered <a href="/wiki/Nojpet%C3%A9n" title="Nojpetén">Nojpetén</a>, the last independent city-state. Millions of <a href="/wiki/Maya_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Maya people">Maya people</a> still inhabit the Yucatán peninsula today.<sup id="cite_ref-total_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-total-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Because parts of Maya civilization unambiguously continued, a number of scholars strongly dislike the term <i>collapse</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Regarding the proposed collapse, <a href="/w/index.php?title=E._W._Andrews_IV&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="E. W. Andrews IV (page does not exist)">E. W. Andrews IV</a> went as far as to say, "in my belief no such thing happened."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Progression_of_the_decline">Progression of the decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classic_Maya_collapse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Progression of the decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Maya often recorded dates on monuments they built. Few dated monuments were being built circa 500 – around ten per year in 514, for example. The number steadily increased to twenty per year by 672 and forty by around 750. After this, the number of dated monuments begins to falter relatively quickly, collapsing back to ten by 800 and to zero by 900. Likewise, recorded lists of kings complement this analysis. <a href="/wiki/Cop%C3%A1n_Altar_Q" title="Copán Altar Q">Altar Q</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cop%C3%A1n" title="Copán">Copán</a> shows a reign of kings from 426 to 763. One last king not recorded on Altar Q was <a href="/wiki/Ukit_Took" title="Ukit Took">Ukit Took</a>, "Patron of Flint", who was probably a usurper. The dynasty is believed to have collapsed entirely shortly thereafter. In <a href="/wiki/Quirigua" class="mw-redirect" title="Quirigua">Quirigua</a>, twenty miles north of Copán, the last king <a href="/wiki/Jade_Sky" title="Jade Sky">Jade Sky</a> began his rule between 895 and 900, and throughout the Maya area all kingdoms similarly fell around that time.<sup id="cite_ref-whyNationsFail_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whyNationsFail-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A third piece of evidence of the progression of Maya decline, gathered by Ann Corinne Freter, Nancy Gonlin, and David Webster, uses a technique called <a href="/wiki/Obsidian_hydration" class="mw-redirect" title="Obsidian hydration">obsidian hydration</a>. The technique allowed them to map the spread and growth of settlements in the <a href="/wiki/Cop%C3%A1n" title="Copán">Copán Valley</a> and estimate their populations. Between 400 and 450, the population was estimated at a peak of twenty-eight thousand, between 750 and 800 – larger than London at the time. Population then began to steadily decline. By 900 the population had fallen to fifteen thousand, and by 1200 the population was again less than 1000.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theories">Theories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classic_Maya_collapse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Theories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Over 80 different theories or variations of theories attempting to explain the Classic Maya collapse have been identified.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> From climate change to deforestation to lack of action by Maya kings, there is no universally accepted collapse theory, although drought is gaining momentum as the leading explanation.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_invasion">Foreign invasion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classic_Maya_collapse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Foreign invasion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The archaeological evidence of the <a href="/wiki/Toltec" title="Toltec">Toltec</a> intrusion into  <a href="/wiki/Seibal" title="Seibal">Seibal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pet%C3%A9n_Department" title="Petén Department">Peten</a>, suggests to some the theory of foreign invasion. The latest hypothesis states that the southern lowlands were invaded by a non-Maya group whose homelands were probably in the gulf coast lowlands. This invasion began in the 9th&#160;century and set off, within 100&#160;years, a group of events that destroyed the Classic Maya. It is believed that this invasion was somehow influenced by the Toltec people of central Mexico. However, most Mayanists do not believe that foreign invasion was the main cause of the Classic Maya collapse; they postulate that no military defeat can explain or be the cause of the protracted and complex Classic collapse process. <a href="/wiki/Teotihuacan" title="Teotihuacan">Teotihuacan</a> influence across the Maya region may have involved some form of military invasion; however, it is generally noted that significant Teotihuacan-Maya interactions date from at least the Early Classic period, well before the episodes of Late Classic collapse.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The foreign invasion theory does not answer the question of where the inhabitants went. David Webster believed that the population should have increased because of the lack of elite power. Further, it is not understood why the governmental institutions were not remade following the revolts, which happened under similar circumstances in places like <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>. A study by anthropologist Elliot M. Abrams came to the conclusion that buildings, specifically in <a href="/wiki/Copan" class="mw-redirect" title="Copan">Copan</a>, did not require an extensive amount of time and workers to construct.<sup id="cite_ref-Abrams_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Abrams-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> However, this theory was developed during a period when the archaeological evidence showed that there were fewer Maya people than there are now known to have been.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Revolutions, peasant revolts, and social turmoil change circumstances, and are often followed by foreign wars, but they run their course. There are no documented revolutions that caused wholesale abandonment of entire regions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Collapse_of_trade_routes">Collapse of trade routes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classic_Maya_collapse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Collapse of trade routes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has been hypothesized that the decline of the Maya is related to the collapse of their intricate trade systems, especially those connected to the central Mexican city of <a href="/wiki/Teotihuac%C3%A1n" class="mw-redirect" title="Teotihuacán">Teotihuacán</a>. Preceding improved knowledge of the chronology of Mesoamerica, Teotihuacan was believed to have fallen during 700–750, forcing the "restructuring of economic relations throughout highland Mesoamerica and the Gulf Coast".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  This remaking of relationships between civilizations would have then given the collapse of the Classic Maya a slightly later date. However, after knowing more about the events and the periods when they occurred, it is believed that the strongest Teotihuacan influence was during the 4th and 5th&#160;centuries. In addition, the civilization of Teotihuacan started to lose its power, and maybe abandoned the city, during 600–650. This differs greatly from the previous belief that Teotihuacano power decreased during 700–750.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> But since the new decline date of 600–650 has been accepted, the Maya civilizations are now thought to have lived on and prospered “for another century and more”<sup id="cite_ref-Webster_2002_pp.232_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Webster_2002_pp.232-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> than what was previously believed. Rather than the decline of Teotihuacan directly preceding the collapse of the Maya, their decline is now seen as contributing “to the 6th-century ‘hiatus’”.<sup id="cite_ref-Webster_2002_pp.232_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Webster_2002_pp.232-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemic_diseases">Epidemic diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classic_Maya_collapse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Epidemic diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">disease</a> theory is also a contender as a factor in the Classic Maya collapse. Widespread disease could explain some rapid depopulation, both directly through the spread of infection itself and indirectly as an inhibition to recovery over the long run. According to Dunn (1968) and Shimkin (1973), infectious diseases spread by parasites are common in tropical rainforest regions, such as the Maya lowlands. Shimkin specifically suggests that the Maya may have encountered endemic infections related to American <a href="/wiki/Trypanosomiasis" title="Trypanosomiasis">trypanosomiasis</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ascaris" title="Ascaris">Ascaris</a></i>, and some <a href="/wiki/Enteropathogen" class="mw-redirect" title="Enteropathogen">enteropathogens</a> that cause acute diarrheal illness. Furthermore, some experts believe that, through development of their civilization (that is, development of agriculture and settlements), the Maya could have created a "disturbed environment", in which parasitic and pathogen-carrying insects often thrive.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Among the pathogens listed above, it is thought that those that cause the acute diarrheal illnesses would have been the most devastating to the Maya population, because such illness would have struck a victim at an early age, thereby hampering nutritional health and the natural growth and development of a child. This would have made them more susceptible to other diseases later in life,  and would have been exacerbated by an increasing dependence on carbohydrate-rich crops.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Such ideas as this could explain the role of disease as at least a possible partial reason for the Classic Maya Collapse.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drought_theory">Drought theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classic_Maya_collapse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Drought theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Large droughts hit the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">Yucatán Peninsula</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pet%C3%A9n_Basin" title="Petén Basin">Petén Basin</a> areas with particular ferocity, as thin tropical soils decline in fertility and become unworkable when deprived of forest cover,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and due to regular seasonal drought drying up surface water.<sup id="cite_ref-Webster_2002,_p.239_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Webster_2002,_p.239-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Colonial Spanish officials accurately documented cycles of drought, famine, disease, and war, providing a reliable historical record of the basic drought pattern in the Maya region.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Climatic factors were first implicated in the collapse as early as 1931 by Mayanists <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gann" title="Thomas Gann">Thomas Gann</a> and <a href="/wiki/J._Eric_S._Thompson" title="J. Eric S. Thompson">J. E. S. Thompson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In <i>The Great Maya Droughts</i>, Richardson Gill gathers and analyzes an array of climatic, historical, hydrologic, tree ring, volcanic, geologic, lake bed, and archeological research, and demonstrates that a prolonged series of droughts probably caused the Classic Maya collapse.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The drought theory provides a comprehensive explanation, because non-environmental and cultural factors (excessive warfare, foreign invasion, peasant revolt, less trade, etc.) can all be explained by the effects of prolonged drought on Classic Maya civilization.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Climatic changes are, with increasing frequency, found to be major drivers in the rise and fall of civilizations all over the world.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Professors Harvey Weiss of Yale University and Raymond S. Bradley of the University of Massachusetts have written, "Many lines of evidence now point to climate forcing as the primary agent in repeated social collapse."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>  In a separate publication, Weiss illustrates an emerging understanding of scientists:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Within the past five years new tools and new data for archaeologists, climatologists, and historians have brought us to the edge of a new era in the study of global and hemispheric climate change and its cultural impacts. The climate of the Holocene, previously assumed static, now displays a surprising dynamism, which has affected the agricultural bases of pre-industrial societies. The list of Holocene climate alterations and their socio-economic effects has rapidly become too complex for brief summary.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The drought theory holds that rapid <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> in the form of severe <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> brought about the Classic Maya collapse.  According to the particular version put forward by Gill in <i>The Great Maya Droughts</i>,
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>[Studies of] Yucatecan lake sediment cores ... provide unambiguous evidence for a severe 200-year drought from AD&#160;800 to 1000 ... the most severe in the last 7,000&#160;years ... precisely at the time of the Maya Collapse.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Climatic modeling, tree ring data, and historical climate data show that cold weather in the Northern Hemisphere is associated with drought in Mesoamerica.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Northern Europe suffered extremely low<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="vague (January 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> temperatures around the same time as the Maya droughts. The same connection between drought in the Maya areas and extreme cold in northern Europe was found again at the beginning of the 20th&#160;century. Volcanic activity, within and outside Mesoamerica, is also correlated with colder weather and resulting drought, as the effects of the <a href="/wiki/Tambora_volcano_eruption_in_1815" class="mw-redirect" title="Tambora volcano eruption in 1815">Tambora volcano eruption in 1815</a> indicate.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mesoamerican civilization provides a remarkable exception: civilization prospering in the tropical swampland. The Maya are often perceived as having lived in a rainforest, but technically, they lived in a seasonal desert without access to stable sources of drinking water.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The exceptional accomplishments of the Maya are even more remarkable because of their engineered response to the fundamental environmental difficulty of relying upon rainwater rather than permanent sources of water. “The Maya succeeded in creating a civilization in a seasonal desert by creating a system of water storage and management which was totally dependent on consistent rainfall.”<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The constant need for water kept the Maya on the edge of survival. “Given this precarious balance of wet and dry conditions, even a slight shift in the distribution of annual precipitation can have serious consequences.”<sup id="cite_ref-Webster_2002,_p.239_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Webster_2002,_p.239-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Water and civilization were vitally connected in ancient Mesoamerica. Archaeologist and specialist in pre-industrial land and water usage practices <a href="/wiki/Vernon_Scarborough" class="mw-redirect" title="Vernon Scarborough">Vernon Scarborough</a> believes water management and access were critical to the development of Maya civilization.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Critics of the drought theory wonder why the southern and central lowland cities were abandoned and the northern cities like <a href="/wiki/Chichen_Itza" title="Chichen Itza">Chichen Itza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uxmal" title="Uxmal">Uxmal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Coba" title="Coba">Coba</a> continued to thrive.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> One critic argued that Chichen Itza revamped its political, military, religious, and economic institutions away from powerful lords or kings.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Inhabitants of the northern Yucatán also had access to seafood, which might have explained the survival of Chichen Itza and <a href="/wiki/Mayapan" title="Mayapan">Mayapan</a>, cities away from the coast but within reach of coastal food supplies.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Critics of the drought theory also point to current weather patterns: much heavier rainfall in the southern lowlands compared to the lighter amount of rain in the northern Yucatán. Drought theory supporters state that the entire regional climate changed, including the amount of rainfall, so that modern rainfall patterns are not indicative of rainfall from 800 to 900. <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_State_University" title="Louisiana State University">LSU</a> archaeologist <a href="/wiki/Heather_McKillop" title="Heather McKillop">Heather McKillop</a> found a significant<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="vague (January 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> rise in sea level along the coast nearest the southern Maya lowlands, coinciding with the end of the Classic period, and indicating climate change.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>David Webster, a critic of the <a href="/wiki/Megadrought" title="Megadrought">megadrought</a> theory, says that much of the evidence provided by Gill comes from the northern Yucatán and not the southern part of the peninsula, where Classic Maya civilization flourished. He also states that if water sources were to have dried up, then several city-states would have moved to other water sources. That Gill suggests that all water in the region would have dried up and destroyed Maya civilization is a stretch, according to Webster,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> although Webster does not have a precise competing theory explaining the Classic Maya Collapse.
</p><p>A study published in <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i> in 2012 found that modest rainfall reductions, amounting to only 25 to 40 percent of annual rainfall, may have been the tipping point to the Maya collapse. Based on samples of lake and cave sediments in the areas surrounding major Maya cities, the researchers were able to determine the amount of annual rainfall in the region. The mild droughts that took place between 800–950 would therefore be enough to rapidly deplete seasonal water supplies in the Yucatán lowlands, where there are no rivers.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A study published in <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scientific_Reports_(journal)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Scientific Reports (journal) (page does not exist)">Scientific Reports</a></i> in 2016 showed that between 750 and 900 a cluster of four earthquakes affected the wet tropical mountains south of the Yucatán lowlands, which are not vulnerable to drought, and include such important cities as Quirigua and Copán. These earthquakes left detectable destruction in several Maya cities and led to the abandonment of Quirigua. The study hypothesizes that repeated destruction combined with declining trade with the Maya kingdoms of the Yucatán lowlands to propagate the collapse to the southern part of the Maya realm.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>LIDAR scanning of the Classic Maya heartlands bolsters the drought theory. A huge population as we now understand existed would not ordinarily disappear from civil war, revolution, soil degradation, disease, earthquake or other suspected factors.  Drought, the absence of water in an agricultural system heavily dependent upon water, is almost the only remaining possibility for the collapse in the entire heavily populated region. The Yucatán may have provided underground water and more rainfall to permit the continuance of Mayan civilization there.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Systemic_ecological_collapse_model">Systemic ecological collapse model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classic_Maya_collapse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Systemic ecological collapse model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some ecological theories of Maya decline focus on the worsening agricultural and resource conditions in the <a href="/wiki/Late_Classic" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Classic">late Classic</a> period. It was originally thought that the majority of Maya agriculture was dependent on a simple <a href="/wiki/Slash-and-burn" title="Slash-and-burn">slash-and-burn</a> system. Based on this method, the hypothesis of soil exhaustion was advanced by <a href="/wiki/Orator_F._Cook" title="Orator F. Cook">Orator F. Cook</a> in 1921. Similar soil exhaustion assumptions are associated with <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>, intensive agricultural, and <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a> grass competition.
</p><p>More recent investigations have shown a complicated variety of intensive agricultural techniques utilized by the Maya, explaining the high population of the Classic Maya polities. Modern archaeologists now comprehend the sophisticated intensive and productive agricultural techniques of the ancient Maya, and several of the Maya agricultural methods have not yet been reproduced. Intensive agricultural methods were developed and utilized by all the Mesoamerican cultures to boost their food production and give them a competitive advantage over less skillful peoples.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> These intensive agricultural methods included canals, terracing, raised fields, ridged fields, <a href="/wiki/Chinampa" title="Chinampa">chinampas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Night_soil" title="Night soil">the use of human feces as fertilizer</a>, seasonal swamps or <i>bajos</i>, using muck from the <i>bajos</i> to create fertile fields, dikes, dams, irrigation, water reservoirs, several types of water storage systems, hydraulic systems, swamp reclamation, <a href="/wiki/Swidden" class="mw-redirect" title="Swidden">swidden</a> systems, and other agricultural techniques that have not yet been fully understood.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Systemic <a href="/wiki/Ecological_collapse" title="Ecological collapse">ecological collapse</a> is said to be evidenced by <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siltation" title="Siltation">siltation</a>, and the decline of <a href="/wiki/Biological_diversity" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological diversity">biological diversity</a>.
</p><p>In addition to mountainous terrain, Mesoamericans successfully exploited the very problematic tropical rainforest for 1,500&#160;years.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The agricultural techniques utilized by the Maya were entirely dependent upon ample supplies of water, lending credit to the drought theory of collapse. The Maya thrived in territory that would be uninhabitable to most peoples. Their success over two millennia in this environment was "amazing."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_explanations">Other explanations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Classic_Maya_collapse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other explanations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Tainter" title="Joseph Tainter">Joseph Tainter</a> wrote extensively about the collapse of the Southern Lowland Maya in his 1988 study <i>The Collapse of Complex Societies</i>. His theory about Maya collapse encompasses some of the above explanations, but focuses specifically on the development of and the declining marginal returns from the increasing social complexity of the competing Maya city-states.<sup id="cite_ref-Tainter_1988_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tainter_1988-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Psychologist <a href="/wiki/Julian_Jaynes" title="Julian Jaynes">Julian Jaynes</a> suggested that the collapse was due to a failure in the social control systems of religion and political authority, due to increasing socioeconomic complexity that overwhelmed the power of traditional rituals and the king's authority to compel obedience.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
