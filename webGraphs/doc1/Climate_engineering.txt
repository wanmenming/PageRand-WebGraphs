<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Climate engineering</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Geoengineering" redirects here. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Geoengineering_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Geoengineering (disambiguation)">Geoengineering (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Climate engineering</b> or <b>climate intervention</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> commonly referred to as <b>geoengineering</b>, is the deliberate and large-scale intervention in the Earth’s <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate system</a>, usually with the aim of mitigating the adverse effects of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IPCC_AR5_WGI_SPM_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_AR5_WGI_SPM-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Royal_Society_Policy_Document_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Royal_Society_Policy_Document-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Climate engineering is an umbrella term for measures that mainly fall into two categories: <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_removal" title="Greenhouse gas removal">greenhouse gas removal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation_management" title="Solar radiation management">solar radiation management</a>. <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_removal" title="Greenhouse gas removal">Greenhouse gas removal</a> approaches, of which <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_removal" title="Carbon dioxide removal">carbon dioxide removal</a> represents the most prominent subcategory addresses the cause of global warming by removing <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gases" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse gases">greenhouse gases</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. Solar radiation management attempts to offset effects of greenhouse gases by causing the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> to absorb less <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a>.
</p><p>Climate engineering approaches are sometimes viewed as additional potential options for limiting <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> or its impacts, alongside <a href="/wiki/Mitigation_of_global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitigation of global warming">mitigation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adaptation_to_global_warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Adaptation to global warming">adaptation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> There is substantial agreement among scientists that climate engineering cannot substitute for <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">climate change mitigation</a>. Some approaches might be used as accompanying measures to sharp cuts in <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Given that all types of measures for addressing climate change have economic, political, or physical limitations,<sup id="cite_ref-lenton09_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lenton09-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-imeche09_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-imeche09-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> some climate engineering approaches might eventually be used as part of an ensemble of measures, which can be referred to as <a href="/wiki/Climate_restoration" title="Climate restoration">climate restoration</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-roysoc09_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-roysoc09-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Research on costs, benefits, and various types of <a href="/wiki/Risk" title="Risk">risks</a> of most climate engineering approaches is at an early stage and their understanding needs to improve to judge their adequacy and feasibility.<sup id="cite_ref-IPCC_AR5_WGI_SPM_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC_AR5_WGI_SPM-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Almost all research into solar radiation management has to date consisted of computer modelling or laboratory tests, and an attempt to move to outdoor experimentation has proven controversial.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  Some <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_removal" title="Carbon dioxide removal">carbon dioxide removal</a> practices, such as <a href="/wiki/Tree_planting" title="Tree planting">afforestation</a><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>, ecosystem restoration and <a href="/wiki/Bio-energy_with_carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage">bio-energy with carbon capture and storage</a> projects, are underway to a limited extent. Their scalability to effectively affect global climate is, however, debated. Ocean <a href="/wiki/Iron_fertilization" title="Iron fertilization">iron fertilization</a> has been investigated in small-scale research trials. These experiments have proven controversial.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/World_Wildlife_Fund" class="mw-redirect" title="World Wildlife Fund">World Wildlife Fund</a> has criticized these activities.<sup id="cite_ref-mongabay2007_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mongabay2007-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most experts and major reports advise against relying on climate engineering techniques as a main solution to global warming, in part due to the large uncertainties over effectiveness and side effects. However, most experts also argue that the risks of such interventions must be seen in the context of risks of dangerous global warming.<sup id="cite_ref-perspectives_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-perspectives-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  Interventions at large scale may run a greater risk of disrupting natural systems resulting in a dilemma that those approaches that could prove highly (cost-) effective in addressing extreme climate risk, might themselves cause substantial risk.<sup id="cite_ref-perspectives_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-perspectives-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Some have suggested that the concept of engineering the climate presents a so-called "<a href="/wiki/Moral_hazard" title="Moral hazard">moral hazard</a>" because it could reduce political and public pressure for emissions reduction, which could exacerbate overall climate risks; others assert that the threat of climate engineering could spur emissions cuts.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> 
Some are in favour of a moratorium on out-of-doors testing and deployment of <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation_management" title="Solar radiation management">solar radiation management</a> (SRM).<sup id="cite_ref-worldchanging1_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-worldchanging1-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">Human impact on the environment</a></div>
<p>With respect to climate, geoengineering is defined by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> as "... the deliberate large-scale intervention in the Earth’s climate system, in order to moderate <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several organizations have investigated climate engineering with a view to evaluating its potential, including the <a href="/wiki/US_Congress" class="mw-redirect" title="US Congress">US Congress</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Bullis_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bullis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/UK_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Parliament">UK Parliament</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Asilomar_International_Conference_on_Climate_Intervention_Technologies" title="Asilomar International Conference on Climate Intervention Technologies">Asilomar International Conference on Climate Intervention Technologies</a> was convened to identify and develop risk reduction guidelines for climate intervention experimentation.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some environmental organisations (such as <a href="/wiki/Friends_of_the_Earth" title="Friends of the Earth">Friends of the Earth</a><sup id="cite_ref-Extreme_action_guardian_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Extreme_action_guardian-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">Greenpeace</a><sup id="cite_ref-Geoengineering_no_solution_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Geoengineering_no_solution-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>) have been reluctant to endorse solar radiation management, but are often more supportive of some carbon dioxide removal projects, such as <a href="/wiki/Afforestation" title="Afforestation">afforestation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peatland_restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="Peatland restoration">peatland restoration</a>. Some authors have argued that any public support for climate engineering may weaken the fragile political consensus to reduce <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_climate_change_science" title="History of climate change science">History of climate change science</a></div>
<p>The 1965 landmark report, "Restoring the Quality of Our Environment" by U.S. President Lyndon B. Johnson’s Science Advisory Committee warned of the harmful effects of fossil fuel emissions, the report also mentioned "deliberately bringing about countervailing climatic changes," including by "raising the albedo, or reflectivity, of the Earth."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Edward_Teller" title="Edward Teller">Teller</a> et al. 1997 suggested to research and deploy reflective particles, to reduce incoming solar radiation, and thus to cancel the effects of fossil fuel burning.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_strategies">Proposed strategies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Proposed strategies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_proposed_geoengineering_schemes" class="mw-redirect" title="List of proposed geoengineering schemes">List of proposed geoengineering schemes</a></div>
<p>Several climate engineering strategies have been proposed. IPCC documents detail several notable proposals.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> These fall into two main categories: <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation_management" title="Solar radiation management">solar radiation management</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_removal" title="Carbon dioxide removal">carbon dioxide removal</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_radiation_management">Solar radiation management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Solar radiation management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation_management" title="Solar radiation management">Solar radiation management</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Stratospheric_sulfate_aerosols_(geoengineering)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stratospheric sulfate aerosols (geoengineering)">Stratospheric sulfate aerosols (geoengineering)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marine_Cloud_Brightening" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine Cloud Brightening">Marine Cloud Brightening</a></div>
<p>Solar radiation management (SRM)<sup id="cite_ref-Royal_Society_Policy_Document_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Royal_Society_Policy_Document-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> techniques would seek to reduce sunlight absorbed (<a href="/wiki/Ultra-violet" class="mw-redirect" title="Ultra-violet">ultra-violet</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Infra-red" class="mw-redirect" title="Infra-red">infra-red</a> and <a href="/wiki/Visible_light" class="mw-redirect" title="Visible light">visible</a>).  This would be achieved by deflecting sunlight away from the Earth, or by increasing the reflectivity (<a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>) of the atmosphere or the Earth's surface. These methods would not reduce <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> concentrations in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, and thus would not seek to address problems such as the <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a> caused by <a href="/wiki/CO2" class="mw-redirect" title="CO2">CO<sub>2</sub></a>. In general, solar radiation management projects presently appear to be able to take effect rapidly and to have very low direct implementation costs relative to greenhouse gas emissions cuts and carbon dioxide removal.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Furthermore, many proposed SRM methods would be reversible in their direct climatic effects. While <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_remediation" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenhouse gas remediation">greenhouse gas remediation</a> offers a more comprehensive possible solution to global warming, it does not give instantaneous results; for that, solar radiation management is required.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (March 2013)">dubious</span></a>&#32;<span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Climate_engineering#Dubious" title="Talk:Climate engineering">discuss</a></span></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Solar radiation management methods<sup id="cite_ref-Royal_Society_Policy_Document_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Royal_Society_Policy_Document-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> may include:
</p>
<ul><li>Surface-based: for example, protecting or expanding polar sea ice and glaciers,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> using <a href="/wiki/Reflective_surfaces_(geoengineering)" class="mw-redirect" title="Reflective surfaces (geoengineering)">pale-colored roofing materials</a>, attempting to change the oceans' brightness, or growing high-albedo crops.</li>
<li>Troposphere-based: for example, <a href="/wiki/Cloud_reflectivity_modification" class="mw-redirect" title="Cloud reflectivity modification">marine cloud brightening</a>, which would spray fine <a href="/wiki/Sea_water" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea water">sea water</a> to whiten clouds and thus increase cloud reflectivity.</li>
<li>Upper atmosphere-based: creating reflective <a href="/wiki/Aerosols" class="mw-redirect" title="Aerosols">aerosols</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Stratospheric_sulfate_aerosols_(geoengineering)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stratospheric sulfate aerosols (geoengineering)">stratospheric sulfate aerosols</a>, specifically designed <a href="/wiki/Photophoresis" title="Photophoresis">self-levitating</a> aerosols,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> or other substances.</li>
<li>Space-based: <a href="/wiki/Space_sunshade" title="Space sunshade">space sunshade</a>—obstructing <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> with space-based mirrors, dust,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> etc.</li></ul>

<p>Carbon dioxide removal (sometimes known as negative emissions technologies or greenhouse gas removal) projects seek to remove carbon dioxide from the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>.  Proposed methods include those that directly remove such gases from the atmosphere, as well as indirect methods that seek to promote natural processes that draw down and sequester CO<sub>2</sub> (e.g. tree planting).  Many projects overlap with <a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">carbon capture and storage</a> projects, and may not be considered to be climate engineering by all commentators. Techniques in this category include:
</p>
<ul><li>Creating <a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">biochar</a>, which can be mixed with soil to create <a href="/wiki/Terra_preta" title="Terra preta">terra preta</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bio-energy_with_carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage">Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage</a> to sequester carbon and simultaneously provide energy</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carbon_air_capture" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbon air capture">Carbon air capture</a> to remove <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> from ambient air</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Afforestation" title="Afforestation">Afforestation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reforestation" title="Reforestation">reforestation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forest_restoration" title="Forest restoration">forest restoration</a> to absorb carbon dioxide</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ocean_fertilization" title="Ocean fertilization">Ocean fertilization</a> including <a href="/wiki/Iron_fertilization" title="Iron fertilization">iron fertilization</a> of the oceans</li></ul>
<p>Many of the IPCC model projections to keep global mean temperature below 2C, are based on scenarios assuming deployment of negative emissions technologies.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Justification">Justification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Justification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tipping_points_and_positive_feedback">Tipping points and positive feedback</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tipping points and positive feedback">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:65_Myr_Climate_Change.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/65_Myr_Climate_Change.png/300px-65_Myr_Climate_Change.png" width="300" height="183" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/65_Myr_Climate_Change.png/450px-65_Myr_Climate_Change.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/65_Myr_Climate_Change.png/600px-65_Myr_Climate_Change.png 2x" data-file-width="650" data-file-height="397" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:65_Myr_Climate_Change.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Climate change during the last 65 million years. The Paleocene–Eocene Thermal Maximum is labelled PETM.</div></div></div>
<p>It is argued that <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> may cross <a href="/wiki/Tipping_point_(climatology)" title="Tipping point (climatology)">tipping points</a><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> where elements of the climate system may 'tip' from one stable state to another stable state, much like a glass tipping over. When the new state is reached, further warming may be caused by <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a> effects.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>  An example of a proposed <a href="/wiki/Causal_chain" title="Causal chain">causal chain</a> leading to more warming is the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_sea_ice_decline" title="Arctic sea ice decline">decline of Arctic sea ice</a>, potentially triggering subsequent release of ocean <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Evidence suggests a gradual and prolonged release of greenhouse gases from thawing permafrost.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The precise identity of such "tipping points" is not clear, with scientists taking differing views on whether specific systems are capable of "tipping" and the point at which this "tipping" will occur.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> An example of a previous tipping point is that which preceded the rapid warming leading up to the <a href="/wiki/Paleocene%E2%80%93Eocene_Thermal_Maximum" title="Paleocene–Eocene Thermal Maximum">Paleocene–Eocene Thermal Maximum</a>. Once a tipping point is crossed, cuts in anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions will not be able to reverse the change. Conservation of resources and reduction of greenhouse emissions, used in conjunction with climate engineering, are therefore considered a viable option by some commentators.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buying_time">Buying time</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Buying time">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Climate engineering offers the hope of temporarily reversing some aspects of global warming and allowing the natural climate to be substantially preserved whilst greenhouse gas emissions are brought under control and removed from the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> by natural or artificial processes.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Costs">Costs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Costs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Estimates of direct costs for climate engineering implementation vary widely. In general, carbon dioxide removal methods are more expensive than the solar radiation management ones. In their 2009 report <i>Geoengineering the Climate</i> the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> judged afforestation and stratospheric aerosol injection as the methods with the "highest affordability" (lowest costs). More recently, research into costs of solar radiation management have been published.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>  This suggests that "well designed systems" might be available for costs in the order of a few hundred million to tens of billions of dollars per year.<sup id="cite_ref-benefits_risks_costs_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-benefits_risks_costs-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>  These are much lower than <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_climate_change_mitigation" title="Economics of climate change mitigation">costs to achieve comprehensive reductions in CO<sub>2</sub> emissions</a>. Such costs would be within the budget of most nations, and even some wealthy individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethics_and_responsibility">Ethics and responsibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ethics and responsibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Climate engineering would represent a large-scale, intentional effort to modify the climate. It would differ from activities such as burning fossil fuels, as they change the climate inadvertently. Intentional climate change is often viewed differently from a moral standpoint.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> It raises questions of whether humans have the right to change the climate deliberately, and under what conditions. For example, there may be an ethical distinction between climate engineering to minimize global warming and doing so to optimize the climate. Furthermore, ethical arguments often confront larger considerations of worldview, including individual and social religious commitments.  This may imply that discussions of climate engineering should reflect on how religious commitments might influence the discourse.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>  For many people, religious beliefs are pivotal in defining the role of human beings in the wider world. Some religious communities might claim that humans have no responsibility in managing the climate, instead seeing such world systems as the exclusive domain of a Creator.  In contrast, other religious communities might see the human role as one of "stewardship" or benevolent management of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>  The question of ethics also relates to issues of policy decision-making.  For example, the selection of a globally agreed target temperature is a significant problem in any climate engineering <a href="/wiki/Governance" title="Governance">governance</a> regime, as different countries or interest groups may seek different global temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-geoengineering_option_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-geoengineering_option-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has been argued that regardless of the economic, scientific and technical aspects, the difficulty of achieving concerted political action on global warming requires other approaches.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Those arguing political expediency say the difficulty of achieving meaningful emissions cuts<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> and the effective failure of the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto Protocol</a> demonstrate the practical difficulties of achieving carbon dioxide <a href="/wiki/Emissions_reduction" class="mw-redirect" title="Emissions reduction">emissions reduction</a> by the agreement of the <a href="/wiki/International_community" title="International community">international community</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> However, others point to support for climate engineering proposals among think tanks with a history of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming_skepticism" class="mw-redirect" title="Global warming skepticism">global warming skepticism</a> and opposition to emissions reductions as evidence that the prospect of climate engineering is itself already politicized and being promoted as part of an argument against the need for (and viability of) emissions reductions; that, rather than climate engineering being a solution to the difficulties of emissions reductions, the prospect of climate engineering is being used as part of an argument to stall emissions reductions in the first place.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Risks_and_criticisms">Risks and criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Risks and criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation_management#Limitations_and_risks" title="Solar radiation management">Solar radiation management §&#160;Limitations and risks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Technological_fix#Concerns" title="Technological fix">Technological fix §&#160;Concerns</a></div>

This <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">ocean acidification</a> will still be a major problem unless <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric</a> CO<sub>2</sub> is reduced.</div></div></div>
<p>Various criticisms have been made of climate engineering,<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> particularly solar radiation management (SRM) methods.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Decision making suffers from intransitivity of policy choice.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Some commentators appear fundamentally opposed.  Groups such as <a href="/wiki/ETC_Group_(AGETC)" title="ETC Group (AGETC)">ETC Group</a><sup id="cite_ref-ETC_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ETC-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> and individuals such as <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Pierrehumbert" title="Raymond Pierrehumbert">Raymond Pierrehumbert</a> have called for a moratorium on climate engineering techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-worldchanging1_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-worldchanging1-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-csmonitor1_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-csmonitor1-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ineffectiveness">Ineffectiveness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ineffectiveness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The effectiveness of the techniques proposed may fall short of predictions. In ocean <a href="/wiki/Iron_fertilization" title="Iron fertilization">iron fertilization</a>, for example, the amount of carbon dioxide removed from the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> may be much lower than predicted, as carbon taken up by <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a> may be released back into the atmosphere from dead plankton, rather than being carried to the bottom of the sea and sequestered.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Model results from a 2016 study, suggest that blooming algae could even accelerate Arctic warming.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moral_hazard_or_risk_compensation">Moral hazard or risk compensation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Moral hazard or risk compensation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The existence of such techniques may reduce the political and social impetus to reduce carbon emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>  This has generally been called a potential <a href="/wiki/Moral_Hazard" class="mw-redirect" title="Moral Hazard">moral hazard</a>, although <a href="/wiki/Risk_compensation" title="Risk compensation">risk compensation</a> may be a more accurate term. This concern causes many environmental groups and campaigners to be reluctant to advocate or discuss climate engineering for fear of reducing the imperative to cut <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-Moral_hazard_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moral_hazard-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> However, several public opinion surveys and focus groups have found evidence of either assertions of a desire to increase emission cuts in the face of climate engineering, or of no effect.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> Other modelling work suggests that the threat of climate engineering may in fact increase the likelihood of emissions reduction.<sup id="cite_ref-doi10.1007/s10584-011-0102-0_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-doi10.1007/s10584-011-0102-0-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation_management#Governance" title="Solar radiation management">Solar radiation management §&#160;Governance</a></div><p>Climate engineering opens up various political and economic issues. The governance issues characterizing carbon dioxide removal compared to solar radiation management tend to be distinct. Carbon dioxide removal techniques are typically slow to act, expensive, and entail risks that are relatively familiar, such as the risk of carbon dioxide leakage from underground storage formations. In contrast, solar radiation management methods are fast-acting, comparatively cheap, and involve novel and more significant risks such as regional climate disruptions. As a result of these differing characteristics, the key governance problem for carbon dioxide removal (as with emissions reductions) is making sure actors do enough of it (the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Free_rider_problem" class="mw-redirect" title="Free rider problem">free rider problem</a>"), whereas the key governance issue for solar radiation management is making sure actors do not do too much (the "free driver" problem).<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Domestic and international governance vary by the proposed climate engineering method. There is presently a lack of a universally agreed framework for the regulation of either climate engineering activity or research. The <a href="/wiki/London_Convention_(1972)" class="mw-redirect" title="London Convention (1972)">London Convention</a> addresses some aspects of the law in relation to <a href="/wiki/List_of_proposed_geoengineering_schemes" class="mw-redirect" title="List of proposed geoengineering schemes">biomass ocean storage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ocean_fertilization" title="Ocean fertilization">ocean fertilization</a>. Scientists at the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Martin_School" title="Oxford Martin School">Oxford Martin School</a> at <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford University">Oxford University</a> have proposed a set of voluntary principles, which may guide climate engineering research. The short version of the 'Oxford Principles'<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> is:
</p>
<ul><li>Principle 1: Geoengineering to be regulated as a <a href="/wiki/Public_good" title="Public good">public good</a>.</li>
<li>Principle 2: Public participation in geoengineering decision-making</li>
<li>Principle 3: Disclosure of geoengineering research and <a href="/wiki/Open_access_publication" class="mw-redirect" title="Open access publication">open publication</a> of results</li>
<li>Principle 4: Independent assessment of impacts</li>
<li>Principle 5: Governance before deployment</li></ul>
<p>These principles have been endorsed by the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons of the United Kingdom</a> Science and Technology <a href="/wiki/Select_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="Select Committee">Select Committee</a> on “The Regulation of Geoengineering”,<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> and have been referred to by authors discussing the issue of governance.<sup id="cite_ref-economist.com_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-economist.com-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Asilomar_conference" class="mw-redirect" title="Asilomar conference">Asilomar conference</a> was replicated to deal with the issue of climate engineering governance,<sup id="cite_ref-economist.com_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-economist.com-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> and covered in a <a href="/wiki/TV_documentary" class="mw-redirect" title="TV documentary">TV documentary</a>, broadcast in Canada.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementation_issues">Implementation issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Implementation issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is general consensus<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (May 2017)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> that no climate engineering technique is currently sufficiently safe or effective to greatly reduce climate change risks, for the reasons listed above. However, some may be able to contribute to reducing climate risks within relatively short times.
</p><p>All proposed solar radiation management techniques require implementation on a relatively large scale, in order to impact the Earth's climate. The least costly proposals are budgeted at tens of billions of US dollars annually.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Space_sunshade" title="Space sunshade">Space sunshades</a> would cost far more. Who was to bear the substantial costs of some climate engineering techniques may be hard to agree. However, the more effective solar radiation management proposals currently appear to have low enough direct implementation costs that it would be in the interests of several single countries to implement them unilaterally.
</p><p>In contrast, carbon dioxide removal, like greenhouse gas emissions reductions, have impacts proportional to their scale. These techniques would not be "implemented" in the same sense as solar radiation management ones. The problem structure of carbon dioxide removal resembles that of emissions cuts, in that both are somewhat expensive <a href="/wiki/Public_good" title="Public good">public goods</a>, whose provision presents a <a href="/wiki/Collective_action" title="Collective action">collective action problem</a>.
</p><p>Before they are ready to be used, most techniques would require technical development processes that are not yet in place. As a result, many promising proposed climate engineering do not yet have the engineering development or experimental evidence to determine their feasibility or efficacy.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_perception">Public perception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Public perception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a 2017 focus group study conducted by the <a href="/wiki/Cooperative_Institute_for_Research_in_Environmental_Sciences" title="Cooperative Institute for Research in Environmental Sciences">Cooperative Institute for Research in Environmental Sciences</a> (CIRES) in the United States, Japan, New Zealand and Sweden, participants were asked about carbon sequestration options, reflection proposals such as with space mirrors, or brightening of clouds, and their majority responses could be summed up as follows:
</p>
<ul><li>What happens if the technologies backfire with unintended consequences?</li>
<li>Are these solutions treating the symptoms of climate change rather than the cause?</li>
<li>Shouldn’t we just change our lifestyle and consumption patterns to fight climate change, making climate engineering a last resort?</li>
<li>Isn’t there a greater need to address political solutions to reduce our emissions?</li></ul>
<p>Moderators floated then the idea of a future "climate emergency" such as rapid environmental change. The participants felt that mitigation and adaptation to climate change were strongly preferred options in such a situation, and climate engineering was seen as a last resort.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evaluation_of_climate_engineering">Evaluation of climate engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Evaluation of climate engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most of what is known about the suggested techniques is based on laboratory experiments, observations of natural phenomena, and on <a href="/wiki/Computer_modelling" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer modelling">computer modelling</a> techniques. Some proposed climate engineering methods employ methods that have analogues in natural phenomena such as <a href="/wiki/Stratospheric_sulfur_aerosols" title="Stratospheric sulfur aerosols">stratospheric sulfur aerosols</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cloud_condensation_nuclei" title="Cloud condensation nuclei">cloud condensation nuclei</a>. As such, studies about the efficacy of these methods can draw on information already available from other research, such as that following the 1991 <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_eruption" class="mw-redirect" title="Volcanic eruption">eruption</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pinatubo" title="Mount Pinatubo">Mount Pinatubo</a>. However, comparative evaluation of the relative merits of each technology is complicated, especially given modelling uncertainties and the early stage of engineering development of many proposed climate engineering methods.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Reports into climate engineering have also been published in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Institution_of_Mechanical_Engineers" title="Institution of Mechanical Engineers">Institution of Mechanical Engineers</a><sup id="cite_ref-imeche09_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-imeche09-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-roysoc09_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-roysoc09-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The IMechE report examined a small subset of proposed methods (air capture, urban albedo and algal-based CO<sub>2</sub> capture techniques), and its main conclusions were that climate engineering should be researched and trialled at the small scale alongside a wider <a href="/wiki/Decarbonisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Decarbonisation">decarbonisation</a> of the economy.<sup id="cite_ref-imeche09_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-imeche09-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Royal Society review examined a wide range of proposed climate engineering methods and evaluated them in terms of effectiveness, affordability, timeliness and safety (assigning <a href="/wiki/Qualitative_data" class="mw-redirect" title="Qualitative data">qualitative</a> estimates in each assessment). The report divided proposed  methods into "carbon dioxide removal" (CDR) and "solar radiation management" (SRM) approaches that respectively address longwave and shortwave radiation. The key recommendations of the report were that "Parties to the <a href="/wiki/UNFCCC" class="mw-redirect" title="UNFCCC">UNFCCC</a> should make increased efforts towards mitigating and adapting to climate change, and in particular to agreeing to global emissions reductions", and that "[nothing] now known about climate engineering options gives any reason to diminish these efforts".<sup id="cite_ref-roysoc09_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-roysoc09-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Nonetheless, the report also recommended that "research and development of climate engineering options should be undertaken to investigate whether low risk methods can be made available if it becomes necessary to reduce the rate of warming this century".<sup id="cite_ref-roysoc09_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-roysoc09-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In a 2009 review study, Lenton and Vaughan evaluated a range of proposed climate engineering techniques from those that sequester CO<sub>2</sub> from the atmosphere and decrease <a href="/wiki/Outgoing_longwave_radiation" title="Outgoing longwave radiation">longwave radiation</a> trapping, to those that decrease the Earth's receipt of <a href="/wiki/Shortwave_radiation" title="Shortwave radiation">shortwave radiation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-lenton09_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lenton09-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In order to permit a comparison of disparate techniques, they used a common evaluation for each technique based on its effect on net radiative forcing. As such, the review examined the scientific plausibility of proposed methods rather than the practical considerations such as engineering feasibility or economic cost. Lenton and Vaughan found that "[air] capture and storage shows the greatest potential, combined with afforestation, reforestation and bio-char production", and noted that "other suggestions that have received considerable media attention, in particular "ocean pipes" appear to be ineffective".<sup id="cite_ref-lenton09_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lenton09-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> They concluded that "[climate] geoengineering is best considered as a potential complement to the mitigation of CO<sub>2</sub> emissions, rather than as an alternative to it".<sup id="cite_ref-lenton09_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lenton09-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2011, a <a href="/wiki/Bipartisan_Policy_Center" title="Bipartisan Policy Center">Bipartisan Policy Center</a> panel issued a report urging immediate researching and testing in case "the <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> system reaches a 'tipping point' and swift remedial action is required".<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Academy_of_Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: National Academy of Sciences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> conducted a 21-month project to study the potential impacts, benefits, and costs of two different types of  climate engineering: carbon dioxide removal and albedo modification (solar radiation management). The differences between these two classes of  climate engineering "led the committee to evaluate the two types of approaches separately in companion reports, a distinction it hopes carries over to future scientific and policy discussions."<sup id="cite_ref-www8.nationalacademies.org_86-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www8.nationalacademies.org-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>
According to the two-volume study released in February 2015:</p><blockquote><p>Climate intervention is no substitute for reductions in carbon dioxide emissions and adaptation efforts aimed at reducing the negative consequences of climate change. However, as our planet enters a period of changing climate never before experienced in recorded human history, interest is growing in the potential for deliberate intervention in the climate system to counter climate change. ...Carbon dioxide removal strategies address a key driver of climate change, but research is needed to fully assess if any of these technologies could be appropriate for large-scale deployment. Albedo modification strategies could rapidly cool the planet’s surface but pose environmental and other risks that are not well understood and therefore should not be deployed at climate-altering scales; more research is needed to determine if albedo modification approaches could be viable in the future.<sup id="cite_ref-nas-sites.org_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nas-sites.org-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote><p>The project was sponsored by the <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a>, U.S. Intelligence Community, <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a>, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">U.S. Department of Energy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-www8.nationalacademies.org_86-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www8.nationalacademies.org-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_engineering&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>
The <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) assessed the scientific literature on climate engineering (referred to as "geoengineering" in its reports), in which it considered carbon dioxide removal and solar radiation separately. Its <a href="/wiki/IPCC_Fifth_Assessment_Report" title="IPCC Fifth Assessment Report">Fifth Assessment Report</a> states:<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup></p><blockquote><p>Models consistently suggest that SRM would generally reduce climate differences compared to a world with elevated GHG concentrations and no SRM; however, there would also be residual regional differences in climate (e.g., temperature and rainfall) when compared to a climate without elevated GHGs....</p></blockquote><blockquote><p>Models suggest that if SRM methods were realizable they would be effective in countering increasing temperatures, and would be less, but still, effective in countering some other climate changes. SRM would not counter all effects of climate change, and all proposed geoengineering methods also carry risks and side effects. Additional consequences cannot yet be anticipated as the level of scientific understanding about both SRM and CDR is low. There are also many (political, ethical, and practical) issues involving geoengineering that are beyond the scope of this report.</p></blockquote>
