<p><b>Lutetium</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> with symbol <b>Lu</b> and <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">atomic number</a> 71. It is a silvery white <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metal</a>, which resists corrosion in dry air, but not in moist air. Lutetium is the last element in the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide" title="Lanthanide">lanthanide</a> series, and it is traditionally counted among the <a href="/wiki/Rare_earths" class="mw-redirect" title="Rare earths">rare earths</a>. Lutetium is sometimes considered the first element of the 6th-period <a href="/wiki/Transition_metal" title="Transition metal">transition metals</a>, although <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">lanthanum</a> is more often considered as such.
</p><p>Lutetium was independently discovered in 1907 by French scientist <a href="/wiki/Georges_Urbain" title="Georges Urbain">Georges Urbain</a>, Austrian mineralogist <a href="/wiki/Freiherr" title="Freiherr">Baron</a> <a href="/wiki/Carl_Auer_von_Welsbach" title="Carl Auer von Welsbach">Carl Auer von Welsbach</a>, and American chemist <a href="/wiki/Charles_James_(chemist)" title="Charles James (chemist)">Charles James</a>. All of these researchers found lutetium as an impurity in the mineral <a href="/wiki/Ytterbia" class="mw-redirect" title="Ytterbia">ytterbia</a>, which was previously thought to consist entirely of ytterbium. The dispute on the priority of the discovery occurred shortly after, with Urbain and Welsbach accusing each other of publishing results influenced by the published research of the other; the naming honor went to Urbain, as he had published his results earlier. He chose the name lutecium for the new element, but in 1949 the spelling of element 71 was changed to lutetium. In 1909, the priority was finally granted to Urbain and his names were adopted as official ones; however, the name <i>cassiopeium</i> (or later <i>cassiopium</i>) for element 71 proposed by Welsbach was used by many German scientists until the 1950s.
</p><p>Lutetium is not a particularly abundant element, although it is significantly more common than <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> in the earth's crust. It has few specific uses.  Lutetium-176 is a relatively abundant (2.5%) radioactive isotope with a half-life of about 38 billion years, used to <a href="/wiki/Lutetium-hafnium_dating" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutetium-hafnium dating">determine the age</a> of minerals and <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteorites</a>. Lutetium usually occurs in association with the element <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Did someone confuse Yttrium and Ytterbium here or something? Seems very odd, **but** possible! (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and is sometimes used in metal <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloys</a> and as a <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalyst">catalyst</a> in various chemical reactions. <sup>177</sup>Lu-<a href="/wiki/DOTA-TATE" title="DOTA-TATE">DOTA-TATE</a> is used for radionuclide therapy (see <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_medicine" title="Nuclear medicine">Nuclear medicine</a>) on neuroendocrine tumours. Lutetium has the highest <a href="/wiki/Brinell_scale" title="Brinell scale">Brinell hardness</a> of any lanthanide, at 890–1300 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">MPa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lutetium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">Physical properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lutetium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A lutetium atom has 71 electrons, arranged in the <a href="/wiki/Electron_configuration" title="Electron configuration">configuration</a> &#91;<a href="/wiki/Xenon" title="Xenon">Xe</a>&#93;&#160;4f<sup>14</sup>5d<sup>1</sup>6s<sup>2</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cotton_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cotton-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> When entering a chemical reaction, the atom loses its two outermost electrons and the single 5d-electron. The lutetium atom is the smallest among the lanthanide atoms, due to the <a href="/wiki/Lanthanide_contraction" title="Lanthanide contraction">lanthanide contraction</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and as a result lutetium has the highest density, melting point, and hardness of the lanthanides.<sup id="cite_ref-Parker_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Parker-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_properties_and_compounds">Chemical properties and compounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lutetium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chemical properties and compounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Lutetium_compounds" title="Category:Lutetium compounds">Category:Lutetium compounds</a>.</div>
<p>Lutetium's compounds always contain the element in the oxidation state +3.  Aqueous solutions of most lutetium salts are colorless and form white crystalline solids upon drying, with the common exception of the iodide. The soluble salts, such as nitrate, sulfate and acetate form hydrates upon crystallization. The <a href="/wiki/Lutetium(III)_oxide" title="Lutetium(III) oxide">oxide</a>, hydroxide, fluoride, carbonate, phosphate and <a href="/wiki/Oxalate" title="Oxalate">oxalate</a> are insoluble in water.<sup id="cite_ref-patnaik_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lutetium metal is slightly unstable in air at standard conditions, but it burns readily at 150&#160;°C to form lutetium oxide. The resulting compound is known to absorb water and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, and may be used to remove vapors of these compounds from closed atmospheres.<sup id="cite_ref-aaaaaa_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aaaaaa-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Similar observations are made during reaction between lutetium and water (slow when cold and fast when hot); lutetium hydroxide is formed in the reaction.<sup id="cite_ref-ffff_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ffff-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Lutetium metal is known to react with the four lightest halogens to form tri<a href="/wiki/Halides" class="mw-redirect" title="Halides">halides</a>; all of them (except the fluoride) are soluble in water.
</p><p>Lutetium dissolves readily in weak acids<sup id="cite_ref-aaaaaa_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aaaaaa-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and dilute <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to form solutions containing the colorless lutetium ions, which are coordinated by between seven and nine water molecules, the average being [Lu(H<sub>2</sub>O)<sub>8.2</sub>]<sup>3+</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-Persson2010_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Persson2010-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2&#160;Lu + 3&#160;H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> → 2&#160;Lu<sup>3+</sup> + 3&#160;SO<span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:80%;text-align:left"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">2–</sup><br /><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline">4</sub></span> + 3&#160;H<sub>2</sub>↑</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Isotopes">Isotopes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lutetium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Isotopes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_lutetium" title="Isotopes of lutetium">Isotopes of lutetium</a></div>
<p>Lutetium occurs on the Earth in form of two isotopes: lutetium-175 and lutetium-176. Out of these two, only the former is stable, making the element <a href="/wiki/Monoisotopic_element" title="Monoisotopic element">monoisotopic</a>. The latter one, lutetium-176, decays via <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a> of 3.78&#215;10<sup>10</sup> years; it makes up about 2.5% of natural lutetium.<sup id="cite_ref-IS_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IS-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
To date, 32 <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_radioisotope" title="Synthetic radioisotope">synthetic radioisotopes</a> of the element have been characterized, ranging in mass from 149.973 (lutetium-150) to 183.961 (lutetium-184); the most stable such isotopes are lutetium-174 with a half-life of 3.31 years, and lutetium-173 with a half-life of 1.37 years.<sup id="cite_ref-IS_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IS-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> All of the remaining <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive</a> isotopes have half-lives that are less than 9 days, and the majority of these have half-lives that are less than half an hour.<sup id="cite_ref-IS_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IS-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Isotopes lighter than the stable lutetium-175 decay via <a href="/wiki/Electron_capture" title="Electron capture">electron capture</a> (to produce isotopes of <a href="/wiki/Ytterbium" title="Ytterbium">ytterbium</a>), with some <a href="/wiki/Alpha_emission" class="mw-redirect" title="Alpha emission">alpha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission" title="Positron emission">positron emission</a>; the heavier isotopes decay primarily via beta decay, producing hafnium isotopes.<sup id="cite_ref-IS_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IS-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The element also has 42 <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_isomer" title="Nuclear isomer">nuclear isomers</a>, with masses of 150, 151, 153–162, 166–180 (not every mass number corresponds to only one isomer). The most stable of them are lutetium-177m, with half-life of 160.4 days and lutetium-174m, with half-life of 142 days; this is longer than half-lives of the ground states of all radioactive lutetium isotopes, except only for lutetium-173, 174, and 176.<sup id="cite_ref-IS_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IS-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lutetium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lutetium, derived from the Latin <i><a href="/wiki/Lutetia" title="Lutetia">Lutetia</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>), was independently <a href="/wiki/Discoveries_of_the_chemical_elements" class="mw-redirect" title="Discoveries of the chemical elements">discovered</a> in 1907 by French scientist <a href="/wiki/Georges_Urbain" title="Georges Urbain">Georges Urbain</a>, Austrian mineralogist Baron <a href="/wiki/Carl_Auer_von_Welsbach" title="Carl Auer von Welsbach">Carl Auer von Welsbach</a>, and American chemist Charles James.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> They found it as an impurity in <a href="/wiki/Ytterbia" class="mw-redirect" title="Ytterbia">ytterbia</a>, which was thought by Swiss chemist <a href="/wiki/Jean_Charles_Galissard_de_Marignac" title="Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac">Jean Charles Galissard de Marignac</a> to consist entirely of <a href="/wiki/Ytterbium" title="Ytterbium">ytterbium</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1st_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1st-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The scientists proposed different names for the elements: Urbain chose <i>neoytterbium</i> and <i>lutecium</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Fra_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fra-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> whereas Welsbach chose <i>aldebaranium</i> and <i>cassiopeium</i> (after <a href="/wiki/Aldebaran" title="Aldebaran">Aldebaran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cassiopeia_(constellation)" title="Cassiopeia (constellation)">Cassiopeia</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Deu_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Deu-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Both of these articles accused the other man of publishing results based on those of the author.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Commission_on_Isotopic_Abundances_and_Atomic_Weights" title="Commission on Isotopic Abundances and Atomic Weights">International Commission on Atomic Weights</a>, which was then responsible for the attribution of new element names, settled the dispute in 1909 by granting priority to Urbain and adopting his names as official ones, based on the fact that the separation of lutetium from Marignac's ytterbium was first described by Urbain;<sup id="cite_ref-1st_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1st-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> after Urbain's names were recognized, neoytterbium was reverted to ytterbium. Until the 1950s, some German-speaking chemists called lutetium by Welsbach's name, <i>cassiopeium</i>; in 1949, the spelling of element 71 was changed to lutetium. The reason for this was that Welsbach's 1907 samples of lutetium had been pure, while Urbain's 1907 samples only contained traces of lutetium.<sup id="cite_ref-rare-earth-handbook_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rare-earth-handbook-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> This later misled Urbain into thinking that he had discovered element 72, which he named celtium, which was actually very pure lutetium. The later discrediting of Urbain's work on element 72 led to a reappraisal of Welsbach's work on element 71, so that the element was renamed to <i>cassiopeium</i> in German-speaking countries for some time.<sup id="cite_ref-rare-earth-handbook_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rare-earth-handbook-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Charles James, who stayed out of the priority argument, worked on a much larger scale and possessed the largest supply of lutetium at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-Emsley240_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley240-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Pure lutetium metal was first produced in 1953.<sup id="cite_ref-Emsley240_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley240-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence_and_production">Occurrence and production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lutetium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Occurrence and production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Monazit_-_Mosambik,_O-Afrika.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Monazit_-_Mosambik%2C_O-Afrika.jpg/220px-Monazit_-_Mosambik%2C_O-Afrika.jpg" width="220" height="195" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Monazit_-_Mosambik%2C_O-Afrika.jpg/330px-Monazit_-_Mosambik%2C_O-Afrika.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Monazit_-_Mosambik%2C_O-Afrika.jpg/440px-Monazit_-_Mosambik%2C_O-Afrika.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1300" data-file-height="1150" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Monazit_-_Mosambik,_O-Afrika.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Monazite</div></div></div>
<p>Found with almost all other rare-earth metals but never by itself, lutetium is very difficult to separate from other elements. Its principal commercial source is as a by-product from the processing of the rare earth <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> mineral <a href="/wiki/Monazite" title="Monazite">monazite</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">Ce</a>,<a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">La</a>,...)<a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">P</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a><sub>4</sub>, which  has concentrations of only 0.0001% of the element,<sup id="cite_ref-aaaaaa_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aaaaaa-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> not much higher than the abundance of lutetium in the Earth crust of about 0.5&#160;mg/kg. No lutetium-dominant minerals are currently known.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The main mining areas are China, United States, Brazil, India, Sri Lanka and Australia. The world production of lutetium (in the form of oxide) is about 10 tonnes per year.<sup id="cite_ref-Emsley240_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley240-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Pure lutetium metal is very difficult to prepare. It is one of the rarest and most expensive of the rare earth metals with the price about US$10,000 per kilogram, or about one-fourth that of <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Crushed minerals are treated with hot concentrated <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> to produce water-soluble sulfates of rare earths. <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">Thorium</a> precipitates out of solution as hydroxide and is removed. After that the solution is treated with <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_oxalate" title="Ammonium oxalate">ammonium oxalate</a> to convert rare earths into their insoluble oxalates. The oxalates are converted to oxides by annealing. The oxides are dissolved in <a href="/wiki/Nitric_acid" title="Nitric acid">nitric acid</a> that excludes one of the main components, <a href="/wiki/Cerium" title="Cerium">cerium</a>, whose oxide is insoluble in HNO<sub>3</sub>. Several rare earth metals, including lutetium, are separated as a double salt with <a href="/wiki/Ammonium_nitrate" title="Ammonium nitrate">ammonium nitrate</a> by crystallization. Lutetium is separated by <a href="/wiki/Ion_exchange" title="Ion exchange">ion exchange</a>. In this process, rare-earth ions are sorbed onto suitable ion-exchange resin by exchange with hydrogen, ammonium or cupric ions present in the resin. Lutetium salts are then selectively washed out by suitable complexing agent. Lutetium metal is then obtained by <a href="/wiki/Redox" title="Redox">reduction</a> of anhydrous Lu<a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">Cl</a><sub>3</sub> or Lu<a href="/wiki/Fluorine" title="Fluorine">F</a><sub>3</sub> by either an <a href="/wiki/Alkali_metal" title="Alkali metal">alkali metal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Alkaline_earth_metal" title="Alkaline earth metal">alkaline earth metal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-patnaik_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-patnaik-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>2 LuCl<sub>3</sub> + 3 Ca → 2 Lu + 3 CaCl<sub>2</sub></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lutetium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of production difficulty and high price, lutetium has very few commercial uses, especially since it is rarer than most of the other lanthanides but is chemically not very different. However, stable lutetium can be used as <a href="/wiki/Catalyst" class="mw-redirect" title="Catalyst">catalysts</a> in <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> <a href="/wiki/Cracking_(chemistry)" title="Cracking (chemistry)">cracking</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">refineries</a> and can also be used in alkylation, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogenation" title="Hydrogenation">hydrogenation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Polymerization" title="Polymerization">polymerization</a> applications.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lutetium_aluminium_garnet" title="Lutetium aluminium garnet">Lutetium aluminium garnet</a> (Al<sub>5</sub>Lu<sub>3</sub>O<sub>12</sub>) has been proposed for use as a lens material in high <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">refractive index</a> <a href="/wiki/Immersion_lithography" title="Immersion lithography">immersion lithography</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, a tiny amount of lutetium is added as a <a href="/wiki/Dopant" title="Dopant">dopant</a> to <a href="/wiki/Gadolinium_gallium_garnet" title="Gadolinium gallium garnet">gadolinium gallium garnet</a> (GGG), which is used in <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_bubble_memory" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnetic bubble memory">magnetic bubble memory</a> devices.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Cerium-doped lutetium oxyorthosilicate (LSO) is currently the preferred compound for detectors in <a href="/wiki/Positron_emission_tomography" title="Positron emission tomography">positron emission tomography</a> (PET).<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Lutetium aluminium garnet (LuAG) is used as a phosphor in LED light bulbs.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Aside from stable lutetium, its radioactive isotopes have several specific uses. The suitable half-life and decay mode made lutetium-176 used as a pure beta emitter, using lutetium which has been exposed to <a href="/wiki/Neutron_activation" title="Neutron activation">neutron activation</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Lutetium%E2%80%93hafnium_dating" title="Lutetium–hafnium dating">lutetium–hafnium dating</a> to date <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">meteorites</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The synthetic isotope <a href="/wiki/Lutetium_(177Lu)_DOTA-octreotate" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutetium (177Lu) DOTA-octreotate">lutetium-177 bound to octreotate</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Somatostatin" title="Somatostatin">somatostatin</a> analogue), is used experimentally in targeted <a href="/wiki/Radionuclide" title="Radionuclide">radionuclide</a> therapy for <a href="/wiki/Neuroendocrine_tumors" class="mw-redirect" title="Neuroendocrine tumors">neuroendocrine tumors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Indeed, lutetium-177 is seeing increasing use as a radionuclide, in neuroendrocine tumor therapy and bone pain palliation.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>  Research indicates that lutetium-ion atomic clocks could provide greater accuracy than any existing atomic clock.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lutetium_tantalate" title="Lutetium tantalate">Lutetium tantalate</a> (LuTaO<sub>4</sub>) is the densest known stable white material (density 9.81&#160;g/cm<sup>3</sup>)<sup id="cite_ref-lu1_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lu1-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and therefore is an ideal host for X-ray phosphors.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-appl_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appl-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The only denser white material is <a href="/wiki/Thorium_dioxide" title="Thorium dioxide">thorium dioxide</a>, with density of 10&#160;g/cm<sup>3</sup>, but the thorium it contains is radioactive.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precautions">Precautions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lutetium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Precautions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like other rare-earth metals, lutetium is regarded as having a low degree of toxicity, but its compounds should be handled with care nonetheless: for example, lutetium fluoride inhalation is dangerous and the compound irritates skin.<sup id="cite_ref-aaaaaa_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aaaaaa-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Lutetium nitrate may be dangerous as it may <a href="/wiki/Explosion" title="Explosion">explode</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">burn</a> once heated. Lutetium oxide powder is toxic as well if inhaled or ingested.<sup id="cite_ref-aaaaaa_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aaaaaa-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Similarly to the other rare-earth metals, lutetium has no known biological role, but it is found even in <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>, concentrating in bones, and to a lesser extent in the liver and kidneys.<sup id="cite_ref-Emsley240_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley240-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Lutetium salts are known to occur together with other lanthanide salts in nature; the element is the least abundant in the human body of all lanthanides.<sup id="cite_ref-Emsley240_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley240-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Human diets have not been monitored for lutetium content, so it is not known how much the average human takes in, but estimations show the amount is only about several micrograms per year, all coming from tiny amounts taken by plants. Soluble lutetium salts are mildly toxic, but insoluble ones are not.<sup id="cite_ref-Emsley240_18-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emsley240-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
