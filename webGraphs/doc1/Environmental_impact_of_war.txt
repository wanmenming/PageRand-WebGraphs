<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Environmental impact of war</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
				<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>				<div id="contentSub"></div>
				<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>				<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BrennendeOelquellenKuwait1991.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/BrennendeOelquellenKuwait1991.jpg/220px-BrennendeOelquellenKuwait1991.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/BrennendeOelquellenKuwait1991.jpg/330px-BrennendeOelquellenKuwait1991.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/BrennendeOelquellenKuwait1991.jpg/440px-BrennendeOelquellenKuwait1991.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="424" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BrennendeOelquellenKuwait1991.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Kuwaiti_oil_fires" title="Kuwaiti oil fires">Kuwaiti oil fires set by retreating Iraqi forces</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a> caused a dramatic decrease in air quality.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Defoliation_agent_spraying.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Defoliation_agent_spraying.jpg/220px-Defoliation_agent_spraying.jpg" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Defoliation_agent_spraying.jpg/330px-Defoliation_agent_spraying.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Defoliation_agent_spraying.jpg/440px-Defoliation_agent_spraying.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2600" data-file-height="2597" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Defoliation_agent_spraying.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Agent_Orange" title="Agent Orange">Agent Orange</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Herbicide" title="Herbicide">herbicide</a>, being sprayed on farmland during the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a></div></div></div>
<p>Study of the <b>environmental impact of war</b> focuses on the modernization of <a href="/wiki/Warfare" class="mw-redirect" title="Warfare">warfare</a> and its increasing effects on the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a>. <a href="/wiki/Scorched_earth" title="Scorched earth">Scorched earth</a> methods have been used for much of recorded history. However, methods of modern warfare cause far greater devastation on the <a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Environment (biophysical)">environment</a>. The progression of warfare from <a href="/wiki/Chemical_weapons" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical weapons">chemical weapons</a> to <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear weapons">nuclear weapons</a> has increasingly created stress on <a href="/wiki/Ecosystems" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecosystems">ecosystems</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a>. Specific examples of the environmental impact of war include: <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Rwandan_Civil_War" title="Rwandan Civil War">Rwandan Civil War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kosovo_War" title="Kosovo War">Kosovo War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_events">Historical events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_war&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Vietnam.2C_Rwanda.2C_and_the_environment"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Vietnam,_Rwanda,_and_the_environment">Vietnam, Rwanda, and the environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_war&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vietnam, Rwanda, and the environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:%27Ranch_Hand%27_run.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/%27Ranch_Hand%27_run.jpg/220px-%27Ranch_Hand%27_run.jpg" width="220" height="158" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/%27Ranch_Hand%27_run.jpg/330px-%27Ranch_Hand%27_run.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/%27Ranch_Hand%27_run.jpg/440px-%27Ranch_Hand%27_run.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1800" data-file-height="1293" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:%27Ranch_Hand%27_run.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Defoliant spray run, part of Operation Ranch Hand, during the Vietnam War by UC-123B Provider aircraft</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> had significant environmental implications by the use of chemical agents to destroy military significant vegetation. Enemies found an advantage in remaining invisible by blending into a civilian population or by taking cover in dense vegetation and opposing armies targeted natural ecosystems.<sup id="cite_ref-dw_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dw-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The US military used “more than 20 million gallons of <a href="/wiki/Herbicides" class="mw-redirect" title="Herbicides">herbicides</a>, were sprayed by the US to defoliate forests, clear growth along the borders of military sites and eliminate enemy crops."<sup id="cite_ref-king_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-king-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The chemical agents gave the US an advantage in wartime efforts. However, the vegetation was unable to regenerate and left behind bare <a href="/wiki/Mudflats" class="mw-redirect" title="Mudflats">mudflats</a> even years after spraying.<sup id="cite_ref-dw_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dw-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Not only was the vegetation affected, but also the wildlife: "a mid-1980s study by Vietnamese ecologists documented just 24 species of birds and 5 species of mammals present in sprayed forests and converted areas, compared to 145-170 bird species and 30-55 kinds of mammals in <a href="/wiki/Intact_forest_landscape" title="Intact forest landscape">intact forest</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-dw_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dw-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The uncertain long-term effects of these herbicides are now being discovered by looking at modified species distribution patterns through habitat degradation and loss in wetland systems, which absorbed the runoff from the mainland.<sup id="cite_ref-king_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-king-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The Rwandan genocide led to the killing of roughly 800,000 Tutsis and moderate Hutus. The war created a massive migration of nearly 2 million Hutus fleeing <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a> over the course of just a few weeks to <a href="/wiki/Refugee_camps" class="mw-redirect" title="Refugee camps">refugee camps</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a> and now modern day <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dw_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dw-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> This large displacement of people in refugee camps put pressure on the surrounding <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>. Forests were cleared in order to provide wood for building shelters and creating cooking fires:<sup id="cite_ref-dw_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dw-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> “these people suffered from harsh conditions and constituted an important threat impact to natural resources.”<sup id="cite_ref-sk_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Consequences from the conflict also included the degradation of National Parks and Reserves. The population crash in Rwanda shifted personnel and capital to other parts of the country, making it hard to protect wildlife.<sup id="cite_ref-sk_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>More broadly throughout Africa, war has been a major factor in the decline of wildlife populations inside national parks and other protected areas.<sup id="cite_ref-DaskinPringle_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DaskinPringle-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However, a growing number of ecological restoration initiatives, including in Rwanda's <a href="/wiki/Akagera_National_Park" title="Akagera National Park">Akagera National Park</a> and Mozambique's <a href="/wiki/Gorongosa_National_Park" title="Gorongosa National Park">Gorongosa National Park</a>, have shown that wildlife populations and whole ecosystems can be successfully rehabilitated even after devastating conflicts.<sup id="cite_ref-Pringle_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pringle-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Experts have emphasized that solving social, economic, and political problems is essential for the success of such efforts.<sup id="cite_ref-sk_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DaskinPringle_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DaskinPringle-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pringle_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pringle-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_war&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>World War II (WW II) drove a vast increase in production, militarized the production and transportation of commodity, while introduced many new environmental consequences, which can still be seen today. World War II was wide ranging in its human, animals, and material destruction. The postwar effects of World War II, both ecological and social, are still visible decades after the conflict. 
</p><p>During World War II, advanced technology was used to create aircraft, which were used in air raids. Aircraft during the war were used for transporting resources from different military bases and dropping bombs. These activities damaged habitats.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Similar to wildlife, ecosystems also suffer from the production of the <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise pollution</a> from military aircraft. During World War II, aircraft acted as a vector for the transportation of exotics whereby weeds and cultivated species were bought to oceanic island ecosystems by way of aircraft landing strips used for refueling and staging station during operations in the Pacific theatre.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Before the war, the isolated islands around Europe were the habitat of a high number of anemic species. Aerial warfare during World War II had an enormous influence on fluctuating population dynamics.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After four years of World War II in August 1945, the United States of America dropped an atomic bomb over the city of Hiroshima in Japan. About 70,000 people died in nine seconds at the bombing in Hiroshima. Three days after the bombing in Hiroshima, United States of America dropped a second atomic bomb in Nagasaki, Japan with the same devastating outcomes.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The nuclear weapon released a catastrophic load of energy. The temperature once the bombs were blasted reached about 7200°F.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> With temperature that high, all the flora and fauna are destroyed along with the infrastructure and human life in the impact zone.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When the atomic bomb was dropped, it released enormous quantity of energy and radioactive particles. The radioactive particles released contaminated the land and water for miles.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The initial blast increased the surface temperature, along with the crushing winds caused by the initial blast, trees and buildings in the path were all destroyed.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Animals which were caught within the nuclear blast wave have been impacted in numerous ways.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Terrestrial species are likely to experience more damages compared to aquatic species due to the overpressure injury.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Aquatic organisms are particularly sensitive to the effects of a blast.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The results from the atomic bomb caused a large die-off in the fish population and caused a cascade in the food web system.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During World War II, the combat zones in European forests experienced traumatic impacts from fighting. Behind the combat zones, timber from cut down trees to clear up the path for fighting. The shattered forests in the battle zones faced exploitation.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The use of heavily hazardous chemicals was first initiated during World War II.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The long-term effects of chemicals result from both their potential persistence and the poor disposal program of nations with stockpiled weapons.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> During World War I (WW I), German chemists had developed chlorine gas and mustard gas. The development of the gases led to many casualties, and poisoned lands on and near the battlefields.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Later in World War II, chemists developed even more harmful chemical bombs, which were packaged in barrels and directly deposited in the oceans.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The disposal of the chemicals in ocean runs the risk of metal-based containers corroding and leaching the chemical contents of the vessel into the ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Through the chemical disposal in the ocean, the contaminates may be spread throughout the various components of the ecosystems damaging marine and terrestrial ecosystems.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Marine ecosystems during World War II were damaged not only from chemical contaminates, but also from wreckage from naval ships, which leaked oil into the water. Oil contamination in the Atlantic Ocean due to World War II shipwrecks is estimated at over 15 million tonnes.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Oil spills are difficult to clean up and take many years to clean. To this day, traces of oil can still be found in the Atlantic Ocean from the naval shipwrecks which happened during World War II. 
</p><p>The use of chemicals during war helped increase the scale of chemical industries and helped show the government the value of scientific research to the government. The development of chemical research during the war also lead to the postwar development of agricultural pesticides.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The creation of pesticides was an upside for the years after the war. 
</p><p>The environmental impacts of World War II were very drastic, which allowed them to be seen in the Cold War and be seen today. The impacts of conflict, chemical contaminations, and aerial warfare all contribute to reduction in the population of global flora and fauna, as well as a reduction in species diversity<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gulf_War">Gulf War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_war&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gulf War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the 1991 <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kuwaiti_oil_fires" title="Kuwaiti oil fires">Kuwaiti oil fires</a> were a result of the scorched earth policy of Iraqi military forces retreating from <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a> in 1991 after conquering the country but being driven out by <a href="/wiki/Coalition_of_Gulf_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Coalition of Gulf War">Coalition</a> military forces. The <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War_oil_spill" title="Gulf War oil spill">Gulf War oil spill</a>, regarded as the worst oil spill in history, was caused when Iraqi forces opened valves at the Sea Island oil terminal and dumped oil from several tankers into the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>.
</p><p>Some American military personnel complained of <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War_syndrome" title="Gulf War syndrome">Gulf War syndrome</a>, typified by symptoms including immune system disorders and birth defects in their children. Whether it is due to time spent in active service during the war or for other reasons remains controversial.
</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_Gulf_wars" title="Environmental impact of Gulf wars">Environmental impact of Gulf wars</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_hazards">Environmental hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_war&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Environmental hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Environmental_issue" title="Environmental issue">Environmental issue</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Resources" class="mw-redirect" title="Resources">Resources</a> are a key source of <a href="/wiki/Conflict_(process)" title="Conflict (process)">conflict</a> between <a href="/wiki/Nation" title="Nation">nations</a>: "after the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> in particular, many have suggested that <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a> will exacerbate scarcities and become an additional source of armed conflict."<sup id="cite_ref-gled_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gled-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> A nation’s survival depends on resources from the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-gled_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gled-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Resources that are a source of armed conflict include territory, strategic raw materials, sources of energy, water, and food.<sup id="cite_ref-gled_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gled-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In order to maintain resource stability, chemical and nuclear warfare have been used by nations in order to protect or extract resources, and during conflict.<sup id="cite_ref-gled_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gled-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rob_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rob-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> These agents of war have been used frequently: “about 125,000 tons of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_agent" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical agent">chemical agent</a> were employed during World War I, and about 96,000 tons during the Viet-Nam conflict.”<sup id="cite_ref-rob_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rob-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nerve_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Nerve gas">Nerve gas</a>, also known as organophosphorous anticholinesterases, was used at lethal levels against human beings and destroyed a high number of nonhuman <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate" title="Vertebrate">vertebrate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrate</a> populations.<sup id="cite_ref-rob_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rob-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> However, contaminated vegetation would mostly be spared, and would only pose a threat to herbivores.<sup id="cite_ref-rob_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rob-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The result of innovations in chemical warfare led to a broad range of different chemicals for war and domestic use, but also resulted in unforeseen environmental damage.
</p><p>The progression of warfare and its effects on the environment continued with the invention of <a href="/wiki/Weapons_of_mass_destruction" class="mw-redirect" title="Weapons of mass destruction">weapons of mass destruction</a>. While today, <a href="/wiki/Weapons_of_mass_destruction" class="mw-redirect" title="Weapons of mass destruction">weapons of mass destruction</a> act as deterrents and the use of weapons of mass destruction during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> created significant environmental destruction. On top of the great loss in human life, “natural resources are usually the first to suffer: forests and wild life animals are wiped out.”<sup id="cite_ref-gled_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gled-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_warfare" title="Nuclear warfare">Nuclear warfare</a> imposes both direct and indirect effects on the environment. The physical destruction due to the blast or by the biospheric damage due to <a href="/wiki/Ionizing" class="mw-redirect" title="Ionizing">ionizing</a> <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Radiotoxicity" class="mw-redirect" title="Radiotoxicity">radiotoxicity</a> directly effect <a href="/wiki/Ecosystems" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecosystems">ecosystems</a> within the blast radius.<sup id="cite_ref-rob_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rob-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Also, the atmospheric or geospheric disturbances caused by the weapons can lead to weather and climate changes.<sup id="cite_ref-rob_13-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rob-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unexploded_ordnance">Unexploded ordnance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_war&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Unexploded ordnance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Unexploded_ordnance" title="Unexploded ordnance">Unexploded ordnance</a></div>
<p>Military campaigns require large quantities of <a href="/wiki/Explosive_weapon" title="Explosive weapon">explosive weapons</a>, a fraction of which will not <a href="/wiki/Detonate" class="mw-redirect" title="Detonate">detonate</a> properly and leave unexploded weapons. This creates a serious physical and chemical hazard for the civilian populations living in areas which were once war zones, due to the possibility of detonation after the conflict, as well as the leaching of chemicals into the soil and groundwater.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agent_Orange">Agent Orange</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_war&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Agent Orange">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Agent_Orange" title="Agent Orange">Agent Orange</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Before_and_after_spraying_agent_orange.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Before_and_after_spraying_agent_orange.jpg/220px-Before_and_after_spraying_agent_orange.jpg" width="220" height="283" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Before_and_after_spraying_agent_orange.jpg/330px-Before_and_after_spraying_agent_orange.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/28/Before_and_after_spraying_agent_orange.jpg 2x" data-file-width="375" data-file-height="483" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Before_and_after_spraying_agent_orange.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mangrove forests, like the top one east of Saigon, were often destroyed by herbicides.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Agent_Orange" title="Agent Orange">Agent Orange</a> was one of the <a href="/wiki/Herbicide" title="Herbicide">herbicides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Defoliant" title="Defoliant">defoliants</a> used by the <a href="/wiki/British_military" class="mw-redirect" title="British military">British military</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Malayan_Emergency" title="Malayan Emergency">Malayan Emergency</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_armed_forces" class="mw-redirect" title="United States armed forces">U.S. military</a> in its <a href="/wiki/Herbicidal_warfare" title="Herbicidal warfare">herbicidal warfare</a> program, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Ranch_Hand" title="Operation Ranch Hand">Operation Ranch Hand</a>, during the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>. An estimated 21,136,000 gal. (80 000 m³) of Agent Orange were sprayed across South Vietnam,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> exposing 4.8 million Vietnamese people to Agent Orange, and resulting in 400,000 deaths and disabilities, and 500,000 children born with birth defects.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Many Commonwealth personnel who handled and/or used Agent Orange during and decades after the 1948-1960 Malayan conflict suffered from serious exposure of dioxin. Agent Orange also caused major <a href="/wiki/Soil_erosion" title="Soil erosion">soil erosion</a> to areas in Malaya. An estimated 10,000 civilians and possibly insurgents in Malaya also suffered heavily from defoliant effects, though many historians likely agreed it was more than 10,000 given that Agent Orange was used on a large scale in the Malayan conflict and unlike the U.S., the British government manipulated the numbers and kept its secret very tight in fear of negative world public opinion.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Testing_of_nuclear_armaments">Testing of nuclear armaments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_war&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Testing of nuclear armaments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons_testing" title="Nuclear weapons testing">Nuclear weapons testing</a></div>
<p>Testing of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_armament" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear armament">nuclear armaments</a> has been carried out at various places including <a href="/wiki/Bikini_Atoll" title="Bikini Atoll">Bikini Atoll</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands" title="Marshall Islands">Marshall Islands</a> <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Proving_Grounds" title="Pacific Proving Grounds">Pacific Proving Grounds</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">New Mexico</a> in the US, <a href="/wiki/Mururoa_Atoll" class="mw-redirect" title="Mururoa Atoll">Mururoa Atoll</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maralinga,_South_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Maralinga, South Australia">Maralinga</a> in Australia, and <a href="/wiki/Novaya_Zemlya" title="Novaya Zemlya">Novaya Zemlya</a> in the former Soviet Union, among others.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Downwinders" title="Downwinders">Downwinders</a> are individuals and communities who are exposed to <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_contamination" title="Radioactive contamination">radioactive contamination</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fallout" title="Nuclear fallout">nuclear fallout</a> from atmospheric and/or underground <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons_testing" title="Nuclear weapons testing">nuclear weapons testing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_accidents" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear accidents">nuclear accidents</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Strontium_90">Strontium 90</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_war&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Strontium 90">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The United States government studied the post-war effects of a radioactive isotope found in nuclear fallout called <a href="/wiki/Strontium_90" class="mw-redirect" title="Strontium 90">Strontium 90</a>. The Atomic Energy Commission discovered that “Sr-90, which is chemically similar to calcium, can accumulate in bones and possibly lead to cancer”.<sup id="cite_ref-Lutts_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lutts-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Sr-90 found its way into humans through the ecological food chain as fallout in the soil, was picked up by plants, further concentrated in herbivorous animals, and eventually consumed by humans.<sup id="cite_ref-Kulp_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kulp-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Depleted_uranium_munitions">Depleted uranium munitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_war&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Depleted uranium munitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Depleted_uranium" title="Depleted uranium">Depleted uranium</a></div>
<p>The use of depleted uranium in <a href="/wiki/Munitions" class="mw-redirect" title="Munitions">munitions</a> is controversial because of numerous questions about potential long-term health effects.<sup id="cite_ref-MillerMcClain_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MillerMcClain-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  Normal functioning of the <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a>, and numerous other systems can be affected by uranium exposure, because in addition to being weakly radioactive, uranium is a <a href="/wiki/Toxic_metal" class="mw-redirect" title="Toxic metal">toxic metal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Craft04_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Craft04-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> It remains weakly radioactive because of its long <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-life</a>. The aerosol produced during impact and combustion of depleted uranium munitions can potentially contaminate wide areas around the impact sites or can be inhaled by civilians and military personnel.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In a three-week period of conflict in Iraq during 2003, it was estimated over 1000 tons of depleted uranium munitions were used mostly in cities.<sup id="cite_ref-gaurdian2003_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gaurdian2003-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Defense" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Department of Defense">U.S. Department of Defense</a> claims that no human <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> of any type has been seen as a result of exposure to either natural or depleted uranium.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Yet, U.S. DoD studies using cultured cells and laboratory rodents continue to suggest the possibility of <a href="/wiki/Leukemia" title="Leukemia">leukemogenic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genetic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reproduction" title="Reproduction">reproductive</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Neurological" class="mw-redirect" title="Neurological">neurological</a> effects from chronic exposure.<sup id="cite_ref-MillerMcClain_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MillerMcClain-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition, the UK Pensions Appeal Tribunal Service in early 2004 attributed <a href="/wiki/Birth_defect" title="Birth defect">birth defect</a> claims from a February 1991 <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a> combat veteran to depleted uranium <a href="/wiki/Poison" title="Poison">poisoning</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Campaign Against Depleted Uranium (Spring, 2004)<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Also, a 2005 <a href="/wiki/Epidemiology" title="Epidemiology">epidemiology</a> review concluded: "In aggregate the human epidemiological evidence is consistent with increased risk of birth defects in offspring of persons exposed to DU."<sup id="cite_ref-Hindin_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hindin-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_fuel_use">Fossil fuel use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_war&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Fossil fuel use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the high degree of mechanization of the military large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a> are used. Fossil fuels are a major contributor to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, issues of increasing concern. Access to oil resources is also a factor for instigating a war.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">United States Department of Defense</a> (DoD) is a government body with the highest use of fossil fuel in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> According to the 2005 <i>CIA World Factbook</i>, when compared with the consumption per country the DoD would rank 34th in the world in average daily oil use, coming in just behind Iraq and just ahead of Sweden.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intentional_flooding">Intentional flooding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_war&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Intentional flooding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Flooding can be used as <a href="/wiki/Scorched_earth" title="Scorched earth">scorched earth</a> policy through using water to render land unusable. It can also be used to prevent the movement of enemy combatants. During the <a href="/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War" title="Second Sino-Japanese War">Second Sino-Japanese War</a>, dykes on the Yellow and the Yangtze Rivers <a href="/wiki/1938_Yellow_River_flood" title="1938 Yellow River flood">were breached to halt the advance of Japanese forces</a>.  Also during the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Leiden" title="Siege of Leiden">Siege of Leiden</a> in 1573 the dykes were breached to halt the advance of Spanish forces. During Operation Chastise in Germany during WW2 the Eder and Sorpe river dams were bombed flooding a large area and halting industrial manufacture used by the Germans in the war effort.
</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/K5_Plan" title="K5 Plan">K5 Plan</a>, an attempt between 1985 and 1989 by the government of the People's Republic of Kampuchea to seal Khmer Rouge guerrilla infiltration routes into Cambodia, resulted in environmental degradation.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Militarism_and_the_environment">Militarism and the environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_war&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Militarism and the environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Human security has traditionally been solely linked to military activities and defense.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Scholars and institutions like the International Peace Bureau are now increasingly calling for a more holistic approach to security, particularly including an emphasis on the interconnections and interdependencies that exist between humans and the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Military activity has significant impacts on the environment<sup id="cite_ref-:5_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:6_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>. Not only can war be destructive to the socioenvironment, but military activities produce extensive amounts of greenhouse gases (that contribute to <a href="/wiki/Anthropogenic_climate_change" class="mw-redirect" title="Anthropogenic climate change">anthropogenic climate change</a>), <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>, and cause resource depletion, among other environmental impacts.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:6_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouse_gas_emissions_and_pollution">Greenhouse gas emissions and pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_war&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Greenhouse gas emissions and pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several studies have found a strong positive correlation between military spending and increased greenhouse gas emissions, with the impact of military spending on carbon emissions being more pronounced for countries of the Global North (ie: OECD developed countries)<sup id="cite_ref-:6_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:5_33-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>. Accordingly, the US military is estimated to be the number one fossil fuel consumer in the world<sup id="cite_ref-:7_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Additionally, military activities involve high emissions of pollution<sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:8_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>. The Pentagon’s director of environment, safety and occupational health, Maureen Sullivan, has stated that they work with approximately 39 000 contaminated sites<sup id="cite_ref-:8_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>. Indeed, the US military is also considered one of the largest generators of pollution in the world<sup id="cite_ref-:8_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>. Combined, the top five US chemical companies only produce one fifth of the toxins produced by the Pentagon<sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>. In Canada, the Department of National Defence readily admits it is the largest energy consumer of the Government of Canada, and a consumer of “high volumes of hazardous materials”<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>.
</p><p>Military pollution is a worldwide occurrence<sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>. Armed forces from around the world were responsible for the emission of two thirds of <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon" title="Chlorofluorocarbon">chlorofluorocarbons</a> (CFCs) that were famously banned in the 1987 <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a> for causing damage to the <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone layer</a><sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>. In addition, naval accidents during the Cold War have dropped at minimum 50 nuclear warheads and 11 nuclear reactors into the ocean, they remain on the ocean floor<sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_and_resource_use">Land and resource use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_war&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Land and resource use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Military land use needs (such as for bases, training, storage etc) often displace people from their lands and homes<sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>. Military activity uses solvents, fuels and other toxic chemicals which can leach toxins into the environment that remain there for decades and even centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, heavy military vehicles can cause damage to soil and infrastructure.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Military-caused noise pollution can also diminish the quality of life for nearby communities as well as their ability to rear or hunt animals to support themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Advocates raise concerns of environmental racism and/or environmental injustice as it is largely marginalized communities that are displaced and/or affected.<sup id="cite_ref-:9_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Militaries are also highly resource intensive.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_37-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:9_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Weapons and military equipment make up the second largest international trade sector.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The International Peace Bureau says that more than fifty percent of the helicopters in the world are for military use, and approximately twenty-five percent of jet fuel consumption is by military vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> These vehicles are also extremely inefficient, carbon intensive, and discharge emissions that are more toxic than those of other vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-:9_39-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Activist_responses">Activist responses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_war&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Activist responses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Military funding is, at present, higher than ever before, and activists are concerned about the implication for greenhouse gas emissions and climate change<sup id="cite_ref-:9_39-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>. They advocate for demilitarization, citing the high greenhouse gas emissions and support the redirection of those funds to climate action<sup id="cite_ref-:9_39-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>. Currently the world spends about 2.2% of global GDP on military funding according to the World Bank<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>. It is estimated that it would cost approximately one percent of global GDP yearly until 2030 to reverse the climate crisis<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>. Moreover, activists emphasize the need for prevention and the avoidance of costly clean up<sup id="cite_ref-:9_39-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>. Currently, the expense for cleaning up military contaminated site is at least $500 billion<sup id="cite_ref-:4_32-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>. Finally, activists point to social issues such as extreme poverty and advocate for more funding to be redirected from military expenses to these causes<sup id="cite_ref-:9_39-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>. 
</p><p>Groups working for demilitarization and peace include the <a href="/wiki/International_Peace_Bureau" title="International Peace Bureau">International Peace Bureau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Voice_of_Women_for_Peace" title="Canadian Voice of Women for Peace">Canadian Voice of Women for Peace</a>, The Rideau Institute, Ceasefire.ca, <a href="/wiki/Project_Ploughshares" title="Project Ploughshares">Project Ploughshares</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Code_Pink" title="Code Pink">Codepink</a>. See <a href="/wiki/List_of_anti-war_organizations" title="List of anti-war organizations">List of anti-war organizations</a> for more groups.
</p>
<h3><span id="The_military.27s_positive_effects_on_the_environment"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_military's_positive_effects_on_the_environment">The military's positive effects on the environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_war&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: The military&#039;s positive effects on the environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The military Is not, entirely in opposition to environmental protection<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>. There are examples from around the world of Nations’ respective departments of defense aiding in land management and conservation<sup id="cite_ref-:10_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>. For example, in Bhuj, India, military forces stationed there helped to reforest the area; in Venezuela, it is part of the National Guard’s responsibilities to protect natural resources<sup id="cite_ref-:10_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:10-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, military endorsement of environmentally friendly technology such as renewable energy may have the potential to generate public support for these technologies<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>. Finally, certain military technologies like GPS and drones are helping environmental scientists, conservationists, ecologists and restoration ecologists conduct better research, monitoring, and remediation<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>. 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="War_and_environmental_law">War and environmental law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Environmental_impact_of_war&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: War and environmental law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/War_and_environmental_law" title="War and environmental law">War and environmental law</a></div>
<p>From a legal standpoint, environmental protection during times of war and military activities is addressed partially by international environmental law.  Further sources are also found in areas of law such as general international law, the <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws of war">laws of war</a>, <a href="/wiki/Human_rights_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Human rights law">human rights law</a> and local laws of each affected country.
</p>
