<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Partial_pressures">Partial pressures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulmonary_gas_pressures&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Partial pressures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The partial pressures (in <a href="/wiki/Torr" title="Torr">torr</a>) for a human at rest:

<p>The alveolar oxygen <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial pressure</a> is lower than the atmospheric O<sub>2</sub> partial pressure for two reasons. 
</p>
<ul><li>Firstly, as the air enters the lungs, it is humidified by the upper airway and thus the partial pressure of water vapour (47 mmHg) reduces the oxygen partial pressure to about 150 mmHg.</li>
<li>The rest of the difference is due to the continual uptake of oxygen by the <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary" class="mw-redirect" title="Pulmonary">pulmonary</a> <a href="/wiki/Capillaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Capillaries">capillaries</a>, and the continual diffusion of CO<sub>2</sub> out of the capillaries into the alveoli.</li></ul>
<p>The alveolar pO<sub>2</sub> is not routinely measured but is calculated from blood gas measurements by the <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_gas_equation" title="Alveolar gas equation">alveolar gas equation</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pathology">Pathology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pulmonary_gas_pressures&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pathology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The partial pressure of carbon dioxide, along with the <a href="/wiki/PH#Living_systems" title="PH">pH</a>, can be used to differentiate between <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_acidosis" title="Metabolic acidosis">metabolic acidosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metabolic_alkalosis" title="Metabolic alkalosis">metabolic alkalosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_acidosis" title="Respiratory acidosis">respiratory acidosis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_alkalosis" title="Respiratory alkalosis">respiratory alkalosis</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Hypoventilation" title="Hypoventilation">Hypoventilation</a> exists when the ratio of carbon dioxide production to alveolar ventilation increases above normal values – greater than 45mmHg. If pH is also less than 7.35 this is <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_acidosis" title="Respiratory acidosis">respiratory acidosis</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Hyperventilation" title="Hyperventilation">Hyperventilation</a> exists when the same ratio decreases – less than 35mmHg. If the pH is also greater than 7.45 this is <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_alkalosis" title="Respiratory alkalosis">respiratory alkalosis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
