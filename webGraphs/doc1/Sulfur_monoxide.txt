<p><b>Sulfur monoxide</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> with <a href="/wiki/Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula">formula</a> <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">S</a><a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">O</a>. It is only found as a dilute gas phase.  When concentrated or condensed, it converts to S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<a href="/wiki/Disulfur_dioxide" title="Disulfur dioxide">disulfur dioxide</a>). It has been detected in space but is rarely encountered intact otherwise.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_bonding">Structure and bonding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure and bonding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The SO molecule has a triplet ground state similar to O<sub>2</sub>, i.e. each molecule has two unpaired electrons.<sup id="cite_ref-greenwood_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenwood-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The S−O bond length of 148.1&#160;pm is similar to that found in <a href="/wiki/Lower_sulfur_oxides" title="Lower sulfur oxides">lower sulfur oxides</a> (e.g. S<sub>8</sub>O, S−O = 148&#160;pm) but is longer than the S−O bond in gaseous <a href="/wiki/Disulfur_monoxide" title="Disulfur monoxide">S<sub>2</sub>O</a> (146&#160;pm), <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">SO<sub>2</sub></a> (143.1&#160;pm) and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide" title="Sulfur trioxide">SO<sub>3</sub></a> (142&#160;pm).<sup id="cite_ref-greenwood_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenwood-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The molecule is excited with near infrared radiation to the singlet state (with no unpaired electrons). The singlet state is believed to be more reactive than the ground state triplet state, in the same way that <a href="/wiki/Singlet_oxygen" title="Singlet oxygen">singlet oxygen</a> is more reactive than the <a href="/wiki/Triplet_oxygen" title="Triplet oxygen">triplet oxygen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_and_reactions">Production and reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production and reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Production of SO as a reagent in organic syntheses has centred on using compounds that "extrude" SO. Examples include the decomposition of the relatively simple molecule <a href="/wiki/Thiirane" title="Thiirane">thiirane 1-oxide</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> as well as more complex examples, such as a trisulfide oxide, C<sub>10</sub>H<sub>6</sub>S<sub>3</sub>O,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>4</sub>OS  → C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>4</sub> + SO</dd></dl>
<p>The SO molecule is thermodynamically unstable, converting initially to S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.<sup id="cite_ref-greenwood_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenwood-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> 
SO inserts into <a href="/wiki/Alkenes" class="mw-redirect" title="Alkenes">alkenes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alkynes" class="mw-redirect" title="Alkynes">alkynes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diene" title="Diene">dienes</a> producing molecules with three membered rings containing sulfur.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Generation_under_extreme_conditions">Generation under extreme conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Generation under extreme conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the laboratory sulfur monoxide can be produced by treating  sulfur dioxide with sulfur vapour in a glow discharge.<sup id="cite_ref-greenwood_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-greenwood-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It has been detected in single bubble <a href="/wiki/Sonoluminescence" title="Sonoluminescence">sonoluminescence</a> of concentrated <a href="/wiki/Sulfuric_acid" title="Sulfuric acid">sulfuric acid</a> containing some dissolved noble gas.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Chemiluminescence" title="Chemiluminescence">chemiluminescence</a> detector for sulfur has been reported<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> that is based on the reactions:
</p>
<dl><dd>SO + O<sub>3</sub> →  SO<sub>2</sub>(excited) + O<sub>2</sub></dd>
<dd>SO<sub>2</sub>(excited) →  SO<sub>2</sub> + hν</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">Occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ligand_for_transition_metals">Ligand for transition metals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ligand for transition metals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a ligand SO can bond in a number different ways:<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>a terminal ligand, with a bent M-S-O arrangement, analogous to bent nitrosyl</li>
<li>bridging across 2 or 3 metal centres (via sulfur), as in Fe<sub>3</sub>S(SO)(CO)<sub>9</sub></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Astrochemistry">Astrochemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Astrochemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sulfur monoxide has been detected around <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">Io</a>, one of <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>'s moons, both in the atmosphere<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and in the <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">plasma</a> <a href="/wiki/Torus" title="Torus">torus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It has also been found in the atmosphere of Venus,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> in the Hale-Bopp comet<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and in the <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_medium" title="Interstellar medium">interstellar medium</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">Io</a>, SO is thought to be produced both by volcanic and <a href="/wiki/Photochemistry" title="Photochemistry">photochemical</a> routes. The principal photochemical reactions are proposed as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>O  +  S<sub>2</sub>  →  S  +  SO</dd>
<dd>SO<sub>2</sub>  → SO  +  O</dd></dl>
<p>Sulfur monoxide has been found in the largest star known, <a href="/wiki/NML_Cygni" title="NML Cygni">NML Cygni</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Marvel1996_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marvel1996-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological_chemistry">Biological chemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Biological chemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sulfur monoxide may have some biological activity, the formation of transient SO in porcine coronary artery has been inferred from the reaction products.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_measures">Safety measures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Safety measures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because of sulfur monoxide's rare occurrence in our atmosphere and poor stability; it is difficult to fully determine its hazards. But when condensed and compacted, it forms <a href="/wiki/Disulfur_dioxide" title="Disulfur dioxide">disulfur dioxide</a>, which is relatively toxic and corrosive. This compound is also highly flammable (similar flammability to methane) and when burned produces <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>, a poisonous gas.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sulfur_monoxide_dication">Sulfur monoxide dication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_monoxide&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sulfur monoxide dication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>SO converts to disulfur dioxide (S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>).<sup id="cite_ref-lovas_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lovas-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Disulfur dioxide is planar molecule with C<sub>2v</sub> <a href="/wiki/Symmetry_group" title="Symmetry group">symmetry</a>. The S-O bond length is 145.8&#160;pm, shorter than in the monomer, and the S-S bond length is 202.45&#160;pm. The OSS angle is 112.7°.  S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> has a dipole moment of 3.17&#160;D.<sup id="cite_ref-lovas_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lovas-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
