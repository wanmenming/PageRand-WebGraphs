<p><b>Chromium(II) oxide</b> (CrO) is an <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_compound" title="Inorganic compound">inorganic compound</a> composed of <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is a black powder that <a href="/wiki/Crystallises" class="mw-redirect" title="Crystallises">crystallises</a> in the rock salt structure.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiberg&amp;Holleman_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiberg&amp;Holleman-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
Hypophosphites may reduce chromium(III) oxide to chromium(II) oxide:
</p>
<dl><dd>H<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>2</sub> + 2 Cr<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> → 4 CrO + H<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>4</sub></dd></dl>
<p>It is readily oxidized by the atmosphere. Chromium (II) oxide is basic, while CrO<span style="display:inline-block; margin-bottom:-0.3em; vertical-align:-0.4em; line-height:1.2em; font-size:70%; text-align:left;"><br />3</span> is acidic, and Cr<span style="display:inline-block; margin-bottom:-0.3em; vertical-align:-0.4em; line-height:1.2em; font-size:70%; text-align:left;"><br />2</span>O<span style="display:inline-block; margin-bottom:-0.3em; vertical-align:-0.4em; line-height:1.2em; font-size:70%; text-align:left;"><br />3</span> is amphoteric.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
